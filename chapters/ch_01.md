## Chapter 1: The Paused Frame

The children sat under painted Mars.

Someone had blacked a line across the old mural at adult shoulder height, too straight to be a child’s work. Below it, the nursery wall still showed red dunes, plastic habitats, a greenhouse dome with smiling carrots inside. Above it, the paint had been left alone: a bright sky no Martian child would ever have seen.

On the screen, Asterion remained crushed against nothing.

The image had been paused for eleven minutes because the youngest boy in the room had started counting hull ribs and would not stop. The moderator had muted the public channel, lowered the room lights by one step, and asked Dr. Venn to take over.

Mara stood beside a bin of cracked foam planets. The bin smelled faintly of disinfectant and canned mandarin, though no one was eating. The smell came from the snack cabinet, from old cartons ruptured at the seams, from some teacher’s attempt to make the Glass Room less like a clinic.

Asterion filled half the wall.

The ship was folded inward along its spine. One deck had gone through another at an angle no impact model could keep. Plates puckered around invisible pressure points. There was no wall behind it, no shadow, no body for the wreck to rest against. Only black vacuum, a smear where Orpheus’s camera had disagreed with itself, and Mars beyond: clean, red, lit as if waiting had remained an innocent verb.

The boy stopped counting at thirty-seven.

“Is it still there?” asked a girl near the carpet line.

The moderator, Ms. Alonzo, looked at Mara.

Mara kept her hands loose at her sides. Children watched hands. Adults watched titles.

“The wreck?” Mara asked.

“The glass.”

A few children turned toward the girl. They knew the word. They used it for the Boundary because the other word had been made by committees, and because glass could be seen through, broken by accident, pressed with a palm until the skin changed color.

“We can calculate where the Boundary affects instruments,” Mara said. “We do not know that it is glass.”

“But Asterion hit it.”

“We know Asterion was found there by Orpheus. We know the ship was damaged by something we cannot yet describe.”

“You always say ‘something.’”

“Yes.”

“Because if you say wall, people draw doors,” said a boy in the second row.

Ms. Alonzo closed her eyes for one second. Mara saw it and did not look at her again.

The Red Nursery had become Glass Room 12B after the Break. The school kept the old reading carpet because procurement forms took longer than grief. Each child sat inside a taped square. The tape was blue, cheerful, and peeling at the corners. Delay notice at the top of the screen: PUBLIC ARCHIVE IMAGE. SIX-YEAR CONTEXT PACKAGE. MODERATED VIEWING ONLY.

Six years, and still the first image could turn a classroom silent.

Mara had read the Orpheus forensic caption so many times the phrases lived in her mouth like old medication. Paused Asterion wreck frame recovered by Orpheus. Uncrewed recovery platform. Boundary-proximate coordinate. Visible Mars beyond target object. Unknown deformation cause. No stable surface detected.

She had helped write the version for schools.

A boy with clipped fingernails raised his hand, then spoke before Ms. Alonzo could call on him. “My mother says it’s not pain if it’s computed.”

The room moved without moving. A sneaker squeaked. Someone’s breath caught and turned into a cough.

Mara looked at the boy. Ten, perhaps. Narrow chin. Red marker on the heel of his left hand, a little door shape half scrubbed away.

“What is your name?” she asked.

“Avram.”

“Who is your mother speaking about, Avram?”

He glanced at the screen. Not at Mars. At the crushed dark where Asterion’s crew cabin had been.

“Them. Or us. She says if bodies are math, pain is math. So it doesn’t hurt the same.”

Ms. Alonzo’s fingers tightened around her tablet.

The approved answer waited. Mara had used it in ward pamphlets, family briefings, school packets. It had passed through the Continuity Office harm-language review and three religious advisory panels. It had survived hearings because it avoided doctrine.

“We do not know whether reality is artificial,” Mara said. “We do know that pain is part of lived experience. When a person says something hurts, our obligation is to care for that hurt.”

Avram stared at her. On the screen, Mars sat past the wreck with the terrible neatness of a fruit in a bowl.

“But if they can be wrong about real,” he said, “can they be wrong about hurt?”

The room waited for an adult to repair the sentence.

Mara put two fingers against the inside of her wrist.

She had taught candidates to do that when panic began converting the room into weather. Pulse first. Then task. Fear became smaller when numbered. Elias had made fun of her for it in Quito, two chairs away from a suspended Mars model, before anyone called him public property.

Her pulse was steady. That annoyed her.

“Pain does not need the universe to agree before it matters,” she said.

Avram’s mouth tightened, not disbelief exactly. More like a child tasting medicine and finding sugar in it.

“Then why did they have to go so far?”

Ms. Alonzo said, “Avram.”

“No,” Mara said, too quickly.

The moderator’s tablet chimed once, a compliance reminder or a warning from the room’s biometric monitor. The children knew the sound. Several looked down at their own hands.

Mara moved closer to the screen so the wreck was behind her left shoulder. She refused the old posture, presenter before evidence, but the room had been built to make it happen.

“They went because adults told them distance could carry hope,” she said. “They went because the mission had science to do. They went because countries and companies and schools wanted Mars to mean that human beings still had somewhere outward to go.”

One child whispered, “Marsing.”

“Yes,” Mara said. “Sometimes that word is fair. Sometimes it is cruel.”

“Did you tell them to go?” Avram asked.

Ms. Alonzo stood. “We are moving to grounding protocol.”

The screen dimmed by another increment. A soft chime sounded from the wall: inhale, hold, exhale. The children had been trained to follow it after difficult images. Mara hated the chime. It made breathing sound licensed.

Avram kept looking at her.

“I helped select the crew,” Mara said. “I helped prepare them.”

“Your brother was one.”

There it was, not accusation yet. Fact, placed on the carpet between them.

“Yes.”

“Was he scared?”

The approved school packet had a paragraph for this. Astronauts receive extensive training. Fear is a normal human response to high-risk work. The Asterion crew demonstrated strong group cohesion until the loss of signal. If a child asks about terror, do not speculate about final moments.

Mara saw Elias in the last clean public feed without looking back at the screen: his laugh off-camera, Leila’s hand near the tethered orange peel, one strap loose and drifting because Jun had failed to tuck it before the broadcast. Elias had laughed at something no one had archived properly. Ordinary sound. Pre-clip. Before the later records made laughter dangerous.

“I do not know,” she said.

Ms. Alonzo lowered the tablet.

Avram blinked. Children still expected adults to hoard knowledge. They had no category for an expert choosing the smaller answer.

Mara added, “I hope he was allowed to say so if he was.”

The room stayed with that. No chime covered it. A girl near the back began to pick at the tape around her square until the blue strip came up in a curl.

On the screen, Asterion’s broken hull remained pressed into an absence. Orpheus’s imaging suite had captured vented dust crossing the Boundary-proximate region in a thin gray fan. One frame showed no surface. The next showed a glossy disagreement, as if black space had become a bad scan of itself. In the public still, that moment had been frozen for evidence and, later, for everything evidence could not carry.

A boy asked if Mars weather was still real.

Mara said spectroscopy still returned dust movement over Valles Marineris.

“What’s spectroscopy?” someone asked.

“Color used carefully,” Ms. Alonzo said, too bright.

The children laughed because the answer was wrong enough to help.

Mara let the teacher keep it.

Glass Room Moderation Record 12B  
Houston District Learning Continuity Pilot  
Participant group: Red Nursery cohort, ages 8-10  
Session object: Paused Asterion wreck frame recovered by Orpheus  
Clinical observer: Dr. Mara Venn, IIA/Continuity  
Incident markers: one child referenced computed pain; one child named familial relation to Asterion crew; no self-harm statements; two grounding prompts accepted; snack refusal persisted in four participants  
Moderator note: Dr. Venn deviated from approved answer sequence at 10:42:18 local. Children remained regulated. Recommend review before future school deployments.

When the session ended, the children filed out past a painted rover with one wheel scratched away. Each placed a token in one of two bowls by the door: ready or not ready. The bowls had been introduced after a parent sued the district for requiring children to “perform confidence.”

Avram held his token, a red plastic disk, until the line stalled.

Mara crouched without touching him. “You can keep it.”

“Then the count is wrong.”

“The count is for the adults.”

He put the disk into not ready.

At the doorway, Ms. Alonzo handed out paper cups of water. A girl asked for plastic, then changed her mind. The line compressed, released. Sneakers on polished floor. A lunch cart rattled somewhere outside with the ordinary authority of noon.

Avram turned back. “If your brother says he wasn’t scared, will you believe him?”

Mara felt the old rumor move in her like a sealed compartment taking pressure. Not audio. Not verified. A family-adjacent transcript, copied badly, sent through a cousin who had cried before asking whether she already knew. Mara, leave the porch light on. Four words enough to make her unfit, if anyone proved she carried them into Witness.

“We would need the record,” she said.

“But you’re his sister.”

“Yes.”

“So you know his lying voice.”

Ms. Alonzo said his name softly.

Mara stood because her knees had begun to ache. “That is why I would need the record.”

Avram accepted this with a child’s contempt for adult rules that sounded true and useless. He followed the others into the hall.

The Glass Room emptied around Mara. The screen saver came up late, a district seal with three languages and a helpline. Behind the seal the paused frame lingered as a pale burn. Asterion through bureaucracy. Mars through Asterion. Nothing through everything.

Ms. Alonzo gathered the foam planets into the bin one at a time. Earth had teeth marks in the Pacific. Mars had been split and taped.

“You answered him,” she said.

“I answered near him.”

“He’ll remember the difference.”

Mara looked at the not ready bowl. Red disks stacked there, almost all of them.

“Is his mother in care?”

“Outpatient. Works nights at the desal plant. She’s not Exit, if that’s the next form.”

“I was asking clinically.”

“I know.” Ms. Alonzo’s shoulders dropped. “I’m tired of proving families are allowed to be frightened without being dangerous.”

Mara folded the blue tape curl flat with her shoe. “Any door drawings?”

“Bathroom stalls twice this month. One on a lunchbox. Avram’s hand today, but he says it was a game.” Ms. Alonzo put the cracked Mars into the bin. “They play wall now. One is Earth, one is Mars, one stands between. They keep changing the rule so somebody can win.”

Mara could hear the hall through the closed door: locker hinges, children arguing about bread, a teacher calling for indoor voices with the worn despair of a person who knew the ceiling had already failed to hold silence.

Her wrist console vibrated.

The first alert line was blank for half a second before the encryption caught up.

CONTINUITY ANNEX PRIORITY SUMMONS  
PAVEL ILYIN REQUESTS IN-PERSON REVIEW  
WITNESS STATUS: FINAL INTERPRETIVE WINDOW OPEN  
DELAY CLASS: RESTRICTED CIVIC HARM

Mara read it twice. The words remained.

Ms. Alonzo saw her face. “Is it the children?”

“No.”

“Then don’t lie with your pause.”

Mara turned the console toward her chest. “Witness has crossed into the final interpretive window.”

The teacher’s hand moved to the foam Mars. Her thumb found the tape seam and worried it once.

“Does that mean Candle?”

“It means the window in which Candle data, Asterion records, and public release planning begin to bind each other.”

“That is not a sentence I can give children.”

“No.”

“What should I tell them tomorrow?”

Mara looked at the dim screen. The helpline number glowed over the place where Mars had been.

“Ask if they ate breakfast,” she said. “Then tell them the adults are still working.”

Ms. Alonzo gave a short laugh with no pleasure in it. “That is also empty.”

“Yes,” Mara said. “But it might get them to lunch.”

The Annex was eighteen minutes away by service car and three security layers from ordinary weather.

Houston had rebuilt its flood roads after Galveston, then rebuilt them again after the inland surge. From the school exit, Mara saw a drainage crew eating sandwiches beside a pump truck. One man had a red stone on the dashboard. Another wore a shirt with the old Asterion launch patch, faded almost white from sun. Life kept borrowing symbols after the symbols failed, because cloth was cloth and a shirt could still cover a back.

The service car smelled of hot vinyl. Mara opened the school packet on her console and reviewed the deviation log while the driver took the underpass. Her own sentences appeared there stripped of breath.

Pain does not need the universe to agree before it matters.

She flagged it for review before anyone else could. Better to enter the breach herself. Better to name the leak before Pavel found the damp wall.

A public feed played on the car’s front panel with sound off. Boundary Vigils in Manila. A denial minister at a factory podium. São Paulo Night Mesh anniversary coverage: three repeated galaxies, printed now on cheap umbrellas because rain had its own timing. The captions moved too quickly to matter.

At a light, a child in the next lane pressed a palm to the car window. On the glass around her hand, someone had drawn a red circle in washable marker. Mars, maybe. Or a warning.

Mara looked down.

Her wrist still held two pale marks from her fingers.

The Houston Continuity Annex had no exterior sign. It occupied the low wing behind Mission Control, the part visitors never reached. The lobby carpet showed worn paths from security gate to elevator, elevator to vending machines, vending machines to the rooms named for calm seas. Tranquil. Beaufort. Sargasso. Old names for water placed over rooms where people learned to speak about fire.

The lights never dimmed fully. Even in power-saving mode they held a hospital glow.

At the inner checkpoint, the guard scanned Mara’s wrist and then her face.

“Dr. Venn.”

“Has Director Ilyin arrived?”

“Room Sargasso.”

“Who else?”

“Continuity legal. Science relay. Two family liaisons on secure hold.” He hesitated. “The vending machine’s got mandarins again.”

The wrong detail. The guard knew it as soon as he said it.

Mara looked past him to the machine. Rows of electrolyte broth. Nicotine gum. Canned citrus in small silver tins, each with a bright orange segment printed on the label.

Asterion’s last clean public frame had made oranges vanish from schools for three months. Then parents complained. Children liked oranges. Grief could not have every fruit.

“I’m fine,” she said.

He cleared her through.

Pavel was standing when she entered Sargasso. He wore a dark suit with no visible wrinkle, though the Annex had swallowed better men by breakfast. His glasses lay folded beside a stack of paper files. Paper, for gravity. Paper, for hearings. Paper, because screens could be clipped and looped by noon.

On the wall monitor, Witness telemetry crawled in delayed columns. Not raw data. Status language: BAND ONE OUTER DEGRADATION CONFIRMED. ARCHIVE LOCK GREEN. CREW COGNITIVE WATCH YELLOW. CANDLE HANDOFF MODEL WITHIN REVIEW MARGINS.

Final interpretive window open.

Pavel said, “Doctor Venn.”

“Director.”

“You were at a school.”

“Yes.”

“Did they ask?”

“They asked better questions than we do.”

He touched the bridge of his nose, then left the glasses on the table. “That habit of yours is expensive.”

“What happened?”

“Witness has entered the window early by six hours against public schedule, by ninety-one minutes against internal margin. The model drift is small enough for operations and large enough for politics.”

“Where is Nadine?”

“Onboard. Communication delay remains within plan. Captain Qureshi has acknowledged window status and declined ceremonial language.”

“That sounds like her.”

“Sera Imani has reclassified three incoming instrument packets from stable to contaminated.”

“Contaminated how?”

Pavel’s mouth worked once before he answered. “Scientifically, she says. Interpretively, she says that is your problem.”

Mara set her bag on the chair without sitting. “What packets?”

“Optical repeat, timestamp duplication, and an audio bleed.”

The room’s air changed. Or Mara’s body spent its ration of numbness.

“From Witness?”

“From archived Asterion material during a sync test. Probably a routing fault.”

“Probably is not a custody category.”

“No,” Pavel said. “It is a prayer people in this building use when the alternative is expensive.”

“Play it.”

“Not yet.”

Mara looked at him.

He slid a paper across the table. His nails were clean. At his left wrist, the old burn scar showed where his cuff had shifted. “You will sign renewed cognitive fitness. You will reaffirm no undisclosed family-specific Asterion material relevant to your interpretive role. You will accept monitored access for the first review.”

There it was, placed politely between them.

Mara read the line.

NO UNDISCLOSED FAMILY-SPECIFIC MATERIAL.

The letters held still. They had no idea how much damage they could do.

“Define relevant,” she said.

Pavel watched her face. “You always ask for the hinge.”

“I am asking for the door.”

“Any record, rumor, transcript, recording, image, or testimony pertaining to Elias Venn’s final condition, final speech, post-loss status, or private address to living family, obtained outside approved chain of custody.”

Mara placed the paper on the table. “That wording is new.”

“The window is new.”

“No. The suspicion is old.”

Pavel picked up his glasses, cleaned one lens with a cloth, then set them down again. “Doctor Venn, your brother is not the only person who can die from a recording.”

She hated him for choosing a true sentence.

On the monitor, Witness status refreshed. A yellow bar pulsed beside COGNITIVE WATCH. Then another line appeared: PATTERN REWATCH REQUESTS: TWO CREW, ONE DECLINED BY COMMAND.

“Who requested?” Mara asked.

“That is not why you are here.”

“It is exactly why I am here.”

“Nadine’s note says Vale and Malik. Sera refused to call hers a request.”

Mara almost smiled. It failed before reaching her mouth.

Pavel sat. “We need your public framing draft before Candle crosses Band Two.”

“That is ahead of review.”

“Everything is ahead of review now.”

“What has the Council authorized?”

“Class A release if stable. Class B summary if contaminated but necessary. Class C existence acknowledgment under seal.”

“And Class D?”

“Silence unless forced.”

Mara heard Avram: can they be wrong about hurt?

She pulled out the chair. Its legs dragged a low sound through the carpet. “What are you afraid is in the audio bleed?”

Pavel’s jaw tightened at the angle near his ear. “Asterion voices.”

“Which ones?”

“That is what we are asking you to help classify.”

“Before I hear it?”

“Before you hear it unmonitored.”

Mara sat at last. The room smelled of stale coffee and the canned citrus from the vending machines beyond the hall, sugar under metal.

On the side monitor, a live public dashboard showed Glass Room readiness rates by district. 12B had already uploaded: majority not ready. Moderator deviation pending review.

“You pulled my school session fast,” Mara said.

“Everything touching you is fast today.”

“Pavel.”

He leaned back. In hearings he became taller by language. Here, without audience, his age showed in the skin beside his eyes.

“There are three leak channels active,” he said. “One from a family server, one from a contractor we cannot yet name, and one from a market desk that knew Witness entered the window before two member states did. Mumbai moved six minutes before our advisory.”

“Then you have a leak problem.”

“I have a civilization problem wearing a leak mask.”

“Do not make that sentence my fault.”

“I am making it your access condition.” He pushed a second page toward her. “You helped select six dead people whose records are about to become a public instrument again. You trained them to make fear useful. You are Elias Venn’s sister. Every one of those facts disqualifies you. Every one makes you necessary.”

Mara took the page. Candidate names appeared in order, the old Quito registry: Sofia Arendt, Jun Park, Amara Okonkwo, Leila Haddad, Elias Venn, Mateo Ríos. Beneath each, an access tier. Beside Elias: SIBLING-CONFLICT PROTOCOL ACTIVE.

The room’s lights hummed.

In Quito, Elias had sat under the suspended Mars model and asked if the stress interview included snacks or only character assassination. The panel laughed because he wanted them to. Mara had written calm under social pressure, humor used to regulate group threat, strong medical self-monitoring. She had written recommended.

She had written it knowing his crooked finger tapped med-kit latches when he lied about fear.

“Who ordered sibling-conflict activation?” she asked.

“I did.”

“On what evidence?”

“On the evidence that I have met you.”

Mara folded the page once, cleanly, then unfolded it. A childish act, paper punishment. “I have no verified private audio.”

Pavel went still.

She had given him the hinge. Not the room behind it. Not the porch light.

“That is a lawyer’s answer,” he said.

“It is an evidence answer.”

“Is there unverified material?”

Mara looked at the monitor instead of his face. Witness, far away, was a string of delayed status lines inside a windowless room. The ship carried Nadine’s dry refusals, Sera’s corrections, Tomas’s ugly little craft with its empty couch. It carried Mara’s bunk with the ration packs she drank when sleep failed. It carried the pull of Asterion’s records like a hand under a door.

“Rumor exists everywhere,” she said.

Pavel exhaled through his nose. “You will put that in writing.”

“I will put chain-of-custody language in writing.”

“You will put enough.”

“For whom?”

“For the living.”

A silence opened. Not clean. Filled with HVAC, distant footsteps, the vending machine’s compressor starting up beyond the wall.

Mara said, “Play the public-safe portion.”

Pavel studied her. “You assume there is one.”

“There is always a public-safe portion. Sometimes it is only the sentence saying we are withholding the rest.”

His mouth made the smallest concession. “That sounds almost institutional.”

“It sounds like triage.”

He tapped the room console. “Sargasso playback. Restricted sample. Ten seconds. Audio only. Observer log active.”

The system answered in a neutral voice. “Confirm listeners.”

“Pavel Ilyin. Mara Venn.”

“Clinical monitor?”

“Deferred.”

Mara looked at him sharply.

Pavel said, “A courtesy. Do not make me regret it.”

The speakers clicked.

For two seconds there was only static, thin and dry.

Then a breath.

Not Elias. Mara knew that before the wish formed. A shorter inhale, clipped at the top. A cabin sound behind it, a tiny impact, utensil or loose cap. Someone laughed once far off, too degraded to place.

A woman’s voice entered mid-word.

“...not sky.”

The sample cut.

Mara’s fingers were already at her wrist.

Jun. Perhaps. Or a record wearing Jun’s terror badly. The famous duplicated cockpit laugh lived elsewhere, cult-cut into proof, stripped of the phrase that ruined it. That’s not sky. This was not that full record. This was a bleed, ten seconds with teeth.

Pavel watched her hand.

“Again,” she said.

“No.”

“Timestamp?”

“Duplicated.”

“Source channel?”

“Sync buffer during Witness archive lock.”

“Original Asterion match?”

“Disputed.”

“Do we have Mars visible in paired video?”

“No paired video released to this room.”

“Do we have it at all?”

Pavel put his glasses on. “You see why you are needed.”

“That is not an answer.”

“It is the answer I have authority to give.”

Mara stood so fast the chair struck the table. The paper files shifted, candidate names sliding under one another. Elias vanished beneath Leila’s access sheet.

She took one breath. Counted it because counting remained available.

“You summoned me because Witness entered the window,” she said. “You played me a contaminated Asterion bleed. You want my signature on conflict protocols and a public frame before I see the record.”

“I want you inside the procedure before the procedure has to decide whether to cut you out.”

“And if I refuse?”

“You return to schools and tell children the adults are working.”

That landed too close to the morning. Pavel saw it. He did not apologize.

The wall monitor refreshed again.

WITNESS INTERNAL NOTE  
COMMANDER QURESHI TO CONTINUITY ANNEX  
NO MARTYR LANGUAGE IN RELEASE PREP. NO SPECULATION ON CANDLE OCCUPANCY. SEND VENN IF CLEARED.  
END NOTE.

Nadine had written it like an order because it was one.

Mara read the note until the letters blurred, then steadied. Send Venn if cleared. Not because Nadine trusted her grief. Because Nadine needed someone who could tell a frightened public that an empty couch was empty on purpose, when the time came. Because Sera needed someone to keep a repeated star field from becoming a scripture by lunch. Because Avram had asked whether pain hurt if computed, and the approved answer had collapsed in the hand.

Pavel slid a pen across the table.

The pen stopped beside Mara’s thumb.

“No undisclosed verified family-specific material,” she said. “That is the sentence I can sign.”

“You are narrowing.”

“I am being accurate.”

“You are protecting yourself.”

“Yes.”

He looked at her for a long time. Then he took back the page, crossed out three words, wrote one above the line, and initialed it.

NO UNDISCLOSED VERIFIED FAMILY-SPECIFIC MATERIAL.

The lie was smaller now. Still load-bearing.

Mara signed.

The pen ink skipped on the last letter of her name. She pressed harder and tore the paper fiber.

Pavel gathered the forms. “Your shuttle to Pacific relay leaves in forty minutes. You will review Quito selection tape en route. We are reopening the crew-selection archive before Witness receives the next Asterion lock.”

“Why Quito?”

“Because every release argument begins there, whether we admit it or not.”

Mara reached for her bag. The room console still showed the sample timestamp field: DUPLICATE INDEX, CONTENT NONIDENTICAL.

At the doorway, she stopped.

“The child this morning,” she said. “Avram. His question goes into release-risk language.”

Pavel frowned. “A school incident is not mission evidence.”

“It is public consequence.”

“We have collapse models for that.”

“No,” Mara said. “You have adults answering adults. Put the question in.”

He removed his glasses.

There it was: the motion before omission.

“What was the question?” he asked.

Mara opened the door. The Annex hall glowed without mercy. Somewhere beyond the vending machines, someone had opened a tin of mandarins, and the syrup smell came through the filtered air as if the last clean frame had found a new room.

She kept her hand off her wrist.

“Whether pain hurts if it is computed,” she said.

Pavel wrote it down.
