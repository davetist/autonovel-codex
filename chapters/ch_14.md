## Chapter 14: Candle Static Test

Tomas had one knee braced against Candle’s hatch rim while the archive cradle warmed under his hands.

The craft’s interior light made every surface look underpainted. White shell, white restraint webbing, white panel foam already nicked by tool cases. No one had made Candle beautiful. Beauty invited cameras. Cameras invited someone to say vessel when the correct word was probe, and then someone else to say witness, and after that the couch became a seat waiting for a body.

“Archive cradle at twenty-one point six,” he said. “Seal pressure nominal. Slot A awake.”

Malik’s voice came from the hangar loop, thin through the open hatch. “Confirming hash handshake.”

“Handshake is not romance.”

“Never said it was, Vale.”

Tomas touched the first cartridge with two gloved fingers. The label had been printed, sealed, barcoded, signed by two committees, then marked again by him in black ink because printed labels shed authority in a hurry when a technician was upside down inside a narrow craft. ARCHIVE / HUMAN LOTTERY / A-01. He had written lullabies under it in small letters.

The screen over his wrist filled with entries.

Fifteen thousand eight hundred and twelve lullabies. Some had full audio. Some were sheet music scanned from paper. Some were a grandmother humming through bad hospital pickup, a father repeating the same two lines in a prison visitation booth, a classroom in Lagos singing off beat because the teacher had started too high. The archive protocol stripped personal identifiers from most submissions, then restored them in a sealed index because the Asterion Families’ Circle argued that nameless love became museum dust. Tomas had attended that hearing by delay and stared at a bolt pattern while lawyers turned lullabies into custody.

“Slot A checksum holds,” Malik said.

Tomas advanced the cradle. “Slot B awake.”

Recipes.

Salt restrictions for renal patients. Festival breads. Field stews scaled for thirty sandbag workers. A casserole with margin notes about which cousin hated onions. Instructions for jam that assumed the reader knew how ripe fruit smelled. He watched the progress bar move across the recipe index and pictured the file riding inside Candle after the tissue trays died and the clocks started arguing.

“Slot B holds,” Malik said. “You’re slow.”

“I’m reading.”

“You’re checking.”

“Those can be adjacent.”

From the hangar deck below, Sera said, “Adjacent is not equivalent.”

Tomas ducked his head enough to see her past the hatch rim. She stood beside the ground cart in compression sleeves, tablet tucked against her ribs. Nadine was farther back, one hand hooked near the yellow guardrail. Mara had arrived without noise. Tomas had not heard the lift cage, only noticed that Nadine’s posture had changed by half a degree, command shifting to include a harder witness.

Mara looked into Candle as if the craft were a room someone had died in already.

Tomas turned back to the cradle. “Slot C awake.”

Court transcripts.

The status light flickered amber, then steadied. He held still through three fan cycles. Court transcripts had drawn the ugliest lottery arguments. Whose law belonged in a human archive sent toward a place that had damaged every law of reach? Civil divorces, asylum hearings, land claims after flood retreat, testimony from miners with dust in their lungs, a child custody case where both parents had gone flat at different times and the grandmother had asked the judge whether she had to prove the children were real before she could keep them fed.

“Slot C hash mismatch,” Malik said.

Tomas’s mouth dried. “Read it again.”

“Reading. Secondary says clean. Primary flags line-ending normalization.”

Sera’s hand tightened on her tablet. “That is a file-format issue?”

“It’s a committee issue,” Tomas said. He scrolled. “Court systems still can’t agree on what a paragraph break means.”

“Can it contaminate packet structure?”

“No.” He opened the subreport and checked the repaired count, then the fallback image layer. “Static archive safe. Content intact. Malik, mark warning, non-operational.”

“Marked.”

Mara stepped closer to the hatch. “What court?”

Tomas found the line. “Dublin Family Court, post-Break guardianship sequence. Restricted summary only.”

Mara’s face did not move. She wore fatigue like a fitted garment, no loose edge to tug.

“Proceed,” Nadine said.

“Slot D awake.”

Children’s drawings.

The file index loaded slower because the images were large and badly behaved. Crayon scans. Tablet sketches. Photographs of paper held down by adult hands. Mars as a red circle with trees. Mars with a black bar through it. Mars and Earth connected by a ladder. Mars behind a door. Earth as a house under a roof of stars. One child had drawn Candle itself, though the public diagrams had never shown the final body. In the drawing it had wings. In the drawing the couch was a bed with a blanket.

“Where did that come from?” Mara asked.

Tomas zoomed the metadata before he could stop himself. “Glass Room 12B educational response packet. Houston District.”

Her gaze stayed on the screen.

The drawing showed a white craft with a round window Candle did not have. Inside the window, a stick figure lay flat. The figure had a red heart drawn over the chest, too large, almost covering the body. Above the craft the child had written in block letters: SEND SOMEONE WHO CAN SAY WHAT IT IS.

“Archive lottery accepted it?” Sera asked.

“It was in the educator batch,” Tomas said. “Low mass. High public representation score.”

“Representation of what?”

He closed the preview. “That’s above my clearance as a person.”

Nadine moved. The tiny shift traveled through the hangar: techs at the far carts lowered their voices, a fan rattle grew audible, the restraint straps inside Candle whispered against one another as the air recirculation stepped up for the static run.

“Slot D?” Malik asked.

Tomas watched the checksum complete. “Holds.”

He moved through the remaining archive groups more quickly: weather reports from years when weather had become a legal argument, jokes with cultural annotations no one would have time to read, last messages from Asterion families wrapped in a seal that made his tablet refuse preview. Each cartridge seated into the archive spine with a small click. Not sacred. A spring latch and a gold contact. A mass budget line that had survived thirty-seven cuts because no voting bloc wanted to be the one that removed lullabies from the probe headed toward the end of reach.

“Archive payload static test complete,” he said. “All lots retained. One non-operational format warning. No seal breach.”

“Log it,” Nadine said.

Malik confirmed.

Tomas backed out of the archive bay until his shoulder hit the couch frame.

He froze, then hated himself for freezing.

The couch occupied more room than any instrument. Human-rated restraint assembly, pressure hood folded behind the headrest, manual oxygen trunk routed under the left rail, med ports capped in blue. Its fabric had the faint gray of unused survival gear, sterile and accusatory. The static test tags still hung from the buckles. He had tied them himself: six paper strips with serial numbers, each one a little flag of failure.

Mara had seen the contact. Of course she had. She watched bodies around objects as if grief left fingerprints on hardware.

“Why is it still here?” she asked.

Tomas found a fastener on the wall and set his eyes there. M5 countersunk, captive washer, torque stripe intact. “You have the design brief.”

“I have the legal brief. Why is it still here?”

Sera looked down at her tablet, then back up. Nadine said nothing.

The old answer sat ready: sponsor class, insurance schedule, family injunction, rescue-capable definition under IIA Annex 4, orbital manufacturer threat of withdrawal, legislative vote math. It was a good answer because it contained no blood. Tomas had used it in review rooms until everyone looked tired enough to sign.

“The assembly is load-bearing for certification,” he said.

Mara waited.

He added, “Removing it reclassifies the craft.”

“Candle cannot perform rescue recovery.”

“Correct.”

“Candle cannot support an occupied Boundary approach without invasive rework.”

“Correct.”

“Then the couch is false capability.”

“It is certified hardware.”

“That is an answer to a different question.”

Tomas shifted and a tether clip tapped the couch rail. He heard the sound in his teeth.

Nadine’s voice stayed level. “Doctor Venn.”

“No,” Tomas said.

The word came out before he had built a sentence behind it. He looked down through the hatch at them. Sera’s eyes were on him now. Malik had gone quiet on the loop.

“No,” he repeated, less sharp. “She’s asking the right thing.”

Mara’s hand had moved to the inside of her wrist, two fingers resting there without pressure. Listening posture. Tomas had seen it in archive playback reviews and in crew safety briefings. He had disliked it at first, the clinical neatness of it, until he recognized the effort. Some people held rails. Some counted screws. Mara counted the body where it still answered.

He took a breath. Candle’s filtered air tasted of plastic and metal shavings. “It remains because four groups made removal more expensive than risk. Sponsor package. Family claims. Insurance category. Vote count. Everyone needed the ambiguity.”

“Everyone,” Mara said.

“I signed drawings.”

“Who asked for the couch to stay human-rated after the recovery function was cut?”

Tomas almost laughed. The laugh would have been ugly. “Which time?”

Sera said, “Tomas.”

“No, that’s the answer. Which time? The manufacturer asked when the sponsor default clause appeared. The families asked when the word unmanned showed up in the draft. The council asked when polling said probe-only lost seventeen votes. Medical asked for a clear removal statement and got buried under indemnity language. I asked for mass relief and got a commemorative plaque proposal.”

Nadine’s mouth tightened at the corner. “There is no plaque.”

“There was a plaque design.”

“Gone.”

“Yes.”

Mara stepped one boot onto the lower hatch rung. She did not climb in. The boundary between hangar and craft held her there. “Why did you stop fighting it?”

Tomas looked past her to the hangar wall where Candle’s umbilicals hung in white sleeves. The craft sat cradled in service arms under bright inspection lamps, too small for all the hunger attached to it. Probe. Coffin. Chapel, if someone needed candles and a body. He had built around those words without writing them anywhere.

“I didn’t,” he said.

The hangar loop gave a soft pop as Malik muted something ground-side.

Sera lowered her tablet. “Explain.”

Tomas pressed his thumb against the couch rail. The material stayed cool. “Static test step thirty-two.”

Malik came back. “Couch isolated.”

“Read the subline.”

A pause. He could hear Malik scrolling. “Manual life-support linkage continuity, left rail to suit loop. Status disabled by design hold. Automated isolation intact. Emergency bottled oxygen independent. No occupied approach certification pending invasive rework.”

Sera’s face changed first, not with surprise exactly, but with the painful irritation of a scientist finding a hidden variable in her own apparatus. “By design hold?”

Tomas said, “My hold.”

Mara’s fingers left her wrist.

Nadine climbed the two steps to the hatch platform. “Tomas.”

He had rehearsed this in maintenance silence for months, usually with better syntax. In the rehearsal he sounded plain. In his mouth now the words snagged.

“I disabled one manual linkage. The one that lets a strapped-in occupant bypass the automated isolation and draw from Candle’s main loop during a guidance fault. It’s a human-rating bridge. Old rescue architecture. If someone sits there during Band Two and the automation cuts life support to protect the avionics, the manual bridge gives them air long enough to keep broadcasting.”

Sera said, “You removed a survival path.”

“I opened a circuit and locked it under design hold.”

“That is removing a survival path.”

“For a scenario that should never be permitted.”

Nadine had reached the hatch. She did not enter Candle either. Her hand closed around the rim near his knee. “Who knows?”

“Me. Now you. Malik if he has ears.”

“I have ears,” Malik said from the loop, too softly.

“Anyone else?”

“The ground review package contains the line. It is buried in static test step thirty-two. I marked it as nonconforming pending occupied certification. Nobody opened the branch because we certify Candle unmanned.”

Sera’s voice thinned. “But the couch stayed human-rated in the political packet.”

“Yes.”

“Those two statements cannot both be operationally clean.”

“No.”

Mara looked at the couch, then at him. “Why that linkage?”

Tomas rubbed the edge of a label until the glove caught. “Because it matters only if someone tries to make Candle a human act at the wrong point.”

“Wrong point,” Sera said. “Be precise.”

“Band Two or deeper. Material cost. Antenna geometry still worth protecting. Life support isolated by automation. Occupant alive only if manual bridge remains.”

Nadine said, “And with your hold?”

“They have emergency bottled oxygen for cabin fault inside safe bands. Enough for a docking mishap, training accident, ground rescue scenario. Not enough for a human-occupied Boundary approach. Not enough to turn the couch into a pulpit.”

The word pulpit lay there, stranger than the engineering terms. Tomas felt heat under his collar. He had not meant to say it. Mara heard it as if he had opened an unmarked file.

“You didn’t trust command,” Nadine said.

He looked at her then. “I trusted you. I didn’t trust Earth.”

Nadine’s grip on the hatch rim stayed steady.

Tomas swallowed. “I have seen hardware repurposed after the paperwork said it wouldn’t be. Inspection pods. Cheap, ugly, easy to print. They became debris cutters with armed guidance. Everyone said the export lock made it impossible. Then impossible killed nine maintenance workers over Ceres Transfer Three.” He stopped. The old report images rose too clear: punctured suits, tumbling tool bags, a pod body rotating with his manufacturer stamp still visible through scarred paint. He had never told Witness crew the full version. Personnel knew. Files knew. The machine knew nothing.

Mara said, “So you made this one refuse.”

“One circuit refuses.”

“That’s still a choice.”

“Yes.”

It came out clean. He had expected relief. Instead his stomach turned as if the hangar had lost spin, though Witness had no spin here. The static lamps hummed. Candle’s couch sat empty with its tagged buckles. A child somewhere had drawn a blanket over it.

Sera folded her arms tightly. “This compromises test transparency.”

“It compromises future misuse.”

“You do not get to hide ethics inside wiring.”

“No,” he said. “I don’t.”

“Then why tell us now?”

He glanced at the archive cradle. “Because static test passes. Because we’re about to call Candle ready. Because if I keep letting the paperwork do the lying, the machine becomes cleaner than I am.”

No one spoke for several seconds.

From the far end of the hangar came the squeak of a cart wheel. A technician whispered an apology to another technician, then both fell silent. Outside the closed bay doors, Witness carried them toward a coordinate whose model shifted by the hour. Inside the bay, Candle remained bolted down and obedient.

Nadine stepped back from the hatch. “Run couch branch diagnostic.”

“Tomas already read it,” Sera said.

“I want the machine to say it out loud.”

Tomas opened the branch screen. His fingers had begun to shake, so he anchored his wrist against the panel edge and keyed the command slower than usual.

“Couch assembly static branch,” he said. “Restraint pyros safe. Seat pressure sensors nominal. Biomedical leads responsive to test load. Cabin suit loop automated isolation nominal. Emergency bottle A pressure green. Emergency bottle B pressure green.” He stopped at the line. It pulsed yellow. “Manual life-support linkage left rail to main loop disabled by design hold.”

“Right rail?” Nadine asked.

“Right rail carries monitoring, not air.”

“Show me.”

He pulled the schematic onto the wall display. The hangar lights dimmed half a step as the projection mapped Candle’s interior in blue and white lines. There was the couch, simplified to geometry. There were the loops: oxygen, coolant, biomedical, power. The disabled linkage appeared as a short amber gap no longer than a finger joint.

Mara looked from the gap to Tomas. “A person could still sit there.”

“Yes.”

“They could still be photographed there.”

“Yes.”

“They could still die there.”

His jaw tightened. “Yes.”

“But not long enough for a controlled occupied approach.”

“Not without rework.”

“Could someone rework it aboard Witness?”

Tomas answered too fast. “Yes.”

Nadine’s head turned.

He forced himself to continue. “Invasive. Ugly. Six hours if I help. Twelve or more if I obstruct. Less if ground sends the sealed retrofit procedure.”

Mara’s gaze flicked to Nadine, then away. Tomas saw the movement and understood there was another sealed thing aboard the mission. He felt the click of a hidden latch without seeing the part.

Nadine said, “There is no active retrofit order.”

Tomas held her eyes. “Good.”

Sera stared at the schematic. “If this leaks, the story will be sabotage.”

“Yes.”

“Not ethics. Sabotage.”

“Probably.”

“Families will say you stole witness from them.”

“Yes.”

“Sponsors will say you degraded insured rescue capacity.”

“They’ll use better lawyers.”

Mara said, “Exit channels will say we fear what a human would see.”

Tomas had missed that one in rehearsal. It struck under the ribs. He pictured Solenne Kade with her red marker, soft voice, patient questions. If they cannot prove the door is locked. If Candle has a couch and no one uses it, they will say the living refused testimony from the only instrument that mattered.

Nadine turned away from the schematic and faced the hangar deck. “Clear the bay to essential personnel.”

Technicians moved at once. No argument, no dramatic looks. Carts rolled back behind the yellow line. A panel cover clicked shut. Malik said, “Hangar loop narrowed. Recording remains under static test custody.”

“Good,” Nadine said. “No one erases anything.”

Tomas almost thanked her. The words would have made her responsible for his relief, so he bit them down.

Sera came up onto the platform, but stayed outside the hatch. Now all three of them made a half-ring around Candle’s opening: command, physics, interpretation. Tomas inside with the couch and his amber gap.

Nadine said, “How long has the hold been in place?”

“Since pre-integration at Gantry.”

“Before Witness launch.”

“Yes.”

“After final public certification.”

“After the public couch language froze. Before full craft mate.”

Sera exhaled through her nose. “You threaded the needle.”

“I made a small illegal hole in the needle.”

“Don’t get cute.”

“I’m not.”

Mara said, “Did you disable it because of Elias?”

Tomas had expected Nadine’s anger, Sera’s precision, Pavel’s eventual polished knife. He had not expected Mara to put her brother on the bench between them so early.

“No,” he said.

Her face stayed still, but the answer had landed wrong. Too blunt. Too bare.

He tried again. “Not personally. I didn’t know him.”

“You know what people made of him.”

“Yes.”

“You know what an empty couch near the Boundary asks a bereaved person to imagine.”

“Yes.”

“Then he is in the wiring whether you put him there or not.”

The hangar air seemed colder. Tomas looked at the couch tags. Six strips. Six crew. Not by design. He had cut them from the same sheet.

“I disabled it because a machine with a survivable-looking seat can become an argument people climb into,” he said. “Your brother is one of the reasons people would call that courage. So are Jun Park’s hands. So is Sofia’s command record. So is Leila’s prayer if someone gets it raw enough. So is Mateo with every child who thinks a person has to go say what it is. I can’t stop what they make of the dead. I can stop one valve.”

Mara’s mouth opened slightly, then closed. She looked back into Candle. For a moment Tomas saw the craft through her: the archive spine loaded with human fragments, the biological trays cold in their racks, the couch waiting under clean straps, the white shell hunched in the hangar like an undecided object.

Sera touched the projected amber gap with one finger. Her hand cast a small shadow on the schematic. “There is no safety case for hidden unilateral modification.”

“It wasn’t hidden from the machine.”

“That sentence is worse than silence.”

“I know.”

“Do you?”

He let the schematic blur, then forced it back into lines. “I know the difference between a fault and a refusal. I labeled it as a hold because I wanted someone to ask before it mattered. Nobody asked.”

Nadine said, “That is not enough.”

“No.”

“Say the rest.”

Tomas shifted. The couch rail pressed into his thigh. “I also wanted deniability. If no one asked, I could keep telling myself Candle was unmanned by design and not because I had helped make it harder to occupy. I could be the engineer, not the person making a moral claim through a connector.”

“Now?”

He looked at the amber gap. “Now I’m the person.”

Malik’s voice, very careful: “Commander, ground static review window opens in eight minutes. They’re expecting pass/fail with exceptions.”

“Ground can wait eight minutes,” Nadine said.

“They’ll ask why.”

“Let them develop character.”

Despite everything, Tomas smiled once. It vanished before anyone had to answer it.

Mara climbed one rung, finally bringing her head level with the hatch. “If the linkage remained intact, could an occupied Candle approach return better data?”

Sera answered before Tomas. “Physiological data, yes. Interpretive data, no. Consciousness has no proven status at the Boundary.”

“I asked Tomas.”

Tomas wanted to hide inside Sera’s sentence. It was clean, rigorous, correctly bounded. It did not smell of the couch fabric.

“Yes,” he said. “More data. Human biomedical response under transition stress. Manual report if comms held. Maybe a voice saying what instruments failed to classify.” He swallowed. “Also a body paying first.”

Mara nodded once.

Sera’s jaw worked. “That answer will be cut into recruitment material.”

“Most true answers can be cut,” Mara said.

Sera looked at her. “Do not make that sound elegant.”

“I am not.”

Nadine stepped between them by half a pace. “Enough.”

Tomas shut the branch screen. The schematic stayed on the hangar wall until Malik killed projection. The amber gap disappeared, leaving Candle whole again to anyone standing too far away.

That was the problem with diagrams. They told the truth only while displayed.

Nadine keyed her wrist comm. “Static test status to narrowed command log. Candle archive payload passes. Couch branch contains pre-existing design hold disabling manual left-rail life-support linkage. Automated unmanned architecture unaffected. Occupied certification invalid without rework. Commander review active.”

Malik repeated it back, stumbling only once over pre-existing. Nadine corrected him.

Sera said, “Scientific readiness?”

“Pending your statement,” Nadine said.

Sera looked into Candle, past Tomas. “For unmanned static operations, Candle passes. For any occupied claim, Candle fails. I want that exact split in the record.”

“Granted.”

Mara said, “Interpretive risk?”

Nadine turned to her.

Mara still stood on the rung. Her face was pale under the hangar lights, but her voice had returned to its work register. “The empty couch is already Class B in public imagination if imaged. With the disabled linkage, it becomes Class C-adjacent if leaked without context. It can be edited into proof of conspiracy, proof of cowardice, proof that a human witness was prevented from speaking.” She glanced at Tomas. “Also proof that someone aboard refused a coffin. That part will not travel as far.”

Tomas felt the words enter him slowly. Refused a coffin. He had said it in private for months. In Mara’s voice it sounded less pure and more dangerous.

Nadine nodded. “Recommendation.”

“Do not image Candle interior for public release. If couch status must be described, say empty, unmanned, not rescue-certified for Boundary approach. Do not mention the disabled linkage outside restricted operational context unless legal disclosure forces it.”

Sera said, “That is concealment.”

“That is context control.”

“Pavel will love that phrase.”

Mara’s eyes cut to her. “Then strike the phrase. Keep the action.”

Nadine said, “I decide release from shipboard safety. Mara drafts if needed. Sera marks evidence. Tomas answers hardware. No one gives Earth a martyr because we were embarrassed by an ugly chair.”

Chair. Not couch. The smaller word made it worse.

Tomas climbed out of Candle. His boots found the platform magnets with two soft clacks. For the first time since boarding Witness, he stood outside the craft while other people kept looking in. He had spent so many hours inside that little white body that the hangar seemed excessive, a cathedral built for maintenance mistakes. Lamps. Rails. Tool shadows. The white sea of painted floor.

Nadine came close enough that only the narrowed loop would catch her. “Can it be reenabled without your authorization?”

“Yes.”

“How?”

“Access panel under left rail. Physical bridge in a sealed kit. Firmware acknowledgement. Then leak checks. Then suit-loop test.”

“Where is the kit?”

“Locker H-12. Seal code under commander authority and hardware lead.”

“Change it.”

“I can’t without logging.”

“I didn’t ask for secrecy. Change it with logging. Add my authority. Add Sera as witness.”

Sera said, “Me?”

“You wanted transparency.”

Sera accepted that with a tight nod.

Mara said, “And me?”

Nadine looked at her for one second too long. “You are not witness to that kit.”

Mara’s hands went still at her sides.

Tomas expected argument. Mara gave none. That absence had more weight than protest. She stepped down from the rung and moved away from the hatch, stopping beside the archive cart. Her fingers hovered over the sealed Asterion family-message cartridge without touching it.

Nadine said to Tomas, “You should have brought this to me.”

“Yes.”

“Before launch.”

“Yes.”

“You put me in command of a craft with a hidden political fracture.”

“Yes.”

“I am angry.”

“I know.”

“No. You know hardware. This is command.” Her voice stayed low. “If Earth learns this before we control the sentence, Candle becomes a scandal before it becomes an experiment. The couch becomes the whole mission. Not the clocks. Not the tissue trays. Not the archive. That chair.”

Tomas looked through the hatch at the restraint webbing.

Nadine continued, “Asterion families sue. Sponsors scream fraud. Exit groups make hymns out of the missing valve. Denial blocs say we sabotaged rescue. Finite Earth says space engineers finally admitted the truth and still took the money. Pavel locks every file he can reach and calls it casualty prevention.”

Mara said, from the archive cart, “He will not be entirely wrong.”

No one answered that.

Nadine’s gaze did not leave Tomas. “Can Candle fly unmanned as planned?”

“Yes.”

“Can the disabled linkage affect unmanned telemetry, antenna geometry, archive burst, clocks, samples, guidance, abort?”

“No.”

“Can it trigger false fault cascades?”

“No. The branch is electrically quiet unless occupied mode is invoked.”

“Can ground misread it during live operations?”

“If they open occupied branch diagnostics. They shouldn’t.”

Sera made a small sound.

Tomas corrected himself. “They could.”

“Fix the display path,” Nadine said. “Any live diagnostic must show the split plainly: unmanned green, occupied invalid. No amber hidden under a subline.”

“That will expose the hold to ground if they look.”

“Good.”

He met her gaze. “That may force the scandal.”

“It may force command to own the sentence before someone else writes it.”

There it was, the difference between a hidden circuit and an order. Tomas felt shame move through him with mechanical patience, finding each contact.

“I’ll patch the display path,” he said.

Sera pointed at him. “No language that implies the craft is safer occupied than it is.”

“It isn’t safer occupied.”

“Say it in the interface.”

He almost objected that interfaces did not carry moral clarity, then stopped. “I’ll say it in the interface.”

Mara turned from the archive cart. “Use plain words.”

Tomas gave a short laugh without humor. “Plain words are what started this.”

“No,” Mara said. “Buried words did.”

He nodded. She had him there.

Malik cleared his throat over the loop. “Commander, ground review window in three minutes. Anika is already in channel queue.”

Nadine lifted her wrist. “Hold them with routine static reconciliation.”

“She’ll hear that as trouble.”

“She is paid to hear trouble.”

“Copy.”

The hangar settled into a smaller quiet. The service arms around Candle maintained their grip. A drop of condensation gathered on a coolant coupling and held there, too stubborn to fall in the low vibration. Tomas stared at it until it shook loose and darkened the white deck.

Mara came to stand beside him. Not close. Close enough for private speech if the loop ignored mercy.

“Did you read the family messages?” she asked.

“No. The seals block preview.”

“Did you try?”

He looked at her, offended before he understood why. “No.”

“Good.”

A pause.

Then she said, “Thank you,” and it struck him as the wrong thing.

His face heated. “Don’t.”

Mara accepted the correction. Her chin lowered a fraction.

“I didn’t do it cleanly,” he said.

“No.”

“I put all of you at risk.”

“Yes.”

“I also would do it again.”

“I know.”

That was worse than accusation.

Sera opened a new log on her tablet, stylus moving in hard strokes. Nadine spoke to Malik in clipped phrases about delay and custody. The static test had passed; the room behaved as if a pressure seal had failed.

Tomas went back into Candle for the display patch.

Inside, the couch waited with its tags. He pulled the left-rail access panel, and the disabled linkage sat exactly where he had left it: bridge socket capped, seal paint unbroken, his initials under the clear strip. T.V. A small mark. A hand raised too late.

He began to inventory aloud because otherwise his fingers would outrun his head.

“Panel six screws. Two quarter-turns. One cap. Seal paint intact. Bridge socket capped. Lead loop stowed. No abrasion. No heat mark.”

Mara heard him from outside. “Are you making a record?”

“Yes.”

“For us?”

“For me.”

He opened the interface text field. The old line appeared first.

MANUAL LIFE-SUPPORT LINKAGE: DISABLED BY DESIGN HOLD.

He deleted it.

The cursor waited.

Plain words.

He typed: COUCH CANNOT SUPPORT OCCUPIED BOUNDARY APPROACH WITHOUT COMMAND REWORK. UNMANNED CANDLE FUNCTIONS UNAFFECTED.

Sera climbed close enough to read over the hatch rim. “Add life support.”

He typed: MANUAL LIFE-SUPPORT BRIDGE OPEN. EMERGENCY BOTTLES LIMITED TO SAFE-BAND CABIN FAULT USE.

Nadine said, “Add no occupied certification.”

He typed it.

Mara said nothing.

Tomas saved the patch. The system asked for authority. He entered his code, then turned the tablet toward Nadine through the hatch. She entered hers. Sera added witness authentication with a thumb press that took two tries because the scanner disliked her cold skin.

The display refreshed.

Green lines for unmanned operation. Red block for occupied Boundary approach. No amber. No hidden subline.

Malik whistled once over the loop, then cut himself off. “Ground can see the revised status if they query full branch.”

“Let them,” Nadine said. “Package the static result.”

Tomas closed the panel and secured the screws. He stayed inside Candle longer than he needed, breathing shallowly under the low ceiling. The craft had become less innocent and more honest by a handful of words. That was all engineering often was: a load path named before it broke.

Outside, Anika Rao’s delayed voice entered the hangar channel from Earth, bright with distance and suspicion. “Witness, Quito/Houston relay. We show Candle static review hold. Please identify exception class.”

Nadine looked into the hatch. Tomas gave her the smallest nod he could manage.

She keyed the channel. “Exception class: occupied-mode invalidation. Unmanned Candle remains green.”

The delay swallowed the sentence. It would reach Earth after its own small crossing, land in rooms with coffee, carpet, flags, attorneys, family representatives, sponsor counsel, Pavel’s clean hands.

Tomas looked at the couch one last time before backing out.

One of the paper static tags had twisted loose from the lower buckle. It turned in the ventilation stream, blank side outward, then printed side, then blank again. He reached to still it, stopped, and let it move.
