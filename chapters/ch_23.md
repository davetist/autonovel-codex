## Chapter 23: What the Data Permits

The second line refused to stay short.

Mara had written it three times. Each version grew a defense, then a condition, then a corridor for Pavel to walk down with his careful shoes.

Candle was unmanned.

No human being was placed aboard Candle to extract testimony.

The archive bay hummed around the sentence. Screen light narrowed Sera’s face. Malik sat at the routing station with one knee braced under the console, fingertips resting above the transmit lock as if the packet had a pulse. Beyond the bulkhead, Witness kept its small ship noises: air through grilles, a latch ticking as metal cooled, breath clipped over an open command channel.

Mara deleted the third draft and left the two lines.

Nadine read them from behind her shoulder. “Good.”

“Thin,” Sera said.

“Also good,” Nadine said.

The delayed conference window opened in six panes. Quito/Houston relay arrived with the usual time marker at the top, black letters over gray: GROUND RESPONSE DELAY: 11 MIN 42 SEC. Pavel’s pane held his office in the Continuity Annex, the calm-sea nameplate half visible behind his shoulder. Banda Sea. Of course. His face had the flat polish of a man kept upright by lights.

Anika Rao appeared in another pane, Flight Dynamics, hair pinned badly, her jaw moving once before the audio caught her. Dr. Yuna El-Amin was listed audio-only. Three council observers sat muted under initials. One Asterion Families’ Circle legal representative blinked in and out as the signal corrected.

Pavel spoke from eleven minutes ago.

“Witness, we acknowledge receipt of the provisional Candle summary. Emergency Science Council position is that public release should be limited to operational facts until ground review of the full burst package. We advise against publication of Asterion overlay content at this stage.”

Mara watched his mouth finish before the sound did. That lag had begun to feel obscene. A man could build a firebreak from the past and call it care in the present.

Sera leaned toward her microphone. “Ground, define overlay content. Candle’s burst packet returned contradictory Asterion-associated fragments. The association is part of the event. Removing all record-derived material gives a false impression of clean instrument data.”

Nadine lifted one hand. “Timestamp before you answer.”

Malik marked the channel. “Witness response mark 17:42:09 ship.”

Mara typed beside the draft.

Candle final burst contained contaminated Asterion-associated overlays. The overlays are contradictory, incomplete, and not sequence evidence.

She stopped. Her fingers wanted to add: they are still the dead.

Sera glanced at the line. “Returned. Not contained. Contained implies storage integrity.”

Mara changed it.

Candle final burst returned contaminated Asterion-associated overlays.

Tomas sat on the far side of the archive bay floor, back against the lower cabinet, one hand closed around a strip of Velcro he had peeled from a tool wrap without noticing. He had left the command deck after closing the Candle systems pane. Nadine had ordered him to stay in the loop because hardware was still evidence. He looked less like a systems architect than a man waiting outside a ward.

“What about the couch?” he asked.

Everyone heard the wrong thing in it first.

Nadine turned. “Candle remained unmanned.”

“No. I mean the public line. The Exit channels already have empty throne. If we say only unmanned, they keep the throne.”

Pavel’s delayed face watched them without yet knowing Tomas had spoken.

Mara placed her hands flat on the console. The metal carried a tremor that might have been attitude correction or her own body becoming inconvenient.

“We name the couch,” she said.

Tomas looked up.

Sera said, “Careful.”

“Candle included an empty human-rated couch by legal and design history,” Mara said. “It remained empty by command decision, hardware hold, and mission ethics.”

Nadine’s mouth tightened.

Mara glanced back. “Too much?”

“Too polished. No sermon around command.”

Mara rewrote.

Candle carried an empty human-rated couch. It remained empty. Occupied Boundary approach was invalid by hardware hold and command seal. Witness command did not concur with any human-occupied approach.

Tomas’s fingers worked the Velcro into a soft curl.

“That says it,” he said.

“It also invites injunction review,” Pavel said from the past, answering Sera now. “The Families’ Circle has already filed notice that any crew-associated fragment returned through Candle may constitute family evidence.”

Mara almost laughed. The sound rose sharp and dry in her throat. Family evidence. Elias reduced to a category with a custody stamp. Ruth had asked for one sentence that was not public property. Mara had hidden it, played it, damaged the chain, and then called damage a professional problem because that was the nearest container that would hold.

Nadine heard the laugh before it became sound. “Doctor Venn.”

“I am here.”

“Then stay in the text.”

Mara nodded once.

The conference panes held their delayed judgment. In eleven minutes Pavel would hear the couch line. In eleven more he would try to sand it down. The public feeds would not wait that long. Malik’s side monitor showed leak channels already filling with wrong names for Candle’s silence: OFFER REFUSED. THRONE EMPTY. PROBE CAME BACK WITH VOICES. THEY LEFT THE SEAT FOR YOU.

He muted the monitor without being asked.

Sera opened the Asterion overlay grid. It appeared in stacked cells, each bordered red for contamination. Sofia. Jun. Leila. Amara. Mateo. Elias-like. No cell was clean. The archive refused even the courtesy of equal damage.

“Before public language,” Sera said, “record facts. If we omit the contradictions, we publish a cleaner lie.”

Pavel’s last answer continued. “The Council is not asking Witness to falsify the record. We are asking you to avoid releasing raw ambiguity during an active collapse environment.”

Mara said, “Raw ambiguity is already out. They are naming the couch.”

“That is not the same as the Asterion material,” Pavel said, late, polished. “Your brother is not the only person who can die from a recording.”

Nobody moved for one breath.

The sentence had been waiting for her since Houston. She had hated him for it then because it was useful and cruel. Now it landed among the stale mandarin packets clipped beside the emergency ration shelf, the cold handrail polished by their touching, Tomas’s peeled Velcro. It did not absolve him. It did not release her.

Malik’s station chirped twice.

He looked down. “Continuity clinical desk is pushing an active alert.”

Nadine said, “Read.”

“St. Odran’s and Manila Children’s have requested a pre-release feed pause for Glass Rooms attached to pediatric wards. They cite suspected leak language around Candle couch. Phrase is already inside one supervised classroom queue.”

Mara’s tongue pressed hard against the back of her teeth.

“Which phrase?” Sera asked.

Malik read from the alert. “They left the seat for you.”

Tomas made a sound under his breath. A screw stripped in soft metal would have sounded cleaner.

Pavel was still talking from the past about collapse environment. His face did not know that children had already received a sentence built from their omission.

Nadine said, “Revise viewing guidance now.”

Mara opened a new section.

RELEASE PACKAGE: PUBLIC CARE AND ACCESS CONDITIONS

Her first version was too broad.

Do not use this material in unsupervised pediatric settings.

Yuna’s audio came in with ground delay, answering a prior request. “If Witness proceeds before full ground review, add clinical framing at top. First screen. No scroll.”

Mara moved the section up. Her fingers moved faster than her breath.

Public access condition: pediatric Glass Rooms, collapse wards, end-of-life units, detention units, and school queues should pause automatic playback until a trained moderator confirms room readiness. If a leak phrase involving Candle seating or personal invitation is present in the room, use local grounding protocol before viewing.

“Local grounding protocol sounds like a manual,” Nadine said.

“It is a manual,” Mara said.

“In front of children?”

Mara deleted the last sentence and wrote:

If viewers have already heard language suggesting invitation, exit, survival, or a reserved seat, stop playback. Give names, weather, food, and one next action before resuming.

Sera read it once. “Keep reserved seat?”

Tomas shook his head. “No. It gives them hardware. It gives them the bracket.”

“What bracket?” Mara asked.

He rubbed the Velcro strip flat along his thumb. “Seat mounts are brackets. A reserved seat sounds empty on purpose. Like we installed absence. Say Candle couch if you have to. Say the ugly part.”

Mara looked at him. “That is useful.”

“It was wrong first.”

“Yes.”

She changed the line.

If viewers have already heard language suggesting invitation, exit, survival, or the Candle couch as a place for a person, stop playback. Give names, weather, food, and one next action before resuming.

Yuna would revise it later. Clinicians would dislike the bluntness and then use it because blunt words stopped faster.

A legal artifact pushed into Malik’s queue before Mara could return to the grid. He put it on the side display.

CONTINUITY OFFICE RELEASE ENVIRONMENT ALERT  
CLASS: PEDIATRIC / CLINICAL / MARKET  
TRIGGER: CANDLE COUCH INVITATION LANGUAGE  
AFFECTED SITES CONFIRMED: MANILA CHILDREN’S GLASS ROOM 4, ST. ODRAN’S WARD SCREEN B, MUMBAI BOUNDARY FUTURES ANNEX SIDE FEED  
REQUEST: PAUSE PUBLIC AUTOPLAY PENDING CARE NOTE INGESTION  
FIELD REPORT: One pediatric queue repeated phrase before moderator intervention. One ward patient refused scheduled feeding after hearing “left the seat.” One exchange analyst removed from floor after attempting to open restricted stream on personal device.  
REQUIRED ACTION: Add invitation-language warning above mission facts. Route to moderators before public frame opens.

Nadine read it without moving. “There. That replaces the abstract fight.”

Pavel’s pane still spoke from old time. “We must not create an object of worship out of an engineering compromise.”

Tomas looked at the alert. “Too late.”

His voice was small. No speech in it. Just inventory.

Mara inserted the alert reference into propagation debts, then opened the Asterion section again.

Sofia first. The disputed order would become blame by morning if it arrived as rumor. The record cell showed the familiar scar: duplicated timestamp; two manual-correction durations; source dispute; Claire Arendt’s privacy injunction flagged in the margin; denial-state overlay risk high.

Mara opened the summary note.

Commander Sofia Arendt’s restricted command material includes a possible limited manual attitude correction order under conditions of contradictory coordinates. The available records duplicate and conflict. The material cannot establish human error as cause of Asterion’s loss.

Sera read over her shoulder. “Add that it cannot clear human action either.”

Claire would hate it. Denial blocs would cut around it. Sofia, if the record had any mercy left in it, might have preferred the harder line.

Mara added:

It also cannot exclude crew action under unknown instrument failure. No causal assignment is supported.

Nadine said, “That will hurt her daughter.”

“Yes.”

“Leave it.”

The bay’s audio popped. Ground packet arriving. Malik fed it into the conference queue. Pavel’s current response to the couch line would arrive later. For now, Anika’s earlier voice filled the archive bay.

“Witness, Flight Dynamics confirms Candle external object lock is lost for mission purposes. We have no recovery track. Be careful with destroyed. We cannot support destroyed. We cannot support intact. We can support no reliable object lock.”

Tomas pressed the Velcro curl against his thumb until the skin blanched.

Mara changed Candle is gone for mission purposes to:

Candle no longer has reliable external object lock or usable guidance telemetry. Its physical state cannot be established from present available data.

“Good,” Sera said.

Jun’s cell waited below Sofia’s. The waveform still carried the shape that had fed the Exit streams for years: the laugh after the ship should have had no cockpit left to laugh inside. The public derivative had made it bright, almost glad. The uncut copy broke it open with the clipped phrase beside it, in conflicting relation to the laugh.

That’s not sky.

Mara had heard it enough times that the words had lost grammar and become a bruise.

Sera said, “Context. No raw laugh.”

Mara nodded. “And we separate the laughs.”

Malik turned from routing. “Please.”

His mother had gone flat after a raw contractor dump. He had been the one to tell Mara that the difference between a laugh and a weapon could be nine seconds of missing context.

Mara typed.

The public feed from mission day 118 includes Elias Venn laughing off camera before signal loss. A separate corrupted cockpit record associated with Jun Park includes a duplicated laugh in a disputed post-failure timestamp group. These are distinct materials. They must not be combined as evidence of crew affect, survival, release, or invitation.

The Jun-associated cockpit record also includes the phrase “That’s not sky” in conflicting relation to the laugh. This context will be released in transcript and analytic note. Raw audio remains restricted due to known and documented self-harm recruitment use.

Pavel’s pane refreshed. His eyes went to some off-screen note. “Witness, your couch language is unacceptable in public form. It exposes unresolved sponsor and family litigation and risks turning an engineering feature into a civic altar.”

Tomas looked at the pane as if Pavel had touched him.

Nadine opened her mic. “It is already an altar.”

“Nadine,” Pavel said, though he was answering a past she had not yet sent. “The public does not need every internal failure in order to understand the relevant fact.”

Nadine waited for the timestamp.

Malik said, “Command response mark 17:49:32.”

Nadine said, “Ground, the relevant fact is the internal failure. We left a couch in a probe and refused to fill it. That is the sentence. We are sending it.”

Mara kept her eyes on the text. Nadine’s anti-martyr rule had always sounded like command discipline. No martyrs. No theater. Now it had become public grammar. A refusal needed nouns, or someone else would supply them.

Leila’s cell opened with two tracks drawn against the same timestamp. Checklist on left. Prayer on right. Same cabin pressure. Same repair signature. Family restriction: raw devotional content sealed. Red Orthodoxies misuse risk high. Exit misuse risk high. Clinical effect unknown, divided. Mara saw Leila’s hands in the recovered video, the elastic wound twice around a sample vial, the small pause after she noticed she had washed past necessity.

Do not make my prayer do your argument’s work, Leila had said in an earlier log, precisely enough that it still governed the room.

Mara wrote:

Leila Haddad’s tray-four record includes file-simultaneous checklist and private prayer tracks under a disputed timestamp group. The public package will identify the structure of the record and provide non-devotional context: sample sequence, tray-four rupture, mass-variance uncertainty, and Leila Haddad’s prior statement that procedure and prayer are distinct. Raw prayer audio remains family-restricted.

Sera nodded. “Add: does not establish that prayer altered the event.”

“Or that the event answered it,” Mara said.

She typed both.

Pavel’s voice returned. “We can support transcript summaries of selected Asterion material after ground clinical review. We cannot support shipboard public interpretation of prayer content, post-failure audio, or personal-address fragments.”

Mara’s fingers stopped on the last phrase.

Personal-address fragments.

Her throat tightened around mandarin acid. She had drunk syrup from a ration pouch after Candle’s first burn because her hands needed something to do and because shame sometimes chose the closest available taste. The sweetness had coated her mouth with the last public frame: orange peel near Leila’s hand, Elias laughing off camera, Mars red and impossible behind them.

Nadine said, “That one separately.”

“No,” Mara said. “Now.”

Sera turned.

Mara opened the sealed note she was still authorized to draft but no longer alone could adjudicate. The file name carried the ugly mercy of procedure:

AST-MD118-MED-VMIX-LOWCONF / PERSONAL ADDRESS / CLASS C / FAMILY-CLINICAL SEAL

The phrase itself remained hidden behind restricted reveal. Mara had set the access to require Sera plus Nadine plus clinical reviewer. She had earned that lock by breaking the earlier one.

“I should not write the family access sentence,” Mara said.

Yuna’s audio-only line clicked. Her voice came through with ground delay, from a clinical room on Earth where someone had probably forgotten to eat. “Doctor Venn, your restriction remains. You may draft public existence language. You may not select or prepare Ruth Venn’s private file without reviewer concurrence.”

“I am asking for reviewer concurrence.”

Sera’s hand went to the old ring mark under her sleeve. “For what action?”

“Log existence under restricted family and clinical seal. Withhold raw fragment from public release. Send Ruth one private reviewed transcript rendering with context. No raw audio. The sentence she requested. The sentence I contaminated.”

Tomas said, wrongly, “Could send her the waveform with a filter.”

Mara looked at him.

He swallowed. “Sorry. I mean, if you strip the gain spikes and cut the carrier hash, she could hear the timber without the bad edges. Like deburring a bracket before it takes skin off.”

“It is not a bracket,” Sera said.

Tomas nodded too fast. “I know. Wrong tool.”

“It is also a known recruitment vector, a corrupted record, a chain breach, and a possible archive collision,” Sera said.

Nadine asked Mara, “Can you write it without reaching for him?”

Mara’s first answer was no. It came with Elias’s crooked finger against a med-kit latch, his voice asking if people had eaten, his private log telling her conversion was useful and dangerous. Then the answer changed shape under the weight of Ruth’s kitchen, the piano silent since the Break, a porch light that had become a legal hazard.

“I can write what I did,” Mara said.

She opened the restricted log.

Personal-address material associated with apparent Elias Venn and addressed to Mara Venn exists in a low-confidence, contaminated audio-bearing fragment. Mara Venn had prior unauthorized transcript exposure and later committed an Elias-specific chain breach by privately replaying the verified audio-bearing fragment. Raw audio remains Class C and will not be included in the public release.

Her hands had steadied. That seemed unfair.

She continued.

Because Ruth Venn previously requested family custody of one private Elias sentence, a reviewed transcript rendering of the apparent sentence will be transmitted to Ruth Venn under restricted family/clinical seal with the following context: low-confidence vocal match, disputed timestamp association, possible archive bleed or household transcript collision, no evidence of survival after death, no instruction, no public claim.

Sera read the paragraph twice.

Yuna’s delayed voice came through. “Clinical condition: the private transmission must include live support contact, no autoplay, no raw waveform, no repeat function in the family portal for first seventy-two hours.”

Ruth would despise that. Ruth would understand it. Ruth might never forgive Mara for either part.

Nadine said, “Add it.”

Mara added it.

Pavel’s pane moved. He had received the first version of the personal-address proposal; his face shifted before his voice arrived. He removed his glasses.

There it was. The old burn scar at his wrist, pale under the office light.

“Doctor Venn,” he said, “you are proposing selective private release of an interpretively lethal fragment to your own mother while withholding it from the public. Every faction will call it a family privilege.”

Mara almost answered before the timestamp. Nadine’s hand cut once through the air.

Wait.

They waited eleven minutes with the sentence on the table. Malik checked the packet locks. Sera reviewed Leila’s context note. Tomas made no sound. The ship’s central display, mirrored from command, showed Mars beyond the coordinate, clean in the optical feed. Dust weather over Valles Marineris continued as measurable light. The visible planet refused every easy accusation.

At mark, Mara opened the mic.

“Ground, the fragment is not privilege.”

Her voice came out flatter than anger, almost clinical.

“It is not public evidence. It is not proof of contact, survival, artificiality, or instruction. It is a contaminated personal-address fragment with known lethal use if stripped of context. We will name its existence and restriction. We will not pretend it is absent. Ruth Venn receives one reviewed transcript rendering because family-specific material was requested under legal hold before Candle, because concealment would falsify the custody record, and because withholding from her while acknowledging it to institutions would be contempt.”

Pavel would hear that later. He would make a corridor through it if he could.

Mara added, after a breath, “Mara Venn will not prepare the support context alone.”

Yuna said, delayed, “Accepted pending ground legal review.”

Nadine said, live, “Accepted onboard.”

Sera said, “Accepted scientifically as restriction, not validation.”

Malik marked all three.

Amara’s cell was easier until it was not. The systems note had always looked plain, almost ugly.

The hull is remembering shapes it never had.

In another copy: held.

Mara saw Amara’s broad hands stripping a fitting down to its shame, saying a child could love Mars and a gasket could not. That line had already escaped into private forums through paraphrase. If released raw, it would become mystic metal by dinner. If buried, engineers would learn from a sanitized structural note that pretended language had not failed at the same time the hull did.

She wrote:

Amara Okonkwo’s bay-two systems annotation describes stress features that crossed discontinuous material and did not match known Asterion geometry. Her phrase “the hull is remembering shapes it never had” appears in one duplicated note; a second copy reads “held.” The phrase is released as engineering distress language attached to structural scans, not as evidence of intention, memory in matter, or external design.

Sera made a small sound. Agreement, maybe pain.

“Do we include the alternate held?” Nadine asked.

“Yes,” Sera said. “Contradiction belongs to the record.”

“People will choose the prettier one,” Tomas said.

“Then they will choose it from an honest page,” Mara said.

Mateo’s cell stayed closed one second longer under her hand.

The children’s astronaut. The basalt lesson. The line Public Learning had wanted: Mars is waiting for all of us. His refusal of waiting had become a small act the edit nearly swallowed. His angry log had broken the classroom wall open: They put Mars in classrooms so no one could cancel us.

Mara had once written symbolically mission-critical beside his name as if naming the pressure kept it from being used. She had been very good at building clean containers for dirty acts.

She opened the note.

Mateo Ríos’s public education records and private MD115 log show that Asterion’s public program placed emotional burden on children before the Boundary event. The public package includes his revised basalt lesson and a contextual excerpt from his private objection, with family notification: “They put Mars in classrooms so no one could cancel us.” This release does not assign blame for Asterion’s loss. It admits institutional use of childhood trust.

Nadine read it. “Legislatures will chew that.”

“They should.”

Pavel would hate the sentence because it gave Earth a bill due before the Boundary. Finite Earth channels would cut it into a verdict against every launch. Space loyalists would call Mateo ungrateful. Children in Red Nurseries would hear an adult admit they had been made witnesses with crayons.

Mara added:

The package must not imply that longing for Mars is a moral failure. It must not answer children’s questions with abandonment.

Ines had given her that brown coat button in Dublin, chipped at one edge from Thomas Keane’s failed morning. Bread first, then names. Weather. One choice small enough to survive. Mara touched the pocket where the button was stitched inside her flight vest. She had sewn it there badly. The thread scratched when she bent.

She opened the release guidance section.

Public viewing guidance: Releases should be accompanied by ordinary care prompts, especially for minors and collapse-risk patients. Eat before viewing when possible. Watch with another person. Keep names, weather, and next bodily needs available. Do not use this package in unsupervised pediatric settings, end-of-life wards, detention units, or collapse recovery rooms without trained support.

Sera stared at the line. “This is not science text.”

“No,” Mara said.

Yuna’s delayed voice answered an earlier note. “Add medication continuity. Do not interrupt scheduled care for live viewing.”

Mara added it.

Malik said, “Public desk wants the title again.”

The field waited at the top, blank and white.

Before Mara could touch it, Malik’s muted side monitor flashed red.

He leaned in. “Legal notice.”

Nadine said, “From?”

“Families’ Circle counsel. Filed to Ground and mirrored to us. They object to any public acknowledgment of family-sealed Elias-associated material before Ruth Venn’s receipt. They are requesting an emergency hold on the personal-address subsection.”

Mara felt the notice in her jaw first. The hinge tightened until her teeth met.

Pavel’s reply arrived to Mara’s private-release defense. “You are making a distinction the public will not honor.”

Mara looked at his pane. “Yes.”

He continued, not hearing. “If we name withholding, we create demand for the withheld material. If we bury it, later discovery destroys the package.”

Sera whispered, “That is the whole problem.”

Pavel’s mouth tightened. “The casualty model for raw personal-address release remains severe. Manila class, possibly worse. Exit channels already have couch language. The Makers’ Address is preparing an operator-contact petition tied to Candle’s final burst. Denial blocs are framing the lost object lock as proof of sabotage. This is not a classroom.”

Mara said, to the delayed man and to herself, “It is always a classroom after we let children watch.”

No one corrected her.

Nadine pointed to Malik’s screen. “Can legal hold transmission?”

“Ground can. We can proceed under shipboard emergency authority. But the objection will attach to custody.”

“Doctor Venn,” Sera said.

Mara looked at the personal-address subsection. The first version had been clean enough to injure Ruth twice: once by naming the fragment publicly, once by letting Earth know before Ruth did that her son had been turned into a sealed appendix.

She revised under the red legal notice, each word a smaller cut.

A low-confidence Elias-associated personal-address fragment exists and is restricted under Class C. Raw release is prohibited. Family-specific handling is governed by a restricted legal and clinical process already in motion before public package transmission. No phrase, audio, waveform, or family recipient is included in the public package.

Sera read it. “You removed Ruth.”

“For public packet.”

“And the custody record?”

“Names her.”

Nadine said, “Does that satisfy the objection?”

Malik looked at the notice. “It satisfies no one. It reduces injury.”

“Send counsel the revised subsection,” Nadine said.

Malik routed it.

Mara’s ribs hurt, as if she had been holding breath for someone else. The revision was worse for her and better for Ruth. Good. That was still possible.

The title field remained blank.

Sera opened the scientific disclaimer and began dictating. Her voice had grit in it now. “Candle’s findings do not establish artificiality. They do not establish external contact. They do not establish survival after death. They do not establish that Mars is false. They do not license claims of practical control over the Boundary.”

Mara typed.

Nadine added, “They establish that an unmanned craft entered a region where our evidence rules degrade.”

Sera said, “Yes.”

Tomas said, “They establish the couch stayed empty.”

Nadine said, “Yes.”

Malik’s alert pane refreshed again. The live artifact sat there like a blood pressure reading.

PEDIATRIC QUEUE REQUEST UPDATED: PHRASE REPEATED BY TWO STUDENTS BEFORE FEED PAUSE.  
EXCHANGE REQUEST UPDATED: AUTOMATED TRADING HALT DENIED BY OVERSIGHT BOT; HUMAN SUPERVISOR APPEALED.  
WARD REQUEST UPDATED: PATIENT ACCEPTED WATER AFTER SCREEN COVERED.

Mara read the last line twice. Accepted water. The universe had not become kinder. A hand had lifted a cup.

Truth could be shaped without becoming false. She had mistrusted that sentence because Pavel lived inside its worst version. He had shaped truth into corridors, valves, quarantine doors. He had saved some people and buried others under calm language. She had wanted the opposite to be purity: everything open, every waveform raw, the dead delivered without institutional fingerprints.

Raw release was also a shape. It turned a person into whatever wound found them first.

Mara wrote the title.

WHAT THE DATA PERMITS

No one spoke for several seconds.

Sera looked at it, then at Mara. “Permits is good. It does not say proves.”

Nadine said, “It also does not promise comfort.”

Tomas gave a short laugh. Broken at the end. “No danger there.”

Pavel’s pane froze for two seconds, his face caught with his glasses halfway off. When motion returned, he was answering something old.

“Witness, if you proceed under emergency shipboard release authority before ground vote, you will carry institutional liability.”

Nadine opened the mic. “Ground, command understands.”

Pavel, still late: “Doctor Venn personally will carry public blame for classification choices involving Elias-associated material.”

Mara opened her mic only when Malik pointed.

“Ground, Doctor Venn understands.”

Yuna’s audio came through. “Cognitive safety objection remains noted. Doctor Venn is compromised.”

Sera looked at Mara.

Mara said, “Doctor Venn agrees.”

That surprised them more than any refusal would have. Nadine’s hand, hooked near the rail, tightened once.

Mara continued, “Compromise is part of the custody record. It is not a reason to make the record lie. Keep my breach in the restricted chain and methodology note. Do not publish the private phrase. Do publish that Elias-associated personal-address material exists and is withheld under Class C because raw release would create foreseeable harm.”

Pavel’s delayed silence had more weight than his words.

Malik asked, “Public statement in archive bay or command?”

“Command,” Nadine said. “No hiding in the library.”

The move took less than a minute. Mara unhooked from the restraint bar and followed Nadine through the narrow corridor. The handrails carried the shine of skin oil where every crew member had held on during broadcasts and burns. A strip of sterilized fabric had come loose at one panel seam and fluttered in the ventilation. Someone had left a half-empty mandarin pouch clipped to a wall loop. The sweetness reached Mara through the packet foil.

Sera walked behind her with the release tablet clutched to her chest. Tomas came last, still carrying the Velcro strip. Malik moved ahead to command, already building the transmit stack.

The command deck was dimmer than the archive bay. Mars sat on the central display, a red disk with weather bands processed in false color on one side and optical clean on the other. Beneath it, Candle’s telemetry pane stayed closed. Tomas had made that choice. The absence looked like work.

Nadine took the command chair and stayed standing beside it. “Record public statement. Joint release package, shipboard emergency concurrence. Mark all raw Class C exclusions named in appendix, unattached.”

Malik’s hands moved over the panel. “Recording live to ship archive. Public release delayed buffer armed. Ground mirror pending.”

Sera said, “One more line before statement.”

Mara waited.

“Do not say the release changes nothing about the dead. That is too big.”

“I was not going to.”

“You might.”

Mara looked at her. Sera’s eyes were red from screen light and held wakefulness. Precision had cost her sleep, kindness, ease. It had not made her cold.

“I might have,” Mara said.

Sera nodded once.

Nadine gave the order. “When ready.”

Mara stood at the public console. The camera indicator remained dark for the first five seconds, then opened to a small green point. She had spoken to Glass Rooms before. Houston 12B. Children with ready and not-ready bowls. Avram asking whether pain hurt if it was computed. She had answered badly then, maybe not falsely, but from a height that failed him.

She set Ines’s coat button on the console edge, where the camera could not see it.

“Begin,” Malik said.

Mara read from the draft until the draft became too smooth. Then she looked at the lens.

“This is Dr. Mara Venn aboard Witness, authorized interpretive officer for the Boundary release environment. I am speaking under joint shipboard review with Captain Nadine Qureshi, Dr. Sera Imani, Candle systems architect Tomas Vale, and communications officer Malik Serrano.
