## Chapter 23: What the Data Permits

The second line refused to stay short.

Mara had written it three times. Each version added a defense, then a condition, then a corridor for Pavel to walk down with his careful shoes.

Candle was unmanned.

No human being was placed aboard Candle to extract testimony.

The archive bay hummed around the sentence. The screen light made Sera’s face look thinner than it was. Malik sat at the routing station with one knee braced under the console, fingertips resting above the transmit lock as if the packet had a pulse. Beyond the bulkhead, Witness kept its small noises: air moving through grilles, a latch ticking as the metal cooled, someone’s clipped breath over an open command channel.

Mara deleted the third draft and left the two lines.

Nadine read them from behind her shoulder. “Good.”

“Insufficient,” Sera said.

“Also good,” Nadine said.

The delayed conference window opened in six panes. Quito/Houston relay arrived with the usual time marker at the top, black letters over gray: GROUND RESPONSE DELAY: 11 MIN 42 SEC. Pavel’s pane held his office in the Continuity Annex, the calm-sea nameplate half visible behind his shoulder. Banda Sea. Of course. His face had the flat polish of a man who had been awake too long under lights that forgave nothing.

Anika Rao appeared in another pane, Flight Dynamics, hair pinned badly, her jaw moving once before the audio caught her. Dr. Yuna El-Amin was listed audio-only. Three council observers sat muted under initials. One Asterion Families’ Circle legal representative blinked in and out as the signal corrected.

Pavel spoke from eleven minutes ago.

“Witness, we acknowledge receipt of the provisional Candle summary. Emergency Science Council position is that public release should be limited to operational facts until ground review of the full burst package. We advise against publication of Asterion overlay content at this stage.”

Mara watched his mouth finish the sentence before the audio did. That lag had begun to feel obscene. A man could arrange a firebreak from the past and call it care in the present.

Sera leaned toward her microphone. “Ground, define ‘overlay content.’ Candle’s burst packet includes contradictory Asterion-associated fragments, but the association is part of the event. Removing all record-derived material gives a false impression of clean instrument data.”

Nadine lifted one hand. “Timestamp before you answer.”

Malik marked the channel. “Witness response mark 17:42:09 ship.”

Mara typed beside the draft.

Candle final burst contained contaminated Asterion-associated overlays. The overlays are contradictory, incomplete, and not sequence evidence.

She stopped. Her fingers wanted to add: they are still the dead.

Sera glanced at the line and corrected it aloud as if reading an instrument. “Not ‘contained.’ ‘Returned.’ Contained implies storage integrity.”

Mara changed it.

Candle final burst returned contaminated Asterion-associated overlays.

Tomas sat on the far side of the archive bay floor, back against the lower cabinet, one hand closed around a strip of Velcro he had peeled from a tool wrap without noticing. He had left the command deck after closing the Candle systems pane. Nadine had ordered him to stay in the loop because hardware was still evidence. He looked less like a systems architect than a man waiting outside a ward.

“What about the couch?” he asked.

Everyone heard the wrong thing in it first.

Nadine turned. “Candle remained unmanned.”

“No. I mean the public line. The Exit channels already have empty throne. If we say only unmanned, they will keep the throne.”

Pavel’s delayed face watched them without yet knowing Tomas had spoken.

Mara put her hands flat on the console again. The metal carried a faint tremor that might have been the ship’s attitude correction or her own body becoming inconvenient.

“We name the couch,” she said.

Tomas looked up.

Sera said, “Careful.”

“Candle included an empty human-rated couch by legal and design history,” Mara said. “It remained empty by command decision, hardware hold, and mission ethics.”

Nadine’s mouth tightened at the last phrase.

“Too much?” Mara asked.

“Too clean,” Nadine said. “No poetry around command.”

Mara rewrote.

Candle carried an empty human-rated couch. It remained empty. Occupied Boundary approach was invalid by hardware hold and command seal. Witness command did not concur with any human-occupied approach.

Tomas’s fingers worked the Velcro into a soft curl.

“That says it,” he said.

“It also invites injunction review,” Pavel said from the past, answering Sera now. “The Families’ Circle has already filed notice that any crew-associated fragment returned through Candle may constitute family evidence.”

Mara almost laughed. The sound rose sharp and dry in her throat. Family evidence. Elias reduced to a category with a custody stamp. Ruth had asked for one sentence that was not public property. Mara had hidden it, played it, damaged the chain, and then called damage a professional problem because that was the nearest container that would hold.

Nadine heard the laugh before it became sound. “Doctor Venn.”

“I am here.”

“Be here in the text.”

Mara nodded once.

The conference panes held their delayed judgment. In eleven minutes Pavel would hear the couch line. In eleven more he would try to sand it down. The public feeds would not wait that long. Malik’s side monitor showed leak channels already filling with wrong names for Candle’s silence: OFFER REFUSED, THRONE EMPTY, PROBE CAME BACK WITH VOICES, THEY LEFT THE SEAT FOR YOU.

He muted the side monitor without being asked.

Sera opened the Asterion overlay grid. It appeared in stacked cells, each bordered red for contamination. Sofia. Jun. Leila. Amara. Mateo. Elias-like. No cell was clean. The archive refused even the courtesy of equal damage.

“Before we argue public language,” Sera said, “we decide what record facts the package must carry. If we omit the contradictions, we publish a cleaner lie.”

Pavel’s last answer continued. “The Council is not asking Witness to falsify the record. We are asking you to avoid releasing raw ambiguity during an active collapse environment.”

Mara said, “Raw ambiguity is already out. They are naming the couch.”

“That is not the same as the Asterion material,” Pavel said, still late, still polished. “Your brother is not the only person who can die from a recording.”

Nobody moved for one breath.

The sentence had been waiting for her since Houston. She had hated him for it then because it was useful and cruel. Now it landed in the archive bay among the stale mandarin packets clipped beside the emergency ration shelf, the cold handrail polished by all their touching, Tomas’s peeled Velcro. It did not absolve him. It did not release her.

Mara opened a new section.

RELEASE PACKAGE: ASTERION-ASSOCIATED RETURNED OVERLAYS

She typed headings, then took them back out. Headings made the dead look arranged.

Sofia first. Not because command deserved hierarchy, but because the disputed order would become blame by morning if it arrived as rumor. The record cell showed the familiar scar: duplicated timestamp; two manual-correction durations; source dispute; Claire Arendt’s privacy injunction flagged in the margin; denial-state overlay risk high.

Mara opened the summary note.

Commander Sofia Arendt’s restricted command material includes a possible limited manual attitude correction order under conditions of contradictory coordinates. The available records duplicate and conflict. The material cannot establish human error as cause of Asterion’s loss.

Sera read over her shoulder. “Add that it cannot clear human action either.”

Claire would hate it. Denial blocs would cut around it. Sofia, if the record had any mercy left in it, might have preferred the harder line.

Mara added:

It also cannot exclude crew action under unknown instrument failure. No causal assignment is supported.

Nadine said, “That will hurt her daughter.”

“Yes.”

“Leave it.”

The bay’s audio popped. Ground packet arriving. Malik fed it into the conference queue. Pavel’s current response to the couch line would arrive later. For now, Anika’s earlier voice filled the archive bay.

“Witness, Flight Dynamics confirms Candle external object lock is lost for mission purposes. We have no recovery track. Be careful with the word ‘destroyed.’ We cannot support destroyed. We cannot support intact. We can support no reliable object lock.”

Tomas pressed the Velcro curl against his thumb until the skin blanched.

Mara changed Candle is gone for mission purposes to:

Candle no longer has reliable external object lock or usable guidance telemetry. Its physical state cannot be established from present data.

“Good,” Sera said. Then, because she was Sera: “Present available data.”

Mara changed it.

Jun’s cell waited below Sofia’s. The waveform still carried the shape that had fed the Exit streams for years: the laugh after the ship should have had no cockpit left to laugh inside. The public derivative had made it bright, almost glad. The uncut copy broke it open with the clipped phrase beside it, not cleanly before or after.

That’s not sky.

Mara had heard it enough times that the words had lost grammar and become a bruise.

Sera spoke softly. “We release context, not raw laugh.”

Mara said, “We name both laughs.”

Malik turned from routing. “Please.”

His mother had gone flat after a raw contractor dump. He had been the one to tell Mara that the difference between a laugh and a weapon could be nine seconds of missing context.

Mara typed.

The public feed from mission day 118 includes Elias Venn laughing off camera before signal loss. A separate corrupted cockpit record associated with Jun Park includes a duplicated laugh in a disputed post-failure timestamp group. These are distinct materials. They must not be combined as evidence of crew affect, survival, release, or invitation.

The Jun-associated cockpit record also includes the phrase “That’s not sky” in conflicting relation to the laugh. This context will be released in transcript and analytic note. Raw audio remains restricted due to known self-harm recruitment use.

Sera’s finger hovered near the screen. “Known and documented.”

Mara added it.

Pavel’s pane refreshed. His eyes went to some off-screen note. “Witness, your couch language is unacceptable in public form. It exposes unresolved sponsor and family litigation and risks turning an engineering feature into a civic altar.”

Tomas looked at the pane as if Pavel had touched him.

Nadine opened her mic. “It is already an altar.”

“Nadine,” Pavel said, though he was answering a past she had not yet sent. “The public does not need every internal failure in order to understand the relevant fact.”

Nadine waited for the timestamp.

Malik: “Command response mark 17:49:32.”

Nadine said, “Ground, the relevant fact is the internal failure. We left a couch in a probe and then refused to fill it. That is the sentence. We are sending it.”

Mara kept her eyes on the text. Nadine’s anti-martyr rule had always sounded like command discipline. No martyrs. No theater. Now it had become a public grammar. A refusal needed nouns, or someone else would supply them.

Leila’s cell opened with two tracks drawn against the same timestamp. Checklist on left. Prayer on right. Same cabin pressure. Same repair signature. Family restriction: raw devotional content sealed. Red Orthodoxies misuse risk high. Exit misuse risk high. Clinical effect unknown, divided. Mara saw Leila’s hands in the recovered video, the elastic wound twice around a sample vial, the small pause after she noticed she had washed past necessity.

“Do not make my prayer do your argument’s work,” Leila had said in an earlier log, not to Mara, not to anyone living now, but precisely enough that it still governed the room.

Mara wrote:

Leila Haddad’s tray-four record includes file-simultaneous checklist and private prayer tracks under a disputed timestamp group. The public package will identify the structure of the record and provide non-devotional context: sample sequence, tray-four rupture, mass-variance uncertainty, and Leila Haddad’s prior statement that procedure and prayer are distinct. Raw prayer audio remains family-restricted.

Sera nodded. “Add: does not establish that prayer altered the event.”

“Or that the event answered it,” Mara said.

She typed both.

Pavel’s voice returned. “We can support transcript summaries of selected Asterion material after ground clinical review. We cannot support shipboard public interpretation of prayer content, post-failure audio, or personal-address fragments.”

Mara’s fingers stopped on the last phrase.

Personal-address fragments.

Her throat tightened around mandarin acid. She had drunk the syrup from a ration pouch after Candle’s first burn because her hands needed something to do and because shame sometimes chose the closest available taste. The sweetness had coated her mouth with the last public frame: orange peel near Leila’s hand, Elias laughing off camera, Mars red and impossible behind them.

Nadine said, “We take that one separately.”

“No,” Mara said. “We take it now.”

Sera turned.

Mara opened the sealed note she was still authorized to draft but no longer alone could adjudicate. The file name carried the ugly mercy of procedure:

AST-MD118-MED-VMIX-LOWCONF / PERSONAL ADDRESS / VENN-RUTH CROSS-REFERENCE / CLASS C / FAMILY-CLINICAL SEAL

The phrase itself remained hidden behind restricted reveal. Mara had set the access to require Sera plus Nadine plus clinical reviewer. She had earned that lock by breaking the earlier one.

“I should not write the family access sentence,” Mara said.

Yuna’s audio-only line clicked. Her voice came through with ground delay, from a clinical room on Earth where someone had probably forgotten to eat. “Doctor Venn, your restriction remains. You may draft public existence language. You may not select or prepare Ruth Venn’s private file without reviewer concurrence.”

“I am asking for reviewer concurrence.”

Sera’s hand went to the old ring mark under her sleeve. “For what action?”

“Log existence under restricted family and clinical seal. Withhold raw fragment from public release. Send Ruth one private verified sentence with context, not raw audio. The sentence she requested. The sentence I contaminated.”

The room held too much air.

Tomas said, wrongly, “Could send her all of it.”

Mara looked at him.

He swallowed. “Sorry. I mean. If it’s hers.”

“It is not only hers,” Sera said. No coldness. Just the load-bearing wall. “It is also a known recruitment vector, a corrupted record, a chain breach, and a possible archive collision.”

Tomas nodded fast. “I know. I know.”

Nadine asked Mara, “Can you write it without trying to keep him?”

Mara’s first answer was no. It came with Elias’s crooked finger against a med-kit latch, his voice asking if people had eaten, his private log telling her conversion was not rescue. Then the answer changed shape under the weight of Ruth’s kitchen, the piano silent since the Break, a porch light that had become a legal hazard.

“I can write what I did,” Mara said.

She opened the restricted log.

Personal-address material associated with apparent Elias Venn and addressed to Mara Venn exists in a low-confidence, contaminated audio-bearing fragment. Mara Venn had prior unauthorized transcript exposure and later committed an Elias-specific chain breach by privately replaying the verified audio-bearing fragment. Raw audio remains Class C and will not be included in the public release.

Her hands had steadied. That seemed unfair.

She continued.

Because Ruth Venn previously requested family custody of one private Elias sentence, the reviewed text of the apparent sentence will be transmitted to Ruth Venn under restricted family/clinical seal with the following context: low-confidence vocal match, disputed timestamp association, possible archive bleed or household transcript collision, no evidence of survival after death, no instruction, no public claim.

Sera read the paragraph twice.

“Change ‘the reviewed text’ to ‘a reviewed transcript rendering.’”

Mara changed it.

Yuna’s delayed voice came through. “Clinical condition: the private transmission must include live support contact, no autoplay, no raw waveform, no repeat function in the family portal for first seventy-two hours.”

Ruth would despise that. Ruth would understand it. Ruth might never forgive Mara for either part.

Nadine said, “Add it.”

Mara added it.

Pavel’s pane moved. He had received the first version of the personal-address proposal; his face shifted before his voice arrived. He removed his glasses.

There it was. The old burn scar at his wrist shone pale under the office light.

“Doctor Venn,” he said, “you are proposing selective private release of an interpretively lethal fragment to your own mother while withholding it from the public. Every faction will call this proof that the IIA keeps heaven for families and emptiness for everyone else.”

Mara almost answered before the timestamp. Nadine’s hand cut once through the air.

Wait.

They waited eleven minutes with the sentence on the table. Malik checked the packet locks. Sera reviewed Leila’s context note. Tomas made no sound. The ship’s central display, mirrored from command, showed Mars beyond the coordinate, clean in the optical feed. It had no opinion. Dust weather over Valles Marineris continued as measurable light. The visible planet refused every easy accusation.

At mark, Mara opened the mic.

“Ground, the fragment is not heaven.”

She heard her own voice in the archive bay, flatter than anger, almost clinical.

“It is not public evidence. It is not proof of contact, survival, artificiality, or instruction. It is a contaminated personal-address fragment with known lethal use if stripped of context. We will name its existence and restriction. We will not pretend it is absent. Ruth Venn receives one reviewed transcript rendering because family-specific material was requested under legal hold before Candle, because concealment would falsify the custody record, and because withholding from her while acknowledging it to institutions would be contempt.”

Pavel would hear that later. He would make a corridor through it if he could.

Mara added, after a breath, “Mara Venn will not prepare the support context alone.”

Yuna said, delayed, “Accepted pending ground legal review.”

Nadine said, live, “Accepted onboard.”

Sera said, “Accepted scientifically as restriction, not validation.”

Malik marked all three.

Amara’s cell was easier until it was not. The systems note had always looked plain, almost ugly.

The hull is remembering shapes it never had.

In another copy: held.

Mara saw Amara’s broad hands stripping a fitting down to its shame, saying a child could love Mars and a gasket could not. That line had already escaped into private forums through paraphrase. If released raw, it would become mystic metal by dinner. If buried, engineers would learn from a sanitized structural note that pretended language had not failed at the same time the hull did.

She wrote:

Amara Okonkwo’s bay-two systems annotation describes stress features that crossed discontinuous material and did not match known Asterion geometry. Her phrase “the hull is remembering shapes it never had” appears in one duplicated note; a second copy reads “held.” The phrase is released as engineering distress language attached to structural scans, not as evidence of intention, memory in matter, or external design.

Sera made a small sound. Agreement, maybe pain.

“Do we include the alternate ‘held’?” Nadine asked.

“Yes,” Sera said. “Contradiction belongs to the record.”

“People will choose the prettier one,” Tomas said.

“Then they will choose it from an honest page,” Mara said.

Mateo’s cell stayed closed one second longer under her hand.

The children’s astronaut. The basalt lesson. The line Public Learning had wanted: Mars is waiting for all of us. His refusal of waiting had become a small act the edit nearly swallowed. His angry log had broken the classroom wall open: They put Mars in classrooms so no one could cancel us.

Mara had once written symbolically mission-critical beside his name as if naming the pressure kept it from being used. She had been very good at building clean containers for dirty acts.

She opened the note.

Mateo Ríos’s public education records and private MD115 log show that Asterion’s public program placed emotional burden on children before the Boundary event. The public package includes his revised basalt lesson and a contextual excerpt from his private objection, with family notification: “They put Mars in classrooms so no one could cancel us.” This release does not assign blame for Asterion’s loss. It admits institutional use of childhood trust.

Nadine read it. “That will move legislatures.”

“It should.”

Pavel would hate the sentence because it gave Earth a bill due before the Boundary. Finite Earth channels would cut it into a verdict against every launch. Space loyalists would call Mateo ungrateful. Children in Red Nurseries would hear an adult admit they had been made witnesses with crayons.

Mara added:

The package must not imply that longing for Mars is a moral failure. It must not answer children’s questions with abandonment.

Ines had given her that brown coat button in Dublin, chipped at one edge from Thomas Keane’s failed morning. Bread first, then names. Weather. One choice small enough to survive. Mara touched the pocket where the button was stitched inside her flight vest. She had sewn it there badly. The thread scratched when she bent.

She opened the release guidance section.

Public viewing guidance: Releases should be accompanied by ordinary care prompts, especially for minors and collapse-risk patients. Eat before viewing when possible. Watch with another person. Keep names, weather, and next bodily needs available. Do not use this package in unsupervised pediatric settings, end-of-life wards, detention units, or collapse recovery rooms without trained support.

Sera stared at the line. “This is not science text.”

“No,” Mara said.

Yuna’s delayed voice answered an earlier note. “Add medication continuity. Do not interrupt scheduled care for live viewing.”

Mara added it.

Malik said, “Public desk wants the title again.”

The field waited at the top, blank and white.

Pavel’s reply arrived to Mara’s private-release defense. “You are making a distinction the public will not honor.”

Mara looked at his pane. “Yes.”

He continued, not hearing. “If we name withholding, we create demand for the withheld material. If we do not name it, later discovery destroys the package.”

Sera whispered, “That is the whole problem.”

Pavel’s mouth tightened. “The casualty model for raw personal-address release remains severe. Manila class, possibly worse. Exit channels already have couch language. The Makers’ Address is preparing an operator-contact petition tied to Candle’s final burst. Denial blocs are framing the lost object lock as proof of sabotage. This is not a classroom.”

Mara said, to the delayed man and to herself, “It is always a classroom after we let children watch.”

No one corrected her.

The title field remained blank.

Sera opened the scientific disclaimer and began dictating with severe patience. “Candle’s findings do not establish artificiality. They do not establish external contact. They do not establish survival after death. They do not establish that Mars is false. They do not license claims of practical control over the Boundary.”

Mara typed.

Nadine added, “They do establish that an unmanned craft entered a region where our evidence rules fail.”

Sera said, “Evidence rules degrade. They do not fail uniformly.”

Mara typed the correction.

Tomas said, “They establish the couch stayed empty.”

Nadine said, “Yes.”

Pavel would object. Pavel would be partly right. Some patients would relapse because the package included too much. Some families would condemn Mara because it included too little. Exit channels would slice out phrases with knives polished by grief. Denial channels would circle Sofia’s possible order. Red Orthodoxies would try to make Leila’s sealed prayer into victory. The Makers’ Address would find “no proof of external contact” and hear “try harder.”

Truth could be shaped without becoming false. She had mistrusted that sentence because Pavel lived inside its worst version. He had shaped truth into corridors, valves, quarantine doors. He had saved some people and buried others under calm language. She had wanted the opposite to be purity: everything open, every waveform raw, the dead delivered without institutional fingerprints.

But raw release was also a shape. It turned a person into whatever wound found them first.

Mara wrote the title.

WHAT THE DATA PERMITS

No one spoke for several seconds.

Sera looked at it, then at Mara. “Permits is good. It does not say proves.”

Nadine said, “It also does not say comforts.”

Tomas gave a short, broken laugh. “No risk of that.”

Pavel’s pane froze for two seconds, his face caught with his glasses halfway off. When motion returned, he was answering something old.

“Witness, if you proceed under emergency shipboard release authority before ground vote, you will carry institutional liability.”

Nadine opened the mic. “Ground, command understands.”

Pavel, still late: “Doctor Venn personally will carry public blame for classification choices involving Elias-associated material.”

Mara opened her mic only when Malik pointed.

“Ground, Doctor Venn understands.”

Yuna’s audio came through. “Cognitive safety objection remains noted. Doctor Venn is compromised.”

Sera looked at Mara.

Mara said, “Doctor Venn agrees.”

That surprised them more than any refusal would have. Nadine’s hand, hooked near the rail, tightened once.

Mara continued, “Compromise is part of the custody record. It is not a reason to make the record lie. Keep my breach in the restricted chain and the public methodology note. Do not publish the private phrase. Do publish that Elias-associated personal-address material exists and is withheld under Class C because raw release would create foreseeable harm.”

Pavel’s delayed silence had more weight than his words.

Malik asked, “Do you want the public statement recording in archive bay or command?”

“Command,” Nadine said. “No hiding in the library.”

The move took less than a minute. Mara unhooked from the restraint bar and followed Nadine through the narrow corridor. The handrails carried the shine of skin oil where every crew member had held on during broadcasts and burns. A strip of sterilized fabric had come loose at one panel seam and fluttered in the ventilation. Someone had left a half-empty mandarin pouch clipped to a wall loop. The smell hit Mara as she passed: sweet, metallic at the edge from the packet foil.

Sera walked behind her with the release tablet clutched to her chest. Tomas came last, still carrying the Velcro strip. Malik moved ahead to command, already building the transmit stack.

The command deck was dimmer than the archive bay. Mars sat on the central display, a red disk with weather bands processed in false color on one side and optical clean on the other. Beneath it, Candle’s telemetry pane stayed closed. Tomas had made that choice. The absence looked like work, not denial.

Nadine took the command chair and stayed standing beside it. “Record public statement. Joint release package, shipboard emergency concurrence. Mark all raw Class C exclusions named in appendix, not attached.”

Malik’s hands moved over the panel. “Recording live to ship archive. Public release delayed buffer armed. Ground mirror pending.”

Sera said, “One more line before statement.”

Mara waited.

“Do not say ‘nothing in this release changes what the dead meant.’ That is too much.”

“I was not going to.”

“You might.”

Mara looked at her. Sera’s eyes were red from screen light and held wakefulness. Precision had cost her sleep, kindness, ease. It had not made her cold.

“I might have,” Mara said.

Sera nodded once.

Nadine gave the order. “When ready.”

Mara stood at the public console. The camera indicator remained dark for the first five seconds, then opened to a small green point. She had spoken to Glass Rooms before. Houston 12B. Children with ready and not-ready bowls. Avram asking whether pain hurt if it was computed. She had answered badly then, maybe not falsely, but from a height that failed him.

She set Ines’s coat button on the console edge, where the camera could not see it.

“Begin,” Malik said.

Mara read from the draft until the draft became too smooth. Then she looked at the lens.

“This is Dr. Mara Venn aboard Witness, authorized interpretive officer for the Boundary release environment. I am speaking under joint shipboard review with Captain Nadine Qureshi, Dr. Sera Imani, Candle systems architect Tomas Vale, and communications officer Malik Serrano. Ground review is delayed by transmission time and continuing.”

Her mouth had gone dry. She took water from the squeeze bulb Malik handed her. No drama. Just swallowing.

“Candle was unmanned. No human being was placed aboard Candle to extract testimony. Candle carried an empty human-rated couch. It remained empty. Occupied Boundary approach was invalid by hardware hold and command seal. Witness command did not concur with any human-occupied approach.”

Nadine’s face did not change.

“Candle entered the Boundary transition zone and returned a final burst package before reliable telemetry ended. We no longer have usable external object lock or guidance data for Candle. We cannot establish its present physical state.”

Sera’s lips moved silently with the next section.

“The returned data includes instrument records, biological payload records, archive-index damage, and contaminated Asterion-associated overlays. The package we release contains contextual transcripts, scientific cautions, and selected analytic summaries. It does not contain raw Class C personal-address material, raw devotional audio, or raw post-failure laughter fragments known to have been used for self-harm recruitment. Those withholdings are named in the appendix. They are not hidden.”

Mara stopped. The green point waited.

She could still choose a better lie. A gentler one. A clean wall around Elias. She could let Ruth have him privately and call the rest clinical risk. She could give Earth every broken sound and call injury freedom. Both had doors. Both had graves.

She touched the coat button with one finger.

“The Asterion-associated material remains contradictory. Commander Sofia Arendt’s disputed command records do not establish human error as the cause of Asterion’s loss and do not clear every crew action under unknown instrument failure. Jun Park’s corrupted cockpit record includes a duplicated laugh in a disputed timestamp group. The unedited restricted context includes the phrase ‘That’s not sky.’ The public feed laugh from Elias Venn before signal loss is a separate recording. Do not combine them.”

Sera closed her eyes for half a breath, then opened them.

“Leila Haddad’s record includes checklist and private prayer tracks under a disputed timestamp group. This release describes the structure and scientific context. It does not publish the raw prayer. It does not show that prayer altered the event or that the event answered prayer.”

Pavel would flinch at that. Red Orthodoxies would too. Good.

“Amara Okonkwo’s engineering annotation is released with structural context. She wrote that the hull was ‘remembering shapes it never had.’ A duplicated copy uses a different final word. This is an engineer’s language under material contradiction. It is not evidence that matter has intention.”

Tomas’s Velcro strip had stilled in his hand.

“Mateo Ríos’s education records and private objection are released in part because children were made witnesses to Asterion before the Boundary was known. The release includes his warning that Mars was placed in classrooms in order to make cancellation harder. That warning does not make longing for Mars wrong. It makes adults responsible for the weight they gave to children.”

Mara breathed in. Metal, recycled air, her own tired skin. Mars stayed red on the display behind the camera, visible and outside reach.

“One Elias-associated personal-address fragment exists. It is low-confidence, contaminated, and restricted. Raw release is prohibited under Class C because it contains apparent personal address and is likely to be used as proof of survival, instruction, or invitation. A reviewed transcript rendering will be sent under family and clinical seal to Ruth Venn with support context, no raw waveform, and no public claim. Mara Venn’s prior unauthorized transcript exposure and later chain breach are recorded in the custody file.”

Her name in her own mouth sounded like a witness called in court.

She left the silence in.

“These data do not prove operators. They do not prove external contact. They do not prove survival after death. They do not prove Mars is fake. Mars remains visible, measured, and unreachable by every successful trajectory we have attempted. The Boundary remains an unresolved physical and interpretive domain.”

Sera’s thumb pressed hard against her old ring mark.

“These data are not a license to die. They are not permission to call pain decorative. They are not permission to make the dead simple.”

The last sentence had not been in the draft. Nadine did not stop her.

“If you are viewing this in a Glass Room, ward, school, detention unit, or home, use the care guidance attached to this release. Eat before viewing if you can. Keep another person nearby. Do not interrupt medication, sleep, or feeding routines for this material. For children, give names, weather, and one next action before questions that no adult can honestly finish.”

She saw Ines folding a towel into a hard square. Saw Thomas Keane choosing jam. Saw Avram with a token in his hand. Saw Ruth at a piano she had stopped playing.

“The public has a right to evidence. The public also has bodies. We will not pretend those duties do not conflict.”

There. Too close to thesis. She cut the next line.

Mara looked straight into the green point.

“What the data permit is narrower than grief wants and wider than secrecy prefers. We are releasing the narrow part. We are naming the rest.”

She stopped.

Malik waited two seconds. “Statement complete.”

No one spoke over the end tone. The archive system sealed the recording with a soft chime that sounded too much like an elevator arriving.

Pavel’s pane refreshed on the command display. He had heard enough to know they were going forward, not enough to stop it. His voice came from eleven minutes behind.

“Doctor Venn, do not make this a confession.”

Mara looked at his delayed face.

Nadine said, “Too late for that advice.”

Sera took the tablet from Mara and ran the final checksum. “Package integrity passes with restrictions attached. Class B public context. Class C raw exclusions named. Family-clinical seal pending Yuna concurrence and Ruth Venn support channel.”

Yuna’s audio arrived almost on top of her. “Support channel for Ruth Venn is live. St. Odran’s family-line clinician available for consult if she accepts. Do not send during local night.”

Mara checked the timestamp. Ruth’s kitchen would be morning. A brutal ordinary morning. Light on the table. Maybe coffee gone cold. Maybe the piano closed.

“Prepare private transcript rendering,” Nadine said.

Sera moved beside Mara, close enough for the tablet to read both reviewer tokens. “I will reveal the sentence. You will not touch the raw file.”

“I know.”

“Say the context first.”

Mara swallowed.

Sera unlocked the restricted rendering. The sentence appeared in plain text, no waveform, no play button, no breath attached.

Mara, leave the porch light on.

The command deck narrowed to those six words and then widened again because the ship did not care, because air still needed pushing and packets still needed routing and bodies still had to be fed.

Mara dictated the context for Ruth with Sera watching every word.

“Mom. This is a restricted transcript rendering of an apparent Elias-associated sentence from a contaminated fragment. It has disputed timestamp association, low-confidence vocal match, and possible archive bleed or household transcript collision. It is not evidence that Elias survived after death. It is not an instruction. It is not public. I breached chain of custody when I first heard it. I am sorry.”

Sera’s face tightened at the apology, but she did not cut it.

Mara added, “A clinician is available on the line. You can open this later. You can choose not to open it.”

Nadine said, very quietly, “Send.”

Malik routed the private seal. His finger hovered.

Mara had expected to feel theft, or mercy, or some clean pain with an old religious name. Instead she noticed the scratch of Ines’s button thread under her vest and Tomas’s Velcro strip stuck to his sleeve, ridiculous and white.

“Send,” she said.

The private packet left first.

The public package followed twelve seconds later, larger, slower, heavy with cautions that would fail in places and save in others. Malik watched the transmit bar move. Sera watched checksum echoes. Nadine watched crew faces. Tomas watched the closed Candle pane as if an empty couch might still accuse him through a screen that showed nothing.

Mars held on the central display, weather moving over red ground no human could reach.

When the transmit bar completed, the command deck made no announcement. There was only the ordinary click of Malik removing his hand from the lock.

Mara picked up the chipped coat button from the console edge and put it back inside her vest. The thread caught again, a small pull against skin, sharp enough to count.
