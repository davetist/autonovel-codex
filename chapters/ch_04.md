## Chapter 4: St. Odran’s

“Bread first,” Ines Calder said.

The man at the table held the slice by its corner. It sagged under a shine of butter. His other hand rested in his lap, thumb rubbing the hole where a coat button should have been.

Mara stood beside the ward door with her visitor badge clipped too high on her jacket. The badge warmed against her throat. St. Odran’s had kept the old hospice proportions: low ceilings, broad windows, corridors built for beds rather than cameras. Someone had taken down the saint pictures and left pale rectangles on the walls.

“Mr. Keane,” Ines said, “look at me.”

He looked at the window.

“Close enough. Weather.”

“Outside,” he said.

“That is a location, not weather.”

“Gray.”

“Good. Bread.”

He lifted the slice halfway. His wrist stalled as if a valve had shut inside the arm.

Ines waited. She wore a green cardigan with stretched pockets and large glasses that slid down her nose when she bent toward him. One pocket held glucose tablets in a clear packet. The other held pens, their caps chewed flat.

“Bread,” she repeated, softer.

Mr. Keane brought it to his mouth. His teeth marked the crust. No bite. Butter touched his lower lip.

Mara’s fingers moved toward the inside of her wrist and stopped. She let the hand hang.

“Name,” Ines said.

“Tom.”

“Full.”

“Thomas Keane.”

“Whose coat is that?”

He looked down as if surprised to be wearing it. Navy wool, hospice issue, one sleeve shiny at the cuff.

“Mine.”

“Buttons.”

His hand found the first button. It slid once against the buttonhole and missed.

Ines took no notes. A nurse at the far counter charted on a tablet with the screen angled away. The dining room smelled of toast and disinfectant, with rain pressed against the old glass. At each table sat a patient and a staff member. Every plate held bread. Some held jam. No one had hung screens on the walls.

Mr. Keane tried the button again. The button knocked the hole’s edge, then fell back.

“Tom,” Ines said. “Weather.”

“Gray.”

“Name.”

“Thomas Keane.”

“Bread.”

This time he bit down. Crumbs fell onto his coat. He chewed without moving his gaze from Ines’s face.

“There,” she said. “That is the morning.”

Mara had watched astronauts in pressure tanks learn to count breaths through panic. She had watched lunar repair crews perform tool inventories while suit alarms screamed through their bone mics. She had trained Elias to turn fear into a set of next actions: check hands, check food, check the nearest living person. Ines Calder had reduced the universe to weather, bread, a name, a button.

Mr. Keane swallowed and coughed once.

“Water,” Ines said.

He reached for the glass and stopped with his fingers around it.

“Am I still a father?” he asked.

The nurse at the counter lowered her tablet.

Ines’s face changed only around the mouth. “Yes.”

“My daughter says I sat with the baby and didn’t pick him up.”

“You were ill.”

“He cried.”

“Yes.”

“The cry was local weather.”

“No,” Ines said.

Mr. Keane looked at the bread. “That’s what I said.”

“I know.”

“Was I wrong?”

Ines set her own hand flat on the table, not touching him. “You were sick. You were also wrong.”

He breathed through his nose. His fingers tightened around the glass until the water trembled.

“Drink,” Ines said.

He drank. A small stream ran from the corner of his mouth into the gray stubble at his jaw. He wiped it with the back of his hand and seemed ashamed of the hand afterward, as if it belonged to the room.

Mara stepped back to let an orderly pass with a tray. The orderly carried four small bowls of porridge and a folded towel over one forearm. St. Odran’s used no wall slogans. No approved sentences about uncertainty. The only sign in the dining room read: ASK BEFORE TOUCHING.

Ines rose when Mr. Keane got the second button through. “Good. Stay for the toast. I’ll be back before the jam fight.”

“There’s a fight?”

“There is always a jam fight. Raspberry has a lobby.”

He made a sound that might have become a laugh if his body had trusted it.

Ines led Mara out into the corridor.

“You watched his hands,” Ines said.

“Yes.”

“Most visitors watch the face. Then they write reports about despair.”

“I am not here for a report.”

“You are here because Pavel wants a conscience with credentials before he puts you on Witness.”

Mara kept pace beside her. “Pavel wants many things.”

“Pavel wants breakfast to scale.”

The corridor turned past a linen cart stacked with hard white towels. Halfway down, a woman in a blue robe stood before a window and said the word rain each time a drop struck the glass hard enough to hear. Her nurse answered yes every third drop.

“Is she counting?” Mara asked.

“She is keeping the word attached to the thing.”

“Does that help?”

“It helped yesterday.”

They passed a room where a boy of perhaps seven sat on the floor, fitting red blocks into a crooked line between two plastic planets. A woman in street clothes, probably his mother, sat on the bed with her shoes still on. Her hands were tucked under her thighs. She watched the boy with the rigid patience of someone afraid to interrupt a ceremony.

“Family admission?” Mara asked.

“His father. Collapse after a public packet leak. The boy built a wall between Earth and Mars before breakfast and then refused to cross the carpet.”

“Does he have a diagnosis?”

“He has a father who stopped packing lunches.”

Mara accepted the correction. The boy lifted one block and moved it closer to the blue planet. His mother whispered his name. He placed the block down anyway.

Ines opened a staff room with a keypad and let Mara enter first. The room held a metal table, six chairs, a kettle with a cracked handle, and a corkboard crowded with medication schedules and school forms. Someone had taped a child’s drawing near the sink: a house under a black line, chimney smoking, red circle above it. The circle had been crossed out and redrawn in the corner.

“Tea?” Ines asked.

“No, thank you.”

“Coffee?”

“No.”

“You people from Houston live on canned citrus and adrenaline.”

“Only in official rooms.”

Ines poured hot water over tea leaves in a chipped mug. “You can ask me what you came to ask. After that I’ll show you the notebook.”

“What notebook?”

“The one that will ruin your clean categories.”

Mara sat. The chair legs scraped tile. “Then show me first.”

Ines studied her over the mug. “No.”

The answer came too quickly for performance. Ines drank, burned her tongue, and swore under her breath.

Mara almost smiled. It arrived and was gone before use.

“You agreed to one interview,” Mara said.

“I agreed to one hour of you being useful. Interview is your word.”

“What counts as useful?”

“You hold a tray if I need one. You do not ask patients whether they feel real. You do not mention Asterion unless they mention it first. If a patient freezes, you say their name if you know it, weather if you don’t.”

“Nothing about belief.”

“Belief can wait until the second slice.”

Mara looked at the corkboard. One form had a school crest and a red stamp: CONTINUITY ABSENCE, APPROVED. A handwritten note under it read: mother requests no Mars units until review.

“What is your current relapse trigger profile?” Mara asked.

Ines leaned back. “There she is.”

“You run one.”

“Of course I run one. Screens. Loop-sky composites. Uncontexted Asterion audio. Denial-state broadcasts. Certain hymns. Certain jokes. Bedrooms with glow stars. Insurance letters. School assignments asking for a future plan.”

“Exit material?”

“That gets its own cabinet.”

“Including speech fragments?”

“Especially those.”

Mara folded her hands on the table. Her nails were cut so short the skin shone at the edges. “Do you classify by content or effect?”

“Both. Content tells me what door they’re selling. Effect tells me who has already put a hand on the handle.”

The phrase sat in the room.

Mara said, “That is theirs.”

“Yes.”

“Soft recruitment.”

“Soft as a pillow over the face.”

The kettle clicked as it cooled. Rain tapped the small window above the sink. Ines’s tea darkened around the leaves.

“Show me,” Mara said.

Ines rose.

They walked to the adolescent wing through a former cloister with its courtyard roofed in glass. The old stone floor had been covered by a rubber path. At one end stood a locked cabinet marked PERSONAL DEVICES. At the other, two teenagers sat at a table with a nurse between them, sorting buttons by size into shallow trays. One girl wore fingerless gloves. The other had drawn a red rectangle around the Mars sticker on the back of her tablet case, though the tablet itself was absent.

Ines stopped outside a room with the door open.

“She is asleep,” Ines said. “You will not see her.”

“I didn’t ask to.”

“That puts you ahead of three ministers and one documentary crew.”

Inside, the bed was neat. A school jumper hung on the back of a chair. On the desk lay a notebook under a clear evidence sleeve. Its cover showed a cartoon greenhouse on Mars, a relic from before the Break, sunflowers under a dome. Someone had crossed out the dome with black marker.

Ines put on gloves. Mara waited at the threshold.

“Fourteen,” Ines said. “Top marks. Plays piano badly, according to her mother. Corrects everyone’s Irish. Two weeks ago she stopped speaking at dinner except to ask whether promises had expiration dates.”

“What preceded the change?”

“Her school reopened Mars history.”

“In Dublin?”

“Everywhere is still pretending it can write a curriculum that does not bleed.”

Ines opened the notebook to a marked page. The handwriting was small and pressed hard enough to dent the next sheet.

If they cannot prove the door is locked, why do they punish us for touching the handle?

Under it the sentence appeared again, with variations.

If no one proves locked, punishment is fear.
If the door is not locked, staying is obedience.
If pain is weather, why fear rain?

Mara read each line once. Then once more.

“Where did she get it?”

“Loop-sky forum. A woman with a teacher’s voice. We have the stream capture. She never tells them to die. She asks questions and lets sleep deprivation do the rest.”

“Name?”

“Mara Solenne Kade.”

Mara’s mouth dried at the shared first name. She gave it no room.

Ines turned another page. A red door had been drawn around a little printed Mars in the margin. Beside it: touch handle after everyone sleeps.

“She was found where?” Mara asked.

“School washroom. Door drawn on the mirror in lipstick. She had taken nothing, thank God, but she had a razor blade wrapped in tissue. She said she wanted to see if the cut hurt locally or completely.”

Mara’s hand found the doorframe. Paint flaked under her thumb.

“Has she spoken since?”

“Enough. She asked for water. She told her mother to stop crying because tears were a user-interface event. Then she vomited after breakfast and apologized to the vomit.”

The sentence landed wrongly, almost comic, and for that reason worse. Mara saw the girl bending over a basin, formal with the body that had betrayed her doctrine. She saw the staff deciding whether to correct her, comfort her, record her exact words. Evidence custody had entered the bathroom.

“What did you say?”

“I told her vomit does not accept apologies. Then I gave her a towel.”

Mara looked at the notebook again. The phrase had the clean edge of a hook. No threat. No doctrine in the open. A child could copy it and believe she had discovered the question herself.

“You reported the stream?”

“To Continuity, police, school board, platform safety, and a priest who asked whether I was certain the girl was not expressing a legitimate spiritual crisis.”

“What did you say?”

“I said all spiritual crises must surrender razor blades at intake.”

Mara let out a breath through her nose.

Ines closed the notebook. “That line will appear in six release hearings before summer. Someone will call it philosophical autonomy. Someone will call it speech. Someone will point out, correctly, that we cannot prove death is not an exit from anything. Then they will look at me as if I have admitted defeat.”

“And have you?”

“No.” Ines stripped off the gloves. “I have admitted I cannot prove the door is locked. I can prove the child’s hand was shaking.”

A patient shouted from down the hall. Not words at first. Then, “No, no, no coat, no coat.”

Ines moved before Mara could ask. They crossed the adolescent common room to a side alcove where a young man stood with his back against a radiator, arms pinned at his sides by a half-buttoned coat. A nurse held both hands up, palms out, giving space. The coat’s third button had gone through the wrong hole, pulling the fabric across his chest.

“Liam,” Ines said. “Weather.”

“No coat.”

“Weather first.”

“No coat.”

“Name.”

He shook his head. His breathing came in clipped bursts. “No coat. I’m flat. I’m flat.”

“You’re Liam Byrne and you’re too warm.” Ines turned to the nurse. “Open the window two fingers.”

The nurse did.

Cold air slid across the alcove. Rain smell entered, carrying traffic hiss from the road beyond the wall.

“Liam,” Ines said. “The button lied.”

He looked at her.

“See? Wrong hole. Happens to cardigans all over Dublin. Terrible civic problem.”

His breath hitched.

“Can I fix it?” she asked.

He looked down at the coat as if the cloth might answer.

“Say yes or no.”

His mouth worked. No sound.

Mara stood three paces back. In Houston, silence in a candidate meant risk flag, intervention, timing. Here the silence had a chair pulled up for it.

Ines waited.

“Yes,” Liam said, or breathed.

Ines undid the wrong button, then guided his fingers to the right one. “You do the next.”

“I can’t.”

“Then we stand here and insult the coat until you can.”

The nurse looked away to hide a smile.

Liam pressed the button with the flat of his thumb. It slipped. He made a small injured sound and bit his lip.

“That was skin,” Ines said. “Ouch?”

He nodded.

“Say it.”

“Ouch.”

“Good. Skin is online.”

Mara felt the phrase move through her with an almost physical pressure. Skin is online. She hated it. She wanted to write it down.

Liam tried again. The button went through.

“There,” Ines said. “One liar corrected.”

He leaned forward suddenly, forehead almost touching her shoulder. He did not embrace her. She raised one hand near his back and let it hover.

“Breakfast room when ready,” she told the nurse.

They returned to the corridor without speaking. Ines’s face had taken on a flush high in the cheeks. She pulled the cardigan straight at the hem.

Mara said, “You make jokes.”

“I make handles.”

“That joke could become doctrine.”

“So could your silence.”

Mara stopped walking.

Ines turned. “There. That was the interview face.”

“I am asking a real question.”

“I know.”

“Is withholding data ever care?”

Ines looked toward the patient rooms. A man in the far dining room was trying to spread jam with the spoon upside down. No one corrected him yet.

“Ask the clean version,” Ines said.

Mara’s jaw tightened. “When does withholding become theft?”

“That’s Pavel’s room. Try yours.”

Mara looked past her to the glass roof, where rain streaked the courtyard light into wavering lines.

“If we release raw fragments from Witness, people will use them. Exit groups, denial blocs, families. Markets. Churches. Children. Some fragments may be true and still lethal. If we hold them back, we become another closed cabinet, and the leak arrives without context. People die either way.”

Ines nodded once. “Better.”

“I need a rule that is not cowardice wearing a clinical badge.”

“You want a rule.”

“Yes.”

“Then no.”

Mara gave a short laugh, without humor. “No.”

“No clean rule. There are patients I would lock every screen away from for a month if law allowed. There are families I have dragged into a room and made them watch the thing they were pretending was gossip. There are children who need fewer facts before breakfast and more truth by bedtime. Same child, different day.”

“That is not policy.”

“Correct.”

“Witness cannot make release decisions patient by patient.”

“Then Witness should stop pretending release is one action.”

Mara started to answer and stopped. Down the corridor, the woman at the window had moved from rain to gray, repeating it with less urgency. Gray. Gray. A nurse beside her folded a towel into a square, unfolded it, offered it to the woman, and waited.

Ines watched Mara watch.

“You people want to know whether the universe is large enough to keep your promises in,” Ines said. “Our patients want to know whether getting dressed obligates them to reach noon.”

“You make us sound obscene.”

“Some days you are. Some days you bring funding for toast.”

Mara looked back at her. “Do you think the Boundary caused this illness?”

“I think people break when the load exceeds the wall. Sometimes the load is a dead husband. Sometimes it is a leaked cockpit laugh. Sometimes it is a sentence from a very calm predator asking why the door has a handle.”

“That is an analogy.”

“That is ward inventory.”

Ines opened another door, this one to the chapel.

Mara had expected old smell, beeswax perhaps, wood polish, the staged hush of a converted room. The chapel had been stripped nearly bare. The pews were gone, replaced by chairs in a loose half circle. No crucifix hung over the altar. The wall above it showed two darker nail holes and a shadow in the plaster where a body had once been fixed in suffering for the comfort of others.

On the altar stood one candle in a glass cylinder.

It burned without ceremony. A blue lighter lay beside it. A laminated card instructed staff to extinguish flame during oxygen use.

“In here,” Ines said, “some pray, some breathe, some come because the dining room is loud. One man came for three weeks to tell the empty wall that its argument was poor.”

“Did the wall answer?”

“It let him finish.”

Mara approached the altar. The flame leaned toward a draft, struck the glass, corrected itself. The cylinder was smudged with fingerprints near the rim.

“You removed the crucifix.”

“The hospice board removed it before the transfer. Then three families asked us to put it back, two asked us to remove all religious matter, and one asked for Mars footage over the altar so her husband would face what frightened him.”

“What did you choose?”

“That.” Ines nodded at the candle. “Fire behind glass. Old enough for half the building, plain enough for the rest.”

“Contained.”

“Observed.”

Mara looked at the flame until it doubled in the curved glass. Two small fires, neither cleanly separable. “Do Exit patients misuse it?”

“Everything is misused.”

“Then why keep it?”

“Because misuse is not the only use.”

The sentence struck too close to release language. Mara moved away from the altar.

Ines sat in one of the chairs. “Your question about withholding. Here is my worst answer. Sometimes I lie by omission for ten minutes. I do not tell a patient his wife left until after he eats. I do not correct a delusion while someone is crossing stairs. I do not hand a teenager her confiscated notebook because she asks with legal language she learned from a stream. That is care if the ten minutes are used to return her to herself. It is control if I enjoy the quiet.”

Mara sat opposite her. The chair was low. Her knees rose awkwardly.

“And public data?”

“Public is not a patient. Public is made of patients.”

“That distinction breaks at scale.”

“So does care.”

Mara heard Pavel in that, though Ines would have hated the kinship. Firebreaks. Casualty vectors. Raw shrapnel. The words were clean until a girl copied them into a notebook and planned to touch a handle after everyone slept.

“In Houston,” Mara said, “a child asked whether pain counts if it was computed.”

Ines waited.

“She was ten, maybe. Glass Room moderator sent the question up after the wreck frame. The approved answer said pain is a signal generated by nervous systems and remains clinically meaningful regardless of metaphysical model.”

“Christ.”

“It was accurate.”

“It was furniture.”

Mara pressed her thumb against the side of her index finger. “What would you have said?”

Ines leaned forward, elbows on her knees. “I would ask where it hurts.”

“The child wanted the metaphysical answer.”

“The child wanted an adult to stay in the room.”

Mara looked at the candle again. The flame made no sound.

Ines’s voice lost some of its edge. “Pain counts because the patient says ouch.”

Mara said nothing.

“That is not small,” Ines said. “People keep trying to make it small because they cannot hang a civilization on it. Fine. Do not hang a civilization before breakfast. Start with ouch. Start with bread. If the girl says her cut hurts locally, we clean the cut. If she asks whether local pain matters, we say yes and mean it. If she asks why, we do not send her a physics briefing.”

“What do we send?”

“A plaster. A person. Later, better words.”

Mara closed her eyes for one breath, opened them. “And if better words never arrive?”

“Then keep changing the plaster.”

A laugh came from the corridor, high and sudden. It broke into a cough. Someone dropped a tray. Staff moved, soft-soled and fast.

Ines stood. “Come.”

They returned to the dining room. The jam fight had taken form as a dispute between two elderly women and one young aide over whether raspberry jam contained seeds that proved plant continuity. One woman insisted seeds were plans. The other said seeds were tricks.

Mr. Keane sat where they had left him, both visible buttons fastened wrong, eating toast with concentration. A little jam marked the corner of his mouth. On the table beside his plate lay a child’s drawing in purple crayon: a baby in a pram under rain. The word sorry had been written at the top, then crossed out so hard the paper had torn.

Ines picked up a clean napkin and set it beside his hand. “Tom. Weather.”

“Gray,” he said through toast.

“Name.”

“Thomas Keane.”

“Baby?”

He swallowed. His throat moved twice.

“Finn,” he said.

“Good.”

“Is he coming?”

“After lunch if your daughter says yes.”

He touched the torn paper. “I need to say…”

Ines shook her head. “Eat first. Apology after protein.”

“Toast has protein?”

“Today it does.”

He took another bite.

Mara stood behind Ines and watched the man chew. Nothing in him resembled an argument. His body worked one task at a time, jaw and throat and hand, each motion negotiated against a thought that had unmade the ordinary chain between hunger and eating. He had called his grandchild’s cry local weather. Now he had said Finn.

That was not enough for policy. It was not enough for Witness, for the Boundary, for Elias breathing inside corrupted archives. It was not enough to answer Pavel’s casualty maps or the families who would pound their fists on hearing tables and demand the dead whole.

It was the morning.

Ines glanced back at Mara. “Tray.”

“What?”

“Hold this.”

Mara took the empty tray from her. It was warm from the dishwasher. Ines moved to the two elderly women and negotiated seed theology with buttered toast as bribe.

Mara held the tray against her hip. A boy at the next table pushed a paper cup toward his mother, who had not lifted her head since Mara entered. “Mum,” he said. “Water is still water.”

The mother’s hand came up slowly. She touched the cup but did not drink.

“Good,” the boy said, using a nurse’s cadence. He could not have been more than nine.

Mara looked away too late. The boy saw her seeing him. His face hardened with adult embarrassment.

She set the tray down on the counter and walked to the sink. Her hands smelled faintly of metal from the tray rim. She washed them longer than needed, then stopped because she had noticed.

Ines came beside her and took two towels from the warmer. She folded one into a hard square, pressed the corners flat, unfolded it with irritation, then handed the other to Mara.

“You leave for the Gantry tomorrow,” Ines said.

“Yes.”

“You will be asked to make hunger into charts.”

“I know.”

“No, you know the charts. Remember the hunger.”

Mara dried each finger. “You think I forget bodies.”

“I think you trust scale when you are frightened.”

The towel scratched Mara’s cuticles. She folded it once, badly.

Ines reached past her to turn off the tap. “There is your report, if you need one.”

Mara looked back into the room. Mr. Keane had found the napkin. The boy had convinced his mother to lift the paper cup with both hands. At the adolescent table, the girl with the red door on her tablet case sorted one button into the wrong tray, corrected it, then covered the red drawing with her sleeve.

From the chapel came no music. Only flame, sealed in glass, burning where a body used to hang.

Mara said, “I need to see the stream capture.”

“In Houston?”

“On Witness.”

Ines’s expression sharpened. “Why?”

“Because if that phrase appears again, I need to know its voice.”

“Do not make a shrine of predators.”

“I won’t.”

“You might make an instrument of one.”

Mara had no answer ready. Ines accepted that as partial credit.

At the exit desk, a volunteer returned Mara’s sealed phone and visitor credentials. Beside the sign-out screen sat a basket of coat buttons, each one clipped from donated clothing that could no longer be repaired. Black, brown, pearl, plastic. No label explained them.

Ines took one, a flat brown button with chipped edges, and placed it in Mara’s palm.

“For the Pacific,” she said.

“I have a coat.”

“Then for when someone asks what St. Odran’s taught you.”

Mara closed her hand around it. The rim pressed a circle into her skin.

Outside, Dublin rain had thinned to a mist. Her transport waited at the curb with its hazard lights blinking. Beyond the clinic wall, traffic moved toward work, school, shops, all the ordinary places people still entered without proof that entry mattered.

Mara turned back once.

Through the dining-room window, Ines stood behind Mr. Keane, not touching him, while he worked the top button free and began again.
