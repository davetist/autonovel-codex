LEILA: I cannot stop them. This is the part I dislike saying. Careful speech is not a seal. Privacy is not a hull. Faith is not a lock. Science is not a lock either, though we polish it until it shines like one.

She leaned forward and adjusted the towel strap, fixing the loose corner Sofia would have corrected later.

LEILA: I can only leave custody. I can say what I meant before fear becomes everyone’s editor.

Her hand vanished from the frame.

LEILA: I do not pray to be rescued. I pray so fear does not become sovereign. I pray so I can wash my hands once, or twice if needed, and stop before washing becomes obedience to fear instead of care. I pray so that when a sample dies, I do not make its death a sermon because I am lonely in the room.

Silence, then the recorder picked up a swallow.

LEILA: A made thing can suffer. I believe this as a scientist before I believe anything else about it. A culture grown in plastic is not false to itself. A child conceived by machine is not less hungry. Bread made in a factory still feeds. If all this is made in some way we do not understand, pain will still arrive without asking permission from our theories. That is not comfort. It is jurisdiction.

She reached toward the recorder. Her fingers hovered.

LEILA: Delete last sentence?

The device chimed, awaiting confirmation.

She let the prompt expire.

LEILA: No. Leave it. Let it embarrass me if I live.

Another pause.

LEILA: End private log.

The camera kept recording for seven seconds after her voice stopped. Leila crossed the frame only as hands. Bare hands, then gel, then water caught in a towel before it could float. She washed past necessity, noticed, and pressed both palms flat against the locker until the trembling spent itself into metal.
