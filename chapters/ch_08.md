Chapter 8: The Mumbai Futures Pit

At 09:17 Mumbai time, Boundary Continuity December futures broke their upper band on no official data.

Kavir Shah saw it first as a thin green jump in the corner of his left screen, too clean to be public fear. Retail panic moved ragged. This came in a line, four desks at once, Singapore first by half a breath, then Zurich, then an insurer pool in Lagos whose brokers usually waited for permission before they sneezed.

“Somebody has a log,” Nisha said beside him.

Kavir adjusted the cuff on his biometric monitor. It had begun to buzz against the inside of his wrist, soft as an insect, warning him his pulse had crossed the exchange floor threshold for mandatory hydration. He pressed two fingers to the metal contact until the buzzing stopped.

“No,” he said. “Somebody has access to somebody with a log.”

On the high wall, above the contract ladder, Mars turned in delayed weather: dust over Valles Marineris, ice signature clean at the northern cap, red disk untroubled by the room that sold the cost of believing in it. Beside it, the Continuity feed refreshed its public harm panel.

COLLAPSE INDEX / GLOBAL 7-DAY: 118.4 ↑  
ADOLESCENT EXPOSURE WATCH: AMBER  
BOUNDARY MEDIA DELAY WINDOW: 14 MIN 30 SEC  
WITNESS APPROACH MODEL: NO PUBLIC CHANGE  
UNVERIFIED LOG-ACCESS RUMOR: DENIED BY IIA PRESS OFFICE

The denial put another three points into the contracts.

Kavir bought collapse protection against a book he already hated.

Across the pit, the morning clerks were still carrying paper cups of tea between desks because the machine near the north doors had broken again. Exchange maintenance had taped a towel under it, the towel gone brown at the center from old spills. Nothing in the room had been built for reverence. The Mumbai Futures Pit had been a metals annex before the Break, all low ceilings and hard light, useful for copper and carbon allotments. After Boundary instruments became legal, the old wall boards were replaced with wider screens, the trading posts were wired for medical alerts, and the floor gained a clinician station behind armored glass. The overhead fans remained. They pushed warm air down over shaved heads, expensive shirts, cheap collars, lunch packets, wrist monitors, temples damp under hair.

“Kavir,” Nisha said. “You heard me? If there is real Witness log access, this is mispriced.”

“Everything is mispriced. That is why people pay us.”

“She wants a number.” Nisha tilted her chin toward the glass room above the floor, where their compliance officer watched with a hand against one ear. “She says no new Boundary exposure without verbal thesis.”

Kavir clicked open the desk channel. “Tell Meera the thesis is rot.”

Nisha stared.

“Say: if Witness crew are reviewing new Asterion material inside the approach window, somebody with a release stake now believes the Boundary event is active, not archival. That moves collapse probability, mission-failure insurance, sponsor default triggers, family injunction risk, and suicide-cluster reserves.”

“You want me to say rot?”

“Say systemic repricing if you are afraid of poetry.”

Nisha spoke into her mic. Kavir watched her mouth form the cleaner words. She was twenty-four, hired after her university shut its aerospace department and reopened half the building as a continuity management school. She kept a school photograph taped below her keyboard: six girls in red Mars caps standing under a mural of bubble helmets and greenhouses. Someone had drawn a black vertical line through the print with marker. Nisha had never removed it.

On the wall, the contract ladder moved again.

BOUNDARY CONTINUITY DEC FUT: +8.7  
WITNESS DELAY VOL: +22.1  
DEEP-SPACE SPONSOR DEFAULT SWAPS: BID WIDENING  
MARS VISIBILITY ANOMALY STRIP: UNCHANGED  
COLLAPSE INDEX OPTIONS / WEEKLY: +31.6  
LUNCH GRAIN DELIVERY / WEST COAST: -0.4

Someone laughed at the grain tick. Too loud. The sound struck the low ceiling and fell back wrong.

Kavir sold a small slice of Mars visibility anomaly. The rumor did not touch Mars. The dust feed held. Spectroscopy held. People outside the pit could say the red planet was a picture or a promise or a wound in the sky, but the instrument price for weather had not moved. If Mars weather stayed measurable while reach failed, the terror narrowed. Narrow terror was tradable. Broad terror ate the trader with the book.

“Why sell MVA?” Nisha asked.

“Because fear is lazy.”

“That is not verbal thesis.”

“Witness log access means interpretation risk, not optical risk. Powerful people are paying for collapse before they pay for proof. Follow the money that acts before language.”

Her fingers moved. “And if it is a false rumor?”

“Then we lose less than the people who believe press offices.”

The monitor on his wrist buzzed again. A red line crawled along the cuff display: PULSE 116 / BREATH RATE HIGH / FLOOR CONTINUITY CHECK RECOMMENDED. He turned the face inward. The exchange required monitors after a trader in 2045 sat down beside the probe-insurance board and stopped responding until his bladder released. The market kept open through that episode. It kept open through the next three, too, until a union complaint forced the Exchange Authority to post medics in the pit and install biometric kill-switch warnings on high-volume accounts. Traders called the monitors collars. The Authority called them care devices.

Kavir had learned to keep his pulse just under intervention.

A runner came through the west entrance with a tray of glucose tablets for the desk. The tablets were chalky, wrapped in orange foil stamped with the exchange seal. Kavir took one, bit it in half, and tasted chemical citrus. Asterion’s last frame had turned oranges into contraband for some families and brand material for others. The exchange cafeteria still sold mandarin cups because the vendor contract predated the Break and no one wanted to litigate fruit.

“Sir,” said a voice behind him.

He turned. A client liaison stood with a man Kavir recognized from a sovereign pension fund. The man wore a linen jacket too pale for the floor and a biometric patch under his collar, visible where the fabric opened at his throat. His face had the padded look of private clinics and bad sleep.

“Kavir Shah,” the liaison said. “Mr. Menon is here from the trustees’ office.”

“I can see him.”

Menon held out a hand. Kavir shook it while watching Zurich jump another two points.

“I need to understand,” Menon said, “whether this is a panic print or an informed print.”

“Both.”

“That answer is not helpful.”

“It is the only honest one you will get before Quito wakes up enough to lie smoothly.”

The liaison gave him a warning look. Menon did not object. He looked at the wall, at the Mars weather panel, at the harm statistics sliding quietly in white text. He had the eyes of a man who had been asked by old teachers and widows to keep pensions alive in a world where the future had begun charging interest.

Kavir opened a risk pane and dragged it onto the client display. “These are your exposures. Blue is mission-delay insurance. Green is sponsor default. Yellow is collapse-linked municipal drawdown.”

Menon leaned close. “Collapse-linked municipal?”

“When ward admissions cross city thresholds, some bonds defer. When school closures follow Boundary media releases, labor productivity drops and tax intake moves. Your fund owns the deferral risk.”

“We bought bridge repair and teacher salary paper.”

“You bought a city after the Break. The city came with people.”

Menon swallowed. The biometric patch at his collar flashed once. “And you make this sound ordinary.”

“No. I make it countable.”

On the high wall, the IIA denial line disappeared. A new notice replaced it.

PUBLIC ADVISORY PENDING / WITNESS APPROACH COMMUNICATIONS  
NO RAW LOG RELEASE AUTHORIZED  
MENTAL HEALTH GUIDANCE TO FOLLOW

The floor answered before anyone spoke. Hands rose. Desks lit. The room became a pump with all valves open.

Kavir bought more collapse protection, sold deep-space sponsor default into strength, widened bid on family-injunction risk. Nisha called numbers into the desk channel with her shoulders set tight. The junior clerk to his right crossed himself, then glanced around to see who had noticed. Nobody had time to make him pay for it.

Menon said, “Should we halt?”

Kavir almost laughed. The exchange halt rules were written after the Manila spike and rewritten after a denial-state minister accused markets of pricing treason. Boundary instruments halted on verified casualty clusters, confirmed mission loss, or regulatory order. Rumor of log access did not qualify. Advisory pending did not qualify. Human dread with clean volume did not qualify.

“The market halts when the rule says halt,” Kavir said.

“And morally?”

Nisha’s hand paused over her keys. She recovered fast, but Kavir saw it.

“Morally,” he said, “you should not have bought instruments you could not bear to see work.”

Menon’s mouth tightened. “People like you sold them.”

“People like me showed you the price.”

“That is a child’s defense.”

On the desk screen, Singapore lifted offers in size. Kavir hit three keys and took the other side.

“Yes,” he said.

Menon waited for more. Kavir had no clean sentence to offer him. He had built his first Boundary book after his wife’s school district froze pension contributions because collapse leave had hollowed half the teaching staff. He had told himself he was finding the honest number inside the fear, the point where civic rot became visible early enough for someone with authority to move money before hospitals ran out of nurses. Then the bonuses arrived, and honesty learned to wear better shoes.

“Your trustees can sell now,” Kavir said. “You will lock in a loss and sleep with a story. Or you can hold protection through the advisory and have money for the next ward expansion. Pick which shame fits your minutes.”

The liaison made a small sound. Menon looked at the harm panel again.

COLLAPSE INDEX / GLOBAL 7-DAY: 121.9 ↑  
ST. ODRAN’S NETWORK CAPACITY: 93%  
MUMBAI PEDIATRIC CONTINUITY WAITLIST: 4,882  
WITNESS PUBLIC DELAY WINDOW: EXTENDED  
SUICIDE CLUSTER WATCH: RED IN 11 REGIONS

“Hold,” Menon said.

Kavir turned back to the ladder.

At 09:25, Arvind Rao went flat under the Mars weather feed.

At first the floor treated him as an obstruction. A man at the next post shifted his stool to avoid Arvind’s dropped tablet. Someone cursed because the tablet had landed screen-down and cracked across an order pad. Arvind stayed upright for three seconds too long, one hand still raised for a trade, his fingers spread as if asking the room to wait. Then his knees folded. He went down slowly, almost polite, shoulder brushing the base of the grain board before his head struck the rubberized floor.

The impact sound was small.

The market did not pause.

“Medic,” Nisha said into the floor channel. Her voice broke on the second syllable and came back flat. “Post C-six. Adult male. Responsive unknown.”

Kavir’s hand kept moving. He canceled an exposed order, rolled a December strip, clipped the Singapore spread. Across the desk, a trader from the weather book shouted for someone to move Arvind’s tablet because it was blocking the underfloor sensor. Two medics crossed from the glass station with a folded carry sheet between them. They wore gray vests with CONTINUITY in blue letters and soft-soled shoes that made no sound against the floor.

Arvind’s eyes were open. That was the worst part for Kavir, though he would not have said it to anyone. Open and working in the narrow biological sense. The screen above him showed Mars dust curling eastward. Beside it, a suicide statistic advanced by one decimal place after a scheduled data refresh. Arvind’s mouth moved once.

The nearer medic knelt. “Arvind. Name and weather.”

The floor kept shouting.

“Arvind. Name and weather.”

His lips formed something. Kavir saw no sound reach the room.

The medic touched two fingers to Arvind’s neck, then nodded to her partner. “Pulse present. Flat response.”

“Full collapse?” the partner asked.

“Acute interruption. Carry.”

Arvind’s wrist monitor flashed white, then red, then white. His trading screen stayed active. A compliance lock should have frozen his account after medical intervention; instead it asked for supervisor override. The supervisor, three rows over, shouted, “Lock Rao book, transfer to desk seven.”

Desk seven took the book. Prices adjusted without a hitch.

A bitter little cheer rose when Boundary Continuity hit its next band. No one owned the cheer after it escaped.

Kavir looked at Nisha. Her face had gone gray under the trading lights. “Drink,” he said.

“I’m fine.”

“That is floor speech for collapse.”

She grabbed her paper cup and drank. Tea ran down one side of her hand. “He was asking about school fees before open.”

“Arvind?”

“His son. Engineering entrance. He said if deep-space was dead then maybe reactor work. Then he said reactor work needed reality fitness now. He made a joke.”

The medics slid the carry sheet under Arvind. He did not fight them. He did not help. His shoes dragged at the heels as they lifted him.

Kavir’s wrist buzzed again. He let it.

Menon said, very quietly, “We are still trading.”

“Yes.”

“A man is on the floor.”

“Yes.”

“Does the Exchange Authority record that as market stability?”

Kavir watched the medics turn sideways to pass between two desks. One of them nudged a fallen glucose tablet away with her toe. The orange wrapper stuck to the rubber for a moment, then came loose.

“They record everything,” he said.

“That is not what I asked.”

“No.”

Nisha took over a hedge without waiting for instruction. Good. Work gave the hands a place to go. Kavir checked her orders, corrected one lot size, left the rest. On the wall, the advisory line began to crawl in formal language. Witness had received restricted Asterion material under approved access protocols. No raw release. No public claim concerning crew final state. Continuity guidance attached. Family notification pending. Kavir read the lines once and knew the rumor had been close enough to make the market look intelligent.

That was the obscenity: the room was useful.

The sponsor default market had moved before the advisory because somebody who insured Candle’s human-rated systems knew the access window mattered. The collapse options had moved before the mental health guidance because somebody with hospital dashboards had seen staff pre-alerts. The family-injunction risk had widened before the press office because legal teams were already being called. The market had priced the living damage before the public sentence arrived with its clean shirt buttoned to the throat.

“Nisha,” he said. “Mark advisory true. Keep MVA flat. Buy pediatric ward capacity bonds if any idiot sells.”

She stared at him. “That’s not our book.”

“It is now.”

“Mandate?”

“I’ll eat the breach.”

“You’ll lose your license.”

“I’ll lose a paragraph in a hearing. Buy them.”

Her fingers moved. “Size?”

“Enough to be noticed by people who hate us.”

Menon turned from the wall. “Why?”

Kavir had no appetite for grace in front of him. “Because ward capacity will get emergency backing after this. Because children going flat on camera makes ministers remember they have faces.”

Nisha’s mouth twisted. She did not look at him. “There’s your moral thesis.”

“Put it in the notes. Spell my name correctly.”

The floor volume climbed. Another advisory translated into Hindi, Marathi, English. A denial-bloc feed on a side screen called the log-access report psychological warfare. A Makers’ Address channel announced a prayer-burst auction before exchange moderators killed the window. Someone in commodities shouted that lunch rice futures were moving with school closures and asked whether that counted as Boundary exposure. The compliance channel told him to submit a form.

Kavir’s monitor switched from buzz to tone.

He swore and slapped the mute field. The tone continued. Mandatory personal continuity check. He had ignored three warnings inside ten minutes. If the clinician station flagged him, his account would lock for a ninety-second spoken response test. Ninety seconds was a lifetime when Zurich was still drunk and Singapore had begun to sober.

Nisha heard the tone. “Go.”

“I can pass here.”

“Go, or I call it.”

“You are enjoying authority too much.”

“I learned from rot.”

He looked at her then, properly. She had both hands on the desk, nails short, tea drying on her knuckles. The black line on the old school photo below her keyboard cut through two girls’ Mars caps.

Kavir stepped back from the trading rail.

The clinician station was nine meters away. It might as well have been an airlock. He crossed through heat and bodies, past a broker eating a dry roll over his keyboard, past Arvind’s cracked tablet now sealed in a custody sleeve. A screen near the north doors showed the paused Asterion wreck frame in miniature beside a warning: HISTORICAL IMAGE / DELAYED / CONTEXT REQUIRED. Someone had stuck a child’s red sticker over the corner of Mars. The sticker was peeling.

The continuity clinician looked up as Kavir entered the glass booth. She was younger than he expected, hair pinned back, sleeves rolled above the elbow. Her desk held a blood-pressure cuff, water cups, glucose tablets, and a small brass bell no one had rung in months.

“Name,” she said.

“Kavir Shah.”

“Date.”

He gave it.

“Weather.”

“Outside or Mars?”

She waited.

“Mumbai humid. Mars dust over Valles Marineris on public delay.”

“Meal intake.”

“Tea. Half glucose tablet.”

“Food, Mr. Shah.”

“That was food-adjacent.”

“Solid intake.”

He looked through the glass at the pit. Nisha was at the desk with her chin down, taking calls. Menon had retreated to the client bench. Arvind’s space on the floor was already filled by another trader’s knee.

“I had toast,” Kavir said.

The clinician marked something. “When?”

He opened his mouth.

The morning came back with the fridge light and his daughter’s school tie hanging from a chair. Anaya standing barefoot in the kitchen, hair wet, refusing the paratha because the school’s Glass Room had a Witness advisory practice and she hated eating before Mars lessons. His mother placing jam on the table though Anaya had outgrown jam at nine. His own phone alive with overnight moves, Singapore blinking, Zurich asleep, rumors gathering at the edge of price. He had taken the call in the hall. He had told Anaya one minute. He had left before she sat down.

The clinician watched him.

“When?” she asked again.

“Before open.”

“That is not an answer.”

“No.”

“Any self-harm ideation, exit-language exposure, or inability to initiate ordinary action?”

“I am initiating too many actions.”

“Answer cleanly.”

“No self-harm. No exit intention. Exposure constant. Ordinary action impaired by profession.”

She wrote that without smiling. “Your monitor says panic threshold.”

“My monitor sells drama.”

“Your monitor cannot sell. That may be why I trust it.”

Through the booth speaker, the pit roared. Boundary Continuity hit limit-up. The exchange did not halt; the second band existed for exactly this kind of advisory. Kavir pressed his tongue against a sore place behind his teeth. He had bitten himself sometime after Arvind fell.

The clinician slid a paper cup toward him. “Drink this.”

“What is it?”

“Water.”

“I need my desk.”

“You need a response test and food.”

“I can do both while walking.”

“No.”

The word landed harder than the floor noise. Kavir picked up the cup. His hand shook once, enough to put a small crescent of water on the desk.

The clinician saw. “Have you called home?”

He laughed then. Wrong sound. Too sharp, too early, leaving no room for anyone else to pretend it was only fatigue.

“I’m at work,” he said.

“So are the medics.”

“My daughter is in school.”

“Then school can receive a call.”

The pit boards flashed behind her shoulder.

WITNESS APPROACH ADVISORY DIGEST: RESTRICTED LOG ACCESS CONFIRMED  
RAW ASTERION MATERIAL: WITHHELD  
FAMILY NOTIFICATION: SEALED  
COLLAPSE INDEX INTRADAY: 129.7 ↑  
PEDIATRIC CONTINUITY ALERT: MUMBAI AMBER

Kavir took his phone from his pocket. The device felt damp from his palm. Personal calls were logged inside the pit during active Boundary events. Compliance would note duration, content risk, possible exposure. He selected Anaya’s school contact, then canceled before the ring.

The clinician said nothing.

He called his daughter’s personal line. Against policy during instructional hours. Against the parent agreement. Against the small cowardly fiction that a rule was stronger than the need to hear a child chew.

It rang five times. Then a whisper.

“Papa?”

The floor sound thinned inside the booth. He turned toward the corner, away from the glass, one hand over his free ear. “Are you in class?”

“Yes.”

“Can you talk?”

“No. Ms. Iyer will take my phone.”

“Did you eat breakfast?”

A pause.

“Papa.”

“Answer.”

“We had continuity assembly.”

“That is not breakfast.”

“We were late.”

“Anaya.”

“I had milk.”

“Food.”

“Why are you calling?”

On the wall beyond the glass, Mars kept its weather. A red planet with dust and ice and no answer for a child’s stomach.

“Because I left before you ate.”

“You always leave.”

The sentence arrived without accusation dressed up for adult use. It was plain, which made it worse.

Kavir closed his eyes and opened them fast. “Listen. There is jam in the blue-lid jar. Ask your teacher for bread if tiffin is still in your bag.”

“I’m not a baby.”

“I know.”

“Then stop doing doctor voice.”

He nearly said he was not doing doctor voice. He heard himself from outside: floor clinician, ward prompt, bread before metaphysics, the corrupted public form of care. Buy the bonds. Feed the child. Hedge the collapse. Same hand on different valves.

“Did the assembly show Witness?” he asked.

“Only the delay screen. Ms. Iyer said no raw clips. Rohan said his brother has one.”

“Do not watch anything from Rohan’s brother.”

“You don’t get to trade it and tell me not to look.”

There it was. Not from Menon, not from regulators, not from the hearing that would one day lift his order notes and read them in a careful voice. From his daughter in a classroom, whispering under a desk while some adult at the front tried to keep Mars from eating the morning.

“I know,” he said.

“No, you don’t. You say that when you want the conversation to finish.”

A bell sounded faintly through her line. School, not market. Small and bright.

“Anaya,” he said. “Eat the bread.”

“I hate jam now.”

“Then butter.”

“I hate butter.”

“Then plain.”

She breathed into the phone. He heard children shifting, a teacher calling for devices away, the ordinary friction of a room being kept together by underpaid patience.

“Papa,” she said, softer. “Is Witness dying?”

Kavir looked at the feed. Advisory language crawled in two lines. No raw release. No claim. No certainty. Prices moving as if certainty had changed hands in a room above another room.

“I don’t know.”

“Everyone says I don’t know.”

“Then everyone is right today.”

“That’s useless.”

“Yes.”

The clinician outside his call radius moved a packet of glucose tablets from one side of the desk to the other, giving him privacy inside a glass box.

“After school,” he said, “call me before you watch anything. Even if it’s from a friend. Especially then.”

“You’ll be trading.”

“I’ll answer.”

“You say that too.”

“I’m saying it now.”

Another pause. He waited through it because interrupting would turn the promise into floor speech.

“I have math,” she said.

“Eat first.”

“You are very boring.”

“Good.”

“I’ll eat plain bread. Happy?”

He pressed the heel of his hand against his breastbone, hard enough to feel the monitor patch under his shirt answer with a buzz. “Yes.”

“That’s weird.”

“Yes.”

“Bye, Papa.”

The line clicked.

Kavir kept the phone against his ear after she was gone. The booth smelled of paper cups and disinfectant. On the desk, his water had settled into a thin ring around the cup. The clinician waited with her pen lifted.

“Response test complete?” he asked.

She studied him for one extra second, then marked the form. “For now.”

He returned to the floor with his phone still in his hand.

At the desk, Nisha had bought the pediatric ward bonds. Too much size, bad price, brave execution. Menon was staring at the fill report as if it were a medical scan.

“You overpaid,” Kavir said.

Nisha looked up. “You said enough to be noticed.”

“I did.”

“The notice is expensive.”

“Yes.”

The pit screens rolled the next line of public guidance. BREAKFAST ROUTINES RECOMMENDED FOR MINORS AFTER BOUNDARY MEDIA EXPOSURE. The phrase sat between a contract price and a mission delay stamp, bland as a napkin over blood.

Kavir placed his phone face down below the trading rail. For three seconds, no longer, he saw the room without spread or hedge: medics writing Arvind’s name on a custody tag, Nisha’s school photograph split by marker, Menon’s pale jacket creased under the arms, the wall where Mars weather and suicide statistics shared a border because that was where the screen vendor had found space.

Then Zurich offered him a bad price.

Kavir took it.
