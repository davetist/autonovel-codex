The first delay marker failed before Claire Arendt spoke.

On Mara’s screen the Quito Interplanetary Assembly Hall held its silence for three extra seconds, then gave it back with a click. The chamber was all glass and basalt, too clean for grief. A suspended Mars hung above the witness table, smaller than the one from the Asterion selection ceremony, or perhaps the camera angle made it smaller. Flags stood behind the council dais in mission-contribution order. Someone had placed water at each family seat in identical cups.

Claire Arendt sat with both hands flat on the table. Lawyer hands, Mara thought, then corrected herself. Daughter hands. They were Sofia’s hands also, long-knuckled, exact, trained by another life to keep tremor below the visible threshold.

The chair said, “Ms. Arendt, you have the floor.”

Claire leaned toward the microphone. “Commander Sofia Arendt’s restricted command records remain sealed.”

The translator track caught up half a breath late in Mara’s headset. Witness comms put a fine static under every Earth voice. Mara had one foot hooked beneath the restraint bar in the release bay, a packet of canned mandarin unopened against her thigh. Malik Serrano floated at the routing console with his fingers resting above the delay controls. Nadine was behind Mara, braced one-handed near the hatch. Sera occupied the second witness tile, compression sleeves pulled over her wrists, her face lit by Quito’s civic feed and the raw-data monitor below it.

The chair said, “You are making a demand or stating a legal position?”

“I am stating family notice under the Asterion privacy injunction and making a demand under Article Seventeen.” Claire’s voice did not lift. “The late command log is not a public file. It is not a scientific finding. It is a corrupted command derivative with duplicated duration, contradictory star-tracker states, and no independent verification of pilot action or command cause.”

Pavel sat two places from the chair, immaculate in dark cloth, his glasses on the table before him. He watched Claire as if listening were a civic service he still trusted.

Mara placed two fingers inside her wrist. Pulse fast. Countable.

Claire continued, “If released raw, it will become the Sofia Arendt steering record by nightfall. You know that. We all know that. There are already denial-state edits using false manual correction overlays. There are synthetic reconstructions putting my mother’s hand on controls she may never have touched at that time. If the Assembly releases a damaged command note, it supplies them the stamp.”

On the lower edge of Mara’s interface, the file tag appeared: AST-MD118-CMD-G118-L / restricted command note / duplicated field: what|whom / family claim pending.

Sera reached forward and muted her own channel before speaking to Mara. “She is correct about the file.”

“About the risk,” Mara said.

“About both, up to the point where she treats sealing as neutral.”

Nadine’s voice came low from behind them. “Open channel in six.”

Mara took her fingers from her wrist.

The chair said, “Dr. Venn, the council will hear your release framework after family statements.”

Claire looked into the witness lens, and for a moment the light delay became personal. She was speaking to Quito, to Witness, to every sealed server between Earth and the dead. “My mother was commander. That made her responsible. It did not make her available for every frightened government needing a human error story. Release the existence of disputed command material if you must. Name it contaminated. Keep the raw command records sealed.”

A murmur moved through the hall. The microphones suppressed most of it. Mara saw shoulders turn, mouths tighten, the little shiver of people hearing another person claim the dead before they could.

Claire added, “And do not call that censorship. Call it custody.”

Mara felt the word enter the room like a coded packet. Custody. Leila had left custody. Sofia had sealed custody. Mara had negotiated custody with Pavel in a windowless room named for a calm sea and had mistaken it, briefly, for control.

The chair thanked Claire.

Tomás Ríos Sr. rose before his name was called.

He wore a brown jacket with a repaired cuff and no tie. Retired mining surveyor, the brief said. Mateo’s father. The camera found him late, after he had already pulled the microphone closer. His hearing tablet remained closed beside his water.

“Call me, then,” he said.

“Mr. Ríos, procedure requires—”

“You have used procedure on my son for seven years.”

The room changed. Even through the feed Mara felt it, the private shift when an institution had to decide whether to correct pain in public. The chair lowered his hand.

“Tomás Ríos Sr.,” he said. “Recognized.”

Ríos did not sit. “Full logs.”

The two words landed badly. Too blunt for the chamber’s taste. Too close to the chants outside. FULL LOGS, RAW SKY, NO MORE PARENTS OF THE DEAD WAITING FOR PERMISSION. Mara had seen the signs in the pre-hearing security sweep. Some were held by leakers. Some by Exit recruiters. Some by families who had never joined anything except grief.

Ríos tapped the closed tablet. “All of them. Mateo’s educational payload, private logs, the angry one you marked interpretive risk, the cockpit derivatives if his voice is in them, the command files if Sofia speaks to him, the medical files if Doctor Venn’s brother tells him to breathe. All.”

Claire turned in her seat. “You do not speak for my mother.”

“No,” he said. “I speak because your mother’s command sealed my son into a public story before he ever had a chance to be angry in it.”

The chair said, “Mr. Ríos.”

“No. You made him the children’s astronaut.” He pointed, not at Claire, but at the suspended Mars. “You put his chipped tooth on breakfast feeds. You put Atacama stone in school kits. You sent his lessons into prisons and nurseries and hospital rooms. You told children he would bring the sky closer. They used his face to sell the sky.”

Mara’s selection file opened inside her without permission. Mateo Ríos: public education strength unusually high. Symbolic value mission-critical. Requires monitoring for mascot role resentment. She had written it in clean professional language. She had believed the warning absolved the use.

Ríos looked toward the Witness screen. “Dr. Venn read him. You all read him. You read the fathers, the mothers, the accents, the classrooms, the countries that would keep paying if they saw themselves aboard. Do not sit there now and tell us privacy is sacred because the record finally makes you look ugly.”

Mara’s channel light went green.

The chair said, “Dr. Venn, you may respond to the accusation as it bears on release classification.”

Nadine made no sound. Malik’s fingers hovered.

Mara leaned toward the pickup. The release bay smelled of warm plastic and the unopened citrus pouch. “Mr. Ríos is right that selection was not neutral.”

The chamber stilled. Pavel lifted his glasses, then set them down without wearing them.

Mara continued, “Mateo’s capacity to teach was part of the mission evaluation. His public role carried funding and political value. My own recommendation named that value. It also named the risk that he would be reduced by it.”

Ríos laughed once. Not humor. A stone against metal. “You named it, so it was clean?”

“No.”

The word left less room than her prepared answer. She had a page of custody language in the side display. Contextual release. Layered access. Family-specific notification. Clinical risk advisories. She let it sit there untouched.

“No,” she said again. “Naming a risk did not make it clean.”

Claire’s face closed. Ríos looked almost angrier for being answered plainly.

“The Mateo MD118 private log,” Mara said, “contains material that indicts the mission’s public education contract and the broader Mars campaign. It should enter the record. I do not recommend raw release today.”

Ríos hit the table with his palm. Water jumped in the cup. “There. There is the machine.”

“Raw release today would strip the log from chain of custody and from the later cockpit context. It would be cut into denial feeds before the whole file is read. It would also be cut into Exit streams because Mateo speaks to children and anger can be edited into permission.”

“He was angry.”

“Yes.”

“He was not your therapy note.”

“No.”

“He was my son.”

Mara’s mouth dried. Quito’s thin air was only memory, but her chest answered it. “Yes.”

That was the wrong thing to say. She knew as it left her. The word belonged to no professional category and no legal response. It gave him nothing useful. It took nothing back.

Ríos sat down slowly, disgusted by her agreement.

The chair moved papers that did not need moving. “The council notes Dr. Venn’s acknowledgment regarding selection factors. The matter of Mateo Ríos’s MD118 private log will remain attached to the release schedule debate.”

“Say the sentence,” Ríos said.

The chair paused.

Ríos looked at the dais. “From the log. The one everyone here has read except the public.”

Pavel’s expression did not change, but the muscles beside his mouth gathered.

Mara saw the file line in memory before the clerk found it. Mateo’s voice, teacherly cadence broken down to something harder: They put Mars in classrooms so no one could cancel us.

The chair said, “The council will not read restricted crew-private content in open session without classification vote.”

Ríos smiled without warmth. “Then hear yourselves withholding him.”

On Witness, Malik exhaled. The pickup caught it and rendered it as a small sea of static. He muted himself fast.

The next name on the docket should have been Ruth Venn, but the chair called a recess for medical reset in the public gallery. Cameras cut to the basalt wall. A clock appeared in three time standards. Quito civil. Witness ship. Public release delay.

Mara pushed the mandarin pouch away. It bumped the restraint web and came back.

Nadine said, “You departed framework.”

“He asked a direct question.”

“He made an accusation in a hearing built to harvest accidents.”

Mara turned. “He made an accurate accusation.”

“That is not the same as answering well.”

Sera unmuted only the local channel. “Your admission changes classification pressure on the Mateo log.”

“Yes.”

“It may force contextual inclusion.”

“Yes.”

Sera’s thumb moved over the pale mark where a ring had been. “It also gives full-release blocs a clean clip of you saying selection was compromised.”

Mara looked back at the frozen basalt wall. “It was.”

Nadine’s eyes stayed on the Quito clock. “We are not here to repair your file by bleeding into his.”

Mara took that. There was no defense against it that did not become another performance of injury.

Malik said, “Family server traffic is spiking. Three tagged channels have clipped Mr. Ríos already. Two clipped you. No Exit tags yet.”

“Yet,” Nadine said.

The hearing resumed with Ruth’s chair still empty.

For one childish second Mara thought her mother had left. Then the camera found her at the side table with a paper cup in both hands. Ruth Venn was small under the hall’s civic light, hair pinned too carefully, coat buttoned though the chamber would be climate-controlled. She had dressed for court or church and landed in neither. A woman from the Families’ Circle staff adjusted the microphone down.

“Mrs. Venn,” the chair said, softer than he had said the other names.

Ruth glanced at the witness wall. Mara was one tile among twelve. She would appear flat, blue-edged, a face beside classification banners and mute icons. Ruth did not search for her. Or she had already found her and chosen the microphone instead.

“I am not here for full logs,” Ruth said. “I am not here to answer Claire or Tomás. They have their dead. I have mine.”

Mara’s hand went to her wrist and stopped halfway.

Ruth turned the paper cup once. “I have asked before. I am asking again because each time I ask, someone writes it down as family distress and moves it to the appendix.”

Pavel closed his eyes for a moment. Mara hated him for knowing what was coming before she did, and for looking tired of it.

Ruth said, “I want one private sentence of Elias that is not broadcast, not analyzed on a school feed, not placed under guidance, not sold to any ward as medicine, not played in court. One sentence. His voice if it exists clean enough. A transcript if that is all there is. I am not asking whether it proves anything. I do not want a proof. I want a sentence I can keep in my kitchen.”

No one in Quito moved.

Ruth looked down at her cup. “There is a porch light on my house. It is stupid. It comes on at six because the timer is old and I have never fixed it. Elias used to complain it made the front room look like a dentist’s office. If there is a sentence about that, or anything like that, I am his mother. I am asking for it before the world chews it.”

Mara heard the blood in her ears. Not metaphor. A dull, timed knock. The unofficial transcript, years old, no verified audio, no full channel metadata. Mara, leave the porch light on. She had kept it under herself like contraband heat.

Sera looked at her.

Mara kept her face toward the screen.

The chair said, “Mrs. Venn, family-specific private fragments fall under restricted notification protocols. The council cannot confirm the existence of any particular unreleased sentence in open hearing.”

“I know what you cannot confirm.” Ruth’s voice stayed mild. “I have been living with what people cannot confirm.”

Pavel leaned toward his microphone. “Mrs. Venn, your request is on record. The Continuity Office recognizes the distinction between private family custody and public release.”

Ruth turned to him. “No, Director. You recognize categories. That is not the same.”

Pavel accepted the cut with a slight bow of his head.

The chair said, “Dr. Venn, are you able to address family-specific private access in the proposed framework?”

Mara’s throat worked once before sound came. “Only generally.”

Ruth looked at the screen then. Directly at her.

Mara said, “A private sentence that appears to address a living family member can meet lethal-fragment criteria if released raw or publicly acknowledged in detail. It can be misused as personal survival, exit permission, doctrine, or proof of communication. That does not mean the family has no claim.”

“Do I have a claim?” Ruth asked.

The question crossed the delay and came into Mara’s headset twice, once through the hall channel and once as a low echo from the public transcript buffer. Do I have a claim? Do I have a claim?

Mara could feel Nadine behind her. Sera beside her. Malik at the route.

“Yes,” Mara said.

The chair glanced toward counsel.

Mara continued before counsel could cut her. “If verified family-specific material exists, I recommend a restricted family-clinical seal. Private access with a trained reviewer. No recording device. No public transcript. The existence of withheld personal-address material may need to be named in a final release to avoid falsifying the record, but the raw sentence should not be public.”

Ruth’s hands tightened around the paper cup until one side folded. “A trained reviewer.”

“Yes.”

“My son cannot speak to me unless supervised.”

Mara had written that protocol. In another room, in another confidence. It had seemed humane there. It had seemed like a firebreak.

Ruth nodded once, as if confirming a hymn tempo no one else could hear. “Mara, are you the trained reviewer?”

The hearing chamber inhaled without sound.

“No,” Mara said.

It cost less than lying and more than she had prepared.

Ruth looked at her a moment longer. Then she set the collapsed cup on the table. Water had leaked onto the paper pad. “Good.”

Mara lowered her hand below the console so the camera would not catch the fingers pressing her wrist.

The chair called Director Pavel Ilyin.

Pavel stood with a tablet and no papers. His suit had crossed the hearing without a crease. On the Witness display, his projection moved into the central tile and the casualty model populated around him in restrained colors: collapse correlation, release class, age band, prior exposure, market amplification, clinical capacity. Blue for uncertainty. Amber for probable harm. Red for deaths, confirmed and projected.

Mara saw Malik look away for half a second. His mother’s chart would have lived somewhere inside these colors, stripped of her kitchen and her refusal of food.

Pavel said, “The Continuity Office submits revised casualty projections for four release scenarios concerning Asterion final-cluster material.”

The hall screens shifted. Scenario One: continued sealing. Scenario Two: family-restricted contextual access. Scenario Three: staged public context with withheld raw lethal fragments. Scenario Four: full raw release.

Pavel did not dramatize. That was part of his danger. “Continued sealing produces lower immediate collapse incidence but higher leak volatility over the next ninety days. Our model estimates a probability-weighted increase in unsupervised exposure events, especially among minors and ward-adjacent households, as rumor channels fill absence with fabricated content.”

Claire’s head turned sharply.

Pavel moved to the next field. “Family-restricted contextual access reduces litigation pressure and lowers targeted family distress markers. It raises leak risk if access protocols are weak. It does not satisfy public-evidence demand.”

Ríos stared at him as if measuring a bad seam in rock.

“Staged public context with withheld raw lethal fragments produces medium immediate distress, lower doctrinal uptake than raw release, and lower long-tail fabrication than continued sealing. It requires admitting that material exists which the public will not receive.”

“Say deaths,” Sera said from Witness.

Her channel was open. The hearing heard her.

Pavel paused.

Sera leaned closer to her pickup. “If you are going to use projections as moral weight, say what the unit is.”

Pavel put his glasses on. “Deaths, Dr. Imani. Attempts. Clinical relapses requiring intervention. Intake refusal in minors. Ward absconding. Market-triggered service loss. The categories should remain distinct.”

“Then keep them distinct on the chart.”

He looked toward an aide off camera. The chart changed. Red split into labeled bars.

Raw release was the tallest.

Pavel said, “Scenario Four, full raw release, produces the highest immediate casualty projection. Depending on leak amplification and suppression failure, we estimate between eight hundred and eleven hundred additional deaths in the first thirty days, with a confidence interval I will not pretend is narrow. Attempts are higher. Relapse events higher again. Pediatric intake refusal rises most sharply when personal-address audio or post-failure laughter circulates without context.”

A sound came from the public gallery. The microphones killed it.

Pavel went on. “This projection includes known effects from the Manila release wave, the North American hospital cluster after the Kade stream, and market-linked media bursts following the Witness advisory. It also includes error. All such models include error. But error is not permission to act as if harm is imaginary.”

Mara watched him. He had removed his old polished phrases until only their structure remained. Quarantine, firebreak, load-bearing walls. He was building a room around the public and calling the locked door care. He was also pointing at bodies on the floor.

Ríos said, “How many deaths from sealing?”

Pavel turned. “There is no clean counterfactual.”

“How many?”

“Sealing contributes to fabricated leaks, extremist recruitment, institutional distrust, and family litigation. Those create harm. The model does not assign a simple death count because causal paths are longer.”

Ríos laughed. “Convenient.”

“Yes,” Pavel said.

The answer startled the room.

He continued, “It is convenient to those of us who seal. It is also true that full release would kill people quickly enough for hospitals to count.”

Claire said, “And Sofia’s command record?”

“Raw release of the command record would likely trigger denial-state prosecution narratives and targeted harassment of Arendt family members within hours. It may also help independent engineers identify contradictions we have mishandled.”

Claire’s jaw moved.

Ruth asked, “And private sentence?”

Pavel’s face softened by a fraction. “A personal-address fragment, if any exists, is among the highest-risk classes for public release.”

Ruth nodded. “That was not my question.”

“No,” Pavel said. “It was not.”

Sera’s tile brightened as the chair recognized her. She had already pulled the casualty overlay off her local monitor and replaced it with an evidence chain. No colors except black text on white fields, checksum notes, timestamp disputes, source gaps.

“Dr. Imani,” the chair said, “the Assembly requests scientific characterization of the contested material.”

Sera folded her hands. She looked cold even through two relay systems. “First correction: these are not simulations of Asterion’s final minutes.”

A council member said, “No one used that term in the formal question.”

“It appears in the public docket twenty-seven times, in family briefs nine times, and in three of Director Ilyin’s appendices as ‘simulated release impact.’ I am not objecting to casualty modeling. I am objecting to the word when attached to the records themselves.”

Pavel’s mouth tightened.

Sera said, “Asterion final-cluster files are corrupted records with partial recovery, duplicated timestamps, checksum repairs, source disputes, and incompatible instrument states. They are not recreations. They are not playable truth. They are not evidence that the universe is a simulation. They are not evidence that it is not. They are evidence that matter and information failed under conditions we do not yet understand.”

The hall held that because it had nowhere else to put it.

She continued, “The Sofia command log contains a real procedural marker attached to a corrupted duration field. Treating that as proof of command error is scientifically false. Treating it as too dangerous to study is also false.”

Claire said, “You can study it sealed.”

“For a time,” Sera said. “Not forever.”

Ríos leaned in. “And Mateo?”

“Mateo Ríos’s private log is not Boundary data in the narrow physical sense. It is mission evidence. It bears on consent, public framing, and institutional use of educational programming. It belongs in any honest context package.”

Mara felt the hearing turn again. Sera had done what Mara should have done first, with less blood in it.

Ríos said, “Raw.”

Sera shook her head once. “Context. Not smoothing. Not inspirational edits. Not raw as a dare.”

“And who writes context?” Ríos asked. “Her?” He pointed at Mara’s tile.

Sera did not look at Mara. “Context should be signed by more than one compromised person.”

A faint heat moved up Mara’s neck. Good, she thought. Correct. Also betrayal. Also care.

The chair said, “Dr. Imani, address the phrase ‘bounded simulation’ as it appears in public filings.”

Sera’s shoulders rose and fell once. “No.”

The chair blinked. “No?”

“I will address evidence. I will not give that phrase a scientific dignity it has not earned. The Boundary has location without stable surface. It produces repeated star fields, radar self-return, material stress, missing mass without radiation signature, and record contradictions. Some people interpret those facts through artificial-world hypotheses. Others through instrument failure, causal breakdown, unknown physics, theology, fraud, or political denial. Science has not selected among them.”

A council member said, “The public uses the term.”

“The public also uses ‘glass.’ Children use it because adults failed them. I do not put children’s word on a physics chart either.”

Nadine made the smallest approving sound through her nose.

Sera went on, “If the Assembly releases Asterion material under language implying a solved diagnosis, it will lie. If it hides every record that can be misread, it will also lie. The data will not become safer because we wait for it to become clean.”

Pavel said, “Nor will people become less dead because we admire ambiguity.”

Sera turned to him. “Do not make precision sound like admiration. I am saying your model is evidence too, not commandment.”

“Then state your recommendation.”

“Class B public context for selected contaminated records. Class C raw restriction for personal-address material, decontextualized post-failure laughter, and prayer-track devotional audio. Family-clinical access where claims are direct. Independent scientific custody for command and instrument data with delayed public digest. The Mateo log included with institutional context and harm advisory.”

Ríos said, “Digest means you chew him first.”

Sera’s face did not change. “Sometimes it means we remove the glass before giving a child bread.”

Ríos stood so fast his chair struck the panel behind him. “Do not use children at me.”

Sera stopped. The wrong phrase hung there. Her precision had crossed into someone else’s wound and cut without aiming.

Mara opened her channel. “Mr. Ríos, that metaphor should be withdrawn.”

Sera’s eyes flicked to Mara. Then to Ríos. “Withdrawn.”

The chair called for order, but order had become a table with too many broken legs.

Claire said, “If Mateo’s log is context, Sofia’s command record is context.”

Ríos shot back, “If Sofia’s command is sealed, Mateo’s anger will be sealed because it embarrasses the same people.”

Ruth said, “If Elias speaks my name, you will put a clinician between us and call it mercy.”

Pavel said, “If we publish raw personal address, people will die calling their own dead to answer.”

Sera said, “If we call all personal address false, we falsify the record.”

Nadine spoke from Witness, not recognized by the chair. “If this hearing becomes release design by injury, I will recommend no shipboard concurrence.”

The chamber startled into silence at command voice.

The chair’s gavel came down late. “Captain Qureshi, you are not in the speaking order.”

Nadine leaned into frame behind Mara. “Then put me in it.”

Pavel’s eyes moved to her. “Captain, the Assembly holds release authority.”

“And Witness holds the next packet,” Nadine said. “We are approaching a place that breaks packets. If you turn every family claim into a lever, you will get a release none of you can carry. No martyrs. No trophies. No raw dumps to prove courage. No sealed rooms to protect reputations. Decide what helps the living act tomorrow and what belongs to the dead unless the living need it.”

The chair stared up at the Witness wall. “Captain, your statement is noted.”

“Good,” Nadine said, and muted.

Mara felt the words strike her later than the room did. What belongs to the dead unless the living need it. She had spent years making the opposite argument in finer language. History needed the dead. Science needed them. Families needed them. The frightened public needed them. She needed Elias most of all and had dressed that need in every available office.

The chair called a procedural suspension. This time no clock appeared. The feed stayed live by mistake for eleven seconds. Long enough for the chamber to become a room instead of an institution.

Claire reached for her water and missed the cup, corrected, drank. Ríos bent over his closed tablet with both hands on the case. Ruth folded the wet paper cup into smaller and smaller squares. Pavel rubbed the old burn at his wrist, then stopped when he saw the camera light. Sera pulled her sleeves lower as if Quito could make her colder through the screen.

Malik whispered, “Still live.”

The feed cut to the IIA seal.

No one in the release bay spoke.

Mara unhooked her foot from the restraint bar. The mandarin pouch drifted up against the console. Its silver skin caught the monitor light and turned briefly the color of the Asterion frame.

“Malik,” she said. “Queue a draft classification note.”

He swallowed. “For which file?”

“All of them touched today. Sofia command. Mateo private. Elias family-specific. Leila prayer-track cross-reference. Jun cockpit derivative.”

Sera looked up. “Jun?”

“The next context problem,” Mara said.

Nadine’s gaze sharpened. “You want to open that now?”

“No. I want the hearing record to show we knew it was coming before it was useful to anyone.”

Malik typed. “Header?”

Mara looked at the blank note field until her reflection in it thinned.

“Plural family claims,” she said. “No single grief authority. Disclosure harms. Withholding harms. Raw evidence remains evidence even when release is refused.”

Sera said, “Add: casualty projections are not truth conditions.”

Mara nodded. “Add it.”

Nadine said, “Add: Witness concurs only with package release, not fragment release.”

“Package,” Malik repeated.

Mara turned toward Sera. “And add that no file is to use the word as diagnosis.”

The IIA seal vanished. Quito returned. The suspended Mars swung almost imperceptibly above the dais, moved by air systems in a hall built to look still.

Mara’s throat closed around the answer she could not give her.
