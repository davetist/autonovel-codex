[ASTERION SYSTEMS RECORD / ENGINEERING BAY TWO]  
[Mission Day 118 / Source family: OKONKWO MAINT-PRIVATE / recovery status partial]  
[Analyst caution: internal clock agrees with Command Log branch A for first 04:12, then follows no verified sequence. Audio channel retains cabin fan and tool contact. Visual frames repaired from two damaged buffers. Reconstruction uncertain where marked.]

The first screw came out hot.

Amara caught it against her palm before it floated into the rack. Heat through glove liner, enough to make her hiss once and close her fist. The screw had no business being warm. Bay two ran cool. The sensor housing sat outside the fluid loop and below the forward panel row, dull gray, boring, faithful hardware. A box for measuring strain. A box to tell the ship when metal changed shape.

“Record thermal,” she said.

The maintenance tablet blinked at her. The number arrived in a small font: 41.8 C.

“No.”

She set the screw into a magnetic dish and touched the housing with the back of two fingers. Warm. Not alarm warm. Body warm. It offended her more that way, like a machine trying to join the crew.

“Okonkwo, repeat?” Sofia’s voice came through the loop, low and clipped.

“Fastener one from B2 strain housing reads forty-one point eight Celsius after extraction. Housing exterior warm to touch.”

“Fire path?”

“None on panel. No smoke. No trace smell.”

“Voltage?”

“Checking.”

Amara braced one knee under the rack and pulled herself closer. The engineering bay had begun its day with ordinary annoyances: adhesive tabs losing grip, Jun swearing at a nav offset, Elias nagging everyone about hydration because he had found one low urine specific gravity and decided to become the angel of electrolytes. Ordinary meant repairable. Ordinary meant the world had agreed to the size of a screw.

She pulled the second screw. Cold.

“Fastener two nominal,” she said.

The tablet offered its polite green check. She hated green checks. They came too early, before the ship finished telling the truth.

She worked the driver into the third head. The pencil nub behind her ear had drifted loose again; she saw it rising near the duct grille and trapped it with her shoulder against the wall.

“Your pencil is escaping,” Mateo said from the hatch.

“Then catch it or mourn it.”

He caught it. His fingers closed around the yellow stub with too much care, like it was a specimen or a child’s tooth. He pushed himself into bay two and hooked a foot under the restraint rail. He had his sample pouch strapped tight across his torso. The little Atacama stone rode inside it, a private weight in a craft where weight had become a memory.

“What are we looking at?” he asked.

“We are looking at you learning hatch discipline.”

He looked behind him. “Hatch is clear.”

“You are in my light.”

He shifted, not far enough.

The third screw broke.

The head came away clean in the driver, leaving the shaft seated in the housing. Amara stopped moving. The fan rattle above them seemed to sharpen. Asterion made many sounds, all of them known to her by now: pump hum with a tired bearing, air through a filter that needed tapping, Mateo’s breath when he tried to pretend he was not waiting to ask something. This sound changed nothing. That was the problem. A screw had failed and the room carried on.

“Fastener three sheared,” she said.

“Torque?” Sofia.

“Within spec. My hand is not a hydraulic press.”

Jun cut in, too fast. “Panel load from correction?”

“No,” Amara said.

“Okonkwo.”

“No. Manual attitude correction cannot give me a hot screw, a cold screw, and a shear line in a housing that reports no load above point three.”

“Can you pull data?”

“I can pull everything except a sensible universe. Stand by.”

Silence from command. Sofia’s silence had shape. It took up less room than other people’s speech and more than their panic.

Amara clipped a capture bag over the housing before she removed the remaining fasteners. The bag was transparent and already scratched from use. Through it the gray cover looked cheap. She remembered teaching a Lagos class from a pump housing older than half the students, the screws mismatched, the gasket cut from truck inner tube. They had kept that pump running for nineteen days during a flood season. No speech had done it. No pledge. A girl with a blue ribbon on her wrist had found the bad seal because she refused to trust the gauge.

Trust the material. Listen until it stops lying.

The cover came free.

Inside, the strain lattice had crazed.

Not cracked. Not melted. The fine printed conductive lines had whitened in branching fans around three anchor points. Stress whitening without bend. Micro-pits clustered along one edge, each pit bright under the inspection lamp, each with raised lips as if something had pressed from inside the board and thought better of emerging.

Amara held her breath and turned the cover slowly.

The underside carried an imprint.

It was shallow, almost too shallow for the camera: a curved pressure mark crossing the flat polymer, then a second angle cutting it. Not a tool mark. Not a known bracket. Not any geometry that belonged inside bay two. The mark looked like the cover had been clamped against a piece of hardware from another part of the ship, but the housing had never moved from its mount. She had signed off on its installation. She remembered the inspection stamp, her own thumbprint smudged on the edge because Jun had made her laugh during inventory.

“Video to command,” she said.

“Received,” Sofia said. Then, after half a breath, “Explain.”

Amara wanted the old room in Lagos, the bad gasket, a student’s hand raised because matter had provided a clue. She wanted rust, heat, force, fatigue. She wanted failure with manners.

“Stress pattern with no matching load path,” she said. “Polymer crazing without heat source. Anchor points show micro-pitting. Cover underside has compression imprint from nonlocal geometry.”

Mateo’s foot slipped from the rail. He caught himself against the cabinet.

“Nonlocal geometry?” he said.

Amara turned her head toward him. “That is me being polite for command.”

“What is impolite?”

“The box has bruises from a thing that was never here.”

Static moved once through the loop. Elias came in after it, voice lower than his public voice. “Any exposure risk?”

“No loose fibers visible. No smell. I have it bagged.”

“Hands?”

“My hands are not samples.”

“Amara.”

She flexed her fingers for the camera. “Sensation intact. No burn except wounded pride from fastener one.”

“Good. Pride is noncritical tissue.”

“Doctor, your charting lacks ambition.”

“Hydrate after this.”

“Log his harassment,” she told the tablet.

The tablet logged nothing. It blinked and displayed two identical timestamps with different battery readings.

Amara stared at the screen.

The first line read 13:42:09 / Tablet power 62%. The second read 13:42:09 / Tablet power 61%. Both claimed to be current. Both pulsed in time with the cabin clock. She tapped one. The screen refreshed and the duplicated entry vanished. It left no deletion mark.

“Mateo,” she said.

“I saw it.”

“Say what you saw.”

“Two battery readings, same timestamp.”

“Good. Do not improve it.”

“I saw it,” he said, sharper.

She regretted the tone before she finished hearing his. Regret was not useful. It did not put the vanished line back.

“Command, maintenance tablet showed duplicated timestamp with conflicting battery value. Visual witness Ríos.”

“Logged,” Sofia said. “Park, confirm no attitude event.”

Jun exhaled too close to his mic. “No attitude event. Star Tracker C is still being a poet, but propulsion is quiet.”

“Kill the word poet,” Amara said.

“I meant liar.”

“Better.”

Mateo moved closer despite her earlier warning. He held her pencil out. “Your pencil.”

“Keep it.”

“It’s yours.”

“It wants you now.”

He tucked it behind his own ear. It looked wrong there, too small against his dark hair, and for a second the wrongness pleased her. A thing displaced for a simple reason. A reason with a hand in it.

She pulled the sensor board forward on its ribbon. The data core behind it had recorded strain within margin for the last eighteen hours. One spike appeared at 03:11 ship time, a thin vertical event so narrow it looked like a cursor left on the graph. The spike rose above full-scale and returned within one sample window. The neighboring thermal channel stayed flat. The vibration channel stayed asleep. The fastener load cells registered less than a sneeze.

“Found a spike,” she said.

“Magnitude?” Sofia.

“Beyond calibrated range.”

“Duration?”

“One sample.”

“That is noise.”

“No.”

Jun made a sound. “One-sample spike beyond range is noise until it repeats.”

“Come say that to the panel.”

“I’m flying the lying sky.”

“You are holding handles while software panics.”

“Children,” Sofia said.

Amara bit the inside of her cheek. “Commander, this is not noise. The board is physically damaged along the same anchor set.”

“Cause?”

“That is the missing noun.”

Elias asked, “Could the board have been damaged during install?”

“No.”

“During launch?”

“No.”

“By you just now?”

“Doctor.”

“Asked and withdrawn.”

Mateo leaned toward the display, his hands tucked under his elbows to keep from touching. He had good hands for rock, Amara had always thought. Expressive, too ready to explain. He could make basalt sound like bread and water to children. He could make a crater patient. That was why they had chosen him, and why the choice sometimes made him furious in quiet ways.

“Could you explain it as stored stress?” he asked.

“I could lie in many clean sentences.”

“I mean for the lesson packet.”

The words arrived badly. Amara heard them enter the room before Mateo seemed to hear himself say them.

“For the what?”

He swallowed. “The schools will ask. If this goes into the public feed later, or even a sanitized engineering clip, they’ll ask what happened to the material. There are children tracking the maintenance modules. They know bay two. Ms. Iyer’s class sent the panel map with the little stickers.”

“Bay two is not a classroom.”

“I know.”

“No, you forget. This is a pressure hull. It is not a red hill on a wall. It is not your little stone in a pouch. It is not a place for a child to practice wonder.”

His mouth opened. The chipped front tooth showed white against his lower lip.

She kept going because anger had found a rail. “Do you want the friendly version? The panel felt confused? The metal had a difficult morning? The ship is learning new shapes? Give them that. Put a cartoon gasket on it. Smile.”

The pencil behind his ear drifted free.

Mateo caught it again, slower this time. He closed his fingers around it and tucked it into the elastic of his sample pouch.

Elias said, “Amara.”

She turned on the open housing. “No.”

“Hands,” he said.

“My hands are fine.”

“I mean stop using them as weapons.”

Sofia’s voice came colder. “Engineering channel remains operational.”

The rebuke should have landed. It bounced.

Mateo looked at the cover floating in the capture bag, at the faint impossible imprint under the lamp. “I asked because they will ask me.”

The sentence had no defense in it. That made it worse.

Amara turned back to the housing. The smell in the bay was only warm polymer and the dry dust of opened electronics. She wanted a stronger smell. Burnt insulation would have given her something to hate.

“Mateo,” she said.

“Leave it.”

“No. I am apologizing badly.”

“Then do it better.”

“I do not have the part.”

He laughed once, without pleasure.

She reached into the tool sleeve and selected a smaller probe. Her hand had begun to press too hard against the restraint. She made herself loosen her grip finger by finger. “The sentence I should have said is: do not explain before we know what hurt the ship. The sentence I said was cruel.”

Mateo rubbed the pencil nub against the pouch seam. “That is still mostly a correction.”

“Yes.”

“Fine.”

“Not fine.”

“No,” he said. “But fine enough to keep working.”

Sofia let the silence hold for two seconds. “Okonkwo, continue inspection. Ríos, remain if useful. No education packet language on open loop.”

Mateo’s jaw worked once. “Understood.”

Amara removed the board fully and clipped it to the inspection frame. The ribbon cable curled in the capture bag like a pale worm. She marked it with a red tab, then froze at the color. Red meant caution, not Mars. Red meant do not touch. Red had a job.

She said the job aloud. “Red tab: unverified material deformation. Do not handle outside bag.”

The tablet recorded the note this time. One timestamp. One battery value.

She magnified the crazing to 200x. The branches around the anchor points were not random. The whitening flowed around absences, three oval voids where stress should have concentrated but had not. She ran the expected-force model from the maintenance library. The model overlaid clean blue lines across the board. It wanted a torque event from the lower left. The actual pattern bent upward, then turned back on itself. The model failed with a soft chime.

She ran micrometeoroid secondary shock. No match.

Launch stress. No match.

Thermal expansion. No match.

Manual attitude correction load from Procedure G-118-L, using Jun’s shorter input branch. No match. Using the longer branch. No match.

She disliked that last test. It gave history an excuse. Jun’s hands on controls, Sofia’s order, the old clean story that a person had done a thing and matter had answered. Blame had a shape people could hold. The board refused even that.

“Command,” she said. “Known load models fail.”

“Define fail.”

“Residuals above tolerance by stupid amounts.”

“Engineering language,” Sofia said.

“That is engineering language.”

Mateo, still watching the display, spoke quietly. “May I ask one thing that is not for children?”

“No.”

He waited.

Amara closed the failed model. “Ask.”

“If the hull records forces, and the forces are not there, then what is the hull recording?”

“That is two questions.”

“Amara.”

She looked at the cover again. The impossible imprint crossed the underside in a curve she almost recognized. She had seen that radius in another assembly. Maybe a handrail bracket from the mid-deck. Maybe part of the exercise frame. Asterion had many radii. Engineers loved repeatable curves. They made fabrication cheaper and repair possible. But this mark had no screw scars, no alignment points, no grime shadow. It was the memory of a contact with no contact.

“It is recording damage,” she said.

“That’s the answer?”

“That is the honest part.”

From the life sciences loop, Leila’s voice entered with care sharpened by urgency. “Engineering, I need contamination path status on bay two.”

Amara straightened. “No breach. Housing bagged. Why?”

“Tray four has entered failure.”

Elias cut in before Amara could speak. “Leila, define failure.”

“Vascular analogues rupturing across three wells. No temperature rise. No radiation flag. Nutrient feed steady. pH within range until rupture, then scatter.”

“Scatter how?”

“Different readings from paired probes in the same well.”

Amara looked at the sensor board. The stress pattern sat under magnification like frost on old plastic.

“Visual?” Elias asked.

“Sending,” Leila said.

A small window opened on Amara’s tablet without her touching it. Biological tray four filled the corner of the screen: six sealed wells, clear gel, red tracer threads through tissue cultures designed to mimic capillary beds without being bodies. In wells two and five, the red threads had burst into clouded blooms. In well three, the tracer line remained intact but the scanner overlay marked absent mass in a green outline, a polite shape around missing material.

Amara wanted to close the window.

Leila said, “Before anyone asks, tray one is nominal. Tray two is nominal. Tray three shows edge artifacts but no rupture. Tray four is dying.”

“Samples are not alive,” Jun said, too fast.

Leila answered without heat. “The tissue is dying.”

Elias said, “Any human exposure?”

“No. Containment intact. I have double seal. No aerosol alarm.”

“Could this be feed pressure?” Sofia asked.

“I checked. Pump curve is clean.”

Amara pressed her tongue to the back of her teeth. Clean. The word should have comforted somebody. It made the room smaller.

“Leila,” she said, “send me tray mount strain data.”

“Already queued.”

Data arrived. Tray four mount had a spike at 03:11.

Same needle. Same impossible thinness. No thermal rise. No vibration answer. One sample wide.

Amara’s breath came in through her nose, out through her mouth. She did it because breathing was a pump and pumps obeyed if their parts remained whole.

“Your tray and my housing share an event,” she said.

Leila was silent long enough for the fan rattle to return.

“Cause?” Sofia asked.

Leila said, “No clean cause.”

Amara almost corrected her. Clean cause was soft language. Cause was cause. But the words stopped at her teeth. The board gleamed under the lamp. The tray window showed ruptured red threading in gel. The same time sat under both failures, thin enough to erase, deep enough to bruise metal and tissue.

Elias spoke in his physician voice, the one that asked the body to stay. “Crew check. Everyone in bay two and life sciences, report sensation, breathing, skin, vision.”

Jun muttered, “Here we go.”

“Park, report anyway,” Sofia said.

“Sensation normal. Breathing normal. Skin normal. Vision annoyed.”

“Arendt: no symptoms.”

“Ríos,” Elias said.

Mateo flexed his hands. “Sensation normal. Breathing normal. Skin normal. Vision normal.”

“Okonkwo.”

“My skin is offended by bad data. Otherwise nominal.”

“Leila?”

“Normal. Hands cold. That is not new.”

“Elias,” Sofia said.

“Normal, except everyone is rude to medicine until they need it.”

Amara found herself smiling, which made her angrier than the failure. She looked away from the tray feed and back into the housing cavity. Behind the removed board, three mounting posts rose from the bay panel. Their bases had tiny pits on the side facing inward. Inward, toward nothing but the cabin wall behind them.

She inserted the inspection probe.

The probe returned a surface map with one smooth depression crossing two posts at once. Impossible. The posts sat three centimeters apart, separate pieces, separate load paths. The depression aligned through the empty space between them, as if something had pressed across both and ignored the gap.

“New deformation,” she said.

No one answered.

She kept her voice flat. “Mounting posts show continuous compression feature across discontinuous material.”

Sofia said, “Translate only if useful.”

“It is not useful.”

“Try.”

“The ship is showing a dent that crosses empty space.”

Mateo whispered something in Spanish. Amara caught only madre and no. He had no public voice in that moment. No lesson. No red hill, no patient explanation of basalt under dry sky. Just a son of mining towns staring at metal that refused the courtesy of being metal.

The engineering bay lights flickered once.

Asterion’s main bus did not flicker. The local lamp did. Amara checked the feed. Power remained nominal. The lamp had registered no drop. Its light had changed anyway, a soft dimming that the meter denied.

Jun’s voice came over the loop, low now. “Optics just returned two Mars positions.”

“Navigation channel only,” Sofia said.

“Commander, one is right and one is wrong.”

“Then you know which to ignore.”

“No. The wrong one is where it should be after correction. The right one is where it is.”

Static. A clipped breath. Someone, maybe Elias, shifted near a mic.

Amara closed the navigation pane before it could open on her tablet. She had enough impossible in the room. Mars could wait outside the hull with its red face and its perfect useless distance. Mars had no screws for her to remove.

“Okonkwo,” Sofia said. “Can bay two remain in service?”

Amara almost answered yes. Engineers learned that yes was sometimes a prayer disguised as confidence. Keep it running. Keep the pump alive one more night. Keep pressure because pressure was life.

She looked at the board. It had been damaged by a force with no source. It had recorded a spike too short to hold and too strong to dismiss. It agreed with Leila’s dying tray in time and disagreed with itself in matter. Service meant trust. Trust meant names.

“No,” she said. “Bay two strain network is unfit for structural assurance.”

“Scope?”

“Unknown.”

“Recommend?”

“Isolate B2 sensor branch. Flag adjacent panels for manual inspection. Suspend any model that uses this branch as confirming data. Do not call it redundant. It is not backup if it lies with confidence.”

“Logged.”

Mateo touched the wall beside him. Not a grip. Two fingers laid flat on the panel above the rack, feeling for vibration or warmth. Amara let him. The wall under his hand had carried them for 118 days. It had held sleep sweat, meal crumbs, Sofia’s quiet orders, Jun’s feet hooked wherever feet could hook, Elias’s medical jokes, Leila’s sealed samples, Mateo’s broadcast voice saying children’s names to the camera as if the ship had room for all of them.

Mateo pulled his hand back. “It feels normal.”

“Yes,” Amara said.

“That is worse.”

“Yes.”

Elias said, “Mateo, come by med after you leave bay two.”

“I’m fine.”

“That was not the question.”

“I am fine enough to keep working.”

“Everybody is stealing Amara’s apology structure today.”

Leila’s voice came again, quieter. “Tray four well three now shows mass variance.”

“How much?” Sofia asked.

“Point zero two grams.”

Jun gave a short laugh, wrong in the mouth. “That’s dust.”

“In a sealed biological well,” Leila said. “With no residue in trap.”

Amara looked at the screw in the magnetic dish. Fastener one, warm as skin. Fastener two, cold. Fastener three, broken under her hand. She added their masses from preflight inventory in her head, a habit from old work when scales failed and budgets failed more often. The magnetic dish scale read them now. The total was off by point zero one grams.

She recalibrated.

The number held.

She removed the broken head from the driver and set it in a second dish. The total corrected, then drifted, then corrected again. Point zero one grams appeared and vanished under the readout like a shy thief.

“Scale on removed fasteners unstable by point zero one grams,” she said.

Sofia answered at once. “Contain hardware.”

“Already contained.”

“Elias, contamination protocol?”

“Material unknown. Treat as mechanical hazard first. No skin contact. No ingestion. No one puts a broken screw in their mouth to prove courage.”

“Who would do that?” Jun asked.

“Pilots.”

“I resent accuracy.”

Amara sealed the first capture bag inside a second. The outer seal ran blue, then amber, then blue again. She pressed until the adhesive line stayed blue. Her broad hands looked clumsy through two layers of plastic. She had repaired pumps under floodwater with less ceremony than this. She had rebuilt an oxygen scrubber in orbit while a sponsor delegation waited for a photo and a child on the uplink asked whether astronauts ever got lonely. She had answered the child while elbow-deep in condensate because loneliness and condensate both had to be handled.

Now the housing sat sealed in a bag and the bag told her almost nothing.

Mateo said, “I’m sorry.”

“For what?”

“For asking too soon.”

“You asked the right wound with the wrong tool.”

He nodded as if that helped. It did not help. It was only true.

Leila came on private engineering-biomed crosslink. Her voice lost none of its measure, but the pauses had changed. “Amara, can you look at the tray mount image?”

“Send.”

The image arrived. Tray four sat in life sciences, white clips, double elastic around each vial because Leila never trusted a single hold when two would fit. The mount had three retaining tabs. One tab showed pitting. Another looked smooth. The third carried a shallow diagonal crease.

Amara magnified. The crease matched nothing in the mount kit. She rotated the image ninety degrees, then back.

“Your tab has a compression mark from nothing we installed,” she said.

“Mine too.”

“You knew?”

“I wanted your words first.”

“My words are ugly.”

“That is why I asked.”

Amara adjusted the contrast. The crease crossed the tab and vanished at its edge. On the adjacent frame, beyond a gap of open air and clear lid, another crease began at the same angle.

She felt the bay shrink around her. Not with fear. Fear was old wiring, useful if it did not chew through the insulation. This was something else, a tool chest opened to find every tool renamed.

“Leila,” she said, “it crosses the gap.”

“Yes.”

“You have checked the physical mount?”

“I am looking at it.”

“With your eyes.”

“With my eyes.”

“And it is there.”

“It is there.”

“Do not say miracle.”

“I was going to say contamination of the stress record.”

“That is worse.”

“It is more precise.”

“It is worse because it may be right.”

Leila breathed once through her nose. “The sample in well two has stopped responding to feed correction.”

“Responding?”

“I know.”

The word hung between them, too close to alive. Amara stared at the red tracer cloud inside the well. The tissue had no mind. No breath. No mother waiting for it to call from a stairwell. It still offended the body when it died. Leila knew that. She had chosen the word because all others were worse.

Mateo said, on the open channel, “If they ask me whether it hurt the ship, what do I say?”

Sofia answered before Amara could. “No public packet has been authorized.”

“That is not an answer for later.”

“No.”

Mateo’s voice tightened. “Commander.”

Sofia said nothing.

Amara slid the sealed housing into the hazard cradle. It clicked once. Good click. Honest click. Latch closed, load borne, state changed.

“Say the material failed under an unconfirmed stress event,” she said.

Mateo looked at her.

“That is ugly and mostly true.”

“Mostly.”

“All language is mostly once the metal starts this.”

He wrote it down. His stylus trembled at the end of stress. He saw her see it and stopped writing.

The tablet chirped.

A system annotation field had opened without command.

Amara frowned. “Did anyone request engineering narrative?”

“No,” Sofia said.

The field blinked white. Cursor at line start. Timestamp: 13:42:09.

Again.

The old duplicate time sat there, impossible and familiar after only minutes, already behaving like a scar. Amara touched the screen edge, not the field. The tablet accepted no input from her gloved hand. The cursor blinked anyway.

“Maintenance tablet opened annotation field at duplicated timestamp,” she said.

“Close it,” Sofia ordered.

Amara tapped close. Nothing happened.

“Power cycle tablet,” Sofia said.

Amara held the side switch. The screen went black, showed the manufacturer mark, returned to the same field. Same timestamp. Same cursor.

Jun said softly, “I hate that.”

“No one invited your feelings,” Amara said.

“They arrived early.”

Elias: “Put them in the log.”

Sofia: “Enough.”

Amara should have obeyed the cursor by refusing it. Annotation fields were traps. Narrative was where clean work went to drown. A damaged housing needed specimen ID, chain of custody, thermal record, strain model. Not phrases. Phrases escaped. Phrases became captions under pictures. Phrases let people put candles before wreckage and pretend the flame explained the metal.

The cursor blinked.

Mateo’s pencil nub floated loose again from the pouch elastic. This time nobody caught it. It drifted between Amara and the open wall, turning slowly, blunt end first.

Leila said, almost too low for the crosslink, “I have no clean cause.”

Amara looked at the magnified stress pattern, the crease crossing absence, the pitted posts, the sealed tray where tissue broke without heat or radiation. Her matter-first truth stood in the bay with no boots on. Matter was still all they had. Matter had started speaking in contradictions.

She placed one gloved finger on the tablet field. The keyboard opened.

“What are you entering?” Sofia asked.

“Engineering annotation.”

“Keep it descriptive.”

“Yes.”

Her finger hovered over the letters. Descriptive meant no prayer, no lesson packet, no friendly grammar for children. It meant the sentence must fail honestly.

She typed:

The hull is remembering shapes it never had.

No one spoke.

The tablet accepted the sentence, duplicated the timestamp, and saved two copies. In the first, the final word was had. In the second, the final word was held.
