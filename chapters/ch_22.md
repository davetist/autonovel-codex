## Chapter 22: Candle Burn

“Candle couch empty,” Malik said.

The phrase crossed the command deck in his flat routing voice, as if it were no stranger than carrier lock or packet health. Mara had heard it in launch checks, static tests, legal arguments, and Nadine’s sealed-order disclosure. Here, with the recognition craft already cold-started beyond Witness’s hull, the words landed with the weight of a body refused.

Nadine kept one hand hooked near the forward rail. “Confirm occupied-mode invalid.”

Tomas answered from the systems well. “Occupied Boundary approach invalid. Manual left-rail life-support bridge remains under command seal. H-12 closed. Automated isolation green for unmanned operation.”

“Say the couch.”

Tomas looked at his board. His shoulders had drawn in around his headset. “Candle couch empty.”

Sera made a correction without looking away from the optical stream. “The craft is unmanned. The couch is a legacy assembly.”

Nadine’s mouth moved once. Not quite a smile. “Legacy assembly empty.”

Mara sat at the interpretive station with her feet locked under the restraint bar and her hands free. Yuna had cleared her for non-Elias general interpretive monitoring four hours earlier with a note so narrow it read like a corridor: no private playback, no family-specific material, no Class C authority alone, no unsupervised Elias-associated review. Sera’s reviewer token now hung on a physical lanyard against Sera’s compression sleeve. Malik had joked that it looked like a school field trip badge. Nobody had answered.

The command deck smelled of warmed plastic and stale mandarin from an opened ration pouch wedged beside the comms panel. Someone had taken three segments and left the syrup. The handrail under Mara’s left palm was polished smooth where broadcasts made people grip harder than training required. Mars stood on the central display: red, measurable, weathering under thin dust above Valles Marineris, clean behind the overlay boxes that named range, burn window, delay, and confidence. Candle was a white marker moving toward a zone no camera could outline.

“Witness local time 21:44:08,” Nadine said. “Candle release burn complete. Remote attitude stable.”

Anika Rao’s ground voice arrived late through the Quito/Houston relay, clipped by distance and error repair. “Witness, Flight Dynamics confirms separation track inside pre-burn tolerance. Our abort-window model is holding but thin. Recommend no discretionary extension beyond Band Two margins.”

“Received late, understood current,” Nadine said.

Sera leaned toward the optical panel. “Band Zero exit in forty seconds.”

Tomas said, “High-tower mast deployed.”

Mara saw it in the schematic: Candle’s narrow antenna tower rising from the squat white body, inelegant and overbuilt, a little spine of angles and hinge stops. It had been called the tower in every engineering meeting because the formal name took too long and because Tomas hated graceful words for ugly tasks. The tower gave Candle enough geometry to keep talking while its body pointed into disagreement.

“Public delay buffer?” Nadine asked.

Malik tapped with two fingers, then stopped tapping when his own sound annoyed him. “Active. Thirty-one minutes nominal. Continuity overlay locked. Ground mirrors restricted. No public raw feed.”

“Leak channels?”

“Three active, two stale, one claiming live Candle optics from before we released the craft.” He swallowed. “It’s an Asterion public-frame crop.”

“Flag and route.”

“Already done.”

Mara watched the public-pressure strip at the lower edge of her screen brighten in sections. It had no faces, no candle smoke, no children at desks, but she knew what each color meant. Continuity wards had shifted staffing. Mumbai had suspended two instruments. Quito had moved to closed emergency session. The Makers’ Address had purchased a full-wall placement across twelve major feeds and was calling it a civic petition. Exit channels had adopted new language in less than eleven minutes.

Empty throne.

It appeared first as a moderation tag, then as a scraped phrase, then as a red warning under “self-harm recruitment risk.” Candle’s empty couch had become furniture for people who wanted a universe with a door.

Mara touched two fingers to the inside of her wrist. Pulse fast. Countable.

Sera said, “Band One.”

Nothing changed on the main view. Mars remained red. Candle’s telemetry arrived in tight packets with the little delays she had learned to mistrust less than smoothness. Mass scale nominal. Tissue trays cold within tolerance. Organoid analogues electrically isolated. Archive drives sealed. Antenna tower locked.

“Optical array A,” Sera said. “Give me raw star map.”

Malik routed the feed. The star field filled the left pane, black pricked by white in the boring, brutal abundance that had once comforted schoolchildren and navigation models. A thin green box marked the background constellation used for attitude solution.

Sera’s thumb moved over the old ring mark at her finger. “Compare to calibration field MD minus three.”

The overlay blinked.

Tomas muttered, “Too early.”

“Say it as a value,” Nadine said.

Sera did. “Optical A returns a ninety-seven point four percent match to calibration field recorded before launch, adjusted for current attitude. It should be below twelve. Optical B?”

Malik brought it up.

For seven seconds, B showed a different sky. Then the stars slid, not as motion but as correction, and settled into the same old field with a current timestamp.

Mara felt the deck’s ventilation against her cheek. Cold air, too steady. The body liked steady things even when steady things lied.

“Repeated star field at outer degradation,” Sera said. “Classify as expected anomaly, stronger than predicted. No inference beyond optical failure or unknown propagation.”

“Candle may continue if two of three navigation systems agree,” Nadine said.

“They agree by becoming wrong together,” Tomas said.

“That is not agreement,” Sera said.

“It is within software margin.”

“It is within a software margin written for ordinary lies.”

Nadine’s hand shifted over the rail. “Hardware recommendation.”

Tomas looked at the tower schematic. “Continue. Unmanned. Transmission health good. If we abort now, we learn that optical repeats at Band One. We know that.”

“Sera?”

“Continue with contamination marker.”

“Mara?”

Mara lifted her eyes from the public strip. She had no command authority over the burn. Nadine asked anyway because interpretive risk had become part of the machinery. “No raw public release. Flag repeated fields as Class B candidate with explanatory context. Do not let anyone call it sky being copied.”

Sera breathed through her nose. “Thank you.”

Candle crossed deeper.

The first mass variance arrived without drama. A sealed tungsten reference block lost nine grams on Scale Two and gained nothing on Scale One. Scale Three reported no change. The radiation counter stayed flat. The thermal map stayed dull.

Tomas said, “No breach.”

“Mass loss contradiction,” Sera said. “Not mass loss. Scale contradiction unless verified.”

“Scale Two has independent housing.”

“Scale Two is the only one making a claim.”

“Scale One is also making a claim.”

“By silence?”

“By mass.”

“Both of you,” Nadine said.

They stopped.

Mara looked at the tissue tray panel. The biological cultures were only colors and numbers from here, regulated matter no person could mistake for a passenger, though people on Earth had tried. The tray showed early vascular rupture analogues in two samples, a spreading red lattice under microscope feed, then a duplicate still of the same lattice with the rupture reversed along one edge. The timestamp matched. The file sizes differed.

“Tray four is ugly,” Tomas said softly.

Sera turned. “Candle has no tray four.”

The deck went quiet enough for Mara to hear the velcro click at Malik’s cuff.

Tomas checked his panel again. “Sorry. Biological tray C. I said four because Asterion.” His face had gone pale in the screen light. “Biological tray C shows rupture artifact. Containment holds.”

Mara wrote the correction into her station log before anyone asked. T. Vale verbal slip: “tray four” corrected to Candle biological tray C. Potential Asterion stress contamination, no evidence of archive cross-source. She removed the word “fear.” She removed “slip” and put “mislabel.”

The public-pressure strip flashed. Malik swore once under his breath.

Nadine looked over. “Comms.”

“Makers’ Address formal demand. They’re calling for immediate prime burst from Candle high-tower antenna before Band Three. They say if the system is bounded, silence is negligence.”

“Source?”

“Adrian Yew’s institute, co-signed by six parliamentary minority blocs, two private orbital insurers, and the New Geneva children’s trust, which is obscene.”

“Do not editorialize on loop.”

“Logged as operational irrelevance,” Malik said, and his voice had gone hard. “They want the tower turned from narrowband telemetry to broadcast primes.”

Tomas looked up. “That would break the link.”

Sera said, “It would contaminate the experiment.”

“It would do nothing,” Tomas said. “It is a radio tantrum.”

Mara read the first sentence from the scraped petition because she had to know the shape of the knife. A closed system can still receive input. We ask humanity’s witness vessel to knock.

She had met Adrian Yew once by delay, his face immaculate in a private hearing, his grief wearing money like a sealed suit. Every neglected child knows this, he had said then, as if the cosmos were a locked parent and the rich had found the correct doorbell.

“Recommendation?” Nadine asked.

“Refuse,” Sera said.

“Refuse,” Tomas said.

Malik said, “Refuse and suppress demand from crew displays.”

Nadine looked at Mara.

“Refuse,” Mara said. “Archive the demand. It will matter for release context. Do not aim the antenna at their hunger.”

Nadine nodded once. “Candle remains telemetry only.”

Anika’s delayed voice arrived, unaware of the petition. “Witness, ground confirms public pressure for active signal payloads. Flight Dynamics recommends no non-mission emissions. Repeat, no non-mission emissions.”

“Received,” Nadine said. “Already refused.”

Candle’s archive index duplicated at 22:03:19.

At first it looked like a harmless software fault. Malik called it a mirror echo. Slot records had two entries, both valid, both carrying the same hash until line 812. Lullabies divided into lullabies and court transcripts. Weather reports picked up children’s drawings. A recipe from Lagos showed under Asterion family messages for four seconds, then moved back. The Dublin Family Court transcript that had thrown a line-ending warning during static test appeared twice, one copy stamped as never loaded.

“Archive drives are sealed,” Tomas said.

“Read-only?” Sera asked.

“Physically sealed. No write authority after release.”

“Then why is the index changing?”

“It isn’t. The received index is changing.”

“Say that in the log.”

Tomas did. His voice steadied when he entered the sentence. Hardware carried him where speech alone did not.

Mara opened the interpretive classification pane and watched fields populate faster than she liked: archive-index duplication, custody concern, human artifact payload, high misuse risk. The sealed archive had been chosen by lottery to represent ordinary life: lullabies, recipes, weather, jokes, last family messages. Earth had argued for months over whether a child’s drawing belonged beside a court transcript, whether jokes dishonored the dead, whether weather reports proved too little, whether last messages were cruelty in a craft built to die. Now the Boundary, or the instruments, or the failing model, or corruption with no doctrine inside it, had made the index contradict itself before the craft reached Band Three.

“Does it touch Asterion archive mirrors?” Mara asked.

Malik’s hands paused above the routing board. “No direct bridge. We isolated mission archives from Candle payload.”

“Does any label collide?”

He searched. “One collision. AST-FAM-MSG bucket maps temporarily to AST-MD118 prefix in the received duplicate. No content access. Index only.”

Mara’s throat tightened before the thought had words. Family messages wearing mission-day death labels.

Sera said, “No content?”

“Index only,” Malik repeated.

“Mark it,” Nadine said. “Continue.”

At 22:06, Candle’s forward radar returned a corridor.

Not Candle’s interior. Not Asterion’s service corridor, which would have been bad enough and possible enough to argue over. The geometry resolved in thin gray lines, handrail patches and bulkhead seams, a maintenance alcove with a locker tag half-obscured by cable shadow. Mara knew the patched rail before the label arrived.

Witness, Deck Two, corridor outside ship medical.

Yuna, on ground-medical delay, was not there. No person appeared. The radar drew the corridor empty, as if Candle had looked ahead into the Boundary and found the ship behind it. The angle was wrong. It saw the handrail from a height no person used, near the ceiling, where the sensor housings were tucked behind acoustic panels. A high-tower view, Mara thought, and disliked the phrase as soon as it formed. It sounded too ready for public use.

“Radar contamination,” Sera said. Her voice thinned but held. “Interior geometry return. Source match?”

Malik compared it against schematics. “Witness internal map. Current configuration. It has the temporary med alcove privacy screen.”

Tomas said, “That screen went up after Candle release.”

No one spoke for two breaths.

Nadine’s hand moved to the abort cover.

“Abort rule,” Tomas said.

Sera corrected him. “Band Four trigger if interior radar return includes live Witness crew compartments in real time.”

“Medical is a crew compartment.”

“It is empty.”

“It has current configuration.”

“Current does not equal live.”

Nadine opened the cover. The movement was small and final. “Sera, recommendation.”

Sera looked at the radar image as if she could make it less intimate by refusing to blink. “Classify as Evidence Failure. Mandatory abort if live compartment. We are below that threshold by wording. Above it by common sense.”

“Tomas.”

“Antenna geometry holding. Guidance still reports smooth approach. Independent tracking shows lateral displacement rising but inside abort margin for maybe twenty seconds. If we abort now, burn authority may take. If we wait for a cleaner trigger, we may lose the tower.”

“Mara.”

The privacy screen in the radar return had a wrinkle near its lower edge. Mara had seen it from the medical cot after Nadine removed her from Elias-associated authority. She had counted that wrinkle until sleep pretended to arrive. Now Candle returned it from ahead.

“Do not wait for a body to make it count,” she said.

Nadine’s thumb hovered over the covered switch. “That is a recommendation?”

“Abort if mechanically possible. Continue burst transmission during retreat. Interior return with current configuration is not a message. It is enough.”

Sera said, “Agreed.”

Tomas said, “Agreed.”

Nadine pressed abort.

Candle accepted the command in one system and ignored it in another.

The deck changed then. Not loudly. It changed in the clipped rhythm of people who had lost the comfort of single instruments. Malik routed the burn acknowledgement to the central display. Tomas called thruster response. Sera demanded raw timing. Nadine’s voice dropped. Candle’s guidance log showed a clean retreat burn, a shallow curve away from the transition zone, exactly what the abort model wanted. Independent tracking from Witness exterior radar showed Candle continuing inward, laterally displaced by twelve meters, then forty, then no longer inside the modeled cone.

“Guidance is lying,” Tomas said.

“Guidance believes itself,” Sera said.

“Same outcome.”

“Different evidence.”

Nadine said, “Burst mode.”

Malik armed it. “Candle burst archive transmission active. Public delay remains locked. Ground mirrors still restricted.”

“High tower?” Nadine asked.

Tomas’s fingers moved over the display. “Minimum transmission angle in twelve seconds. Maybe.”

“Maybe is not a value.”

“Nine to thirteen. Hinge jitter.”

The tower camera showed a white strut against black. At the hinge, the polymer fairing had crazed into a frost pattern though the thermal reading stayed warm. Tiny pits opened along the mast. No impact registered. The angle fell by half a degree, recovered, fell again.

Mara’s station flashed with Earth update overflow. Exit channels had found the phrase and were building liturgy at machine speed. Empty throne. Empty throne. Empty throne. A scraped clip showed a child’s drawing from Glass Room 12B, Candle with a stick figure inside, now edited so the figure was missing and a red door stood behind it. Continuity had suppressed the first stream. Copies used the suppression banner as proof.

Mara selected the phrase for release-risk watch and added: cult language tied to Candle couch vacancy; do not repeat phrase without clinical warning and anti-sacrifice context.

Her fingers stopped over “anti-sacrifice.” Too clean. Too easy to make into a banner. She changed it: unmanned status must be stated as operational safety and moral refusal; no implication that absence creates invitation.

“Mara,” Malik said.

She looked up.

“I have pressure from ground release desk asking if we classify the couch feed as public-safe after abort.”

“No.”

“It is empty.”

“That is the problem.”

He nodded and sent the refusal.

Candle’s optical feed split into three panels without command. Mars stayed visible in all of them. In the left panel, the red disk sat clean against black. In the center, the same disk appeared with repeated stars around it, three identical clusters arranged where the sky had no right to echo. In the right panel, Mars was partly occluded by a white line that no camera on Candle could focus on. The line cast no shadow. It arrived only when dust from Candle’s own pitted fairing drifted across it. The dust crossed, vanished, returned as a bright smear, then continued.

Sera said, “Do not name the line.”

Nobody had tried.

The antenna angle dropped below minimum for four seconds.

“Transmission holding through side lobe,” Malik said.

Tomas said, “That will not last.”

The final packet began before anyone called it final.

It came under the burst archive header with Candle’s current timestamp, then duplicated itself with Asterion mission day 118 metadata. Malik tried to quarantine the second header. The packet routed through the evidence-preservation queue, hit Sera’s contamination lock, bounced to Mara’s interpretive pane as a redacted preview, then threw audio into the command deck speakers for one and a half seconds before Malik killed the open channel.

A breath.

Then Jun’s voice, too close and laughing at the wrong edge of panic: “That’s not—”

Cut.

Leila, underneath him or beside him or in a different file: “Tray four shows vascular—”

Mateo: “No, don’t give them—”

Sofia: “Park, hands off.”

Elias, or the shape of Elias made by repair and hunger: “Everybody hands.”

Static snapped shut.

Mara’s fingers were against her wrist before she chose the motion. Pulse. Count. Convert the flood into numbers small enough to cross. The old drill rose, obedient and obscene.

“Audio spill contained,” Malik said. His face had gone gray. “I’m sorry. It crossed a bad route. One point five seconds open deck, no public path, no ground mirror.”

“Record chain breach,” Nadine said.

“Recording.”

Sera’s voice cut through his. “No one repeats content from memory. No one. We work from quarantined transcript after classification.”

Tomas said, “It was Asterion.”

“It was a Candle packet with Asterion overlays,” Sera said. “Say the evidence.”

“It had Sofia.”

“It had a source collision resembling Sofia.”

Mara swallowed against the taste of mandarin syrup in the air. “It had contradictory voices that do not align with known sequence. That is all we can say now.”

Sera glanced at her. Something moved across her face, costly and gone.

Nadine kept her hand near the abort cover though the command had already failed in the ways that mattered. “Malik, status.”

“Candle burst continuing. Antenna angle below minimum for seven seconds. Eight.”

Tomas whispered, “Come on.”

“Nine,” Malik said.

The tower hinge tore without sound. On the camera, it looked like a white bone bending too far, except bone belonged to animals and this was only hardware paying its part. The narrowband strength fell. Came back through a reflected path none of the models liked. Fell again.

Candle sent one more block.

No audio this time. Index data, mass scales, radar frames, optical repeats, clock arrays. Sera pulled it open in raw view. Duplicate timestamps flowered down the screen, same second, different content. One line said Candle retreated. One line said Candle advanced. One line placed Witness six meters inside a geometry that belonged to no coordinate system on the vessel. The mass scale reported the tungsten block restored to original weight, then absent, then never loaded. Archive slot 11,204 carried a weather report from Manila and a checksum matching a child’s drawing. Asterion prefix labels appeared as ghosts in the index and vanished under review.

The clock arrays disagreed by less than a blink and more than any margin could carry.

“Burst ended,” Malik said.

They waited for the next block because waiting was also procedure.

None came.

Candle’s marker remained on the display, still moving according to guidance, lost according to tracking, silent according to comms. Mars remained.

Nadine closed the abort cover. The click was too small for the room.

“Candle status,” she said.

Tomas stared at the empty signal window. “Unconfirmed. Guidance reports retreat burn complete. External tracking lost object lock. Telemetry terminated after tower failure.”

“Plain.”

He breathed once. “Candle is gone for mission purposes.”

Sera rubbed the old ring mark until the skin around it reddened. “Candle returned stronger contradictions. No doctrine. No stable surface. No reliable message. No occupied data. No proof of consciousness effect.”

“No answer,” Malik said, and the words came out young.

Sera turned to him. “No answer is too broad. We have data.”

He nodded too quickly. “Yes.”

Mara looked at the couch indicator still pinned in the corner of the Candle schematic. Empty. Green. Invalid for occupied approach. It should have felt like victory. Instead it looked like a door someone would draw around a wound if they were frightened enough.

Nadine followed her gaze. “Doctor Venn.”

Mara took her fingers from her wrist. “Do not show the couch alone.”

“No.”

“Do not let the final public image be absence.”

“No.”

Sera said, “The raw overlays will be Class C until reviewed.”

“Some will be Class B with context,” Mara said. Her voice sounded steadier than her hands were. “The contradiction is the finding. If we hide all of it, Earth will receive only rumor and the throne language.”

Malik flinched at the phrase.

Nadine looked at him. “Route that term into restricted harm lexicon. Do not repeat on open channels.”

“Done.”

Anika’s delayed voice arrived into the room, still living fifteen minutes behind them. “Witness, Flight Dynamics sees early antenna degradation in your downsample. Recommend preparing burst mode if geometry continues to decay.”

Nadine closed her eyes for one beat, opened them, and answered the past. “Flight, Candle telemetry terminated. Burst received. Abort command sent. Object lock lost. No crew casualty.”

No crew casualty.

The words moved through the command deck and found every person differently. Tomas put both hands flat on the edge of his console. Malik bent over his routing board as if a loose cable needed him. Sera saved three copies of the raw packet under contamination seal, then checked the seal again.

Mara opened the release composition shell.

She lacked authority over Elias-associated Class C raw material. She lacked the right to touch the personal address that had already broken her custody. She still had authority to recommend structure, context, and harm language for non-family general release, and Nadine had shipboard emergency power if ground delay turned into public harm. The final decision had not arrived clean. It had arrived like the data: duplicated, contested, damaging no matter which field she trusted.

Nadine saw the screen. “We are not writing yet.”

“We are preserving what the packet permits.”

“Doctor.”

“The first leak will say Candle found the empty throne. The second will say the Makers were silenced before contact. The third will sell Jun laughing without the words around it.” Mara kept her eyes on the blank fields. “If we wait for clean, we give them raw.”

Sera said, “We need review.”

“Yes.”

“Full chain.”

“Yes.”

“No Elias private handling from you.”

Mara’s throat moved. “Yes.”

Tomas turned from his dead craft. “What do you write first?”

Mara looked at the first field. Public summary, provisional. She typed with two fingers because the tremor worsened when she used both hands.

Candle was unmanned.

She stopped.

That sentence was too small to carry what people would try to hang on it, but it had to stand first. Before anomalies. Before voices. Before the failed antenna and repeated stars. Before any cult could make the empty couch into proof or accusation, the public record needed to state the refusal plainly.

She added the next line.

No human being was placed aboard Candle to produce testimony.

Sera watched over her shoulder. “Produce testimony?”

“Better?”

“Extract testimony,” Sera said. “It names the act.”

Mara changed it.

No human being was placed aboard Candle to extract testimony.

Nadine’s voice came from the rail. “Add: by command decision.”

Mara did.

Tomas’s mouth tightened. “And hardware status.”

She typed.

Occupied Boundary approach remained invalid by documented hardware hold and command seal.

“That will make them come for you,” Malik said to Tomas.

“They already have my name,” he said.

The next field waited. Evidence summary, provisional. Mara could feel Elias in the quarantined packet like heat behind a closed hatch, but she kept her hands on the living sentence. She wrote the words that would hurt because they refused to become more useful than they were.

Candle returned repeated star fields, contradictory mass-scale records, archive-index duplication, and a transient radar return matching current Witness interior geometry. These findings strengthen prior Boundary anomaly patterns. They do not establish an explanation for the Boundary.

Sera said, “Acceptable. Replace explanation with ontology.”

“No,” Mara said.

Sera looked at her.

“Most people will hear ontology as a locked door. Explanation is enough here. You can define later.”

Sera held the objection on her tongue, then let it go. “Then add: They do not establish artificiality, external contact, survival, or falsity of Mars.”

Mara typed slowly.

They do not establish artificiality, external contact, survival after death, or that Mars is false.

Mars glowed on the central display as if offended by nothing.

Nadine asked, “Can you write the audio line?”

Mara’s hand went to her wrist and stopped before touching skin. “Not alone.”

“Then don’t.”

The command deck held around that refusal. It had more mercy in it than comfort would have.

Malik said, “Ground release desk is asking for preliminary packet title.”

Nobody answered.

Outside, whatever remained of Candle offered no signal. The high-tower mast, if it still existed, had no angle left to speak from. The empty couch had crossed far enough to become dangerous without carrying a body. The Boundary had taken hardware, order, and confidence. It had returned evidence with no mercy in its formatting.

Mara looked at the title field. Her first impulse was technical: Candle Band Three Burst, Provisional Contaminated Summary. Accurate, bloodless, easy for Pavel to put behind a delay wall until rumor did the public work.

She deleted the field before saving.

Nadine came beside her, one hand still hooked to the rail, her gray temples bright in the screen light. “What are you doing?”

“Leaving room for the package.”

“For Pavel?”

“For the living.”

Sera lowered herself into the chair at the adjacent station. Malik routed the sealed packet copies into the review cage. Tomas, after a moment, closed the Candle systems pane so the couch indicator vanished from the command deck.

Mara placed her hands flat on the console until the tremor had to travel through bone and metal before anyone could see it. Then she opened a new release note under joint review and wrote the first line again, because some truths needed repetition before they could hold.

Candle was unmanned.
