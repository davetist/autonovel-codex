## Chapter 6: Mission Day 118

Asterion public feed, mission day 118, scheduled crew-health segment. Source: primary public archive. Delay stamp intact. Video stable until final ninety-one seconds.

Elias caught the electrolyte pouch with the heel of his hand and let the impact turn him half around. The public camera tracked late, as it always did when a body moved too softly for its prediction. For one second his knees were tucked under him and his left sock filled the feed, gray at the toe where the fabric had thinned.

“Good morning, schools with sensible teachers,” he said. “Good evening, schools with cruel teachers. Good whatever it is to my mother, who has already written to complain that I look underfed.”

Off camera, Jun made a sound through his teeth.

Elias hooked one foot under the restraint loop by the med locker. His tablet floated against his wrist tether, blinking six small names in descending order of compliance. 

Jun and Leila remained yellow.

Elias tapped the tablet.

“Pilot Park.”

“No.”

“That was fast.”

“I’m working.”

“You are avoiding a straw packet with minerals in it. This is not test-pilot material.”

Jun’s voice came from the forward station, thin on the cabin mic. “Put that on the mission poster.”

Mateo drifted into view upside down with a squeeze bulb of coffee clipped to his harness. “They already printed them. Heroic jaw, red planet, human destiny.”

“Human destiny is dehydrated,” Elias said. “Everyone gets a revelation after electrolyte checks.”

Amara’s laugh came from maintenance bay two, not warm yet but close. “Nobody gets a revelation until after electrolyte checks?”

“That is my medical opinion.”

“Put that on your grave.”

“Rude. Accurate. Hydrate.”

 No one needed the schoolchildren to see Sofia’s jaw set when comms scheduling slipped again. No one needed Earth to hear Mateo mutter the same sentence of a classroom segment into his sleeve until the words lost grammar.

But the health segment was allowed to show bodies. Bodies kept the mission from becoming only distance.

Elias pinched the straw seal open with his crooked finger. “Today’s science: salt is a civilization.”

Mateo, still upside down, said, “That one is too good for you.”

“It’s stolen from my sister.”

“Then credit Dr. Venn.”

“Never. She charges in footnotes.”

Sofia’s voice entered from command, low and clipped. “Doctor, public segment remains four minutes.”

“Copy. Four minutes to save humanity from fainting.”

“Less humanity. More crew.”

“Yes, Commander.”

He pushed off with two fingers. The move carried him past the table, where a tethered orange peel had escaped its mesh and turned in slow wet curls beside a meal pouch. 

Elias caught the peel before it crossed the camera field, then let it go. It drifted back toward the table, bright against white plastic.

“Leila,” he called.

“In the lab.”

“Your minerals miss you.”

“My minerals are in range.”

“Your tablet is yellow.”

“My tablet is dramatic.”

The public camera cut to the biology bay because the feed director on Earth liked Leila’s hands. Everyone liked Leila’s hands. They moved without waste, small and certain, wrapping elastic twice around sample vials before securing them into the cold tray. Her headscarf was tucked into the collar of her flight shirt, dark fabric held close with a plain clip. Behind her, the plant-growth chamber glowed with a light calibrated to make seedlings honest rather than pretty.

She held one vial to the label reader. “Asterion biological sequence nine, tray four. Pre-correction control secured. Temperature stable. Visual inspection unchanged.”

“Yellow tablet,” Elias said.

“I heard you.”

The camera stayed with her.  In the public comments Mateo sometimes read aloud to irritate her, viewers called her the mission’s soul, the scientist-saint, the proof that faith and the future had shaken hands above the old planet. She had deleted the thread summaries from her feed and told Elias that if anyone called her luminous again she would file a contamination report.

Now she secured a second vial and looked toward the camera lens, not at it. “Please do not cut this next sentence into a devotional clip.”

Mateo made a strangled noise.

Elias turned his body sideways, a trick to keep himself from laughing into the public mic. “That request may exceed mission bandwidth.”

Leila slid the tray into its rack. Lock. Check. Two fingers on the seal. “I have been asked whether I pray over the samples.”

Sofia said, from command, “Haddad.”

Leila’s mouth tightened, then released. “I do pray. I also label them, chill them, document chain of custody, and wash my hands. The prayer is not part of the protocol. It is mine.”

Silence crossed the cabin. The kind that came after a hatch closed.

Elias looked down at his tablet. Leila Haddad, hydration: yellow.

He made his voice lighter than the room. “And then she drinks the packet her physician sent her, because privacy does not protect kidneys.”

Amara said, “Doctor of ghosts saves the segment.”

“Doctor of ghosts is adding you to sodium counseling.”

“I cleared mine.”

“With hostility.”

“Still cleared.”

Leila clipped the sample drawer shut. The sound was small and final. “Send it here.”

Elias sent the pouch. It bumped once against Mateo’s shoulder; Mateo corrected its path with two fingers, too gentle to make a joke of it. Leila caught it without looking away from the tray.

[Private medical loop, same ship time plus twelve seconds. Source: recovered from damaged archive block. Video absent. Audio first pass checksum valid; secondary channel carries intermittent public-feed bleed.]

ELIAS: You okay?

HADDAD: No clinical question begins like that.

ELIAS: Mine do. You okay?

HADDAD: I should have let it pass.

ELIAS: You never let bad wording pass.

HADDAD: That was not bad wording. That was theft arranged in advance.

ELIAS: The question?

HADDAD: The editing.

[metal contact, likely sample rack]

ELIAS: They won’t run it raw.

HADDAD: That is exactly why I answered.

ELIAS: Fair.

HADDAD: Do not write me up as resistant.

ELIAS: I’m writing you up as hydrated if you drink that.

HADDAD: Elias.

ELIAS: I’m listening.

HADDAD: I am not the mission chaplain.

ELIAS: No.

HADDAD: If things become difficult, tell them before they come to me for comfort.

ELIAS: Before?

HADDAD: Before they make me refuse badly.

ELIAS: I can do that.

HADDAD: Can you?

[three beats. Public-feed bleed: Mateo saying “basalt is a record of cooling, not intention.”]

ELIAS: I can try early.

HADDAD: Good enough for a doctor.

[swallowing; pouch crinkle]

HADDAD: There. Kidneys saved.

ELIAS: Humanity stands down.

HADDAD: I said kidneys.

ELIAS: You said kidneys. I heard civilization.

[End private insert.]

The public feed returned to Mateo in mid-lesson. He had one hand hooked through a wall strap and the other around a packet of powdered basalt simulant that Asterion was carrying for comparison work. A teacher in Santiago had asked whether Mars rocks would feel lonely before people touched them. Mateo had spent two hours making the answer less cruel.

“Rocks do not wait,” he said, too brightly. “That is something people do to rocks. We say a mountain is patient because it has the courtesy to outlive us.”

“Cheerful,” Jun called.

Mateo flipped him a hand sign beneath the camera frame.

Elias checked the tablet again. Leila moved green. Jun stayed yellow.

The course-correction window sat on every panel as a thin amber bar: ATTITUDE TRIM PREP, COMM ADJUST IN 00:43:18. Planned low-communication interval. 

He pushed toward the forward station, packet in hand.

Public feed, alternate cabin camera. Image minor compression artifacts at edges. Audio clean.

Jun had wedged himself at the pilot station with one foot braced under a console lip, loose-limbed even when locked in place. His hands moved over projected numbers, not touching yet, rehearsing. He had once told Elias that good flying began before contact: hands learned the shape of a lie first.

A narrow strip of Mars sat in the forward window, still more bead than planet. Red with white at one pole, small enough to deny all the songs written for it. It had been growing for weeks by increments too small for romance and too large for sleep.

Elias held out the pouch.

Jun glanced at it. “If I say I drank one, would you respect me less for lying?”

“Yes.”

“Then I drank two.”

“Worse.”

Jun accepted the pouch with his teeth, bit the seal, and made a face. “This tastes like a battery went to medical school.”

“It’s orange.”

“No. Orange is a fruit. This is panel cleaner with ambition.”

“Drink.”

Jun drank. His eyes stayed on the overlay. The amber bar reflected in his cheek shield.

Elias muted his public mic with two taps. “You’ve been chewing your mouth.”

“Medical surveillance state.”

“Pilot chewing creates debris.”

“Tell Sofia. She’ll add it to stowage.”

“What are you watching?”

Jun swallowed, grimaced again, and gave the empty pouch back. “Nothing yet.”

“That is pilot for something.”

Jun looked past the overlay to the window. Mars stayed there with its polite red insistence.

“Star tracker C disagrees with A and B by point zero zero three,” he said.

“That sounds small.”

“It is small.”

“And?”

“And it keeps choosing the same wrong answer.”

Elias clipped the empty pouch to his belt. “Can you fly it?”

“If it’s lying, it’s lying consistently. I can fly consistent.”

Sofia’s command voice cut through the open loop. “Park.”

Jun’s posture changed before his face did. “I have it.”

“Report it.”

“Star tracker C has a repeat offset. A and B stable. No burn effect. No action requested.”

“Language.”

Jun’s jaw worked. “Navigational inconsistency. Repeatable. Within tolerance.”

Amara came on from maintenance, sharper now. “Repeatable wrong is not the same as within tolerance.”

“Thank you, engineer.”

“You are welcome, pilot who loves dying beautifully.”

“Children are watching.”

“Good. Children should learn tolerance is not a lullaby.”

Mateo’s mic picked up one soft laugh and then cut as he remembered the classroom feed. Elias left his own mic muted. He watched Jun’s right thumb move over the manual input pad without touching it, a small restless orbit.

Sofia said, “Park, keep C out of primary weighting until after trim. Okonkwo, verify mount thermal.”

“Already pulling it.”

“Doctor Venn.”

Elias straightened as if posture could be heard. “Commander.”

“Return to public audio.”

Jun said, quieter, “See? Hydration saved me.”

Elias unmuted before he answered. “Public health announcement: Pilot Park has bravely consumed one pouch and survived a flavor no nation will claim.”

Mateo turned in the cabin behind them, catching the thread. “Can rocks be lonely, Doctor?”

“Yes,” Elias said. “But only if they miss their electrolytes.”

Mateo put his forehead briefly against the strap by his shoulder, hiding his smile. The feed would catch the movement and make it tender. 

The cabin gave a tiny shudder as a pump cycled.

Nothing on the alarm board changed.

Amara said, off public mic but not far enough, “That sounded ugly.”

Sofia: “Specify.”

“Coolant pump three had a cough.”

“Machines do not cough.”

“Tell pump three.”

Elias watched the public audio levels. Amara’s line had entered the feed. 

He pushed back toward the central table. The orange peel had come free again. It drifted at chest height, turning its wet white inner surface toward the camera, then away. A loose strap near the table mount lifted with the air circulation and settled, lifted again. Elias had told Mateo twice to secure it. No. Not Mateo. He had told himself once and assigned it nowhere.

He caught the strap and fed the end through its clasp. The teeth missed.

“Who left this loose?” Sofia asked.

“I’m fixing it.”

“That was not the question.”

“It’s mine,” Mateo said.

“It is everyone’s once it floats,” Sofia said.

Mateo reached for it, but Elias waved him off. “I’ve got it.”

His fingers slipped on the webbing. The cabin was dry enough to split skin at the knuckles; hand cream turned every surface into an accusation. He pulled the strap tight, heard the clasp click, then tugged twice. Secure.

On the public feed it would vanish as an object. Before that, for two seconds, the strap hung in front of Mars.

[Private log: Elias Venn. Mission day 118. Timestamp recovered from auxiliary med archive. Header damaged; entry likely recorded thirty-one minutes before signal loss. Source classification disputed after Orpheus recovery due to duplicate timestamp found in unrelated systems log.]

I am recording early because after trim I will pretend I planned this. That is a medical technique called lying to your future self.

Crew status first, because Mara would haunt me through procedure if I began with feelings.  I am choosing not to ask until after the trim, because sometimes a ritual is only a handrail.

Jun is chewing his cheek. Hydration poor. Confidence high in the fake calm style pilots prefer because it gives them something to polish. Star tracker C is repeating a wrong offset. He says he can fly it if it lies consistently. I would like that line better if he had not meant it.

Amara heard coolant pump three miscycle before the board did. The board now agrees with her, which has made her less happy. She has a pencil behind her ear again. It floated away during the public segment and she caught it with her elbow. I will remember that if we get old enough to make fun of her properly.

Mateo is rehearsing one answer to children and one answer to himself. He thinks no one can tell the difference. He is wrong. He asked me yesterday whether we should stop saying Mars is waiting. I said rocks do not care about verbs. He said that was Leila’s answer and I should get my own.

Leila asked me to protect her prayer from being used as mission property. That is not the phrase she used. She said she was not the mission chaplain. I said no. I should have said more.

I am writing this because the cabin has become too aware of small sounds. Velcro separating. Pump bearings. Jun’s teeth. My own pen against the tablet. We are within expected parameters. 

Mara would ask for the evidence. Here: sleep down nine percent average across crew in the last three cycles. Irritability up, if anyone ever admits that count. Error reports up but clustered around trim prep. Food intake adequate except Jun. Sofia overcontrolled in public but not dangerously. Leila’s blood chemistry good. Amara’s pulse ran high during the pump check and dropped when she found a cause. Mateo watched the window for four minutes after his education segment and then apologized to the rock packet. I am choosing to call that stress.

There was also the star field.

No, that is too dramatic. There was a calibration image that looked familiar to Jun. He said, “We already passed that patch,” then corrected himself before anyone asked. Space has repeated patterns if you are tired. Humans find faces in toast and messages in fan noise. My sister built a career warning people that fear is a bad instrument and a useful alarm.

Mara, if you ever review this, do not underline that sentence.

[breath; soft impact, likely tablet against chest restraint]

I know you will underline it.

[End entry.]

Public feed, mission day 118, education wrap. Video stable. Audio contains six-second level drop during course-correction prep call.

Mateo had finally found the sentence.

“A rock records conditions,” he told the classrooms, voice lower now. “Pressure, heat, cooling rate. It does not record purpose. Purpose is what we bring when we decide how to treat the record.”

Sofia’s public smile did not move. Amara, behind a service panel, said something the transcript rendered as bearable, barrel, burial. The cleanup file chose barrel. Elias, listening live from the med station, heard burial and chose to say nothing.

Leila moved past the table with a secured cold tray hugged against her chest. The camera caught the elastic bands, doubled around each vial. She looked at the floating orange peel, then at Elias.

“Yours?”

“Public health prop.”

“It is leaking oil.”

“It is an orange peel.”

“It is biological residue near my tray.”

He caught it and pinched it between two fingers. The rind left a bright wetness on his glove. “Evidence secured.”

“Waste secured,” Amara said.

“Everyone is correcting me today.”

“Because you are wrong today,” Leila said.

It landed with a shape no one expected. Not cruel. Too exact for teasing.

Elias turned the peel in his fingers. “Only today?”

Leila’s face changed by almost nothing. “Drink water.”

Mateo looked from one to the other. His education voice faltered for half a breath, then resumed. “If you have a basalt sample in your classroom kit, you can see vesicles, the little holes left by gas—”

“Mateo,” Sofia said.

He stopped. “Commander?”

“Prep call.”

“Copy.”

The public-feed overlay appeared: CREW ACTIVITY SHIFTING TO TECHNICAL OPERATIONS. SIGNAL MAY ENTER LOW-BANDWIDTH MODE DURING SCHEDULED COURSE CORRECTION. THANK YOU FOR WATCHING WITH US.

Elias hated the line. With us. As if watching made a room aboard.

Jun began reading numbers aloud. “Tracker A stable. B stable. C excluded. IMU green. Burn clock in sync. Trim package loaded.”

Amara: “Coolant three within margin after reset. I still hate it.”

Sofia: “Hatred noted.”

Leila secured the tray in the biological locker. “Samples nine through twelve locked. Pre-correction controls complete.”

Mateo clipped the basalt pouch into its mesh. “Education payload secure.”

Elias checked the tablet. Six green names. His own hydration blinked yellow.

Amara saw it from across the cabin. “Doctor.”

“Busy.”

“You are yellow.”

“Physicians are exempt.”

“No one is exempt from your annoying religion.”

“It’s medicine.”

“Same robes.”

He took a pouch from the med locker, tore it open, and drank. Warm citrus salt flooded his tongue. The taste was too close to the peel in his hand, too close to childhood fever cups and Mara pressing a glass into his palm after their mother had gone silent at the kitchen table. He swallowed half, clipped the rest to his belt.

Jun said, “That’s new.”

Every conversation stopped.

Sofia turned toward the pilot station. “Report.”

Jun bent close to the window overlay. “Optical background has a smear. Could be attitude shift.”

“We have not shifted.”

“Then not that.”

Amara’s panel chimed once. “Star tracker C just agreed with A.”

“Good,” Mateo said.

Jun shook his head once. “No. It agreed by changing the stars.”

Elias felt the pouch tap his hip, a soft repeated knock. Public feed delay kept running. Earth was still watching the overlay. Maybe classrooms still had children asking whether the basalt kit holes were bubbles or little caves. Maybe Ruth Venn had turned up the volume for the health segment and was now watching a polite notice instead of her son’s face.

Sofia said, “Put it on internal.”

Jun’s fingers moved. The forward display mirrored to the cabin panel. A star field appeared: black, pinpricked, ordinary enough for the first look to fail. Then Jun overlaid the prior calibration frame.

Three stars sat where three stars had already been.

Mateo said, too softly for public audio if the levels behaved, “We passed that.”

“No,” Sera would say years later aboard another ship, or perhaps she had not yet earned that correction in anyone’s mouth. No, reach is failing is not Mars is false. No, repetition is not doctrine. No, no, no. Elias had no Sera here. He had Jun’s thumb over a control, Sofia’s voice becoming steel, Leila’s tray locked and cold, Amara already pulling raw data, Mateo with basalt dust on his sleeve.

Elias said, “Jun.”

“I know.”

“What are your hands doing?”

Jun looked down as if someone else had placed them there. He lifted both palms from the panel. “Behaving.”

“Keep them that way.”

A half-second of laughter came out of him. Wrong size. Not joy. Air under pressure finding a seam.

Elias moved closer. “Can you feel your fingertips?”

Jun flexed both hands. “Yes.”

“Any tingling?”

“No.”

“Any nausea?”

“Only from your orange battery.”

“Good. Stay insulted. It’s diagnostic.”

Sofia: “Okonkwo.”

“Pulling all optical buffers. No thermal cause. No mount cause yet. Give me ten seconds and a universe that wants to be caught.”

“Use one you have.”

Amara swore softly.

Leila’s voice entered from the biology bay. “Do you need me clear of the locker?”

“Stay where you are,” Sofia said.

“I am clear of the hatch.”

“Stay.”

Elias took the last swallow from his pouch because Amara was watching. The citrus clung at the back of his throat. He pressed two fingers against the med-kit latch and tapped twice before he caught himself.

Public feed switched back from the technical overlay to cabin camera for reasons no one aboard had commanded.

Later, analysts would argue over that cut. Earth director error, auto-curation rule, compression artifact, early feed contamination. In the room, it was only the public lens waking at the wrong time.

It found the table first.

The orange peel had slipped from Elias’s glove and floated free again, curling in the center of the frame. Its outer skin shone under cabin light. Behind it, the loose strap Elias had fixed lifted from its clasp, not fully free, just enough to arc away from the table mount. Past both, through the forward window, Mars burned small and red above Jun’s shoulder.

Elias was outside the frame.

Jun’s head was turned toward the display, mouth slightly open around the start of a word. Leila’s hand entered from the right, reaching for the peel or the strap or nothing. Mateo’s basalt pouch bumped the table edge. Sofia’s reflection held in the dark panel beside Mars, jaw set. The cabin looked arranged. Almost ceremonial. It had taken less than a second for work to become image.

Elias laughed off-screen because Amara, somewhere below the camera line, had said, “If that peel becomes historic, I resign.”

He laughed before he could stop it. A real laugh. Too much breath, one rough edge at the end.

Then Jun said, “That’s not—”

Audio clipped.

The public feed held the frame for less than the length of a blink before low-bandwidth mode swallowed motion. In the archive copy, Mars stayed clean. The orange peel did not turn. The strap remained half lifted, refusing both failure and repair.

On the private medical channel, under static, Elias’s voice came back once, closer to the mic than before.

“Jun, say the word.”

No verified answer survived.
