### Chapter 21: Mateo’s Lesson

AST-PUB-EDU-MD115-RIOS-BASALT-SEGMENT  
Public education feed, delay package for Red Nurseries.  
Video mostly intact. Audio repair confidence 0.94 until 00:08:31.  
Set: Asterion public lesson wall, removable Mars map, sample tray B.  
Analyst caution: child-question packet appended from Earth uplink queue. Timing may be editorial, not live exchange.

Mateo clipped the basalt pouch to the lesson rail and missed the loop on the first try.

“Good,” he said to the camera. “We will keep that. If you are watching from a classroom with the nice chairs, you should know astronauts also miss the loop.”

The pouch floated, small and gray, the old Velcro rasp loud against the microphone when he caught it. Behind him the lesson wall held its approved Mars map: Tharsis shaded in copper, Valles Marineris cut long and dark, the planned Asterion landing ellipse marked with a white ring. Someone in public engagement had added tiny greenhouse icons along the lower margin. Mateo had asked them to remove the helmets with smiling faces. They had removed two. One remained near the south polar inset, a round child’s head behind a bubble of glass.

He turned the pouch so the children could see the label.

ATACAMA COMPARATIVE SAMPLE / RIOS PERSONAL / MASS 41.3 G / NON-STERILE / EDUCATIONAL ONLY

“This one is not from Mars,” he said. “This is from Earth. North of where I grew up, near the observatories, from a place dry enough that every shadow looks expensive. It is a basaltic andesite, if your teacher wants the exact fight. For today, we are going to call it a cousin.”

He pinched the drawstring open. The stone slid into his palm with a soft, disappointing sound. It had no red glow, no dramatic fracture. Dark gray, one pale seam, a chipped edge where his father’s hammer had struck too hard because the desert wind had been picking up and the truck battery had been weak.

His father had wrapped it in newspaper from Antofagasta and said, Take one thing that does not care about cameras.

Mateo held the stone between thumb and forefinger.

“Basalt begins as melt. Rock that was once hot enough to forget its shape. Then it cools and becomes very patient. On Earth we find it in old flows, ocean floors, places where the ground opened and then closed its mouth. On Mars, basalt covers huge parts of the surface. That is one reason Mars looks the way it does. Not just red dust. Under the dust, old fire.”

The teleprompter gave him: THIS CONNECTS EARTH AND MARS THROUGH SHARED PLANETARY HISTORY.

He read the line in his head and let it pass.

He had argued with Public Learning about shared. Earth had rain that broke stone down. Earth had lichen. Earth had mining trucks and men who came home with dust in the creases of their hands. Mars had basalt under a sky no child had breathed. Shared was true if you used a long enough instrument. It was false if you meant dinner.

“Your classroom might have a Mars wall,” he said. “If it does, look at the dark places. Ask whether they are high or low. Ask what cooled fast and what cooled slowly. A rock is not a picture. It is an event that stopped moving.”

Off camera, Jun made a small approving noise.

Mateo kept his eyes on the lens. The red tally light sat above it like a single patient insect. He knew the feed would go to schools in Santiago, Lagos, Guangzhou, Dublin, Quito, Houston, Manila, the Red Nurseries built in places where children had never seen a sky without floodlights. Some of those rooms had low ceilings, plastic habitats painted on plaster, shelves with rover toys, a corner for quiet children who cried during launch footage. He had toured twelve before quarantine. A boy in São Paulo had pressed both hands to the glass between visitors and students and asked if Mars dirt would make his shoes dirty or famous.

“Now,” Mateo said, “I am going to do the part teachers like. I will hold up two images. One is a basalt sample from Hawai‘i. The other is a rover image from Mars. You will see vesicles. That means little holes left by gas. Like bread, if bread were made by a planet having a fever.”

A message flashed at the bottom of the crew-side display.

PUBLIC ED: avoid disease metaphor in early primary cut.

Mateo smiled at the camera with his lips closed. “Or like bubbles in a cooling pancake, if your school makes pancakes that can survive vacuum.”

Elias laughed somewhere to the left. “Medically improved.”

“Thank you, Doctor. Nobody ask him about planetary bread.”

“I heard my title.”

The lesson wall advanced. Two images appeared, side by side. The Earth basalt was blacker, sharper in classroom contrast. The Mars image carried rover dust and distance, the color corrected twice before Public Learning would let children see it. Mateo had asked for raw color, then for a caption naming correction. He had lost both fights and won a smaller one: the caption now said representative image, not true view.

“See this texture? This tells us cooling history. It also tells us that planets keep records without caring who reads them. Basalt does not write for humans. It does not prepare a speech. It sits. We arrive late and try to learn manners.”

The child helmet icon looked at him from the map.

He had been chosen partly because he could do this without sounding like an advertisement. That was what Mara Venn’s selection note had said in the version his father had never seen. High public trust. Strong symbolic mission value. Excellent cross-age explanation under affective load. There was a phrase like that in every file where an adult had decided children were a resource with desks.

“Question packet,” Sofia said from off camera.

Mateo glanced toward her. “Now?”

“Public Learning requested natural insertion before sample close.”

Jun muttered, “Natural insertion. Beautiful.”

Sofia said, “Park.”

Mateo reached for the tablet anchored near the lesson rail. The uplink queue opened to a screen of preselected classroom questions. Children’s names had been replaced by initials and region tags. Some had tiny audio icons. The first question was approved for Grade 3-5.

He tapped it.

A child’s voice filled the set, high and careful, with classroom echo behind it.

“Hello Asterion. My name is A. and our room is Red Nursery Fourteen. We have basalt on the table. Ms. Iyer says it is Earth basalt but Mars has basalt too. When you get there can you tell Mars we said hello? And can Payload Specialist Ríos say Mars is waiting for us?”

The recording held one extra second after the question. A scrape of chair leg. Another child whispering, Say it right.

Mateo’s hand stayed over the tablet.

The teleprompter changed.

MARS IS WAITING FOR ALL OF US, AND WE ARE COMING CAREFULLY.

He saw the sentence in white. He saw the children watching it through school policy and wall paint. In one Red Nursery, a teacher would pause after the phrase and ask them to repeat the vocabulary word. Waiting. Basalt. Vessel. In another, a child would put a red circle around the landing zone. In another, someone’s mother would have taken a night shift to stand behind a glass partition and see her daughter wave at a screen.

Asterion made a small adjustment burn somewhere in the body of the ship. The lesson set trembled through the rail. The Atacama stone drifted a millimeter in his palm and touched the base of his thumb.

“Mateo,” Sofia said, still off camera. The warning was clean enough for later editing.

He looked at the red tally light.

“Mars is...” he began.

His mouth dried. Not from awe. From the heat of a schoolroom in Calama when he was eight, a map of Mars curling at the edges because the window seals had failed and the dust got in. The teacher had told them the future was waiting. His father had come late from a survey job and stood in the back with his helmet under one arm, face lined by sun and copper dust, listening to a woman paid by the ministry explain that the sky belonged to all children.

Waiting.

Stone waited only if you made waiting mean existing without asking.

“Tell me the verb you want,” Mateo said.

The set went quiet.

The tablet strap clicked softly against its anchor.

He heard Sofia shift. Elias stopped moving. Jun made no rescue joke.

Mateo held the stone closer to the camera because his hands needed a task.

“I am sorry,” he said, and this apology, he knew at once, could be cut into several useful shapes. “That was for the grown-ups in the room.”

The teleprompter froze on the approved line.

He put the stone into the sample clamp. The clamp was ugly, a little too large for the rock. Amara had printed it after the first one cracked under rehearsal pressure, and she had written THIS ONE DOES NOT CARE ABOUT YOUR CLOSE-UP along the underside where Public Learning would never see it.

“To the student in Red Nursery Fourteen,” Mateo said, “thank you for the question. When we reach Mars, we will greet it by doing careful work. We will measure. We will look at rocks before stepping on them. We will try to leave fewer marks than we want to. Mars is there. That is already enough for one sentence.”

A breath came through the set audio. His own, maybe. Or Elias’s. Records later gave it no source.

“Mars is there,” he said again. “We are going carefully.”

Sofia let the silence run two seconds, then three.

“Segment hold,” she said.

The red tally light went dark.

Mateo unlatched the basalt clamp too fast. The stone spun out, struck the soft wall, and rebounded toward Jun, who caught it with a pilot’s lazy hand.

“Nice pitch,” Jun said.

“Give it back.”

Jun tossed it underhand. “Teacher voice got teeth.”

“Park,” Sofia said.

“No, Commander,” Mateo said.

Sofia turned toward him. In the lesson set her gray hair caught the flat panel light. She had the look she used before a correction became permanent record. “We will rerecord the answer.”

“Which answer?”

“The last minute.”

“The child asked me to say a sentence.”

“Public Learning supplied phrasing.”

“That is not an answer.”

“It is a mission education segment.”

“It is a child.”

Elias came half into frame, then stopped as if he had met an invisible hatch. “Everybody drink water before this becomes history.”

Amara’s voice arrived from the engineering loop, open by accident or because she had been listening. “Too late.”

Sofia looked at the active channel indicator. “Okonkwo, close your loop.”

“It is closed enough.”

“Now.”

The indicator blinked out.

Mateo turned the Atacama stone in his hand. The pale seam looked like a river that had failed to become one. “You want me to rerecord?”

“Yes.”

“Say the exact verb.”

Sofia’s jaw set. “Waiting.”

“For.”

“Mateo.”

“For whom? For flags? For children? For the investment ministries? For the classrooms with our faces over the pencil bins?”

Elias said, lower, “Mat.”

Mateo hated that. The softening. The little hand on the valve before pressure had a chance to show itself.

Sofia unclipped the slate from the lesson rail. “We have eighteen minutes before the next comm window. The children do not need our internal discomfort.”

“They have wall-size Mars maps funded by launch partners. They already have our internal discomfort. It has dunes painted on it.”

Jun’s hand moved toward the camera power, then away. He was still smiling, but the smile had slipped into something less useful.

Sofia said, “You are crew. You will make the public record survivable.”

“Survivable for whom?”

This time Elias did step closer. His socks caught on a Velcro patch and pulled loose with a small ripping sound. “Water,” he said. “Then you two can keep inventing new ways to make Public Learning sweat.”

Mateo took the squeeze bulb because refusing would make Elias right in the wrong direction. The water was warm. He swallowed too fast and coughed once.

Sofia watched him cough. The commander in her held; the person behind it moved by less than a breath. “We can record a revised answer without the approved phrase.”

Mateo wiped his mouth with the back of his wrist. “Now we negotiate grammar.”

“Yes.”

Jun said, “That may be the most Asterion thing anyone has ever said.”

Elias put two fingers against Jun’s shoulder and pushed him toward the hatch. “Go bother a panel.”

“I am crew.”

“Then crew elsewhere.”

Jun lifted both hands and drifted out, taking his unspent joke with him.

Sofia lowered her voice. “Public trust matters.”

“I know.”

“Children are part of the public.”

“I know.”

“You were selected to speak to them.”

There. The door under the sentence. Mateo felt it open in his chest, old desert air coming through, hot and grainy. He saw Quito, the suspended Mars model above the six chairs, camera drones sliding through the hall like obedient insects. He saw himself in a suit that never sat right at the shoulders, smiling while children from Red Nurseries sang a song about red soil in four languages. His father had been in the third row, retired hands folded around the program, face harder than the polished basalt under the flags.

“You mean I was selected so they would speak through me,” Mateo said.

Sofia did not answer at once. That was the surprise. Command had a reply for schedule variance, for insubordination, for grief that appeared in a procedure. Here she only held the slate against her thigh.

Elias said, “Mat, that is a different recording.”

“Is it?”

“Yes,” Elias said. “And maybe make it where only fewer people can ruin your life.”

The public lesson file preserved none of the next seven minutes. The edited feed sent to Red Nurseries showed Mateo holding the Atacama stone beside the Mars image.

The released version said:

“Mars is there. We are going carefully. When you study basalt, you are learning how to greet a place before you touch it.”

Children in at least sixty-two classrooms repeated greet.

The unreleased crew camera held Sofia after the cut, still by the lesson rail, the map of Mars behind her with the last helmet icon smiling over her shoulder.

Mateo had meant to wait until sleep cycle.

Instead he locked himself into payload storage, clipped one knee under the netting, and set the recorder against a box of sealed spectrometry flags. The space smelled of plastic warmed by equipment and the faint mineral dust that came off training samples no matter how many times logistics vacuumed them. Every object had a label. Every label promised a purpose.

The recorder asked for mood state.

He laughed once.

“Unsuitable,” he said.

The recorder waited.

“Fine. Agitated. Angry. Pick the clinical word that makes everyone less embarrassed.”

He pressed the Atacama stone into the elastic at his thigh. His father had told him never to keep it loose in cabin, and his father was usually right about objects that could become projectiles.

“Private log, Mateo Ríos. Mission day one-one-five. This is not for Public Learning. If Public Learning receives it, please picture me saying something obscene about their greenhouse mascots.”

He stopped. Too easy.

The little red recording mark held steady.

“They asked me to say Mars is waiting. A child asked it, yes. I know that. I hear her voice. I hear the room behind her. Someone moved a chair. Another child told her to say it right. That part is what I cannot wash off.”

His mouth filled again with the taste of warm water from the squeeze bulb. He rubbed his thumb over the stone’s chipped edge until it bit.

“They taught them the sentence first. That is the part. A child does not invent that verb alone. Waiting. Like Mars has a calendar. Like it has been patient with us. Like if we fail to go, we have disappointed a planet instead of disappointing each other.”

The recorder displayed a mild stress prompt.

CONSIDER PAUSE / BREATH COUNT.

“Do not prompt me,” he said.

It kept the prompt up for three seconds, then cleared.

He leaned his head back against the storage wall. The padding gave under him with a soft complaint. In the public set he had kept his teacher voice almost intact. Here his voice had a rough edge, Chilean vowels heavier, English no longer arranged for children.

“They put Mars in classrooms so no one could cancel us.”

The words came out plain. There was no clean satisfaction in hearing them.

“They painted habitats behind reading circles. They put our crew portraits over homework bins. They mailed basalt kits with sponsor logos. They asked children to record launch prayers and science questions and songs about first footprints. Then every budget hearing had a room full of eight-year-olds in bubble helmets behind the witness table. Every legislator who wanted to cut mass had to explain it under a red paper planet with glitter glue on it.”

He pulled at the elastic, freed the stone, trapped it in his fist again.

“I am not saying children should be kept away from wonder. There. Put that in the margin if you need to save me. I became a geologist because a woman came to my school with a rock that had once been under ocean. I loved her for it. I still remember her cracked nail polish. Blue, I think. She let me hold the sample before the mayor’s nephew, which was possibly the first political act of my life.”

A dropout removed one second. The transcript engine supplied [breath].

“But this was different. We used them. I used them. I let a little girl in Manila ask me if Mars dust would stick to her lunchbox and I answered like we were already there. I said dust is clingy. I said she would need a brush. She laughed into both hands. Her teacher cried behind the camera and I pretended not to see because the room needed the laugh more than the truth.”

He pressed the recorder harder into the box netting because it had begun to drift.

“My father saw it before I did. Of course he did. Surveyor. He sees stakes in the ground even when people call them decorations.”

He shifted into Spanish, then stopped and returned to English.

“He said, Mateo, they are buying witnesses before the accident. I told him he was bitter. I said every mission needs public trust. I said he did not understand international funding architecture. I said architecture. To my father. A man who stood in mine shafts and knew exactly which beams were lies.”

His throat worked.

“Good line, Papá. If you ever hear this, you were right and I was educated.”

The storage bay fan changed pitch. Mateo listened until it settled. He had grown up with machines that told the truth before people did: pump bearings, truck belts, old survey tablets whose batteries swelled in heat. A new sound meant stop talking and find the load. Asterion had too many sounds. It carried air with the manners of a building, water with the patience of a clinic, power through walls that pretended to be smooth.

He looked at the spectrometry flags beside the recorder. Each would mark a sample site if they landed. Tiny national bands, mission bands, sponsor bands, field-safe adhesive. The flags were absurd in his hands during training. On Mars they would be smaller than pebbles from orbit. On camera they would prove arrival.

“They want me to make Mars bearable for children,” he said. “That is the job under the job. Basalt, volcanoes, water history, all true. Then the soft sentence around it. Mars is waiting. Mars is for all of us. Mars makes us better. Mars means the story keeps going outward.”

His laugh returned, dry and short.

“You know what basalt means? It means melt cooled. It means pressure found a path and then lost heat. It means the planet does not owe a school district a future tense.”

The recorder marked pulse elevation from voice.

Mateo turned the stone over and over. The pale seam came back under his thumb each time, a small orbit. He had brought it as a private anchor and a public prop, which was how every compromise in this mission seemed to work. The same object could hold his father’s hand and a ministry caption. The same lesson could feed a child and feed a bond market. The same smile could calm a classroom and make cancellation impossible.

He said, “I am crew. I believe in the mission. That is the worst part. I want Mars. I want the rocks. I want to stand in a place where no one has lied yet by touching it, although that is probably another lie. I want the first sample bag to have dust in the seal. I want Amara to complain that the hinge is badly designed. I want Leila to find nothing alive and still treat the nothing as worthy of clean method. I want Jun to say the landing was ugly. I want Sofia to pretend she never doubted. I want Elias to ask if I can feel my hands.”

A long breath.

“I want children to know there is more ground than the ground under the classroom.”

The storage bay clicked as the thermal cycle changed. Mateo folded his fingers around the stone until the chipped edge pressed a crescent into his skin.

“But adults owe children more than beautiful unreachable futures.”

He sat with that until the recorder threw another prompt, gentler this time.

CONTINUE / SAVE / DISCARD

“Continue.”

The prompt cleared.

“If something happens to us, the children become witnesses. That is the phrase nobody says. Not audience. Witnesses. Witness is a heavy job. We gave it to them with crayons.”

He let the stone go. It floated between his hand and the recorder. For a moment it looked like the small dead center of a lesson no one had approved.

“My selection file says symbolic value. I know because I saw the phrase reflected in a glass table when I was signing the last consent forms. I did not report it. I liked being useful. I liked being the person who could make a mining town and a nursery class and a minister sit in the same sentence without throwing anything. I liked that the old crew called me professor when they wanted a joke and specialist when the cameras were on. I liked the suit. I hated the suit. Both are true. See, Doctor Venn? I can hold contradiction. Please give me a sticker.”

His voice cracked on the joke and he hated that more than the joke.

He caught the stone and shoved it back into the elastic at his thigh.

“I am going to rerecord the basalt lesson. I will say less. That may be the only honest improvement available.”

He reached to stop the recorder, then pulled his hand back.

“One more thing. If this log ever goes to the families, tell my father I heard him. Tell him the sky people knew what they were doing with the children. Tell him I knew too. Late is not innocence.”

He ended the file.

The log saved after four seconds. A duplicate timestamp appeared and cleared. Mateo stared at the screen until it asked whether he wanted to attach a clinical flag.

He selected no flag.

The recorder asked again.

He selected no flag, harder, as if pressure changed software.

Mateo wore the same shirt for the rerecording, because continuity staff preferred children not notice seams. The lesson wall had been reset. The greenhouse icon remained. Someone had cleaned a smudge from the Mars map and left a streak over the landing ellipse.

This time Amara stayed in the set, officially to monitor the sample clamp. Unofficially, she had wedged herself behind the camera with a strip of repair tape stuck to one knuckle and the expression of a woman ready to break any device that asked for the wrong kind of hope.

Elias sat cross-legged near the hatch with a water bulb hooked to his belt. Sofia stood out of sight but close enough that command had weight. Leila passed once in the corridor, slowed at the edge of the set, then continued without offering a blessing or a warning.

Jun’s voice came over a local loop. “Public Learning says please avoid pancake vacuum.”

“Public Learning can send a better metaphor,” Amara said.

“Careful. They will.”

The red tally light came on.

Mateo lifted the Atacama stone.

“Hello to the Red Nurseries,” he said. “And to classrooms that do not use that name, and to anyone watching from a room where Mars is on the wall.”

He waited half a beat. Not long enough for Public Learning to cut cleanly.

“This is basalt from Earth. It is old, but not special because I own it. I brought it from the Atacama because I wanted a stone near me that knew dry air before I did.”

Amara’s tape crackled as she folded her arms.

“Basalt tells us about heat. A planet cools, and the cooling leaves a record. On Mars, basalt can help us read volcanoes, old surfaces, maybe paths where water changed minerals before any of us were born. On Earth, basalt can be a road cut, a lesson sample, a thing your teacher lets you touch if you promise not to throw it.”

He tilted the stone. The pale seam caught the set light.

“I was asked yesterday whether Mars is waiting for us. I gave a messy answer. This is the cleaner one.”

Off camera, Sofia made no sound.

“Mars is not waiting. Waiting is something people do. Your parent waits at a clinic. Your teacher waits for quiet. A pilot waits for a burn window. A child waits for an answer from adults who should be careful.”

The set air seemed to still, though the fan kept running.

“Mars is there,” Mateo said. “That matters. It does not have to wait to matter.”

The teleprompter had gone blank. Someone had killed it. Amara, probably. Or Sofia. He would never ask.

“Basalt does not care what flag you plant in it. That was always the lesson no one wanted.”

He moved the stone from one hand to the other, slow enough that the camera could keep focus.

“People still care. We care who gets to go, who pays, who is asked to watch, whose classroom becomes proof that the mission must continue. We care because flags are not for rocks. They are for us. That makes them dangerous and sometimes useful. Both.”

Elias’s water bulb clicked softly against the hatch.

Mateo looked straight into the lens. The red tally light held him better than glass.

“If you have basalt on your table, touch it if your teacher says you may. Ask where it came from. Ask who picked it up, who packed it, who paid for it, who wanted you to hold it while we flew. Those are science questions too.”

He set the Atacama stone in the clamp. This time the loop caught on the first try.

“When Asterion reaches Mars, we will work. If we do not reach Mars, the rocks will still be rocks. Your breakfast will still count. Your homework will still count. The person beside you will still count. Do not let any adult make a planet so beautiful that it steals the room you are in.”

Sofia stepped into the edge of the frame.

Mateo stopped. That part would be cut. He had known it as he said it.

The commander looked at the camera, then at him. Her mouth tightened, not in anger. In calculation, perhaps. In something like grief forced through a narrow hatch.

“Continue,” she said.

Mateo blinked once.

Elias lowered his head. Amara looked at the floor.

The red tally light stayed on.

Mateo picked up the second sample image with hands that had begun to tremble and pinned it beside the rover photograph.

“Now,” he said, voice thinner but usable, “let us look at vesicles.”
