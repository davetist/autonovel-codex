## Chapter 20: Sealed Orders

The command deck was running a harmless loop when the dead entered it.

The loop belonged to the comms watchdog, a two-second housekeeping file that played under the narrowband carrier every six minutes so Malik could confirm the channel had not frozen clean. Ship-made. Bland. Checked too often to be interesting: a soft tone, then the neutral cabin voice saying, “Carrier live.”

At 11:08 ship time, the tone bent.

Nadine heard it first as a flaw in the deck’s ordinary breath. Witness had its own small noises, fans behind panels, velcro lifting from a sleeve, someone swallowing too close to a pickup. Commanders learned the ship’s throat. This was not Witness.

The cabin voice began, “Car—”

Static covered the second syllable. Under the static, a human inhaled.

Malik’s hand stopped above the routing board. Sera looked up from the clock-array spread. Tomas, seated at the aft systems station with ink on two fingers, said nothing at all.

The loop completed.

“Mark source,” Nadine said.

Malik’s fingers moved. “Watchdog file COM-HK-2. Local cache.”

“Archive access?”

“None scheduled.”

“Say actual.”

He leaned closer to the board. “No authorized archive access in the last eleven minutes. Quarantine fork starting.”

The loop came again too early. Tone. Broken cabin voice. Breath.

It was small. That made it worse. A scrape of static. One clipped intake, close to a mic, followed by the wet catch of a person trying to stay quiet.

Sera said, “Stop playback.”

Malik killed the carrier monitor. The deck stayed silent except for fans.

Nadine kept one hand hooked near the rail beneath the command display. The habit had begun after lunar rescue. It had become a gauge: if she needed both hands, the room was already lying about calm.

“Report.”

“The watchdog loop checksum passed when loaded. It fails now. Local copy shows two extra waveform features.”

“Classification.”

“Audio bleed until Sera says worse.”

Sera was already pulling the trace. Her compression sleeves were thumbed over her wrists, dark against cold hands. “Do not call it bleed as if we have identified direction. It is anomalous contamination in a local housekeeping file.”

Malik nodded once. “Anomalous contamination.”

“Match?” Nadine asked.

“I can run restricted comparison. I need authorization.”

“No.”

Sera turned toward her.

Nadine met the look. “No restricted Asterion comparison from the command deck for one breath in a dirty housekeeping loop. Quarantine the file. Preserve the raw carrier log. Hash the cache. No replay outside evidence review.”

Malik’s shoulders dropped a fraction, then locked again. “Copy.”

Tomas said, very quietly, “Band One is early.”

Sera corrected him without heat. “We are within modeled outer degradation by optical confidence, not material bands.”

“Audio in a local loop.”

“Information degradation is allowed in Band One. Allowed does not mean understood.”

Nadine heard the cost under the correction. Sera’s precision had begun to sound like someone tightening bolts by feel in the dark.

On the forward display, Mars sat beyond the modeled zone, red and measurable. A navigational overlay held it in pale geometry. The planet looked clean in optical, which meant nothing useful and too much to the part of the mind that wanted pictures to be honest.

“Medical,” Nadine said.

Yuna’s ground-linked voice entered the command channel after delay compensation, clipped at the edge. “Medical monitor active. Crew pulse variance rising but within—”

“No threshold language. I want names.”

A pause.

“Malik elevated. Tomas elevated. Sera stable by pulse, not by blink rate. Commander, you are stable.”

Nadine almost smiled. “Doctor Venn.”

“In ship medical. Awake. Under observation. She heard the contamination through the secondary channel before Malik cut it.”

Of course she had.

At the bottom left of Nadine’s command slate, the sealed-order indicator sat inside its gray lock. It had been there since launch. It never blinked. It never asked. It waited like a sealed hatch with pressure behind it.

The deck timer rolled into the next window.

CANDLE PREFLIGHT ROUTING: 00:43:12 TO COMMIT.

Too soon.

“Malik,” Nadine said, “private command packet to my slate. No ground mirror.”

He looked at her once. “Yes, Commander.”

Sera’s gaze sharpened. “That packet touches sealed authority?”

“It touches command risk.”

“That is not an answer.”

“It is the answer I am giving on the deck.”

The deck accepted rank before it accepted truth. Nadine disliked that more on days when it helped her.

The gray lock opened under palmprint, blood oxygen, voice, and a confirmation code she had memorized under Pacific salt air while Witness still smelled faintly of coolant from gantry checks. The order rendered in plain black text on white, no emblem until the final page. The IIA always saved flags for the part where obedience was supposed to feel like law.

She knew every line.

Human-occupied Candle approach under exceptional interpretive necessity.

Crewed witness.

Manual testimony.

Cultural stabilization value.

Final authorization dependent upon mission commander concurrence, medical clearance, hardware readiness, interpretive officer recommendation, and emergency ground concurrence where communications allowed.

A chain long enough to look safe. A chain designed to snap when fear needed it to snap.

Below the order, an automated prompt had opened.

ANOMALOUS PERSONAL-AUDIO EVENT DETECTED.
OCCUPIED INTERPRETIVE OPTION: STATUS REVIEW REQUIRED BEFORE CANDLE COMMIT.
FAILURE TO RESOLVE WILL HOLD PREFLIGHT ROUTING.

Sera saw Nadine’s face change.

“What is it?”

Nadine closed the slate.

“Maintain containment,” she said. “No one opens Asterion audio. No one says the breath belongs to anyone. Tomas, Candle occupied-mode status in three. Sera, evidence note in four. Malik, cache isolation now.”

“Now,” Malik said.

“Tomas,” she added, “do not answer from the interface. Go to hardware.”

His mouth went thin. “Commander.”

“Move.”

He unstrapped from the aft station too fast and caught his knee on the restraint bar. He did not swear. That worried her more than swearing would have.

Nadine unhooked her hand from the rail.

Sera said, “Where are you going?”

“To tell Doctor Venn a thing that should have been told before we left Earth.”

“She is removed from Elias-associated authority.”

“This is not Elias.”

“That breath may be.”

“The order is older than the breath.”

Sera looked toward the timer.

CANDLE PREFLIGHT ROUTING: 00:41:58.

“Then tell fast,” she said.

Mara was sitting on the medical alcove bench with the pulse cuff loose around her wrist. One foot hooked under the restraint bar. The position made her look less patient than detained, which was close enough.

A wall screen showed no feed. Yuna had ordered it blank after the Ines Calder file. The alcove smelled of sterilized fabric and canned mandarin from an opened ration pouch on the counter. Mara had not touched the fruit pieces. Syrup had gathered at the lip of the packet, a bright chemical shine.

She turned when Nadine entered. Two fingers were already pressed to the inside of her wrist.

“You heard it,” Nadine said.

“Yes.”

“Do you know what it was?”

“No.” Mara removed her fingers. “Do you?”

“No.”

The answer sat between them, serviceable and too small.

Nadine sealed the alcove door. The panel clicked once. Doors had become a bad instrument since the Break; everyone heard too much in them.

“I am going to show you a command document,” Nadine said. “You are still removed from Elias-associated release authority. This does not restore it. This is crew safety and mission design.”

“Why me?”

“Because you have lost authority.”

A faint line appeared between Mara’s brows.

Nadine took the slate from her thigh pocket and set it on the fold-down tray. “Because if I show it only to people still clean, it stays clean on paper. That is how these things survive.”

Mara looked at the slate but did not touch it.

“Read the header.”

Mara read. Her mouth moved once around no sound.

Then she reached for the tray edge with one hand. Not the slate. The tray.

Nadine let her have the time. Command was often the rationing of seconds.

Mara read down through the first page, then the second. Her face changed by procedure. First the eyes tracking clauses. Then the small tightening at the jaw at “human-occupied.” Then the stillness at “interpretive necessity.”

She stopped there.

“Say it,” Nadine said.

Mara’s voice had been scraped flat. “Witness is authorized to put a person in Candle.”

“Yes.”

“Candle. The little craft. The probe.”

“The recognition craft.”

“With the human-rated couch they swore was legal ballast.”

“With a seat, manual bridge, and short-duration life support sufficient to let one person ride closer to the Boundary than Witness can go.”

Mara looked back at the page. “A witness in the old sense.”

“A body near the glass.”

“Do not make it gentle.”

“No.”

Mara touched the line with one trimmed nail, not quite on the screen. “Interpretive officer recommendation.”

“Yes.”

“You brought me aboard partly because this order needed a priest.”

“I brought you aboard because there was no neutral person left who could read the damage without lying in one direction.”

“That is almost a denial.”

“It is not a denial.”

Mara closed her eyes for one breath. When she opened them, she looked older in the deck light leaking around the door seal. “Who knew?”

“Me. Pavel. Emergency council. Legal. Medical in sealed review. Probably three sponsor lawyers and a printer in Quito that should have been melted.”

“Tomas?”

“No.”

“Sera?”

“No.”

“Families?”

“Their injunctions helped create the language. They were not shown this form.”

Mara gave a small, ugly laugh without humor. It came too early in the room, a sound out of sequence. “They asked for the option of witness. The Accord built a coffin around the noun.”

Nadine said nothing.

Mara slid the slate back without finishing the final page. “Did you intend to use it?”

“No.”

“Did you intend to tell anyone?”

“No.”

The room held that. The fan behind the medical cabinet changed pitch and steadied.

Mara said, “No one dies for a photograph.”

Nadine had not expected the words from her mouth. Her own conduct addendum returned altered by another voice. It should have pleased her. It did not.

“No one dies because a committee needs a photograph,” Nadine said.

“This is worse than a photograph.”

“Yes.”

“It is testimony.”

“Yes.”

“It is the old hunger in clean legal shoes.”

Nadine looked at the pulse cuff creased beside Mara’s wrist. “It is an option people can reach for when data stops satisfying the part of them that wants a person at the threshold.”

The alcove speaker clicked.

“Commander,” Malik said, “preflight routing just flagged occupied-option unresolved. We have forty minutes and change before Candle commit. If it stays unresolved, the routing hold costs the release window.”

Mara’s hand tightened on the tray.

Nadine keyed response. “Understood. Keep ground out of sealed text until my command.”

“Sera is asking for you in the hangar.”

“I know.”

Mara looked at the slate. “Why now?”

“Because the housekeeping loop grew a breath. Because Candle’s couch is still bolted in. Because the system has begun asking whether our dirty audio makes a human witness necessary before the craft commits.”

“That prompt is automatic?”

“Automatic enough to be dangerous.”

“Tomas disabled something.”

Nadine was still for half a second.

Mara said, “I saw the static-test exception. One manual life-support linkage under design hold. I did not know what it stood against.”

“No one did except him. Not enough.”

“Sera?”

“She will know in minutes.”

“She will burn him alive.”

“She will be correct.”

“And he may still be right.”

Nadine put the slate back into her pocket. It felt heavier with Mara having read it, as if knowledge added mass after all.

Mara said, “Commander.”

Nadine paused at the door.

“If you had kept this sealed through Candle approach, and the crew later learned the human option existed, what would have broken first?”

Trust, Nadine could have said. Command. The mission. The part of her that had stood before cameras and told the crew their survival was the margin.

Instead she said, “The next order.”

Mara nodded once. It was not forgiveness. Nadine had not asked for it.

By the time Nadine reached the Candle hangar, Sera was already there.

The hangar was colder than the command deck. It had to be, for the organoid analogues and for batteries that sulked when warm. Candle sat under maintenance light in its cradle, small and blunt, white hull marked by access numbers and hand-applied labels Tomas distrusted machines to print correctly. Its antenna ribs were folded along one side. The open service hatch showed the couch: gray restraint webbing, left rail, right rail, head cradle, no human in it.

Empty furniture could accuse.

Tomas stood at the hatch with a tablet clamped to the rail. Sera stood opposite him, one hand on the equipment rack, the other pressed so hard at her old ring mark that skin had gone pale around her thumb.

The timer above the inner hatch read 00:36:22.

“Tell her,” Nadine said.

Tomas swallowed. “I put the manual left-rail life-support bridge under design hold before launch.”

Sera’s voice was quiet. “Say disabled.”

“I disabled one manual life-support linkage.”

“Say which function.”

“Occupied Boundary approach cannot maintain controlled life-support continuity through manual couch mode without installing the retrofit bridge.”

“Locker H-12,” Sera said.

Tomas glanced at Nadine.

Nadine said, “Answer.”

“Yes. Retrofit bridge kit is in H-12. Commander authority and hardware lead access.”

Sera stepped closer to Candle, not to Tomas. “You let the mission interface report couch status as a valid installed assembly.”

“Installed, yes. Valid for unmanned operation.”

“Do not do that.”

Tomas flinched as if the correction had touched skin.

Sera turned on him then. “A status line can be true and still deceive. You know that. You built an ethical refusal into a hidden hardware state and left the scientific design carrying a false option.”

“The option was false before I touched it.”

“That was not yours to hide.”

“It was mine to wire.”

“No.” Sera’s voice rose, cracked once, repaired itself. “It was yours to report. We measure contradictions for a living. You added one inside the craft.”

Tomas looked at the couch. “If I reported it before launch, legal would have reenabled it.”

“Then write that. Say that. Put your name under the refusal.”

“I did.”

“Where?”

“In a maintenance exception no one outside hardware read.”

Sera laughed once, sharp and disbelieving. “That is burial.”

Nadine stood between them without moving into the physical line. A commander could ruin an argument by rescuing the wrong person too soon.

Tomas’s voice lowered. “You think I wanted this hidden?”

“Yes,” Sera said. “Because hidden ethics feel clean. No one votes on them. No one sues them. No one checks their math.”

“The math was checked.”

“Not the moral load path.”

He looked at her fully. His face had gone blotched at the neck. “The couch is a political wound with straps. Sponsor clauses kept it. Family injunctions kept it. Insurance kept it. Everyone said leaving it in place preserved options. Options for whom? For the first person who cannot bear an instrument reading? For a director who wants one human sentence before the signal dies?”

Nadine heard Mara’s voice in the alcove: the old hunger in clean legal shoes.

Sera pointed at Candle’s open hatch. “You corrupted mission design to prevent mission corruption.”

“Yes,” Tomas said.

The word landed more heavily than a defense would have.

Sera took one step back. Her breathing had gone shallow, the sort Yuna would flag if Yuna could see through hangar bulkheads. “Then you made me certify a lie.”

“I made the interface wrong. Nadine corrected it after the static test.”

“After launch.”

“Yes.”

“After I witnessed the patch authentication without knowing the sealed authority behind it.”

Tomas looked at Nadine. There was no accusation there. Worse: relief that someone else had become visible.

Nadine said, “That is on me.”

Sera turned. “You knew the occupied order existed.”

“Yes.”

“And after Tomas disclosed the hold, you still did not tell us.”

“Yes.”

The hangar fans pushed cold air across Candle’s hull. On the service hatch, a small fabric tag fluttered, then stuck against a screw head.

Sera said, “How many sealed deaths are we carrying?”

“One,” Nadine said. “And we are killing it now.”

Tomas stopped breathing for a beat.

Sera’s eyes cut to the timer.

00:32:04.

“What does that mean in hardware?”

Nadine looked at Tomas. “Can occupied mode be permanently invalidated before commit?”

His answer came out too fast. “Yes.”

“Define permanently.”

“Command-fuse the manual bridge bus, break the oxygen spur coupling, and remove the H-12 bridge kit from available inventory. Once the fuse burns, the couch can remain installed, but it cannot become a piloted approach seat without depot rebuild.”

“Time?”

“Twelve minutes if I do it ugly. Eighteen clean.”

“You have twelve.”

Sera said, “Irreversible?”

Tomas nodded. “The bus fuse is a melt link. It becomes slag inside the rail. No rollback in flight.”

Nadine opened a command log. “Recommendation?”

Sera stared at Candle. The physicist in her wanted every path named, every condition preserved as evidence. The woman in her looked at the empty restraint web and understood how quickly a possibility became appetite.

“Burn it,” Sera said.

Malik’s voice broke into the hangar channel. “Commander, ground ping asking for routine status because our carrier monitor dropped for four seconds.”

“Tell them carrier monitor fault. No raw detail until evidence note.”

Sera’s head snapped toward the pickup. “That is omission.”

“That is containment,” Nadine said. “For the next four minutes.”

“Four minutes becomes policy in this mission.”

“Then write fast.”

Sera stared at her. For a second Nadine saw the public Sera, the one who had corrected senators and children with the same disciplined mercy. Then that face fell away, leaving a woman in a cold hangar beside a craft everyone had lied around.

“I need the order,” Sera said.

“You will have it.”

“Now.”

Nadine passed her the slate.

Sera read standing, because she would not give the document the comfort of sitting. Tomas stayed by the hatch, hands open at his sides. He began, under his breath, to inventory visible fasteners.

“Left rail four captive screws. Bridge cover two. Harness clamp. Manual oxygen spur. Return valve.”

“Tomas,” Nadine said.

He stopped.

Sera reached the line Mara had touched. “Exceptional interpretive necessity.” She repeated it without inflection, as if testing for poison.

Then she looked at the couch.

“No,” she said.

No one answered.

“No,” she said again, to the order this time, to the rail, to whoever had written the phrase in a room with breathable air and coffee. “Scientific validity cannot be purchased with a person. A body in Candle would contaminate every record before the Boundary did.”

Tomas exhaled.

Sera heard it. “Do not look relieved. You still broke chain.”

“I know.”

“You will document the disabled linkage as design interference.”

“Yes.”

“You will include the exact mechanical path that once would have reenabled it.”

He went still.

Sera’s voice cut colder. “If you hide the path, someone else discovers it under worse conditions. If you name it, command can guard what remains.”

Nadine said, “Locker H-12 goes under command seal until the kit is destroyed.”

“And ground?”

“Receives occupied-mode invalidation, sealed order existence, and commander nonconcurrence.”

Sera looked back at the slate. “Pavel will call.”

“He can wait his delay.”

“He will say the order was never a plan.”

“He will be right and lying.”

The surprise was Tomas laughing.

It was small, almost a cough, and completely wrong for the hangar. Sera looked at him as if he had dropped a sample tray.

“Sorry,” he said. “That was not—sorry. It is just the most accurate thing anyone has said in here.”

No one rescued him from the apology.

The hangar hatch opened behind Nadine.

Mara entered with Yuna on the wall channel and a medical monitor clipped to her sleeve. Nadine had not authorized her movement. That fact crossed the room and found every person in it.

“I cleared a supervised transfer,” Yuna said through the speaker before Nadine asked. “Doctor Venn is not accessing restricted audio or Elias material. Her pulse is ugly but functional.”

“Medical language has become generous,” Nadine said.

“Command language started it.”

Mara did not smile. She moved slowly in the hangar’s cold, one hand near the rail but not touching it. Her gaze went first to Sera. Then Tomas. Then the open hatch and the couch.

Sera held the slate out. “Did she show you?”

“Yes,” Mara said.

“Before me.”

“Yes.”

Sera’s face tightened again, different this time. “Of course.”

Mara accepted that too. Witness had become a room where everyone accepted blows because the alternative was choosing which truth to pad.

Nadine said, “Doctor Venn, you are here under observation only.”

“I know.”

“Then observe.”

Mara looked at Candle’s interior. The couch webbing was neatly clipped. A small manufacturer’s tag remained on the underside of the left rail, absurdly domestic, like furniture delivered to the wrong house.

“Does it work?” Mara asked.

Tomas answered, “For launch, yes. For unmanned approach, yes. For occupied Boundary approach, no.”

“Without H-12.”

“Without H-12 and command rework.”

“After the fuse?”

“Never in flight.”

The word struck the room harder than no.

Sera looked down at the slate. “We need the formal action.”

Nadine opened the command channel to hangar-only record. “Witness command note, sealed-to-operational transition. Human-occupied Candle authority exists under IIA emergency order. Commander nonconcurrence is active. Candle will proceed unmanned or not at all. Retrofit bridge Locker H-12 is under command seal. Occupied-mode bridge will be physically invalidated before Candle commit. No one dies for a photograph.”

She stopped.

Sera said, “Add: no one dies for testimony.”

Nadine looked at her.

“Photograph is too narrow,” Sera said.

Nadine restarted the recorder.

“Addendum. No one dies for testimony.”

The log accepted her voiceprint.

00:27:19.

“Tomas,” Nadine said. “Execute.”

He clipped his tablet to the hatch rail and pulled himself halfway into Candle. The craft shifted against its cradle with a small complaint. His knees braced against the threshold. He removed the left-rail cover with a torque driver and set each captive screw into the magnetic tray.

“Bridge cover open,” he said.

Sera moved to his shoulder with the slate recording. “Video on. Serial visible.”

Malik came over channel again, voice lower. “Ground is asking why H-12 moved to active seal.”

“Stand by,” Nadine said.

“Nadine,” Malik said, no rank this time, “they just copied Pavel on the routing hold.”

“Of course they did.”

Mara’s eyes stayed on the couch. “An empty couch can still make a promise.”

Tomas’s voice came from inside Candle. “That is why I broke the promise.”

Sera said, “You hid the fact that you broke it.”

“Yes.”

Mara turned toward Nadine. “And command hid the fact that the promise was authorized.”

“Yes,” Nadine said.

For a while only Tomas’s tools spoke. The hangar lights reflected off Candle’s hull in flat white panels. Mars was not visible here. That had been one of Nadine’s requirements for the bay: no window, no accidental altar. Yet the couch carried Mars into the room better than glass would have. A person could sit there, strap in, and turn the next bad reading into a death record.

Tomas held up a thumb-sized ceramic piece with two gold contact throats.

“Manual bridge bus,” he said. His voice shook, then steadied. “This is what lets the couch controls talk to guidance during occupied approach.”

“Before,” Sera said.

He looked at her.

“Use the past tense.”

Tomas nodded. “This was what let the couch controls talk to guidance.”

Nadine inserted her command key into the rail-side fuse port. The key had been issued for abort pyros and classified archive purge. Using it here felt improper until she remembered that impropriety was not the same as error.

The slate asked for concurrence.

COMMANDER QURESHI: OCCUPIED-MODE BUS FUSE / BURN LINK / IRREVERSIBLE.
CONFIRM MISSION IMPACT.

She pressed her thumb to the sensor.

SERA IMANI: SCIENCE AUTHORITY WITNESS.

Sera stared at the prompt as if it had insulted her, then placed her thumb beside Nadine’s.

TOMAS VALE: HARDWARE AUTHORITY EXECUTE.

Tomas pressed last.

The link fired with almost no drama. A tiny pop inside the left rail. A smell like overheated dust. On the tablet, a green line went gray.

OCCUPIED MANUAL BRIDGE: DESTROYED.
CREWED APPROACH CONFIGURATION: INVALID.
UNMANNED CANDLE CONFIGURATION: GREEN.

No one cheered. No one prayed.

Tomas reached back into the rail, withdrew the ceramic piece with its center blackened, and placed it in an evidence pouch. His fingers left ink on the label.

Sera said, “Oxygen spur.”

He took a crimp tool from the kit. “Manual couch oxygen spur isolated.”

“Say the action.”

“Crimping and tagging the spur.”

The tool closed. Metal complained once. A second status line changed.

OCCUPIED LIFE-SUPPORT CONTINUITY: BROKEN.
DEPOT SERVICE REQUIRED.

Mara closed her eyes at that. Not relief. Something smaller and more tired.

Nadine keyed Malik. “Open ground notification. Text only. Delay locked. No summary substitution.”

“Ready.”

She dictated slowly.

“Witness command reports sealed emergency order authorizing human-occupied Candle approach has been opened under onboard authority after anomalous local-carrier contamination and automated occupied-option routing hold. Commander nonconcurrence entered. Science authority concurs that human occupation would contaminate mission evidence and exceed acceptable crew risk. Hardware authority has physically invalidated occupied Candle mode by irreversible bridge-bus fuse and manual oxygen-spur break. Candle remains green for unmanned commit. Do not recategorize as routine hardware fault. Do not describe as crew availability issue. This is command refusal of human-occupied Boundary approach.”

Malik was silent long enough for Nadine to know he was reading it twice.

Then he said, “That is hard to spin.”

“That is the idea.”

“Sending.”

The channel clicked.

00:19:44.

Sera looked at the timer. “Routing hold?”

Malik answered from the deck. “Ground notification queued. Local system accepts occupied-option resolved. Candle preflight routing resumed. We lost eight minutes.”

Tomas pulled himself out of Candle and sealed the left-rail cover. His forehead shone with sweat in the cold hangar.

“Eight minutes matters,” Sera said.

“It does,” Nadine said.

“Do we still make commit?”

“Flight says yes if nothing else lies.”

Mara gave a thin sound that might have become a laugh in another room.

The hangar pickup chimed with an incoming priority call. Pavel’s name rendered on the wall panel, delayed and severe in block letters.

IIA DIRECTOR ILYIN REQUESTS COMMAND CONFERENCE.
REASON: SEALED ORDER STATUS CHANGE.

Nadine looked at it until the request aged by one second, then two.

“Decline live,” she said.

Malik answered before the system could ask twice. “Reason?”

“Operational priority. Send him the log.”

The panel cleared.

Sera passed the command slate back. Her anger had not gone out; it had moved into structure. “Now H-12.”

They crossed to the sealed locker together. Locker H-12 was unremarkable from the outside, a white rectangle with two red handholds and a small inspection window. Inside waited the retrofit bridge kit, packed in foam, legal ambiguity made portable.

Nadine opened the outer seal. Tomas opened the hardware latch. Sera filmed.

The kit slid out in its gray case. A printed card sat beneath the transparent cover.

CANDLE OCCUPIED APPROACH RETROFIT BRIDGE / HUMAN-RATED / INSTALL UNDER COMMAND AUTHORITY ONLY.

Tomas touched the case with two fingers. “This is the hinge.”

Nadine said, “Then take the hinge off.”

He opened the case.

Inside, the bridge was smaller than Mara expected. She had thought, absurdly, that a sacrificial option would have more mass. It was a curved length of shielded cable, two locking plugs, a controller wafer, and a folded sheet of installation steps printed in five languages. A person could have held it against their chest.

Sera removed the installation sheet and sealed it in evidence film.

Nadine said, “Procedure?”

Tomas looked once at Sera. “Cut controller wafer. Shear both lock plugs. Sever cable at marked points. Without depot parts, the bridge cannot be installed.”

“Execute,” Nadine said.

This time there was drama because hands made it.

Tomas set the wafer in the shear clamp. His first attempt slipped. The wafer shot against the foam and skidded to the edge of the case. Everyone froze, and for one stupid second the tiny black square seemed alive with intention.

Mara caught it against the lip of the case with the back of her hand.

No one had expected her to move.

Yuna’s voice snapped through the speaker. “Doctor Venn, step back.”

Mara lifted her hand slowly. The wafer lay against her knuckle, harmless and obscene.

“I did not touch the contacts,” she said.

Sera took it with tweezers. Her face had gone white around the mouth. “Back.”

Mara stepped back.

Tomas looked sick. “Sorry.”

“Again,” Nadine said.

He placed the wafer properly. The shear came down. The controller split with a brittle crack.

Sera marked the time.

The plugs went next. Then the cable. Tomas cut it into three lengths and sealed each in a separate pouch at Sera’s instruction.

H-12 INVENTORY: BRIDGE KIT DESTROYED.
OCCUPIED RETROFIT AVAILABILITY: ZERO.

Mara exhaled when zero appeared. Tomas did not.

Nadine closed the empty case and placed a red command seal across it anyway. Some rituals were not lies. Some were reminders to future hands.

“Physical state,” Sera said.

Tomas read from the tablet. “Couch installed. Restraints installed. Human-rated shell remains as inert structure. Manual bridge bus destroyed. Manual oxygen spur broken. Retrofit bridge kit destroyed and logged. Candle cannot support occupied Boundary approach in flight.”

“Say it shorter,” Nadine said.

Tomas looked at the couch through the open hatch. “No one can ride it now.”

Mara’s fingers went to the inside of her wrist, then dropped before they found pulse.

From the command deck, Malik said, “Candle commit in sixteen minutes. Ground acknowledges receipt. Pavel has sent four priority objections and one legal preservation notice.”

“Queue them,” Nadine said.
