## Chapter 20: Sealed Orders

The command deck was running a harmless loop when the dead entered it.

Not a message. Not even a word. The loop belonged to the comms watchdog, a two-second housekeeping file that played under the narrowband carrier every six minutes so Malik could confirm the channel had not frozen clean. It was ship-made, bland, checked too often to be interesting: a soft tone, then the neutral cabin voice saying, “Carrier live.”

At 11:08 ship time, the tone bent.

Nadine heard it first as a flaw in the deck’s ordinary breath. Witness had its own small noises, fans behind panels, velcro lifting from a sleeve, someone swallowing too close to a pickup. Commanders learned the ship’s throat. This was not Witness.

The cabin voice began, “Car—”

Static covered the second syllable. Under the static, a human inhaled.

Malik’s hand stopped above the routing board. Sera looked up from the clock-array spread. Tomas, seated at the aft systems station with ink on two fingers, said nothing at all.

The loop completed.

“Mark source,” Nadine said.

Malik’s fingers moved. “Watchdog file COM-HK-2. Local cache.”

“Archive access?”

“None scheduled.”

“Say actual.”

He leaned closer to the board. “No authorized archive access in the last eleven minutes. Quarantine fork starting.”

The loop came again too early. Tone. Broken cabin voice. Breath.

It was not dramatic. That made it worse. A scrape of static. One clipped intake, close to a mic, followed by the tiny wet catch of a person trying not to breathe aloud.

Sera said, “Stop playback.”

Malik killed the carrier monitor. The deck stayed silent except for the fans.

Nadine kept one hand hooked near the rail beneath the command display. The motion had begun as a habit after lunar rescue. It had become a private gauge: if she needed both hands, the room was already lying about calm.

“Report,” she said.

Malik swallowed. “The watchdog loop checksum passed when loaded. It fails now. Local copy shows two extra waveform features.”

“Classification.”

“Audio bleed until Sera says worse.”

Sera was already pulling the trace. Her compression sleeves were thumbed over her wrists, dark against the pallor of hands kept too cold by the deck air. “Do not call it bleed as if we have identified direction. It is anomalous contamination in a local housekeeping file.”

Malik nodded once. “Anomalous contamination.”

“Match?”

“I can run restricted comparison,” he said. “I need authorization.”

“No,” Nadine said.

Sera turned toward her.

Nadine met the look. “No restricted Asterion comparison from the command deck for one breath in a dirty housekeeping loop. Quarantine the file. Preserve the raw carrier log. Hash the cache. No replay outside evidence review.”

Malik’s shoulders dropped a fraction, then locked again as he worked. “Copy.”

Tomas said, very quietly, “Band One is early.”

Sera corrected him without heat. “We are within modeled outer degradation by optical confidence, not material bands.”

“Audio in a local loop.”

“Information degradation is allowed in Band One. Allowed does not mean understood.”

Nadine heard the cost under the correction. Sera’s precision had begun to sound like someone tightening bolts by feel in the dark.

On the forward display, Mars sat beyond the modeled zone, red and measurable. A navigational overlay held it in pale geometry. The planet looked clean in optical, which meant nothing useful and too much to the part of the mind that wanted pictures to be honest.

“Medical,” Nadine said.

Yuna’s ground-linked voice arrived inside the command channel after the expected delay compensation, clipped at the edge. “Medical monitor active. Crew pulse variance rising but within—”

“No threshold language. I want names.”

A shorter pause. “Malik elevated. Tomas elevated. Sera stable by pulse, not by blink rate. Commander, you are stable.”

Nadine almost smiled. “Doctor Venn.”

“In ship medical. Awake. Under observation. She heard the contamination through the secondary channel before Malik cut it.”

Of course she had.

Nadine looked at the sealed-order indicator buried at the bottom left of her command slate. It had been there since launch, a small gray lock that never blinked, never asked, never admitted itself to anyone who lacked command clearance. She had trained herself not to glance at it during crew briefings. The eye still went there during anomalies, the same stupid hunger by which crowds asked for a person at a window.

No martyrs. No theater.

“Malik,” she said, “route a private command packet to my slate. No ground mirror.”

He looked at her once. “Yes, Commander.”

Sera’s gaze sharpened. “That packet touches sealed authority?”

“It touches command risk.”

“That is not an answer.”

“It is the answer I am giving on the deck.”

The deck accepted that because decks accepted rank before they accepted truth. Nadine disliked that more on days when it helped her.

The gray lock opened under her palmprint, blood oxygen, voice, and a confirmation code she had memorized under Pacific salt air while Witness still smelled faintly of coolant from gantry checks. The order rendered in plain black text on white, no emblem until the final page. The IIA always saved its flags for the part where it wanted obedience to feel like law rather than appetite.

She did not read it. She knew every line.

Human-occupied Candle approach under exceptional interpretive necessity.

Crewed witness.

Manual testimony.

Cultural stabilization value.

Final authorization dependent upon mission commander concurrence, medical clearance, hardware readiness, interpretive officer recommendation, and emergency ground concurrence where communications allowed.

A chain long enough to look safe. A chain designed to break exactly when fear needed it to break.

Nadine closed the slate.

“Maintain containment,” she said to the deck. “No one opens Asterion audio. No one says the breath belongs to anyone. Tomas, Candle status in five. Sera, evidence note in six. Malik, cache isolation now.”

“Now,” Malik said.

Nadine unhooked her hand from the rail.

“Where are you going?” Sera asked.

“To tell Doctor Venn a thing that should have been told before we left Earth.”

Sera’s mouth tightened. “She is removed from Elias-associated authority.”

“This is not Elias.”

“That breath may be.”

“The order is older than the breath.”

Tomas looked down at his hands. Ink had smudged across his left thumb, a small black crescent in the skin fold. Nadine saw it, filed it, carried it with her through the hatch.

Mara was sitting on the medical alcove bench with the pulse cuff loose around her wrist. She had one foot hooked under the restraint bar. The position made her look less patient than detained, which was closer to the truth.

A wall screen showed no feed. Yuna had ordered it blank after the Ines Calder file. The alcove smelled of sterilized fabric and canned mandarin from an opened ration pouch on the counter. Mara had not touched the fruit pieces. The syrup had gathered at the lip of the packet, a bright chemical shine.

She turned when Nadine entered. Two fingers were already pressed to the inside of her wrist.

“You heard it,” Nadine said.

“Yes.”

“Do you know what it was?”

“No.” Mara removed her fingers. “Do you?”

“No.”

The answer sat between them, serviceable and too small.

Nadine sealed the alcove door. The panel clicked once. It was an ordinary sound. Doors had become a bad instrument since the Break; everyone heard too much in them.

“I am going to show you a command document,” Nadine said. “You are still removed from Elias-associated release authority. This does not restore it. This is crew safety and mission design.”

Mara’s eyes stayed on Nadine’s face. “Why me?”

“Because you have lost authority.”

A faint line appeared between Mara’s brows.

Nadine took the slate from her thigh pocket and set it on the fold-down tray. “Because if I show it only to people still clean, it stays clean on paper. That is how these things survive.”

Mara looked at the slate but did not touch it.

“Read the header.”

Mara read. Her mouth moved once around no sound.

Then she reached for the tray edge with one hand. Not the slate. The tray.

Nadine let her have the time. Command was often the rationing of seconds.

Mara read down through the first page, then the second. Her face changed by procedure. First the eyes tracking clauses. Then the small tightening at the jaw at “human-occupied.” Then the stillness at “interpretive necessity.”

She stopped there.

“Say it,” Nadine said.

Mara’s voice had been scraped flat. “Witness is authorized to put a person in Candle.”

“Under exceptional necessity.”

“Do not use their adjective for them.”

“No.”

Mara looked back at the page. “Who knew?”

“Me. Pavel. The emergency council. Legal. Medical in sealed review. Probably three sponsor lawyers and a printer in Quito that should have been melted.”

“Tomas?”

“No.”

“Sera?”

“No.”

“Earth families?”

“Their injunctions helped create the language. They were not shown this form.”

Mara gave a small, ugly laugh without humor. It came too early in the room, a sound out of sequence. “They asked for the option of witness. The Accord built a coffin around the noun.”

Nadine said nothing.

Mara touched the line with one trimmed nail, not quite on the screen. “Interpretive officer recommendation.”

“Yes.”

“You brought me aboard partly because this order needed a priest.”

“I brought you aboard because there was no neutral person left who could read the damage without lying in one direction.”

“That is almost a denial.”

“It is not a denial.”

Mara closed her eyes for one breath. When she opened them, she looked older in the deck light leaking around the door seal. “Did you intend to use it?”

“No.”

“Did you intend to tell anyone?”

“No.”

The room held that. The fan behind the medical cabinet changed pitch and steadied.

Mara said, “No one dies for a photograph.”

Nadine had not expected the words from her mouth. Her own conduct addendum returned altered by another voice. It should have pleased her. It did not.

“No one dies because a committee needs a photograph,” Nadine said.

“This is worse than a photograph.”

“Yes.”

“It is testimony.”

“Yes.”

“It is the old hunger in clean legal shoes.”

Nadine looked at the pulse cuff creased beside Mara’s wrist. “It is an option people can reach for when data stops satisfying the part of them that wants a body at the threshold.”

Mara slid the slate back without finishing the final page. “Why now?”

“Because the housekeeping loop just grew a breath. Because Candle’s couch is still bolted in. Because Tomas made an unauthorized ethical edit to the machine, and I let the interface patch stand without opening the sealed order to the crew.”

“Tomas disabled it.”

“You knew?”

“I knew from the static test that one manual life-support linkage was under design hold. I did not know it stood against this.”

Nadine took the slate. “No one did except him, and not enough.”

“Does Sera know the order?”

“She will.”

Mara’s gaze moved to the blank wall screen. “She will burn him alive.”

“She will be correct.”

“And he may still be right.”

Nadine put the slate back into her pocket. It felt heavier with Mara having read it, as if knowledge added mass after all.

Mara said, “Commander.”

Nadine paused at the door.

“If you had kept this sealed through Candle approach, and the crew later learned the human option existed, what would have broken first?”

Trust, Nadine could have said. Command. The mission. The part of her that had stood before cameras and told the crew their survival was the margin.

Instead she said, “The next order.”

Mara nodded once. It was not forgiveness. Nadine had not asked for it.

By the time Nadine reached the Candle hangar, Sera was already there.

The hangar was colder than the command deck. It had to be, for the organoid analogues, for the tissue cultures, for batteries that sulked when warm. Candle sat under maintenance light in its cradle, small and blunt, white hull marked by access numbers and hand-applied labels Tomas distrusted machines to print correctly. Its antenna ribs were folded along one side like a closed instrument. The couch was visible through the open service hatch: gray restraint webbing, left rail, right rail, head cradle, nothing human in it.

Empty furniture could accuse.

Tomas stood at the hatch with a tablet clamped to the rail. Sera stood opposite him, one hand on the equipment rack, the other pressed so hard at her old ring mark that the skin had gone pale around her thumb.

Nadine entered without announcing herself. Both of them knew anyway.

“Tell her,” Nadine said.

Tomas swallowed. “I put the manual left-rail life-support bridge under design hold before launch.”

Sera’s voice was quiet. “Say disabled.”

“I disabled one manual life-support linkage.”

“Say which function.”

“Occupied Boundary approach cannot maintain controlled life-support continuity through manual couch mode without installing the retrofit bridge.”

“Locker H-12,” Sera said.

Tomas glanced at Nadine.

Nadine said, “Answer.”

“Yes. Retrofit bridge kit is in H-12. Commander authority and hardware lead access.”

Sera’s hand left her wrist. She stepped closer to Candle, not to Tomas. “You let the mission interface report couch status as a valid installed assembly.”

“Installed, yes. Valid for unmanned operation.”

“Do not do that.”

Tomas flinched as if the correction had touched skin.

Sera turned on him then. “A status line can be true and still deceive. You know that. You built an ethical refusal into a hidden hardware state and left the scientific design carrying a false option.”

“The option was false before I touched it.”

“That was not yours to hide.”

“It was mine to wire.”

“No.” Sera’s voice rose, cracked once, repaired itself. “It was yours to report. We measure contradictions for a living. You added one inside the craft.”

Tomas looked at the couch. “If I reported it before launch, legal would have reenabled it.”

“Then write that. Say that. Put your name under the refusal.”

“I did.”

“Where?”

“In a maintenance exception no one outside hardware read.”

Sera laughed once, sharp and disbelieving. “That is not conscience. That is burial.”

Nadine stood between them without moving into the physical line. A commander could ruin an argument by rescuing the wrong person too soon.

Tomas’s voice lowered. “You think I wanted this hidden?”

“Yes,” Sera said. “Because hidden ethics feel clean. No one votes on them. No one sues them. No one checks their math.”

“The math was checked.”

“Not the moral load path.”

He looked at her then, fully. His face had gone blotched at the neck. “The couch is a political wound with straps. Sponsor clauses kept it. Family injunctions kept it. Insurance kept it. Everyone said leaving it in place preserved options. Options for whom? For the first person who cannot bear an instrument reading? For a director who wants one human sentence before the signal dies?”

Nadine heard Mara’s voice in the alcove: the old hunger in clean legal shoes.

Sera pointed at Candle’s open hatch. “You corrupted mission design to prevent mission corruption.”

“Yes,” Tomas said.

The word landed more heavily than a defense would have.

Sera took one step back. Her breathing had gone shallow, the sort Yuna would flag if Yuna could see through hangar bulkheads. “Then you made me certify a lie.”

“I made the interface wrong. Nadine corrected it after the static test.”

“After launch.”

“Yes.”

“After I witnessed the patch authentication without knowing the sealed authority behind it.”

Tomas looked at Nadine. There was no accusation there. Worse: relief that someone else had become visible.

Nadine said, “That is on me.”

Sera turned. “You knew the occupied order existed.”

“Yes.”

“And after Tomas disclosed the hold, you still did not tell us.”

“Yes.”

The hangar fans pushed cold air across Candle’s hull. On the service hatch, a small fabric tag fluttered, then stuck against a screw head.

Sera said, “How many sealed deaths are we carrying?”

“One,” Nadine said. “And we are not using it.”

“You do not get to make it harmless by refusing it privately.”

“I know.”

“Do you?”

Nadine accepted the hit. Sera’s anger had better aim than most people’s calm.

Malik’s voice broke into the hangar channel. “Commander, command deck. Quarantined watchdog file preserved. No further contamination in local loops. Ground ping asking for routine status because our carrier monitor dropped for four seconds.”

“Tell them carrier monitor fault. No raw detail until evidence note.”

“Copy.”

Sera’s head snapped toward the overhead pickup. “That is omission.”

“That is containment,” Nadine said. “For the next four minutes.”

“Four minutes becomes policy in this mission.”

“Then write fast.”

Sera stared at her. For a second Nadine saw the public Sera, the one who had corrected senators and children with the same disciplined mercy. Then that face fell away, leaving a woman in a cold hangar beside a craft everyone had lied around.

“I need the order,” Sera said.

“You will have it.”

“Now.”

Nadine took the slate out and passed it over.

Sera read standing, because she would not give the document the comfort of sitting. Tomas stayed by the hatch, hands open at his sides. He began, under his breath, to inventory the visible fasteners.

“Left rail four captive screws. Bridge cover two. Harness clamp. Manual oxygen spur. Return valve—”

“Tomas,” Nadine said.

He stopped.

Sera reached the line Mara had touched. “Exceptional interpretive necessity.” She repeated it without inflection, as if testing for poison.

Then she looked at the couch.

“No,” she said.

No one answered.

“No,” she said again, to the order this time, to the rail, to whoever had written the phrase in a room with breathable air and coffee. “Scientific validity cannot be purchased with a person. A body in Candle would contaminate every record before the Boundary did.”

Tomas exhaled.

Sera heard it. “Do not look relieved. You still broke chain.”

“I know.”

“You will document the disabled linkage as design interference, not moral exception.”

Tomas nodded. “Yes.”

“You will include the exact mechanical path needed to reenable it.”

He went still.

Sera’s voice cut colder. “If you hide the path, someone else will discover it under worse conditions. If you name it, command can guard it.”

Nadine said, “I will seal Locker H-12 under two-person physical watch.”

“And ground?”

“Receives occupied-mode invalidation, sealed order existence, and commander nonconcurrence.”

Sera looked back at the slate. “Pavel will call.”

“He can wait his delay.”

“He will say the order was never a plan.”

“He will be right and lying.”

The surprise was Tomas laughing.

It was small, almost a cough, and completely wrong for the hangar. Sera looked at him as if he had dropped a sample tray.

“Sorry,” he said. “That was not—sorry. It is just the most accurate thing anyone has said in here.”

No one rescued him from the apology.

The hangar hatch opened behind Nadine.

Mara entered with Yuna on the wall channel and a medical monitor clipped to her sleeve. Nadine had not authorized her movement. That fact crossed the room and found every person in it.

“I cleared a supervised transfer,” Yuna said through the speaker, before Nadine asked. “Doctor Venn is not accessing restricted audio or Elias material. Her pulse is ugly but functional.”

“Medical language has become very generous,” Nadine said.

“Command language started it.”

Mara did not smile. She moved slowly in the hangar’s cold, one hand near the rail but not touching it. Her gaze went first to Sera. Then Tomas. Then the open hatch and the couch.

Sera held the slate out. “Did she show you?”

“Yes,” Mara said.

“Before me.”

“Yes.”

Sera’s face tightened again, different this time. “Of course.”

Mara accepted that too. Witness had become a room where everyone accepted blows because the alternative was choosing which truth to pad.

Nadine said, “Doctor Venn, you are here under observation only.”

“I know.”

“Then observe.”

Mara looked at Candle’s interior. The couch webbing was neatly clipped. A small manufacturer’s tag remained on the underside of the left rail, absurdly domestic, like furniture delivered to the wrong house.

“Does it work?” Mara asked.

Tomas answered too fast. “For launch, yes. For unmanned approach, yes. For occupied Boundary approach, no.”

“Without H-12.”

“Without H-12 and command rework.”

“Time?”

He glanced at Nadine.

“Answer,” Nadine said.

“If someone had both authorities, knew the path, and accepted ugly risk? Fifty minutes. Maybe less if they did not care about clean rollback.”

Sera said, “We care.”

“I know.”

Mara stepped closer to the hatch. Nadine watched her hands. No wrist touch now. No reach for the couch. She studied it like evidence she wished would stay mute.

“An empty couch can still make a promise,” Mara said.

Tomas’s voice was low. “That is why I broke the promise.”

Sera said, “You hid the fact that you broke it.”

“Yes.”

Mara turned toward Nadine. “And command hid the fact that the promise was authorized.”

“Yes,” Nadine said.

For a while no one spoke. The hangar lights reflected off Candle’s hull in flat white panels. Mars was not visible here. That had been one of Nadine’s requirements for the bay: no window, no accidental altar. Yet the couch carried Mars into the room better than glass would have. A person could sit there and make the whole mission into a sentence no instrument could check.

Mara asked, “Can an empty couch be a lie?”

The question did not sound like philosophy in her mouth. It sounded like a seal test.

Tomas looked down at the left rail. Sera closed the order slate. Nadine felt the old lunar clock start in her body, the six hours she had made a crowd wait while two people breathed thin air behind a rock ridge. Back then the audience had wanted flame, risk, a bright rescue arc. She had waited for a safer burn and been called bloodless for three days, until the survivors spoke.

Here there would be no survivors from that couch. There would be data, static, public hunger, a body made into punctuation.

“Yes,” Nadine said.

Mara nodded, once.

Nadine took the slate back from Sera and opened a new command log. Her thumb hovered over the recorder. The first sentence mattered because it would become a door other people tried to pry open.

She recorded anyway.

“Witness command note, sealed-to-operational transition. Human-occupied Candle authority exists under IIA emergency order. Commander nonconcurrence is active. Candle will proceed unmanned or not at all. Retrofit bridge Locker H-12 is under command seal. No one dies for a photograph.”

She stopped. The log accepted her voiceprint.

Sera said, “Add: no one dies for testimony.”

Nadine looked at her.

Sera held the look. “Photograph is too narrow.”

Nadine restarted the recorder.

“Addendum. No one dies for testimony.”

Tomas rubbed both inked fingers against his palm. Mara looked into Candle’s empty couch as if it had finally answered one question by making every other question harder.

From the command deck, Malik came back on channel.

“Commander. Ground is asking why H-12 just moved to active seal.”

Nadine closed the service hatch. The couch vanished behind Candle’s white skin.

“Tell ground,” she said, “we found a door and locked it.”
