## Chapter 10: The Door Around Mars

Solenne Kade held the red marker with the cap on.

On the screen behind her, Mars hung at the center of the lesson board, a clean rust disk with a pale dust system moving over Valles Marineris. She had chosen a feed with no wreckage in it. No crushed ship. No orange peel. No loose strap. Children stayed longer when the first image was only a planet.

The room looked like a classroom because she knew classrooms made people lower their guard. White board. Two rows of empty chairs. A paper weather calendar with the wrong month still clipped to the wall. A shelf of plastic habitat models left over from her Red Nursery years. She had turned the little greenhouses sideways so the cameras would not catch their painted windows.

The stream counter climbed by hundreds at a time.

Solenne smiled with her mouth closed and uncapped the marker.

“Good morning,” she said. “Or evening. Or night, depending where you are. If you are watching from a ward, you can keep your water cup in your hand. If you are watching from a school terminal, you do not need to answer out loud.”

A comment flare rose and vanished: TOUCHING THE HANDLE.

She ignored it. The first rule from teaching four-year-olds and grieving adults was the same. The loudest child was rarely the one who needed you first.

“I want to start with a question.”

She drew a rectangle around Mars. Slowly. Top line, downstroke, bottom, upstroke. A door, crude and red, enclosing the planet without touching it.

“When a door is locked,” she said, “how do we know?”

The first copy of the stream would later show her hand clean and steady. The second would smear the marker into a red band each time she moved. The third would freeze for half a second with the line unfinished, as if Mars sat inside a corner of blood-colored light.

Solenne saw only the live room. The softbox glare on the board. Her thumbnail stained red near the cuticle. The small chip in the desk where her brother had once carved a triangle with a compass point while waiting for her to finish grading.

“Maybe someone tells us.” She turned back to the camera. “A parent. A guard. A doctor. A director in a glass room. They say: Do not touch. That is dangerous. That is not a door. That is not for you.”

The comments flickered faster. She let them run.

“Maybe there is a lock we can test.”

She tapped the marker cap against the board, near the drawn handle she had not yet made.

“Maybe there is no lock at all. Maybe there is only a warning sign placed by people who are frightened of what happens if the room empties.”

She paused for the language delay, though the stream was distributed through mirrors now and delay meant little. In her first classroom, she had learned to pause after questions because some children needed to move the answer from picture to mouth. Adults needed the same grace and called it reflection.

Her brother had been twenty-one when he died. Not a child. Still, in the intake record they had written subject resisted meal prompt and subject verbalized door imagery. He had left his shoes under the bed in the flat he shared with two other apprentices. One shoe upright, one fallen on its side. Solenne had kept them for three months beside her own door because returning them to his room made him finished.

She lifted the marker again.

“Here is the public sentence: We do not know what the Boundary means.”

She wrote the sentence on the board beneath Mars. The old teacher habits stayed in her wrist: large letters, clear spacing.

“Here is the institutional sentence: raw data may cause harm.”

Below it, she wrote: HARM.

“Here is the sentence they punish: if they cannot prove the door is locked, why do they punish us for touching the handle?”

The comment field pulsed. The phrase returned in different languages, auto-translated badly in the margin. TOUCHING THE HANDLE. TOUCH THE HANDLE. HANDLE IS MERCY. MARTE PUERTA. DOOR AROUND MARS.

Solenne should have stopped there. The safety script from the quieter circle said to end after the question, before proof, before hunger became instruction. She had written that rule herself after a boy in Rotterdam sent her a photograph of a hospital window and asked whether glass counted if you opened it from the inside.

But proof was what kept the frightened in the room. Proof, or the shape of it.

She clicked the desk control.

The Mars feed shrank to the upper corner. The last clean public frame of Asterion appeared, softened by compression. Leila Haddad’s hand near the orange peel. Jun Park’s shoulder. A loose strap at the edge. Mars, small and almost polite in the window.

Solenne did not look at the orange peel. If she looked at the orange peel, she heard her old classroom reciting the colors of planets in six languages, small voices making Mars into a destination before any of them knew adults could sell a destination as a promise and then lose the road.

“You have all seen this frame,” she said. “They call it clean.”

She tapped the audio icon.

Elias Venn laughed off-screen.

It was a small laugh, warm and unprepared. Not triumph. Not terror. A man in a cabin with people he knew, caught before history reached in. The sound carried a faint background hum and a tiny click where the public feed had been trimmed for school distribution.

Solenne let it play once.

Then again.

Then she stopped it before the signal clip.

“No screaming,” she said.

A message burst through before the filters caught it: RELEASE LAUGH.

“No panic in that second. No begging to be spared.”

She stepped away from the board so the door around Mars and Elias’s paused waveform could share the screen. The editing software had placed the waveform under the planet like a low fence.

“I am not telling you what he saw. I was not on Asterion.” Her voice thinned on the ship name, and she corrected it, gentler. “I am asking what they are allowed to call evidence when it closes a path, and what we are allowed to call evidence when it opens one.”

The comment field filled with Elias’s name.

ELIAS KNEW.
DR VENN LAUGHED.
HE SAW THE DOOR.
HE WAS ALREADY FREE.
NOT JUN, ELIAS TOO.
THE DOCTOR LAUGHED BEFORE RELEASE.

Solenne felt the old irritation, sharp and useful. They always mixed the laughs. Jun’s duplicated cockpit laugh was later, corrupted, dangerous even by their own standards. Elias’s was public, soft, pre-clip. Different evidence. Different wound. If the dead were going to teach, they deserved correct attribution.

She almost said that.

Instead she clicked to the second panel.

This one carried the standard Continuity caution strip across the bottom: UNVERIFIED DERIVATIVE. DO NOT DISTRIBUTE WITHOUT CONTEXT. The strip made people lean closer. Prohibition gave the artifact a border, and borders made things look holy.

A thinner laugh crackled through. Jun Park this time, duplicated from a cockpit record no one outside the restricted channels was supposed to have in any clean form. In this cut, the laugh arrived after a black bar of timestamp, after a caption: STRUCTURAL FAILURE +00:07. The sound was too bright. Almost a bark. Then the clip ended.

The phrase that belonged after it was missing.

Solenne knew it was missing. The first time the file had reached her, a voice, maybe Jun, maybe not, had said, “That’s not sky.” The circle archivist argued the words were a fear artifact added by Continuity Office poisoners. Solenne had listened in headphones for an hour, bread going hard on a plate beside her, and heard fear in the gap either way.

She had cut the phrase herself.

For three breaths she could still undo it. She could click the longer version. She could teach properly. Evidence includes the parts that damage your argument. Children knew that before adults paid them to forget.

The stream counter doubled.

She folded her hands on the desk.

“They tell you context,” she said, and the word came out with more heat than she wanted. She softened it. “Context is useful. A teacher loves context. A doctor loves context. A court loves context when it has time.”

She touched the printed attendance sheet beside her, blank except for a list of first names submitted before the stream. Alina. Ben. Cyrus. Devi. Someone had typed ELIAS in the ninth slot.

“But context is also how locked rooms stay locked. There is always another review. Another panel. Another official who says the sentence is not safe unless he stands beside it and tells you what you may do with it.”

The red marker rolled toward the desk edge. She stopped it with two fingers.

“Here is what I know. A dead man laughed before they clipped the world. Another dead man laughed where no one living should have had breath. They tell us neither laugh opens anything.”

She drew the handle inside the red rectangle.

“Then show us the lock.”

Witness review room, restricted feed copy 10.2.11, ship time 22:48.

Mara Venn watched Solenne Kade’s hand close around the red marker as if around a valve.

The review room had no window. Witness kept its public-facing screens narrow and matte, bracketed by restraint rails that had been polished where fingers found them during hard updates from Earth. A ration pouch of mandarin slices was caught under the table strap near Mara’s knee, unopened, the syrup settled in one bright seam.

Malik had routed the stream through the isolation buffer after Continuity flagged it. He stood behind the comms console with one shoulder against the wall, not sitting. Sera sat with her sleeves pulled over her wrists, raw dataset windows minimized behind the intercept. Nadine occupied the end rail, one hand hooked, thumb still.

Pavel appeared in the Quito/Houston relay box with the mild delay that made every expression look rehearsed.

On the screen, Solenne’s classroom patience continued to do its work.

Mara touched the inside of her wrist and found the pulse too fast.

“Pause on the waveform,” she said.

Malik froze the stream. Elias’s laugh sat in blue amplitude under Mars. Public-feed compression had flattened the breath before it. Someone had boosted the midrange until his warmth became invitation.

“That is not a final record,” Mara said. “It is the public feed. Mission day one-one-eight. Before the clip.”

“We know,” Sera said.

“They are pairing it with Jun’s derivative.”

“We also know.”

Mara heard the correction inside Sera’s brevity: knowledge without intervention was not enough to stop distribution. The file had already crossed too many mirrors. Suppressing the source stream only gave the copies a story to wear.

Pavel removed his glasses. On the relay, the gesture arrived half a second after his hand began to move.

“Continuity Office has issued takedown notices on the primary channel and seven high-volume mirrors.”

Malik gave a dry laugh with no humor in it. “Seven.”

“Twenty-nine now,” Sera said. She had reopened a side feed. “No. Thirty-four.”

Nadine leaned forward. “Can we poison the clip?”

Malik looked at her.

“Insert correction payloads,” Nadine said. “Metadata, not theater.”

“We can attach labels on platforms that honor the protocol. The rest strip them.” Malik’s fingers hovered over the console, moving only when a new mirror reached the threshold. “Some copies are already burned into reaction streams. People talking over it. Teachers. Priests. A woman selling black glass pendants. Two prison feeds.”

Mara kept her eyes on Elias’s waveform. The laugh was small. Smaller than the use being made of it. He had laughed because Amara had threatened to resign if the orange peel became historic. He had laughed before the ship was hurt in any way the public could see. It had been ordinary, which was why it killed her now. Ordinary things were easy to steal. They carried no armor.

“Play her next sentence,” Pavel said.

Malik looked to Nadine.

Nadine nodded once.

The stream resumed.

Solenne had turned sideways, teacher’s profile, hair pinned with a dull metal clip. Her voice remained low enough that listeners had to meet it.

“If a child asks whether pain counts if this is made,” she said, “what do they answer?”

Mara’s hand closed around the rail.

“If a patient says I want to go where the astronauts went, what do they answer?”

In the comments, someone posted a door emoji around a red circle. Then a cascade. Door, red circle, door. Touching the handle.

Solenne’s mouth trembled and settled. It happened fast. The camera caught it because cameras loved weakness when it could be sold as conviction.

“They answer with bread. With pills. With locked cabinets. With kind nurses who take your shoelaces because somebody made a graph.”

Pavel’s eyes lowered. Mara knew he would be seeing a report. A table. Manila, Dublin, São Paulo, juvenile ward, adult ward, unclassified domestic cluster. He had built a public language out of those tables and called it a firebreak.

Solenne put both palms on the desk. “I taught in a Red Nursery. I taught children to draw Mars habitats with breakfast gardens and dust seals. We told them the red planet was waiting. We put it on walls before they could spell atmosphere. And when the wall appeared, we told them patience.”

The word wall brought a tremor through the comments.

“They are asking us to wait in a room they cannot prove is real, for a future they already sold once.”

Sera whispered, “No.”

Mara glanced at her.

“That is the hinge,” Sera said. “That sentence. Clip writers will cut everything before it.”

They did.

Malik’s side monitors populated with short edits. Solenne’s face duplicated into panels, each with different captions.

THE ROOM THEY CANNOT PROVE IS REAL.
THE FUTURE THEY SOLD.
SHOW US THE LOCK.
ELIAS LAUGHED.
TOUCHING THE HANDLE.

The Continuity Office suppression banner hit the original stream. Its main window went gray.

BROADCAST INTERRUPTED UNDER BOUNDARY DELAY PROTOCOL 8: SELF-HARM RECRUITMENT RISK. IF YOU ARE WATCHING FROM A WARD OR SCHOOL SETTING, CONTACT YOUR MODERATOR. WATER. FOOD. NAME. WEATHER.

For one full second the review room held only fan noise.

Then the copies multiplied.

Malik exhaled through his teeth. “Primary down. Mirrors climbing. The interruption banner is now part of the edit.”

On screen, in the nearest mirror, Solenne reappeared because someone had captured the stream thirty seconds behind.

She was smiling sadly at the interruption she had expected.

“If they stop this feed,” recorded Solenne said, “ask why a locked door needs guards outside it.”

Mara stood. The restraint strap caught her thigh and snapped back against the table. The mandarin pouch shifted, bright syrup climbing its plastic corner.

“No,” she said.

No one answered.

“That laugh has no context in their use. None. It is a laugh before the event, stripped from a crew-health segment, paired with a corrupted derivative from another crew member and made to say release. Elias is not saying release. Jun is not saying release. They are not saying anything to her.”

Pavel’s face remained still in the relay box. “Doctor Venn.”

“No.”

“Context rarely survives distribution.”

“Then distribution is damage.”

“Yes,” Pavel said. “That has been the Office position since Manila.”

Mara turned toward the relay. “Do not use Manila as a handrail every time you want silence.”

His mouth tightened at the corner. The delay made it arrive late enough to seem like pain before discipline covered it.

“Seventy-one confirmed dead in the first wave,” Pavel said. “More under disputed classification. I will use the number as many times as the room requires.”

“The number does not make Elias hers.”

“No,” Pavel said. “It makes him dangerous when he leaves you.”

The review room changed around that sentence. Malik stopped typing. Sera looked at the black edge of her monitor. Nadine’s hand stayed on the rail, but her knuckles went pale.

Mara felt the blood in her wrist under her fingers because at some point she had touched it again.

Pavel replaced his glasses. “I am not saying this to wound you.”

“Your motive does not clean the sentence.”

“Agreed.”

That surprised her. The agreement struck harder than defense would have.

On the mirrored stream, Solenne had begun answering comments as if calling on students.

“Yes, Aram, that is the public feed. Good listening.”

Her voice brightened by a degree, trained praise. Mara hated the care in it.

“No, L, I am not telling you to hurt yourself. I am asking why they get to call your wish illness before proving the room is not a waiting room.”

Sera leaned forward. “She knows exactly where the legal line is.”

“She taught children,” Mara said.

The words came out wrong. Too soft. They sounded like defense.

Nadine heard it. Her eyes moved to Mara, then back to the screen.

Solenne read another comment.

“If you are frightened, put your feet on the floor if you have one. If you are in bed, put your hand on the sheet. We are not rushing. We are learning the handle.”

Mara tasted mandarin though she had opened nothing.

“Cut the sound,” Nadine said.

Malik muted the room feed.

The subtitles kept scrolling.

THEY SAY DEATH IS FINAL BUT THEY CANNOT SHOW THE LOCK.
THEY SAY STAY FOR THE LIVING BUT THEY EDIT THE DEAD FIRST.
THEY USED THE ASTRONAUTS TO SELL MARS. NOW THEY HIDE WHERE MARS GOES.

Mara read the last line twice. The letters stayed where they were. No Boundary distortion. No timestamp contradiction. Human editing. Human hunger. Enough.

A red priority frame opened over Malik’s console.

“St. Odran’s?” Mara asked before he could speak.

“Not Dublin.” Malik swallowed. “North American hospital network. Pediatric and oncology mixed campus. Their moderator left a Glass Room feed open after the advisory assembly. The stream got through a parent channel.”

Pavel turned away from someone outside his camera. When he faced them again, his voice had lost the hearing-room polish.

“Preliminary only.”

“Say it,” Sera said.

He looked at her through the relay.

She held his gaze. “If you are going to classify using the dead, say the living.”

Pavel read from an incoming sheet. “Twelve attempts reported across two buildings. Five fatalities unconfirmed. Three confirmed resuscitations. Seven missing from supervised care.” He stopped. His eyes moved once across the unseen page. “Door symbols drawn on two ward windows and one Mars education display.”

Mara sat down because her knees had bent without permission.

The muted stream continued. Solenne’s face in a dozen copies, classroom-soft, asking another question the room could no longer hear.

Malik’s console filled with hospital advisories. WATER. FOOD. NAME. WEATHER. REMOVE MARS FEEDS FROM PEDIATRIC COMMON AREAS. SECURE WINDOW ACCESS. DO NOT ARGUE METAPHYSICS DURING ACUTE PHASE. OFFER JAM OR BUTTER. RECORD EXACT PHRASES.

Nadine said, “Archive every copy with source chain where possible. Separate Elias public-feed usage from Jun derivative in the incident packet.”

Mara looked up.

Nadine’s voice remained flat. “They will be separated now, or they will be the same by morning.”

Sera nodded once. “Mark Elias laugh: public, pre-clip, ordinary context available. Mark Jun derivative: corrupted cockpit record, phrase omitted, release misuse.”

“Phrase?” Pavel asked.

Sera did not answer immediately.

Mara did. “That’s not sky.”

The relay room went very still around Pavel.

He said, “That phrase remains restricted.”

“It is already present by omission,” Mara said. Her voice sounded close and dry to her own ear. “They cut around it. The absence is working.”

Pavel pressed his thumb and forefinger against the bridge of his nose. “We cannot publish that raw.”

“I am not asking raw.”

“What are you asking?”

Mara looked at the paused monitor where Solenne had drawn the red door around Mars. A child, or an adult speaking in a child’s need, had added in the comments: IF HANDLE THEN ROOM.

She had trained crews to treat fear as material. Solenne had learned the method without permission. Name the fear. Give the body an action. Make the unbearable into a sequence: feet, sheet, handle.

Mara’s hunger for Elias’s final words moved in her like a hand reaching toward an unlocked cabinet. If one ordinary laugh could do this, what would a private sentence do? What would the porch light become in Solenne’s mouth, clipped into mercy, sold as directions?

She removed her fingers from her wrist.

“Incident classification,” she said. “Lethal fragment criteria. Two laughs, separated. Missing context named without raw release. Door symbol flagged. Phrase ‘touching the handle’ added to ward prompt sheets.”

Pavel studied her. “You are drafting policy in shock.”

“Yes.”

Nadine said, “She is also right.”

Malik’s hands resumed moving. “I can build the incident shell.”

“Do it,” Nadine said.

On the muted stream, Solenne Kade lifted the red marker one more time. She drew a small line through the handle, a teacher correcting a letter.

The copies stuttered at different points. In one, her hand never reached the board. In another, the door closed cleanly around Mars. In a third, the frame froze with the marker touching the handle, red point pressed to white, while the comments kept climbing over her still face.
