Chapter 9: Sofia’s Hatch

Asterion command log, mission day 118. Command module internal record. Source composite from CM voice bus, pilot station camera, and command private notation. Integrity warning: two timestamp runs occupy the same thirty-seven-second interval with non-identical star-tracker states. Analyst caution: manual-input record exists in both runs but with different authorization latency. Do not treat sequence as settled.

00:00:00 / LOW-COMMUNICATION INTERVAL BEGINS

Sofia Arendt braced one knee under the command rail and watched Star Tracker C disagree with the universe.

It had disagreed before. Instruments had moods if one had served long enough beside them, though Sofia never permitted that sentence into mission language. A lens iced. A chip warmed half a degree past its curve. A software patch found its own shame in public. The instrument erred, the crew named the error, the procedure received it, and the ship continued.

This reading arrived with manners.

Tracker A held Mars at expected bearing within tolerance. Tracker B lagged by a small, ugly fraction. Tracker C returned the same wrong offset for the third cycle, clean as a signed form. Then it corrected itself by showing a field Sofia had seen eleven minutes earlier.

“C repeat confirmed,” Jun said. His voice had the bright edge it took when he wanted his hands before the rules arrived. “Same stars. Same centroid. Wrong time.”

“Log as tracker disagreement pending procedure entry,” Sofia said.

“That’s not a disagreement.”

“It is until the procedure says otherwise.”

Jun had one foot hooked through the pilot stirrup. His left hand hovered near the manual trim guard without touching it, which meant he had already put himself there twice in his head.

Amara’s diagnostic panel threw a white code, then stripped the code back to green. She slapped the housing with two fingers, not hard enough to count as repair. “Panel twelve telemetry just called a pressure spike and then apologized.”

“Language,” Sofia said.

“I am using language. The panel lied.”

“Say it as record.”

“Panel twelve reported transient pressure increase in the avionics bay. No matching mechanical reading. No thermal. No strain. It withdrew the event from active fault tree after two seconds.”

“That is record.”

“That is also lying.”

Elias floated above the medical station with his knees tucked under him, a hydration pouch stuck by velcro to his sleeve. He had been timing Leila’s pulse before the low-comm interval began because she had said her hands were cold, and then the public feed had cut to the last scheduled crew-health segment, and then all of them had become six people inside a machine again.

“Anyone else tasting metal?” he asked.

“No,” Sofia said.

Mateo said, “Maybe.”

Leila, from the biology rack, said, “No. My sample lamps flickered.”

“Which answer do you want first?” Elias asked.

“Operational,” Sofia said.

He clipped his pouch back to the med rail. “Mateo reports metallic taste. One person, no corroborating symptoms. Leila reports sample-lamp flicker. I have no crew-med fault.”

Sofia marked it. The stylus trembled once against the tablet from a vibration the command chair had not reported.

She lowered her voice. “Asterion command to crew. We do not name a thing before it enters procedure. We have tracker disagreement, a withdrawn pressure report, and uncorroborated crew sensations. We have a scheduled course correction in fourteen minutes. We hold language to observable state.”

Jun made a sound through his nose. “Observable state says the ship is trying to rotate around a sky that keeps repeating.”

“Park.”

“That is pilot record.”

“It is not.”

He turned in the stirrup. His face was lit from below by a nav display that painted him in green, then amber, then green again. “Sofia, if C is repeating and B is lagging, the model is stale. If the model is stale, auto-correction will burn us clean into its own math.”

“Flight dynamics sent the update forty-one minutes ago.”

“Forty-one minutes before the sky began copying itself.”

Mateo’s hand moved toward the sample pouch at his chest. The small Atacama stone clicked against the clasp, a dry little sound in the filtered cabin. Sofia heard it too clearly. She preferred alarms. Alarms had shape.

“Jun,” Elias said, “hands report?”

“My hands report that the attitude platform is honest and the nav solution is not.”

“Medical translation?”

“He wants to fly,” Amara said.

“I want to stop the autopilot from trusting a picture of last Tuesday.”

“It is eleven minutes ago,” Leila said.

Jun pointed at her with his chin. “Thank you. Correct, useful, worse.”

Sofia shifted the command tablet to her left thigh. Asterion’s low-comm interval meant Earth received less, later, and with public buffers no one aboard pretended were only technical. The mission had sold intimacy to five billion watchers and privacy to the crew in carefully rationed grams. Here, inside the gap, command became older. Metal. Breath. The rail under her palm.

Years before, two hundred meters under the Barents, the battery room alarm had started as a sweetness in the air. Not smoke first. Sweetness. Then heat. Then the young electrician in the aft compartment saying her name too formally because she was already calculating hatch weight and pressure and burn spread. Sofia had sealed the hatch with two sailors behind it because the vessel lived if the compartment died. The report called it decisive. Her hands had smelled of insulation for three days after rescue, though she had worn gloves.

The hatch memory came without invitation. She returned to Asterion by reading the panel names aloud in silence. Guidance. Attitude. Thermal. Pressure. Crew.

A command note blinked for acknowledgment. AUTOMATED COURSE CORRECTION ARMED. CONFIRM PRIMARY STAR SOLUTION.

“Manual tactile correction,” Jun said.

“No.”

“Limited. Two-axis trim only. I can damp the drift before auto-burn takes the wrong reference.”

“No.”

“You haven’t heard the proposal.”

“I heard it eleven minutes ago, before you said it.”

Amara reached across her station and pulled a diagnostic band tighter around a cable trunk. The cable did not need it. Her pencil nub had escaped from behind her ear and floated near the vent intake until Mateo caught it and pushed it back toward her.

“Commander,” Amara said, “the star tracker is not the only bad witness.”

“Continue.”

“Panel twelve withdrew a pressure fault. The hull microphones along bay two carry a scrape I cannot place. Very short. Like a panel edge tearing.”

“Tearing?”

“Not flexing. Tearing.”

Sofia opened the structural overlay. Bay two showed nominal load. Bay two always showed nominal load until it did not; all vessels lied first in green.

“Source?”

“Three sensors, same sound. No strain match.”

Jun said, “There. We trim.”

Amara turned on him. “No, pilot. A panel tears, you do not cure it by touching the stick because your wrists are famous.”

“My wrists landed Tycho.”

“Your wrists landed a machine in dust. This is not dust. This is metal saying something stupid.”

“Metal doesn’t speak.”

“Then stop pretending your hands listen.”

“Enough,” Sofia said.

The word was soft. It carried. Elias’s fingers paused over the med-kit latch. Leila stopped wrapping elastic around a vial. Mateo tucked the pencil against Amara’s workstation and removed his hand as if returning a relic to an altar he distrusted.

Sofia hated that thought as soon as it formed. Altar. No. Workstation.

She opened Procedure G-118-L, Navigation Confidence Loss During Scheduled Burn. It had been written by people who trusted disagreement to arrive in categories. Sensor fault. Clock drift. Star occultation. Software divergence. Human visual confirmation. No line for a sky repeating politely while a panel reported an injury and retracted it.

“We are in G-118-L,” she said. “Step one: isolate suspect sensor.”

Jun’s jaw moved. He chewed the inside of his cheek until a pale mark appeared. “C isolated already.”

“Step two: compare inertial platform to A and B.”

“I have. Platform agrees with my hands, not with B.”

“Hands are not listed.”

“They should be.”

“Bring that to review after Mars orbit insertion.”

Elias laughed once, too early. It hit the cabin and died there. He lifted both palms. “Sorry. Bad joke window.”

Sofia kept her eyes on Jun. “Report inertial comparison.”

Jun looked back at the panel. “Inertial platform shows micro-drift pitch negative point zero six, yaw positive point zero three. A sees it. B delays it. C repeats old sky then agrees with A after changing the star field.”

“Changing how?”

“It stops being the stars it was using.”

Leila’s lamp flickered again. This time the video caught it: a thin pulse across the sample tray, then normal light. The vials stayed sealed. Inside tray four, the culture medium trembled in a line not aligned with ship vibration.

Leila said, “I have a biological tray event.”

Elias pushed off toward her. “Define event.”

“Fluid displacement without rack motion. No leak.” She touched the tray’s side with one gloved finger. “Temperature stable.”

Amara’s panel stuttered white. For less than a second, bay two structural view showed an interior outline that did not belong to bay two. Ladder rungs, a rounded hatch, handhold patches rubbed dull by use.

Sofia saw it before it vanished.

Her throat closed around old air.

The submarine battery room had a round hatch, too heavy after heat warped the frame. The two sailors had been on the other side, one breathing, one already quiet. The chief had said, Wait. Sofia had said, Seal. She had lowered her voice then because command that had to be repeated became plea.

“Did anyone else see that?” Mateo asked.

“Describe,” Sofia said.

“A hatch,” he said. “Maybe. Not ours.”

Amara had both hands on her panel now, fingers spread as if holding the display down. “Bay two just returned geometry from nowhere.”

“From archive?”

“No archive uses that hardware.”

Jun said, “We trim now.”

“No.”

“If auto-burn starts off stale nav, we are passengers.”

“If you input manual correction during unclassified geometry return, you may become cause.”

He stared at her. There it was. Cause. The word had entered the cabin without procedure. Once released, it altered the air.

Elias moved from Leila to the center rail. “Sofia.”

“Medical?”

“No acute crew impairment.”

“Then keep medical.”

“That was not medical.”

“Then hold it.”

He held the rail. His long fingers bent around it, one crooked from old injury. He looked younger in the monitor light and older in the mouth. “You are making a clean box around a dirty thing.”

“I am keeping command.”

“No argument.”

“Then stop.”

“I’m asking if the box has air in it.”

Jun slapped the manual trim guard with the heel of his hand, still not opening it. The sound made Leila flinch. She recovered before anyone could name it.

“Commander,” Jun said. “Give me two seconds. Rate limit point zero one. No burn. No translation. I bias the platform toward A and inertial, away from B. If it worsens, you cut me out.”

“Manual correction before scheduled burn requires fault isolation and command concurrence.”

“You have both.”

“I have neither.”

“You have a ship reporting an old sky, a fake pressure spike, a ghost hatch, and a tearing sound. That is fault enough.”

“Ghost is not record,” Amara snapped. “Say display artifact.”

Mateo said, “It looked like a submarine hatch.”

Silence.

Sofia kept her eyes on the command screen. The others had learned her biography from public education packets, from memorial profiles written before anyone died, from the mission’s appetite for useful wounds. Former submarine officer. Battery fire. Saved eighty-six. Two lost. They knew the official sentence. They had never heard the hatch wheel under her palms.

“Mateo,” she said. “Record: anomalous interior geometry, non-Asterion, observed by payload specialist and systems engineer for less than one second.”

Amara corrected, “Observed by systems station display. My eyes are not instrumentation.”

“Accepted.”

Jun bent closer to the attitude display. “Drift increasing.”

“Rate.”

“Pitch negative point zero nine. Yaw positive point zero five. Roll now active, small.”

“B?”

“B still late.”

“C?”

“C agrees with A for one cycle.” He swallowed. “Now C shows Mars in a place Mars is not.”

The forward display remained off during low-comm burn prep; still, the small navigation render showed the red disk like a drop of blood under glass. It sat inside a grid Sofia had trusted for years. The grid updated. Mars jumped half a degree and returned. No warning flag followed. No system admitted the insult.

Leila spoke from the biology rack. “Sample tray four shows surface tension changes. Not temperature, not vibration.”

Elias said, “Any breach?”

“No.”

“Any organism exposure?”

“No.”

“Then breathe.”

“I am breathing.”

“I know. Keep doing it to annoy me.”

Her mouth tightened, not quite a smile.

Sofia scrolled G-118-L to discretionary command actions. Every procedure had a mercy valve if one knew where to look. Discretionary language, the most dangerous engineering ever built: commander may authorize limited manual control input to maintain attitude stability when automated guidance confidence falls below mission margin and delay prevents ground concurrence.

The confidence score sat at 0.81. Mission margin required 0.79 before manual authority.

Jun saw it. “We wait for two hundredths?”

“We wait for state.”

“The state is here.”

Amara said, “The panel edge tore.”

“Confirmed?”

“No.”

“Then say suspected.”

“Suspected panel-edge tear in bay two. Happy?”

“No.”

“Good.”

Another white pulse crossed the structural overlay. This time the geometry returned as a corridor. Not submarine. Asterion? The shape bent wrong, as if a deck plan had been folded and asked to remain polite. The hatch outline sat at the corridor end, round again, then square, then gone.

The ship made a sound through its bones.

No alarm.

Asterion’s procedures were full of alarms. They had trained for hull breach, fire, toxic leak, decompression, medical incapacitation, guidance fault, communications loss, solar event, crew conflict, sudden grief on public feed. Sofia had signed off on each drill and added three of her own because public heroics killed crews in private. The ship knew how to scream for ordinary danger.

This time the cabin listened to a scrape and received no red light.

Sofia lowered her voice further. “Park. Limited manual correction authorized.”

Jun’s hand was already on the guard.

“Wait for order.”

His fingers froze.

“Parameters: two-axis attitude trim only. Rate limit point zero zero five. Duration two seconds maximum per input. No translation. No burn commit. Amara, monitor bay two structure and flight bus.”

“Monitoring.”

“Elias, crew-med watch and verbal interrupt if you see impairment.”

“I’ll be rude.”

“Leila, secure biological rack.”

“Securing.”

“Mateo, hands off sample pouch, eyes on auxiliary camera four.”

Mateo’s hand left the stone. “Aux four.”

Sofia opened the authorization window. It asked for command biometric. Her thumbprint registered on the second press. The first press left a faint crescent of moisture on the pad.

MANUAL ATTITUDE CORRECTION: LIMITED
COMMANDER AUTH: ARENDT, S.
PILOT INPUT: PARK, J.
AUTO-BURN: HELD
GROUND CONCURRENCE: DELAYED / UNAVAILABLE

The record chimed once.

Jun opened the guard. “Taking manual trim.”

“State before input,” Sofia said.

“Pitch negative point one one. Yaw positive point zero six. Roll point zero two and ugly. Inertial still smooth. A agrees. B late. C is garbage.”

“Kill poetry,” Amara said.

“C is invalid.”

“Better.”

Jun’s thumb moved.

For two seconds nothing in the cabin changed except the small tendons at his wrist.

“Input complete,” he said.

The attitude error fell by half.

Mateo exhaled too loudly. Elias snapped two fingers toward him. “Again. Slower.”

Mateo nodded and breathed through his nose.

“Second input?” Jun asked.

“Report.”

“Pitch negative point zero five. Yaw point zero two. Roll zero. C still invalid. B catching up.”

Sofia watched the confidence score climb to 0.83, then blink down to 0.78.

The manual correction log duplicated on her screen.

Two entries. Same timestamp. Different duration.

PILOT INPUT: 01.96 SEC.
PILOT INPUT: 04.21 SEC.

“Amara.”

“I see it.”

“Which is true?”

“My bus says one point nine six. Flight archive says four point two one.”

Jun pulled his hand back from the control as if the panel had heated. “I gave two seconds.”

“Park, hand visible.”

He lifted both hands.

Sofia marked the visual. The pilot’s hands were open, fingers spread. Camera caught them. Record caught them, unless record had already begun to choose favorites.

Asterion shuddered. Small. The kind of motion a crew learned to ignore because every joint and pump had its own grammar. This one crossed grammar. It traveled through the rail into Sofia’s palm, paused, returned from the other side.

Leila’s rack chimed yellow. Elias moved.

“Tray four vascular analogues rupturing,” Leila said. “No heat signature.”

“They are cultures,” Elias said, close now. “No person.”

“I know what they are.”

“Good. Say it anyway.”

“They are cultures. They are data.”

Amara’s voice cut across them. “Bay two panel confirmed torn. Microfracture along fastener row. No impact source. No debris strike. No pressure differential.”

“Containment?”

“Holding.”

“Repair?”

“Not during burn prep. It is not through-hull.” She took a breath through her teeth. “It tore like wet paper.”

Sofia heard the anger under it. Amara was angrier at the panel than at fear. A material failure deserved a material accusation, and the ship had denied her a culprit.

“Manual correction achieved partial attitude recovery,” Sofia said for the log. “Automated burn remains held pending revised confidence.”

Jun’s laugh came sharp and wrong. “Pending revised confidence. There’s a sentence.”

“Park.”

“I’m here.”

“Are you impaired?”

“No. Angry.”

“Anger is not a control mode.”

“Neither is waiting for the ship to invent more hatches.”

Mateo, still at aux four, said, “Commander, camera four has Mars steady.”

“Good.”

“No. I mean steady while nav render jumps.”

Sofia brought aux four to her panel. Mars stood in the window, red and clean, beyond the dark edge of Asterion’s own hull. The camera did not know what the star trackers knew. Or it refused. The planet remained there with terrible administrative calm.

Elias drifted back from Leila’s rack. “Crew check. Leila steady. Mateo breathing. Amara angry but accurate. Jun angry and wants that counted as accuracy.”

“Doctor,” Jun said.

“I’m being generous.”

“And you?”

Sofia had asked it before she knew she would. Elias looked at her, then at the med rail, where his hydration pouch still clung by one corner.

“I forgot to drink,” he said.

“Fix it.”

“Yes, Commander.”

He took the pouch and drank. The cabin watched him because it was easier than watching Mars.

The command tablet flashed for log continuation. Sofia opened a private command note, sealed by default until post-mission review. She had written such notes after training accidents, after crew conflict drills, after a coolant leak during assembly that the public office had called a minor supply-chain delay. Command notes carried a different duty. They did not soothe. They preserved the shape of decisions before committees sanded them into inevitability.

Her thumb hovered.

The submarine report had asked whether sealing the hatch was the only available action. She had answered yes. The word had passed through inquiry, commendation, lecture circuit, candidate screening. Years had polished it. Yes became clean. The hatch wheel had never agreed.

Asterion rolled point zero one degrees starboard without command.

“Jun?”

“Not me.”

“Amara?”

“Flight bus registers no input.”

“Source?”

“No source.” Amara bent closer. “No, wait. It attributes it to manual correction residual.”

Jun swore.

Sofia looked at the duplicate manual log. One point nine six seconds. Four point two one. A correction he had made and not made. A residual that could become blame. A ship that had moved and then filed the motion under his hands.

“Command note,” she said aloud, because private notes begun in silence could later be accused of invention. “Arendt. Sofia. Mission day 118, low-communication interval, scheduled course-correction window.”

The recorder indicator lit.

Jun turned toward her. “What are you doing?”

“Record custody.”

“We have live fault.”

“We have future fault too.”

Amara muttered, “That one may kill slower.”

Sofia accepted the sentence by refusing to correct it. She held the tablet near the command mic.

“Automated navigation confidence degraded during scheduled burn preparation. Star Tracker C produced repeat field and invalid Mars solution. Panel twelve reported and withdrew transient pressure event. Bay two structural sensors now show suspected panel tear, later confirmed microfracture along fastener row without known impact source. Biological tray four shows rupture event without heat signature. Manual attitude correction was authorized by command under discretionary clause G-118-L, limited to two-axis trim, rate limit point zero zero five, maximum two seconds per input. Pilot Park reports input within authorization. Command visual confirms hands clear after input. Flight archive now contains duplicated manual-input durations. This command note states that the duplicated duration cannot be treated as pilot action without independent verification.”

Jun’s mouth opened, then closed.

Sofia continued. Her voice went lower. The crew leaned in despite themselves.

“If later review seeks a clean causal chain, this record objects. Procedure received the event after the event had already damaged procedure. Manual correction may have occurred before, during, or inside data corruption. It is a fact of command authorization. It is not, from present evidence, a sufficient cause for subsequent anomaly.”

She stopped.

The cabin hummed. A pump cycled somewhere behind the wall. The little noises of life-support went on doing their jobs without reverence.

“Subsequent anomaly,” Amara said.

“Accepted wording for unclassified state.”

“No. The panel tore. The tray ruptured. The sky repeated. Those are words.”

Sofia looked at her. “Give me the sentence.”

Amara pushed off her station and caught the rail harder than needed. Her pencil nub floated away again. No one reached for it.

“The ship is being hurt,” she said. “We do not know by what.”

Sofia entered the line.

Jun stared at the manual trim guard. “That note protects me.”

“It protects the record.”

“Same thing today.”

“Perhaps.”

He turned back to the panel. “Attitude error rising again.”

“Rate.”

“Pitch negative point zero seven. Yaw point zero four. Roll stable. B agrees now. That is new.”

“C?”

Jun’s hands remained visible above the console. He read without touching anything. “C shows a field I cannot identify.”

“Stars?”

“Looks like stars.”

“Identify.”

“I’m trying.”

Mateo whispered, “Aux four still has Mars.”

Leila said something too low for the command mic. Sofia heard only the breath at the front of it, the private shape of a prayer or calculation. She let it remain private.

The command screen asked whether to resume automated course correction.

YES / NO / HOLD

Three boxes. A clean interface for a dirty thing.

The confidence score climbed to 0.80.

“Ground packet not available for eight minutes,” Mateo said.

“They’ll get the note?” Jun asked.

“If archive holds,” Amara said.

Elias finished his hydration pouch and crushed it flat between both palms. “Sofia, crew can continue. That is medical, not comfort.”

“Logged.”

“Also, I want everyone to eat after burn.”

Jun gave him a look. “If we have an after burn, you can prescribe soup.”

“Electrolyte broth. Soup has hope in it.”

“Doctor Venn,” Sofia said.

“Medical morale contamination withdrawn.”

The joke landed unevenly, but it landed. Mateo’s shoulders dropped a fraction. Leila secured the last vial with elastic twice. Amara caught her pencil out of the air and placed it behind her ear, where it immediately began to drift loose.

Sofia selected HOLD.

The box turned amber.

Asterion accepted the command. For one clean second, every major system displayed state rather than argument.

Then the private command note duplicated.

Two copies. Same timestamp. Same words until the final line.

COPY A: The ship is being hurt. We do not know by what.

COPY B: The ship is being hurt. We do not know by whom.

Sofia stared at the screen.

She had not said whom. No one had. The cabin audio, if it could still be trusted, would prove that. Or fail to prove it. Or carry both.

Jun saw it next. His face lost its pilot impatience and became, briefly, the face of a man waiting for a door to open on the wrong deck.

Amara’s voice came from very close. “Commander.”

Sofia reached for the log field. Her fingers met glass warmed by the ship, ordinary heat under impossible text.

“Seal both copies,” she said.

“Classification?” Mateo asked.

Sofia watched the altered word hold steady on the screen. The amber HOLD command pulsed beside it, patient as a hatch light.

“Command restricted,” she said. “Manual correction file attached. No public feed derivative.”

“For what reason?” Mateo asked, because he was payload and education and record, because later children would be given verbs chosen by adults in rooms without windows.

Sofia lowered her voice until the ship seemed to quiet around it.

“Because someone will want a person to fit inside that word.”
