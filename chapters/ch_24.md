## Chapter 24: Nearer Stars

Ruth Venn opened the private packet at her kitchen table because the clinician on the line told her she could wait, and being told she could wait made waiting impossible.

The table had been wiped clean twice. She had set out a glass of water, two glucose tablets she did not need, and the old church bulletin where she wrote grocery lists in the margins. The porch light had burned all morning outside the back door, pale against day. She had left it on after the Witness transmission because turning it off felt like answering a question no one had asked properly.

The clinician’s face filled the tablet in a small square. Young. Tired. Kind in the professional manner Ruth trusted less than bad coffee. “Mrs. Venn, before you open the transcript rendering, can you name where you are?”

“My kitchen.”

“Can you name the date?”

Ruth gave it.

“Can you tell me what this file is not?”

Ruth looked at the sealed line. ELIAS-ASSOCIATED PERSONAL ADDRESS / RESTRICTED FAMILY-CLINICAL RENDERING / NO RAW WAVEFORM.

“It is not proof he survived,” she said. “It is not an instruction. It may be contaminated. It may be a household transcript collision.” The last phrase tasted ridiculous in her mouth. “It is not public.”

“Do you want someone physically with you?”

“No.”

“We can offer a monitored family line after. Voice or text.”

“No.”

The young clinician absorbed that without flinching. “All right. Open when ready.”

Ruth touched the screen.

No voice played. That was part of the terms. No raw waveform. No repaired pitch. No chance for millions of strangers to decide that grief had a sound file they could edit into doctrine. Only text, with confidence bands and a note written by her daughter in the careful language of injury.

APPARENT ELIAS-ASSOCIATED SENTENCE, LOW-CONFIDENCE RENDERING:
“Mara, leave the porch light on.”

Ruth read it once.

Her hand went to the water glass and stopped before touching it. The kitchen clock ticked above the sink. The refrigerator motor came on with a shudder. Across the table, the church bulletin still advertised a hymn sing from three years earlier, before the parish took the stars out of the sanctuary and argued for six months about whether the ceiling should be plain.

“Mrs. Venn?” the clinician said.

Ruth closed the packet.

“Mrs. Venn, can you name where you are?”

“My kitchen.”

“Can you tell me what you need next?”

Ruth stood. The chair legs made a dry sound against linoleum. “I need to ruin a hymn.”

“I’m sorry?”

“You heard me.”

The piano sat in the front room under a sheet because dust was easier to blame than refusal. Elias had hated the old hymn she chose. Too slow, he used to say, and then he would sing it too fast while washing dishes until Mara corrected the tempo from the hallway. Ruth had stopped playing after the Break because every hymn asked for a shape the world no longer gave. Verse. Return. Amen.

She pulled off the sheet. Dust rose in a soft gray breath. The clinician remained on the tablet in the kitchen, calling her name once, then quieter, then leaving the line open.

Ruth sat. Her knees complained. She placed both hands on the keys.

The first chord came out wrong. Her left hand struck a note that belonged nowhere. She swore, sharply, as she had sworn only in cars and once at a senator after Elias’s memorial. She started again.

The hymn limped.

Halfway through the second line, her right hand remembered and her left hand refused. She stopped, pressed her palms against the keys until the sound turned into a low wooden bruise, then lifted them and began from the measure Elias used to mock. The porch light shone through the thin curtain behind her, useless in daylight, faithful to nothing except the switch still being on.

In the kitchen, the private packet stayed closed.

Ruth played badly for seven minutes. Then she shut the fallboard, returned to the table, and drank the water because the clinician asked her to.

The clinician said, “The monitored line is still open if you want to send text. No call.”

Ruth looked at the porch light burning white in daylight. “Text.”

Mara’s first line arrived with the delay marker attached.

I am here.

Ruth typed with one finger because two made her hurry.

Was there more?

The answer took long enough for the refrigerator motor to stop.

Yes.

Did you keep it from me?

I kept the raw audio restricted. I sent the sentence.

Ruth read that twice. “That is not an answer,” she told the clinician.

The clinician said nothing.

Ruth typed: That is not an answer.

Mara’s reply came back: No.

Ruth put the tablet flat on the table. The old church bulletin had a grocery list in the margin: eggs, soap, oranges. She added batteries, because the porch light would keep burning until she chose otherwise.

Then she typed one more line.

Do not make him into your courage.

The delay held. Ruth almost stood before the answer came.

I do not know how not to.

That was the first honest thing Mara had sent.

Ruth typed: Then do not explain him to me.

I won't.

You already are.

The cursor blinked. Ruth watched it until the refrigerator motor came on again.

Mara typed: Tell me what to do with the rest.

Ruth set both hands on the edge of the table. The question was the oldest trick grief knew: make another person choose the shape of your guilt.

She answered: Eat when they tell you. Open what you have to open. Leave me this sentence until I decide what it is.

Then she stood before Mara could send apology and took two eggs from the refrigerator.
