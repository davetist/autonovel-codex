## Chapter 19: The Ward Without Stars

“Stop.”

Ines Calder set the toast plate on the bedside table hard enough to rattle the knife.

The room went quiet in the wrong order. First the daughter stopped talking. Then the son-in-law, who had one hand lifted toward the wall screen as if the paused image could be cross-examined. Last, Thomas Keane’s wife closed her mouth around a sentence she had already sharpened.

Thomas sat upright against two pillows, shirt buttoned wrong at the throat, hands folded on the blanket. He had not eaten since the Witness advisory. A thin crescent of butter had softened on the saucer. The toast had gone cold once already. Ines had ordered it replaced.

On the wall screen, the Continuity Office seal held over a gray notice: RESTRICTED ASTERION MATERIAL UNDER REVIEW. NO RAW AUDIO RELEASE. CLINICAL GUIDANCE FOLLOWS.

The guidance had followed. So had every amateur priest on the family net.

Mrs. Keane pointed at the screen. “They are hiding it because it proves the dead are still in there.”

Her daughter said, “Mam.”

“No, don’t mam me. They would not seal a voice if it was only noise.”

“It isn’t proof of survival,” the son-in-law said. “It could be memory bleed. It could be artificial substrate artifact. If the whole structure is generated, then personhood is a process state, not a body state.”

Thomas blinked at the butter.

Ines pulled the screen remote from the holder and turned the wall blank. The seal collapsed into black glass.

“Out,” she said.

The daughter looked at her. “We’re his family.”

“You are currently weather.”

Mrs. Keane drew herself up. She had dressed for the ward visit as if for Mass: navy coat, pearl pin, gloves folded in her lap. “Doctor Calder, I will not be spoken to like a nuisance while my husband is being lied to.”

Ines pushed the toast plate six inches closer to Thomas. “Your husband is in front of you.”

“He has a right to know what his death means.”

“He needs breakfast.”

“That is not an answer.”

“It is the only answer with calories.”

The son-in-law gave a short laugh and looked ashamed before it finished. Ines marked that. Shame was still contact. He could be useful if he shut up for two minutes.

Thomas’s daughter pressed her fingers to her eyes. “Dad, please. Just half.”

Thomas did not move. His hair, combed by someone else, lay too flat against his scalp. Yesterday he had asked for Finn and then stared past the child as if the name had opened into a corridor he could not cross. Today he had accepted water from Nurse Brenna only after she changed the plastic cup for paper.

Paper counted.

Ines turned to the family. “You can sit in the day room and hate me there. You can come back when you can ask him whether he wants jam or butter without making the universe adjudicate it.”

Mrs. Keane’s gloves creaked in her fist. “He believes he is waiting for a sign.”

“No,” Ines said. “You believe that for him because waiting makes you less helpless.”

The daughter flinched. The son-in-law stared at the floor.

A nurse appeared in the doorway. Brenna, broad-shouldered, mask hanging below her chin, carrying a folded linen bag full of paper stars. She took in the room and stopped with one hand on the jamb.

Ines kept her voice flat. “Brenna will show you the day room.”

Mrs. Keane stood too quickly. The chair legs scraped. “Thomas.”

He turned his face toward her by a degree or two. Enough for hope to become dangerous.

She said, softer, “Tom.”

His lips parted. No sound. His gaze dropped to the plate.

Ines stepped between them, not touching either one. “Five minutes.”

The daughter opened her bag, took out a small packet of oat biscuits, put it on the windowsill, then took it back as if contraband had burned her palm. “He used to like these.”

“Bring them later,” Ines said.

“When is later?”

“When breakfast has stopped being a hearing.”

Brenna guided them out. Mrs. Keane left last, smelling of cold wool and church soap. At the threshold she looked back at her husband, then at Ines.

“He is not a child.”

“No,” Ines said. “Which is why I am letting him choose.”

The door closed with the soft ward click that never sounded final enough.

Thomas remained folded around his silence.

Ines sat in the visitor chair. It had a vinyl split at the left arm, sealed with clear tape that yellowed where hands gripped it. Outside, Dublin rain worried the window. St. Odran’s had been built for dying Catholics and revised, badly, for living citizens who had forgotten how to begin. The crucifix had been removed from the chapel after two patients used it as an argument with their nurses. The candle stayed. People disagreed less with flame.

“Thomas.”

His eyes came to her.

“Your wife is frightened.”

A swallow moved in his throat.

“So is your daughter. Your son-in-law has read two papers and become unbearable. That is treatable in some cases.”

Nothing.

“I am going to ask a medical question. You can answer with a word or a finger. Have you nausea?”

His right forefinger twitched once against the blanket.

“No nausea?”

Again, a twitch.

“Good. Mouth dry?”

The finger moved.

“Yes?”

A small nod.

Ines poured water from the jug into a paper cup. She held it near his hand, not his mouth. After a while he took it. His fingers shook, but the cup did not spill.

“Witness news came fast,” she said. “Too fast for breakfast. That is on us.”

Thomas looked at the blank wall screen.

“No raw audio here today. I made that order. You can be angry after toast.”

His mouth worked. She leaned in, not too close.

“If voice is body,” he said.

The sentence stopped. It had the thickness of something dragged from mud.

Ines waited.

“If voice is body,” he said again, “then he is there.”

“Elias Venn?”

Thomas blinked. Yes.

“Maybe the file contains a voice,” Ines said. “Maybe it contains damage that sounds like one. Maybe both sentences will sit in the same chair until the end of the world. None of that chews for you.”

He looked down at his hands. His wedding ring hung loose. Collapse took weight quietly, with no dramatic courtesy.

“I’m tired,” he said.

“I know.”

“No, you don’t.”

That surprised her into stillness. His voice had more heat than strength.

“You come in,” he said. “Bread. Weather. Name. Coat button. As if we are machines with a stuck switch.”

“We are meat with habits. It is less elegant.”

“I’m not stuck.”

“No?”

“I’m refusing.”

There it was. Ines had feared this sentence in other mouths. The neat philosophical refusal. The lucid patient who might be choosing with more honesty than the clinic allowed. She let her hands rest on her knees, palms down, because she wanted to fold towels.

“What are you refusing?”

Thomas lifted the cup, forgot why, set it down. “To perform real.”

Ines breathed through her nose.

The rain worked at the window in small taps. Somewhere in the corridor a meal cart squealed, then stopped. Brenna’s voice rose, warm and false-bright, telling someone that Wednesday had not been canceled by physicists, no matter what the feed said.

Ines put the toast plate on her lap and scraped the cold butter from one corner with the knife. “When your grandson fell last month and split his chin, you pressed a towel under it.”

Thomas’s jaw tightened.

“Finn bled on your sleeve. You told him to count the blue tiles. He counted five twice because he was crying. You corrected him. Cruel old man.”

His eyes filled, but the tears stayed low.

“If you were performing, he still stopped bleeding.”

“You use him.”

“Yes,” Ines said. “Because he is real enough to be used against you.”

Thomas shut his eyes.

“Open.”

He did.

“Jam or butter?”

His mouth hardened.

“I know. Obscene question. Answer it anyway.”

He looked at the plate as if it had been placed at the far end of a launch window. Ines had seen patients cross rooms more easily than they crossed choice. Choice required a small claim on sequence. This, then that. Hunger, hand, mouth. The world had to consent to being local.

“Butter,” he whispered.

Ines nodded. “Good.”

He stared at it.

She waited until the refusal changed texture. It was no longer philosophy. It was fatigue, pride, the body’s old contempt for being managed.

Then he said, almost angrily, “No. Jam.”

Ines kept her face still. “Jam.”

“Raspberry.”

“We have strawberry.”

“That’s a downgrade.”

“It is institutional jam. Downgrade is assumed.”

His breath came out through his nose. Not laughter. Near enough for clinical purposes.

She opened the small foil cup. The seal clung to her thumb. Red gel trembled on the knife. She spread it to the edge because he had once complained that hospitals rationed sweetness as if it were morphine.

He took the triangle of toast with two hands.

The first bite was tiny. Crumbs caught at the corner of his mouth. He chewed as if being watched by the dead and resenting them for it.

Ines looked away.

The corridor outside carried a rustle of paper.

When Thomas had swallowed twice, Brenna knocked once and entered with the linen bag tucked under her arm.

“Family in day room,” she said. “Son-in-law is reading the tea instructions like a legal document.”

“Let him steep,” Ines said.

Brenna’s gaze went to the toast. She did not smile. Good nurse.

Thomas took another bite because nobody congratulated him.

“I need the ladder,” Brenna said.

Ines frowned. “Now?”

“Ceiling order came through. Common room first, then adolescent corridor. Before afternoon feed.”

Thomas looked up. Above his bed, the ceiling had been painted ten years earlier by hospice volunteers: cream background, soft blue along the edges, small silver stars near the light fixture. Most rooms had already been stripped. His had been missed because he had liked them before the Break. He had said once they looked less like astronomy than cheap wrapping paper.

“Take them,” he said.

Ines turned to him. “Your room?”

“Take them.”

“You can keep them until discharge.”

His face closed.

Brenna shifted the bag. In it lay the adhesive paper stars already removed from Room Six and the pediatric quiet nook. Some were torn. One had stuck to the linen and flashed when the overhead light caught it.

“Thomas,” Ines said, “this is not a loyalty test.”

He lifted the toast, looked at the red jam, then at the ceiling. “If Finn comes, he’ll ask.”

“Then you can say the ward is careful with pictures.”

“He’ll ask if Mars is gone too.”

Brenna’s hand tightened on the bag.

Ines said, “Mars is not on your ceiling.”

Thomas gave her a look with some of the old man in it. “Pedant.”

“Former hospice physician. We charge extra.”

He ate the last corner of toast. The plate held crumbs and a smear of red. He pushed it away with one finger.

“Take them,” he said again. “But don’t put the screen back.”

“No screen,” Ines said.

Brenna brought the short ladder from the storage cupboard. Its rubber feet squeaked against the floor. She climbed with the care of someone whose knees had begun making private objections. Ines stood below, one hand near the rail, because nurses fell like anyone else and paperwork never broke the floor.

The first star peeled away with a soft tearing sound.

Thomas watched. His face gave nothing. Brenna worked slowly, warming each adhesive patch with her palm before lifting it, so the paint came away less often. The ceiling underneath showed faint oval ghosts. Not stars. Absences with tidy edges.

Ines hated the policy on bad days. On better days she had written parts of it. Remove high-trigger celestial imagery from patient rooms after Boundary-media exposure. Offer replacement images: weather, local maps, family-approved domestic scenes. Avoid false reassurance. Avoid doctrine. Permit patient-specific exceptions after feeding stability resumes.

Permit. As if hope came to clinic doors with forms.

Brenna dropped each star into the linen bag. By the light fixture she paused. Her fingers found a small red circle taped behind the metal rim, not visible from the bed unless one already knew where to look.

Ines saw it before Thomas did.

A paper Mars. Child-sized, cut from construction stock, shaded with orange pencil and a darker red thumbprint. It had likely come from Finn. Or from the old ward supply drawer. Or from some nurse’s act of disobedience during a night shift when a patient needed a planet more than policy.

Brenna held it between two fingers.

The room tightened.

Thomas stared at the little disk.

“Bin?” Brenna asked, too flat.

Ines looked at Thomas. He looked back with wet on his lower lashes and jam at his thumb.

“Drawer,” Ines said.

Brenna climbed down. She crossed to the bedside cabinet, opened the top drawer, and placed the paper Mars under a packet of tissues where it could not look at anyone by accident.

Thomas swallowed. “That’s cheating.”

“Yes.”

“Against your own order.”

“Yes.”

He looked at the drawer.

Ines picked up the plate. “You can file a complaint after lunch.”

“Will it be answered?”

“By a committee with chairs. You may wish to save your strength.”

This time he made a sound. Thin, unwilling, human.

Brenna closed the drawer, but not fully. A red edge remained visible for half a second before the shadow took it.

The afternoon feed was delayed twice. St. Odran’s received the Continuity bulletin through the hospital channel first, then the public version twelve minutes later with the harmful phrases removed and the useful ones flattened until they could not cut or feed.

Ines read both in her office with a glucose tablet dissolving chalky under her tongue.

Witness had restricted raw replay of an Elias-associated fragment. Family-specific material under legal hold. Provisional Class C. Personal address risk. Chain breach aboard vessel. Authorized interpretive officer removed from Elias-associated release for at least twenty-four ship hours.

Mara, then.

Ines took off her glasses, rubbed the bridge of her nose, put them back on because blurred text helped nobody. Her office had once been a confessional storage room. The shelves still held hymnals nobody wanted to throw away and binders of collapse intake forms. The radiator knocked even in mild weather. On the desk sat three paper cups, one with cold tea, one with pens, one with buttons patients had surrendered from coats until they could manage them again.

The chipped brown button she had given Mara before the launch had come from Thomas Keane’s winter coat. She had kept its twin in her drawer without admitting why.

On the security monitor, Mrs. Keane sat in the day room with her gloves in her lap. Her daughter leaned against the vending machine, crying without covering her face. The son-in-law stood by the tea station, reading his phone, then placing it screen-down as if he had learned one small manners too late.

Ines opened Thomas’s chart.

Intake: post-advisory relapse following restricted Asterion-fragment rumor. Refused breakfast. Refused family. Accepted water in paper cup. Verbalized voice-body equivalence. Verbalized refusal to “perform real.” Responded to grandchild-specific prompt with distress but intact contact. Chose strawberry jam over butter. Consumed approximately three quarters slice toast. Requested removal of room stars. Hidden patient-specific Mars image retained in drawer by clinician order.

She stopped there. The cursor pulsed after the sentence.

Hidden patient-specific Mars image retained.

The phrase would look absurd in an audit. It was also the most accurate line in the chart.

She added: No screen exposure before evening meal. Family coached to avoid doctrine during feeding. If unable, visits separated from meals.

A knock.

“Come,” she said.

Brenna leaned in. “Keane family wants back.”

“Can they behave?”

“The wife says she can. The son-in-law says he has revised his frame.”

“God preserve us from revisions.”

Brenna’s mouth twitched. “The daughter brought the biscuits.”

“Good. Ten minutes. Food only.”

“And Ward B?”

Ines already knew. The adolescent corridor always heard the news first and ate last. “How many?”

“Four refusals. A. drew the door again, this time around the blank television.”

“Marker?”

“Contraband lipstick.”

“Color?”

“Red.”

Ines put the glucose tablet wrapper in the bin and stood. Her knees hurt. “Of course it was.”

Brenna did not move. “You should record for Venn.”

Ines looked up.

“She asked before launch, didn’t she? Clinical counterpoint if Witness got near trouble.”

“That was a formal request.”

“Then formally tell her to stop breaking herself on files.”

Ines stared at the nurse.

Brenna shrugged with one shoulder. “Or don’t. You’re the doctor.”

The corridor smelled of soup and floor cleaner. From Room Three came the soft beat of a metronome used for dressing practice. A man in a brown cardigan stood with one arm in his coat sleeve, counting under his breath. One. Two. Button. One. Two. Button. His wife watched from the chair with her hands trapped under her thighs so she would not help too soon.

At the adolescent door, a patient had written IF LOCKED WHY LIGHT in lipstick across the observation glass. The letters sloped downward after LIGHT as the arm tired.

Ines took the wipe Brenna handed her and cleaned the words herself. Red came away in oily streaks. The child inside sat cross-legged on the floor, face turned to the wall, blanket over her shoulders. Thirteen, maybe fourteen. Too thin at the wrist. The door drawn around the blank television had a handle.

“Water,” Ines said through the glass.

No response.

“In a paper cup.”

The blanket shifted.

“Jam after.”

A pause.

“Toast first,” said the child.

“Extortionist.”

“Doctor.”

“Fair.”

By evening, three of the four adolescents had eaten something that could be charted without lying. The fourth held a biscuit in her hand until it softened, then asked whether soft counted. Ines said yes and wrote it down. The son-in-law in Thomas Keane’s room managed twelve minutes without doctrine and then ruined it by using the phrase substrate continuity. Mrs. Keane slapped his arm with a force that improved the clinical environment. Thomas ate one oat biscuit and told them all to stop saying words with too many syllables while he was chewing.

At 21:40, Ines went to the chapel.

The old altar had been stripped to wood. No crucifix. No projected Mars. No sanctioned image from Witness. Just the candle in its glass cylinder, flame steady because the ventilation had been adjusted after a patient accused the draft of trying to speak.

Ines sat in the back pew with the recorder on her knee.

She had recorded for committees, hearings, medical boards, grieving parents, denial-state lawyers, and one bishop who wanted to know whether removing the crucifix had made the ward less Catholic. She had told him the ward was full of people washing feet, feeding strangers, and sitting with the afraid at three in the morning, and he could decide what to call that after he had done a night shift.

For Mara she waited longer.

The recorder light glowed red. Ines disliked red lights now. Every red light wanted to be Mars if watched long enough.

She pressed start.

“Dr. Venn. Mara.”

Her voice sounded older on playback while it was happening. That always offended her.

“This is Ines Calder, St. Odran’s, recorded under the clinical advisory request you left before Witness departure. I am sending it through the Continuity medical channel, not the interpretive chain. If Pavel’s office edits it, tell him I will haunt his paperwork while alive.”

She stopped. Too much. Or exactly enough.

She continued.

“We had a relapse this morning after the restricted-fragment advisory. Thomas Keane. You met him. Coat button, grandson Finn, wife who frightens junior nurses because she has correct posture. His family came in with doctrine loaded and breakfast forgotten. One argued survival through voice. One argued artificial substrate. One argued hiding as proof. Thomas had eaten nothing.”

The candle flame shortened, then stood.

“I removed them. He took water. He told me he was refusing to perform real. That sentence will please people with good shoes. It should not. He was also dry-mouthed and underfed and angry that we kept offering routine as if routine were an answer.”

Ines looked toward the altar table. The glass cylinder held a smear from someone’s thumb. No one had polished it.

“He chose jam over butter. Strawberry, under protest. He ate most of a slice. Then he asked us to remove the ceiling stars so his grandson would not ask the wrong question before Thomas could bear answering. Nurse Brenna found a small paper Mars hidden by the light fixture. We left it in the drawer. That is my whole philosophy tonight, if philosophy is required. Remove what stops him eating. Keep one small thing where he can choose to open the drawer.”

She rubbed her thumb against the recorder edge.

“You are aboard a ship with recordings that may contain your brother and may contain the injury done to your brother and may contain only the machine making a brother-shaped wound. I do not know. You know I do not know. I am not telling you to stop wanting. Want is allowed. It is not a clinical error.”

A door clicked somewhere beyond the chapel. Night staff, changing rounds.

“But do not ask a recording to do a meal’s work.”

She let the sentence stand long enough to make her uncomfortable.

“Do not ask Elias to feed Thomas Keane. Do not ask him to button a coat, get a child through breakfast, keep a ward from turning a screen into a door. If there is a true thing in those files, treat it as true with clean hands. If there is a dangerous thing, treat it as dangerous without pretending danger makes it false. But the living will still need food at eight, medication at noon, curtains opened or closed by consent, and someone to say weather without making weather into proof.”

Ines almost stopped. Then the wrong thing arrived.

“I am tired of saving people who call my work small.”

She held still, surprised by her own voice.

The recorder caught the chapel hum.

“I should not have said that. Leave it in. It is true enough to be useful.”

She leaned back. The pew pressed into her spine.

“Treatment is not proof, Mara. A patient eating toast does not settle the Boundary. A child accepting water does not answer Mars. A man choosing jam does not refute despair for history. It means he chose jam. Then we ask again at lunch. That is the work. It repeats until it fails, then we adjust and repeat. If meaning comes, it comes late and unevenly and often after the chart is filed.”

The flame moved behind glass. It looked less like faith than combustion under care.

“You once told me your conversion drills taught fear to become function. I have a ward full of people who cannot be converted by argument. They can sometimes be invited by bread. There is a difference. Do not miss it because the voice is his.”

She pressed stop before she could soften the ending.

The recorder saved the file with a small chime. Ines named it CALDER_TO_VENN_STODRANS_CLINICAL_KEANE_JAM and sent it before courage became editing.

Much later, after the file crossed relays and filters and whatever private machinery decided which kinds of care were safe enough to move quickly, Mara Venn listened in a medical alcove aboard Witness with a pulse cuff still creased around her wrist.

Ines would only know that from the playback receipt: accessed by M. Venn under observation, ship medical, no reply recorded.

The receipt arrived at 04:12 Dublin time while Ines was back in Thomas Keane’s room, where he had woken confused and asked whether Finn had school.

“Yes,” Ines said. “Raincoat day.”

Thomas’s hand moved toward the bedside drawer.

Ines opened it for him.

The paper Mars lay under the tissues, red edge showing. Thomas looked at it for one breath, then closed the drawer himself.

“Jam at breakfast?” Ines asked.

He turned his face to the window, where the city had not yet made up its mind about morning.

“Butter,” he said. “Don’t get sentimental.”
