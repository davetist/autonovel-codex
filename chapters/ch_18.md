## Chapter 18: Porch Light

The archive bay cut the lights when no motion crossed the floor for seven minutes. Mara sat still enough to fool it.

Only the console stayed awake, three narrow panels in maintenance blue, each one asking for confirmation she had already given. Reviewer token: VENN-M. Session purpose: Elias-specific derivative exclusion audit. Second-reviewer field: IMANI-S, present.

Sera was not present. Sera had left seventeen minutes earlier to answer a clock-array dispute in the forward lab after saying, “Do not advance material. Mark only cross-links.” Mara had nodded. She had even said, “Understood,” with the plainness people mistook for obedience.

The bay smelled of warmed plastic and old sterilizing wipes. A ration bulb of mandarin syrup floated near the intake grille, trapped by a weak current, turning slowly as if it were choosing a direction. Mara watched it instead of the waveform. Her fingers rested against the inside of her wrist.

The fragment sat inside a dependency tree attached to Jun Park’s cockpit record. Not the public derivative. Not the laugh. A support file. The archive had labeled it AST-MD118-MED-VMIX-LOWCONF / SOURCE BLEED POSSIBLE / DO NOT CLASSIFY BY CONTENT.

Vmix. Voice mix.

Low confidence.

The danger in archival language was always the clean little adjective that said a room had decided to move on.

Mara opened only the metadata first. That was allowed. Source association: medical channel, cockpit bleed, service corridor return, Elias Venn vocal match 0.62, Elias Venn vocal match after pitch repair 0.81, unknown household transcript collision, custody incomplete until Orpheus packet recovery. Three checksums had passed. Two had failed in the same place.

Unknown household transcript collision.

Her hand left her wrist. The console read the small motion and brightened the room.

“No,” she said to the bay.

The bay accepted it as speech noise.

The phrase had lived in her life for years without sound. A family-adjacent leak, someone’s pity, someone’s payment, nineteen words around one sentence and no chain of custody. The rumor had passed from a cousin of a contractor through Ruth’s church friend, then into Mara’s kitchen at 02:40 on a night when rain struck the window hard enough to make Houston sound coastal again. Mara had written it by hand on the back of an old intake form and burned the form in a metal bowl because she had still believed destruction could make knowledge private.

Mara, leave the porch light on.

No audio. No timestamp. No proof.

Ruth had brought it to the hearing without naming it. One private sentence, she had asked, hands folded around a paper cup. One sentence of my son that is not for classrooms.

Mara set the playback volume to minimum.

Then she lowered it again.

The console required two-person confirmation for audio. Sera’s token remained active in the session because the archive bay did not know a body from a credential. It knew proximity, pulse band, heat signature, and workflow. Sera’s sleeve still lay on the second chair where she had left it after the bay grew too warm for her. Compression fabric, tagged, seeded with her biometric thread.

Mara looked at the sleeve.

The right action had a shape. Close the file. Call Sera. Call Nadine. Create a sealed note: possible Elias family-specific material, prior unauthorized exposure, reviewer conflict. She could write it in twenty-one words. She could preserve the record.

She pressed play.

At first there was only static with a wet edge, the sound of a recording repaired from pressure and absence. Under it came a relay tone from another channel, then a click that belonged to a med kit latch. Elias had made that sound all his adult life when thinking. Two fingers on plastic. Tap, tap. Wait. Tap.

Mara’s throat locked on the second tap.

[AST-MD118-MED-VMIX-LOWCONF]
[Timestamp association disputed: 03:11:21.887 / 03:10:04.112 / no stable origin]
[Transcript engine confidence below release threshold]
[Possible archive bleed from MD117 private log, family server transcript, or unknown source]

Static.

A breath.

Not Jun. Not Sofia. Too close to the microphone, lips dry, air pinched through pain or bad repair.

“Mara,” Elias said.

The room changed size without moving.

Mara’s fingers found her wrist and pressed too hard. Pulse. Count. Convert the flood into numbers. The first number would not arrive. The second failed behind it.

The waveform continued, thin and ordinary.

“Mara, leave the porch light on.”

A small click came after porch, as if his tongue had touched the back of his teeth. On light the signal tore, then returned for one soft consonant. On on, the channel dropped half a tone. The machine drew certainty from fragments and put his name beside the line.

Mara stopped the playback.

The file offered SAVE REVIEW NOTE.

She stared at the word save until it became a physical instruction. Preserve this. Protect this. Lock it away from every hand that would draw a door around Mars, every lawyer who would ask whether grief had standing, every committee that would turn Elias into a risk category, every feed where children watched adults decide which dead voices were safe.

Again, a cleaner part of her answered.

Evidence custody.

She opened the note field.

Family-specific personal address. Possible Elias Venn. Prior unauthorized transcript exposure held by reviewer. Immediate removal from Elias review required.

The words waited. Black letters in a white box. Workable. Sufficient.

Her hand moved to commit the note.

In the next panel, the transcript engine refreshed. Unknown household transcript collision changed to KNOWN TRANSCRIPT COLLISION: VENN FAMILY / PRE-BREAK DOMESTIC PHRASE / SOURCE NOT IN IIA INDEX.

The bay had indexed it from her. From her search patterns, her sealed declarations, Ruth’s hearing allusion, some old family testimony folded into Continuity archives. Or from the fragment. Or from the Boundary making a household into a channel. The order mattered. The order could not be proved.

Mara deleted the note.

She did not breathe for four counts, then took in air through her teeth.

The bay logged the deletion in a side panel. Of course it did. Nothing aboard Witness forgot cleanly. It only waited for a better witness.

She created a new note.

Low-confidence voice bleed attached to Park cockpit derivative. Recommend defer until full cluster audit.

It was a lie by omission. Its grammar was perfect.

She saved it.

The console asked whether to mark the audio as reviewed. She moved the cursor away. Marking it reviewed would require classification. Leaving it unmarked would leave a counter discrepancy, but counters could be explained by archive preview. Cross-link audit. Source check. Stress. The machine did not care why a person wanted one more minute.

She opened playback again.

This time she watched the waveform as the voice arrived. It was not identical to the rumor she had carried. The leaked transcript had no comma after Mara. It had rendered porch-light as one hyphenated domestic object. Here, Elias paused after her name. Not a long pause. Enough for injury. Enough for recognition to enter before instruction.

“Mara,” he said.

Her childhood had a yellow porch light with a cracked insect cover and a switch that stuck halfway unless you hit it with the heel of your palm. Their father came home late when he came home at all. Ruth would leave the light on even after she stopped waiting in the front room, even after Elias learned to make grilled cheese by standing on a chair, even after Mara began telling herself that ritual was what frightened people did when procedure failed them. Elias, eight years old, used to stand barefoot at the door and say it as a joke before school trips, before sleepovers, before one week at a church camp he hated.

Leave the porch light on.

Meaning: expect me.

Meaning: keep the house addressed to my return.

Meaning: lie kindly if the dark has already decided.

The archive bay light cut to standby again. The console threw her face back in dim reflection. Compact, gray at the mouth, one hand at her wrist. A woman listening to a dead man ask for an old mercy inside a file that might have been built from her own hunger.

She played it a third time.

The third time, something after on emerged under the static. Not a word. A mouth beginning one. The transcript engine tried to catch it and failed. The failure looked like three empty brackets.

Mara leaned closer.

The bay door opened.

Sera entered with a data slate tucked under one arm and stopped at the threshold. Her compression sleeve was still on the second chair. She looked from it to Mara’s hand on the console.

“What are you playing?”

Mara closed the file.

The room lights rose.

Sera’s face tightened around the question before she spoke again. “What are you playing?”

“A low-confidence support bleed.”

“From which cluster?”

“Park derivative.”

“Then why is the Elias family filter live?”

Mara’s wrist beat under her fingers. Too fast. She took her hand away.

Sera crossed the bay in three steps and put her palm on the second console. “Session audit.”

“Sera.”

“No.”

The console showed the recent access chain with the stupid calm of a machine that had never been asked to spare the living. Playback preview: three. Note created. Note deleted. Note created. Reviewer token active: IMANI-S. Second reviewer physical divergence warning: deferred.

Sera read it once. Her thumb went to the old ring mark on her finger and rubbed until the skin reddened.

“What was in the deleted note?”

“Do not do this here.”

“What was in the deleted note, Mara?”

Mara looked toward the closed file. It sat inside its folder like a sealed wound. “Elias address. Possible. Low confidence.”

Sera’s voice lost heat. That made it worse. “You played an Elias-addressed fragment alone.”

“You were logged in.”

“Do not make me answer that.”

“I know.”

“You used my token.”

“I know.”

The words gave nothing back. Too small. Too late.

Sera reached for the command strip. “Nadine needs to hear this.”

“No.”

Sera’s hand paused above the strip.

Mara heard herself continue, and the surprise was not the refusal but its texture. Thin. Almost childlike. “Not yet.”

Sera turned. “Say the operational reason.”

Mara had trained candidates for this. If you ask the body for a reason before panic finishes its first work, the mind may give you a tool instead of a confession. Say the operational reason. Name the valve. Name the risk. Convert fear.

“It contains family-specific content with high collapse and cult misuse risk,” Mara said. “It should be sealed before command replay.”

“That is a reason to call Nadine.”

“It is Elias.”

“That is the risk, not the exception.”

Mara’s mouth opened. The next sentence rose too quickly. “He said the porch light.”

Sera went still.

The bay seemed to catch the phrase and hold it with the other corrupted things. Porch. Light. Domestic, useless, lethal.

Sera lowered her hand from the command strip. “You knew that phrase.”

Mara said nothing.

“Before this file.”

“Rumor. Transcript only. No custody.”

“You signed a declaration.”

“I signed no undisclosed verified family-specific material.”

Sera stared at her.

Mara wanted the technicality to remain technical. The sentence had been built for courtrooms and release panels, not for this small room with Sera’s sleeve lying in evidence against them both. It had allowed her to stand inside the truth by drawing a thin line around one word: verified. She had lived there. She had worked from there.

Sera said, “That distinction saved your access.”

“Yes.”

“And you used the access to hear it first.”

Mara looked at the mandarin bulb caught near the grille. Its orange shine had dulled under the blue light.

“I needed to know whether it was real.”

Sera’s laugh had no humor in it. One breath, cut short. “Listen to yourself.”

“I am.”

“No. You are listening to him.”

The command strip chirped. Sera had opened a shipboard alert before Mara saw her move.

Mara stepped toward her. “Sera, wait.”

Sera flinched. Not fear. Revulsion at being made into the person Mara would try to stop.

“Do not touch me.”

Mara stopped.

The strip connected to command.

Nadine’s voice came through the wall speaker, low and near. “Archive.”

Sera answered. “Commander, I need you in Bay Two. Elias-specific chain breach. Mara played restricted audio under my active reviewer token while I was absent.”

Silence lasted less than a second. Mara counted it anyway.

Nadine said, “Seal the bay. Do not replay. Malik to archive custody. Medical standby.”

“I do not need medical,” Mara said.

Nadine ignored that. “Doctor Venn, hands off console.”

Mara placed both hands on the desk surface.

It was cold. Someone had cleaned the metal too recently, and the solvent smell came up under her palms. She watched Sera log out her token, watched the console close around the file, watched the three playback counts remain like a small accusation no one could reduce.

The bay door stayed open but locked to yellow. Malik arrived first, hair flattened on one side from sleep webbing, shirt sealed crooked at the collar. He had a packet router clipped to his belt and a mouth that looked unready for any sentence.

“Tell me what to quarantine,” he said.

Sera pointed. “Session AST-MD118-MED-VMIX-LOWCONF. All derivatives. Lock physical and remote replay. Preserve deleted note record.”

Malik’s eyes moved once to Mara, then away. He did not ask. His mother had gone flat after a raw contractor archive dump. He knew what voices could do when stripped from rooms.

Nadine came behind him without hurry. She wore socks inside ship shoes, no jacket, one hand hooked near the rail even in the doorway. Her face had the plainness of command before punishment.

“Mara,” she said. “Step back from the console.”

Mara stepped back.

“How many playbacks?”

“Three.”

Sera said, “Audit confirms three.”

“Anyone else heard content?”

“No,” Sera said.

Nadine looked at Mara. “Content category.”

“Possible personal address from Elias to me. Family-specific. Low confidence. Chain contamination severe.”

“Words.”

The room tightened.

Mara’s first answer failed in her mouth.

Nadine waited. Waiting was one of her command skills, a refusal to rescue people from the sentence they owed.

“Words,” she repeated.

Mara heard Ruth at the hearing: one private sentence. Saw Ruth’s hands, church pianist hands gone dry around a paper cup. Heard Elias at eight at the door. Heard Elias in the file, or the Boundary wearing Elias, or an archive repair engine making a key from every lock it had been given.

“Mara, leave the porch light on,” she said.

Malik shut his eyes.

Sera turned back to the console as if a dataset might steady her.

Nadine did nothing for two breaths. Then she said, “Class C provisional. Raw sealed. Existence logged. No release discussion until review board.”

Mara’s voice came too sharp. “You cannot classify from my recitation.”

“I can classify operational risk from your breach.”

“That is not evidence classification.”

“No. It is crew safety.”

Mara put both hands behind her back because they wanted her wrist again. “The record may be personally impossible.”

“May be.”

“It may contain material from outside any known mission source.”

“It may contain your prior leak, family testimony, archive bleed, pattern repair, or your brother saying a thing he once said before.” Nadine’s gaze did not move. “You know the list.”

“Then play it with full board.”

“No.”

“Nadine.”

“No.”

The word struck with no raised volume.

Sera said, “Commander, I recommend immediate removal of Doctor Venn from Elias-specific classification and release authority pending medical evaluation and custody review.”

Mara turned toward her. “Sera.”

Sera kept her eyes on Nadine. “She created and deleted a conflict note. Then she saved a materially incomplete note. She concealed content. She used my token.”

“I know what I did.”

“Then stop making us prove it to you.”

Malik’s hands moved over the packet router. “Archive quarantine set. Remote mirrors paused. Ground sync delayed under evidence-preservation flag. I can hold the delay for twenty minutes before Quito asks why.”

“Give them hardware custody review,” Nadine said.

“That buys six.”

“Then six.”

Malik nodded.

Mara said, “Ground must know the content class.”

“Ground will know a restricted Elias-addressed fragment exists,” Nadine said. “They will not receive raw audio in this window.”

“That is the same suppression Pavel uses.”

Nadine looked at her then with something sharper than anger. “Pavel is on Earth. I am in a ship with you. Do not spend his name to avoid mine.”

The sentence landed where a hand would have.

Mara found the edge of the desk behind her. Metal under fingers. No wrist. No pulse count.

Nadine turned to Malik. “Send sealed event notice to Yuna. Commander-ordered medical fitness review. Include Sera’s custody report. No content phrase in open packet.”

Malik hesitated. “The families’ seal?”

“Flag Ruth Venn request cross-reference under legal hold. No notification yet.”

“No notification?” Mara said.

Nadine did not turn. “Correct.”

“She asked for one private sentence.”

“And you just proved you cannot carry it to her.”

Mara had answered harder questions in hearings. She had faced Pavel’s casualty charts, Claire Arendt’s legal precision, Tomás Ríos Sr. accusing her of selling children a sky. None of them had found this soft part of the bone.

The archive bay felt airless though ventilation hummed under the floor. Her body wanted to sit down. She refused it, then hated the small vanity of refusal. Elias would have asked if she could feel her hands. Elias would have made the body first. The file had not. The file had used her name.

Nadine stepped closer, lowering her voice until it no longer belonged to the room. “Doctor Venn, you are temporarily removed from release authority over Asterion family-specific material, Elias-associated material, and any Class C personal-address fragment. Effective now.”

Mara heard the procedural door close.

“For how long?”

“Until medical and custody review clear you. If they clear you.”

“Sera becomes acting interpretive officer?”

“Sera controls scientific classification. Malik controls packet custody. I control onboard release posture. Pavel can choke on the delay from a great distance.”

That almost made Malik laugh. It came out as a cough.

Mara looked at Sera. “You cannot interpret family harm from checksum maps.”

Sera’s face changed then, not softening, but showing the cost under the discipline. “I know.”

The wrong answer would have been anger. The right answer had no defense.

Nadine said, “Medical alcove. Now.”

“I can submit my own conflict statement first.”

“You can dictate it under observation.”

“I am not impaired.”

“No one asked for your diagnosis.”

“I am mission psychologist.”

“You are off duty.”

The yellow lock released. Nadine gestured toward the corridor.

Mara walked out because command had left her no useful disobedience. The corridor outside archive bay was narrow enough for shoulders to learn humility. Handrails ran on both sides, polished in oval patches. Someone had fastened a strip of velcro along a junction panel and it rasped against Mara’s sleeve as she passed.

Behind her, Malik spoke softly into his router. Sera answered a system prompt. The bay door sealed with a pressure kiss.

No one replayed the voice.

The crew medical alcove had one privacy curtain and no room for privacy. A crash kit occupied the lower wall. The diagnostic chair folded from the bulkhead, its restraints clipped open. The place smelled of antiseptic fabric and electrolyte broth. A pouch of glucose tablets sat beside the pulse cuff, labeled in Tomas’s square handwriting because he labeled everything that might save a person if found fast enough.

Yuna’s face appeared on the wall screen after the relay delay, flat with compression artifacts. Her image broke at the hairline, reassembled, then held.

“Mara,” Yuna said. “Sit.”

Mara sat.

The chair accepted her weight and locked a hip strap with more tenderness than the room deserved. Nadine stayed by the curtain. Sera remained outside, a shadow beyond fabric. Malik had gone back to comms or custody or whatever clean task could hold his hands.

Yuna’s voice came through a half-second late. “Do you consent to a focused cognitive safety review?”

“No.”

Nadine said, “Medical authority can compel limited review after a classified-data breach.”

Yuna waited for the delay to carry that, then nodded once. “Correct. I am asking because consent still matters where it can.”

Mara looked at the pulse cuff. “I consent to limited review. No content replay.”

“No content replay,” Yuna said. “You will answer from present orientation first. Ship time.”

“04:38.”

“Mission phase.”

“Witness approach window. Pre-Candle handoff. Asterion final-cluster review under restricted custody.”

“Current duty status.”

Mara’s jaw tightened. “Removed from release authority over specified material pending review.”

“By whom?”

“Commander Qureshi.”

“Reason.”

“Data custody breach. Concealed Elias-addressed fragment. Unauthorized private playback.”

The words sounded trained. They also sounded true. That was the horror of procedure. It could make confession legible without making it complete.

Yuna glanced down, reading a delayed packet. “Any current auditory persistence? Hearing the phrase now as external sound?”

“No.”

“As memory?”

“Yes.”

“Body check. Hands.”

Mara looked down. Her hands lay on the chair arms, fingers curled. Nails cut too close. “Present. Cold.”

“Feet.”

“Present.”

“Breath.”

“Too high.”

“Bring it lower if you can. Do not perform calm for me.”

The sentence struck sideways. Elias could have said it with a joke tucked under it. Yuna did not joke. Her face on the screen was tired, professionally lit by rooms on Earth where the lights never dimmed fully.

Mara drew breath down until the strap across her hip became an object again.

Yuna said, “State your interpretation of the fragment in one sentence with uncertainty preserved.”

Mara almost laughed. It would have been ugly.

“Possible Elias Venn vocal fragment containing a family-specific personal address whose source may be original, contaminated by prior transcript exposure, drawn from archive bleed, or produced by unknown Boundary-related information failure.”

Nadine listened from the curtain without moving.

“State the risk if raw audio is released,” Yuna said.

“Exit recruitment. Family exploitation. Personal-address doctrine. Collapse events in exposed groups. Misuse by denial edits. Ruth harmed by public seizure of a private phrase.”

“State the risk if it is buried.”

Mara looked at the cuff again.

“Mara.”

“Institutional lie. Family theft. Scientific loss. My own contamination hidden inside release design. Pavel’s model wins by default.”

Yuna accepted that with a small mark on her slate. “State why you played it.”

Mara’s tongue touched the back of her teeth. The line between explanation and plea was thinner than the console cursor had been.

“I wanted Elias to be there before the Boundary was.”

No one spoke.

The alcove ventilation clicked over to night cycle. Air moved across Mara’s wrists. The body noticed before the mind did.

Yuna’s face held steady. “That is not an operational reason.”

“I know.”

“Is it the true one?”

Mara closed her eyes, opened them because closing them made the file louder. “Part of it.”

“What is the other part?”

“I wanted one sentence before Earth took him again.”

The privacy curtain shifted. Sera had turned away outside it.

Nadine’s voice came from the same place. “Earth did not take that playback.”

Mara looked toward the curtain.

Nadine continued, “You did.”

It was too exact to reject.

Yuna gave the delay room to settle. “I am recommending temporary removal from interpretive authority across all Elias-associated content, not only personal-address fragments. You may advise on general release language after command approval, but no custody decisions, no raw access, no family seal routing.”

“For how long?”

“Minimum twenty-four ship hours, then reassess. Given approach stressors, likely through Candle Band Two transition.”

Mara sat forward against the strap. “That window determines the release frame.”

“Yes.”

“You cannot take me out of that.”

Yuna’s face hardened. “I can when your continued role makes the frame dirty.”

Mara had no answer that would help.

Nadine stepped inside the curtain. “We are done for now.”

Yuna said, “One more. Mara, do you have any other unreported prior exposure to Elias-specific restricted material?”

The porch light had taught the room to distrust every pause.

“No verified material,” Mara said, and heard the old line trying to build itself again.

Yuna did not look away. “That was not my question.”

Mara’s chest tightened under the strap. “No other unreported prior exposure.”

“Your answer is logged.”

The screen cut to standby, leaving Yuna’s last compressed frame in a ghost of pixels before the system cleared it.

Nadine unlatched the hip strap herself. Not tenderly. Not roughly. Work.

“You’ll stay off archive systems,” she said. “Crew quarters or medical alcove. Your choice.”

“My quarters have Elias in the walls.”

Nadine’s hand paused on the latch.

Mara had not meant to say it. That made it worse. The wrong sentence, arriving without authorization.

Nadine finished unclipping the strap. “Medical, then.”

“I am fit for other duty.”

“Maybe. I am not spending crew margin to find out tonight.”

The curtain opened. Sera stood in the corridor with her arms folded tight against the cold. She looked smaller without the sleeve, one arm bare from wrist to elbow, the skin marked by the pressure seam. In her other hand she held the forgotten fabric.

“I need it back,” she said.

Mara understood a second late.

“The sleeve.”

Nadine looked between them. “Sera.”

“It has my biometric thread.” Sera’s voice stayed level. “Custody needs it.”

She held it out, not to Mara, but past her, toward Nadine.

The moment should have been humiliating. It was, but not where Mara expected. She saw the sleeve as evidence, saw Sera’s body reduced to a credential Mara had borrowed without permission, saw the old ring mark under Sera’s thumb where grief had worn its own quiet groove. Mara had made another person’s discipline into a tool.

Nadine took the sleeve in a sterile pouch from the med wall. “Logged.”

Sera nodded. Her eyes met Mara’s then.

“I will classify the fragment as contaminated,” Sera said. “Scientifically. That does not make it meaningless.”

Mara’s mouth hurt from holding back whatever came next.

Sera added, “It also does not make it yours.”

She left before Mara could answer.

Nadine sealed the pouch and clipped it to her belt. “You have ten minutes before I lock your archive credentials.”

“I should write Ruth.”

“No.”

“A holding note. No content.”

“No.”

“She will hear from a committee.”

“She will hear when we can speak without turning her son into a loaded instrument.”

Mara laughed once. Barely sound. “You think that is still possible?”

Nadine’s face did not change. “No. I think some harms can still be made smaller.”

From somewhere down the corridor came the muted chime of a ship clock correcting itself against another ship clock. The two tones nearly matched. Nearly was enough to trouble the ear.

Mara looked past Nadine to the strip of corridor visible beyond medical: handrail, velcro, a smear on the wall where someone’s shoulder had rubbed during a drill. No window. Witness saved windows for navigation and for the moments command wanted to remind people that Mars was still there, clean and red beyond all this damaged custody.

“Did you hear it?” Nadine asked.

Mara turned back.

“The phrase,” Nadine said. “When you recited it. Did you hear it as his voice?”

“Yes.”

“Do you now?”

Mara waited for the answer to become clinical. It did not.

“Yes.”

Nadine nodded once, as if that settled only the next procedure. “Then you sleep under observation.”

“I will not sleep.”

“Then you will fail to sleep under observation.”

A human answer, dry enough to stand.

Nadine left the alcove curtain half-open. That was policy for watch risk. It was also mercy withheld from privacy because privacy had become evidence.

Mara sat on the diagnostic chair after the commander left. The pulse cuff remained on the tray. She picked it up, wrapped it around her own wrist, and tightened it one-handed until the velcro caught.

The screen blinked awake.

PULSE ACQUIRING.

She watched the numbers search for her through skin and pressure. For three beats the device found nothing. Then it caught, too high, real enough for medicine.

In the archive bay, sealed behind two doors and Malik’s custody flag, Elias waited inside a file that might not contain him.

Mara pressed her thumb against the cuff’s plastic edge and listened to the little pump work.
