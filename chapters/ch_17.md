Jun had his left foot hooked under the lower brace and his right hand resting open above the manual attitude pad. Resting, not gripping. Gripping lied to the wrist. A hand that gripped too early started hearing its own blood as data.

“Star tracker A holding,” he said. “B within expected spread. C is being artistic.”

Sofia stood behind and above him, strapped at the command rail. In the curved dark of the cockpit glass, her reflection had no face unless the panel lights caught it. Then it was only jaw and headset.

“Define artistic.”

“Wrong in a repeatable direction.”

“Numbers.”

“Offset point zero three two degrees radial, point zero zero six plane-normal. Before you ask, no, I don’t like radial as a verb either.”

“Park.”

“C is repeating the last calibration set against current gyros. It’s not random. It’s old.”

Amara’s voice came from systems, one deck aft. “Old how?”

“Like it found a shirt in the laundry and decided that was the day.”

“Useful.”

“It’s a cockpit. We’re all poets when the machine embarrasses us.”

Elias laughed once on the medical loop, low and close, though he was not in the cockpit. “Nobody tell Mara.”

Sofia’s reflection lowered its chin. “Public feed off?”

Mateo, from payload, too fast: “Public feed is on delay hold. Education segment killed. My face is not saving anyone today.”

“Good,” Sofia said.

Jun looked past the panel, through the forward glass. Mars sat where it should have sat. Red, clean, almost courteous. No smear, no doubled rim, no lazy drift from camera lag. The disk had a pale scab of cloud over Valles Marineris, exactly where the weather packet had said it would be, and the sun edge put the faintest line on the cockpit frame.

Destination with manners.

“Optical forward is sane,” Jun said. “Mars visual agrees with ephemeris to inside tolerance.”

“Star field?”

“C is still old. A and B current.”

“Which one flies the ship?”

He smiled before he meant to. “Me, if everyone stays kind.”

No one answered that.

The attitude pad warmed under his palm. Not heat. A ready-state pulse through the glove, a small pressure language designed in committee and tested by pilots who lied on forms about superstition. Jun had argued for the pulse. Human hands read rhythm faster than human pride admitted. During Tycho, with dust closing the skimmer windows to a brown plate, his hands had felt the lander slide before three abort warnings found language. He had been right. Afterward they printed his face on recruitment panels beside the phrase TRUST HUMAN JUDGMENT UNDER UNCERTAINTY. He had hated the font. He had kept one of the panels folded in a drawer.

“Course-correction burn in ninety,” Sofia said.

“Guidance accepts burn solution.”

“Say full state.”

Jun breathed through his nose. “Main nav: green. Inertial: green. Optical A/B: green. Optical C: contaminated, consistent wrong. Lidar forward: low return but inside margin. Radar quiet. Cabin pressure nominal. Tank slosh inside predicted. Attitude error small and boring.”

“Do not flatter boring.”

“Boring is the only saint I trust.”

“Park.”

“Eighty-three seconds.”

A soft clink came from somewhere behind him. Loose utensil or sample cap. Asterion was never truly silent. It ticked, moved air, rubbed fabric against restraint, made itself known as a built thing trying to keep six bodies alive between a planet they had left and a planet every child had been promised.

Leila said, “Tray four has a pressure ripple.”

Sofia’s voice narrowed. “Biology, repeat.”

“Tray four mount registered a pressure ripple. Sample containment intact. No rupture.”

Amara cut in. “That mount sits on bay two structure. I want strain branch B2 on my panel.”

“You have it,” Mateo said. Then, softer, away from the channel or too near his mic: “Come on.”

Jun kept his eyes on the burn timer. Seventy-one.

“Pilot,” Sofia said.

“Here.”

“I need your threshold for manual hold.”

“Two primary disagreements or one disagreement plus tactile drift.”

“Tactile drift is not a measurement.”

“It is when I’m right.”

“Park.”

He almost said something funny. The words came up, saw Sofia’s reflection, and left.

“Manual hold at two primary disagreements,” he said. “If C stays alone, we burn.”

“Good.”

Not praise. Sofia did not spend praise in a cockpit. Good meant the procedure had found the next rung.

“Already have.”

Jun’s mouth moved before the laugh reached him, a breathy little leak. He checked it, bit the inside of his cheek, tasted old blood and recycled air.

“Forty-five.”

The star field on his left repeater blinked.

Not blinked. Returned.

He had seen that exact scatter twelve minutes earlier during calibration handoff: three faint points tight above a brighter blue-white anchor, a dark scratch where the sensor had dropped a column, one background galaxy like dust on a lens. It came back with the same dead column. Same framing. Same tiny error in the grid overlay where C had been lazy after the last patch.

“C repeated again,” he said.

Sofia did not move. In the glass, her mouth vanished into the dark. “Timestamp.”

“Current timestamp. Old content.”

“Checksum?”

“Valid.”

Amara swore under her breath. “Valid how?”

“Don’t know. It thinks it is clean.”

Sera would have hated that, if Sera had been born into this room instead of years later into its forensic hunger. Jun did not have Sera. He had Sofia’s command voice tightening by fractions and the cockpit glass showing Mars with perfect ordinary patience.

“Thirty-five,” he said. “A and B still current. Mars still right.”

“Wrong in no measurable way,” Mateo said.

Sofia: “Payload, say only your panel.”

“Sorry.”

But the sentence stayed in the cockpit. Wrong in no measurable way. Jun wanted to throw it out an airlock. Instruments were allowed to be wrong. Pilots were allowed to be wrong. A planet in the window was not allowed to be right while the sky behind it pulled old paper from a file.

He flexed his hand once above the attitude pad.

“Pilot recommendation,” Sofia said.

“Continue to burn. Flag C contaminated. We can fly consistent.”

“Can you fly duplicated?”

“If it lies the same way twice, yes.”

“That was not the question.”

“It’s the answer I have.”

Asterion made another sound. Not clink now. A dry pop from the frame above his left shoulder. The hull contracting? Thermal shift? A fastener giving up a fraction of a millimeter?

Amara heard it through structure before the loop carried it. “Bay two strain spike.”

Sofia: “Magnitude.”

“Small. Ugly. Wait.”

“Do not wait on channel.”

“Then stop asking before the number settles.”

Jun watched the burn timer pass twenty.

The stars on A and B changed.

They did not smear. They did not cut to black. They changed with the crisp confidence of a display obeying a command. A became C. B became C. The old calibration field sat on all three optical repeaters, each with a current timestamp, each with a valid checksum, each showing the dead column that should have lived only in a memory buffer already overwritten.

Mars stayed.

Mars did not jump. Mars did not duplicate. It sat in the forward glass, not on the repeaters, red and physically there, the destination that had taught humans to point upward in classrooms and then punish anyone who stopped.

Jun said, “All optical star fields repeated.”

Sofia’s command voice went soft enough that everyone leaned toward it. “Burn hold.”

“Burn hold,” Jun echoed. His finger moved. The pad accepted. The timer froze at fourteen seconds.

Asterion’s engines remained quiet.

For one foolish beat the cockpit looked saved because nothing loud happened.

Then the radar returned a corridor.

It appeared on the auxiliary panel as a low-confidence wire trace, too close, too intimate. Rails, angles, a rectangular void. It looked like a passage inside the ship, but the radar was forward-facing and the space ahead contained black distance, Mars, and whatever the model said they had nearly reached. Jun tried to reject it as an internal reflection. The panel accepted the rejection, then redrew the corridor with higher confidence.

“That’s ours,” he said.

Sofia: “Explain.”

“I’m getting interior geometry on forward radar.”

“Which compartment?”

He enlarged, rotated, matched the angle. The line of a handrail with one patched grip. The little maintenance notch Amara had complained about because it caught sleeves. The cockpit did not have that notch.

“Service corridor between med and payload,” he said.

Elias, very quiet: “I am in med.”

Mateo said, “I’m in payload.”

“Do you see each other?” Sofia asked.

“No,” Elias said.

“No,” Mateo said, overlapping.

Amara: “Radar cannot see through us like that.”

“Correct,” Sofia said.

Jun hated her for saying correct. He loved her for it.

The attitude pad pulsed under his palm. It wanted new authority. The ship had held the burn. The ship now asked for the next human lie: continue, abort, hold, correct. Four buttons. Four small altars for people who needed the universe to come in choices.

“Manual attitude request,” the guidance system said in its calm recorded voice.

“No,” Sofia said to the machine.

It asked again without hearing her. “Manual attitude request.”

Jun’s right hand drifted lower.

Sofia saw it in the reflection. “Park.”

“I’m not engaging.”

“Hand away.”

“It’s asking because the inertials still see a drift.”

“Hand away from the pad.”

He lifted two fingers. Not enough.

“Jun.”

His name from her, not role. That was worse than alarm.

He pulled his hand back and hooked it under the restraint edge. His fingertips found the metal lip and pressed until the glove seam bit skin.

“Inertial state,” Sofia said.

Jun swallowed. “A says smooth approach. B says lateral displacement point four meters per second above correction margin. C says we are where we were three minutes ago.”

“Say again.”

“I can’t make that better by saying it again.”

“Say it.”

“Inertial A: smooth. B: lateral drift. C: old position.”

Leila’s breathing crossed the channel. “Tray four has rupture.”

Amara: “No impact. No heat.”

Elias: “Leila, can you feel your hands?”

“I can feel them.”

“Good. Stay with the sample.”

“I am with it.”

The cockpit lights dipped once. Not a failure dip. More like the system had blinked and returned from somewhere else embarrassed.

Jun scanned velocity, attitude, acceleration. Numbers existed. Numbers had old authority because a number did not care whether you believed in it. He read them out.

“Velocity relative solution one: nine point eight four kilometers per second. Solution two: nine point eight four. Solution three: no update. Attitude error increasing by point zero one per second on B only. Fuel pressure stable. Thermal forward nominal. Radar still drawing our own throat.”

“Own throat?” Mateo said.

“Sorry.”

“Do not be sorry,” Sofia said. “Be accurate.”

“Forward radar returns interior Asterion corridor geometry, probable service corridor med-payload, impossible source.”

“Better.”

It was not better.

The old star field repeated again, but repetition had lost shape as a word. It was there, then there, then there, like a hand tapping the same bruise to prove the body still owned pain.

Jun remembered Tycho dust not as hero story but as texture: grit hissing across the lander skin, suit sweat cooling in the small of his back, abort tone repeating until it became a cheap song. He had found the drift by how the craft’s vibration changed under his calves. His hands had known the surface through machine and dust. Afterward, people said instinct. They needed instinct to be almost holy because otherwise it was only training, luck, and a body scared enough to listen.

Asterion gave him no vibration he could trust.

“Manual path,” Sofia said.

“Bad.”

“Say why.”

“Because every correction vector depends on which lie we privilege.”

Amara snapped, “Good sentence. Hate it.”

Sofia: “If we hold?”

“We enter drift on B. Or we are stationary on C. Or A is right and nothing happens because we held too early.”

“If we burn?”

“We may correct into a wall I can’t see.”

Silence arrived too fast. The word wall had no place in the cockpit. It belonged to childhood, prisons, old cities, pressure bulkheads. It did not belong between Earth and Mars.

Sofia said, “There is no wall in procedure.”

“Then into whatever the procedure was too polite to name.”

“Park.”

“I know.”

Elias broke in, all physician now. “Jun, unclench your jaw.”

Jun found pain near his ear. He released. “Thank you, doctor of my beautiful face.”

“Still ugly but perfused.”

The laugh came out of Jun then.

Short. Stupid. Wrong. It cracked against the cockpit glass and died inside his helmet mic. He had meant to make a smaller sound. A pressure vent. A human valve opening because the alternative was teeth through cheek. It came out bright enough that later anyone could cut it free and sell it as joy.

Sofia said, “Park.”

He looked at Mars.

The red disk remained clean. The star field behind it repeated. The forward radar showed their own corridor. The optical overlay labeled the repeated stars as current. The inertials disagreed with each other in units too small for terror and too large for flight. The cockpit smelled faintly of warmed polymer where the attitude pad had heated under his hand.

Then the glass changed.

Not Mars. Not the red disk, not the weather line, not the light on the frame. Around Mars, the black stopped being deep. It flattened without becoming a surface. It did not shine. It gave the cameras nothing they could name. Jun had flown over oceans at night where cloudless dark swallowed distance; he had flown above lunar pits where shadow was a physical fact. This was neither. The stars sat on it.

No. The stars repeated on it.

The old field was not behind Mars. It was near the window, near the ship, near his own hand reflected on the glass. Distance had lost its load path.

Jun heard himself breathe. The channel compressed the sound into a clipped scrape.

“That’s not sky,” he said.

Nobody answered.

He looked down because pilots looked down when the window became treacherous. The attitude pad had gone from warm to hot. A small blister had risen on the polymer near his thumb mark, cloudy white as old glass. His glove had not touched it in seconds.

“Pad thermal,” he said. The words came out useful. Bless the mouth for its training. “Manual pad thermal event. No command input.”

Amara: “Power draw?”

“None listed.”

“That’s cute.”

“Don’t say cute.”

Sofia: “Can you isolate manual attitude?”

“Yes.”

“Do it.”

Jun opened the guard and keyed isolation. The panel asked for confirmation. He confirmed. The indicator went amber, then green, then amber again.

“Status unstable.”

“Hard isolate.”

“That removes my manual authority.”

“Hard isolate.”

His hand moved across the panel. A pilot’s hand. Expensive, celebrated, insured. It took away its own job.

“Manual attitude hard isolated,” he said.

The ship rolled one degree left.

Not much. Enough for Mars to climb a finger’s width in the glass, wrong in the body before it was wrong in numbers.

“Uncommanded roll,” he said. “One degree, correction pending.”

Sofia: “Source.”

“No thruster fire. No wheel command.”

Amara: “Wheel three just logged torque without power.”

“Impossible.”

“Yes, Park, I was waiting for you to approve the word.”

The old star field vanished from B. Returned on A. C showed a black plane with Mars printed through it.

Jun stared at C.

He knew the phrase would sound insane if he said it. A black plane. Printed through. That was not engineering. That was a child describing a window after a rock hit it. He needed numbers. He needed the discipline of a cockpit where every terror stood in line and gave its name.

“Optical C returns non-depth image,” he said.

Sofia: “Non-depth is not a term.”

“I’m making it one.”

“Describe.”

“Flat black field. Mars visible through or on it. Star absence except repeat artifacts at edges.”

Mateo whispered, “Oh God.”

Leila said something under her breath. It did not enter the transcript cleanly. Prayer or breath or a word the machine refused.

Sofia: “No theological language on command.”

Mateo snapped, “That was fear, Commander.”

The wrong thing, too early.

Sofia turned toward him though he could not see her from the cockpit. “Payload, mute unless your panel changes.”

“My panel changed five minutes ago. We just didn’t know where to put it.”

“Mute.”

Mateo’s channel clicked off hard.

Elias: “Sofia.”

“Medical?”

“His pulse is high.”

“Whose?”

A pause. “All of ours.”

Jun almost laughed again. It caught behind his teeth and stayed there, safer as pain.

The ship rolled another half degree. Mars slid along the window frame. The forward radar corridor changed angle in perfect match, as if the returned interior were obeying the roll more faithfully than the real ship.

“Roll increasing,” Jun said. “One point five. No source. Hard-isolated manual remains isolated.”

“Can you counter on auxiliary?”

“I can command it. I can’t promise what hears me.”

Sofia’s reflection leaned closer to the glass. “Pilot recommendation.”

He wanted to say burn. He wanted the old bright madness of action. A craft under threat should be flown. That had been his bargain with fear since the first trainer that dropped under him like a bad elevator. Touch the controls. Make the world answer.

The world had stopped answering in cockpit grammar.

“No burn,” he said. “No manual correction past this point.”

Sofia was still for one breath. “Say again.”

“No burn. No correction. Passive hold if we can get it. Data burst. We are not flying this.”

Amara’s channel opened. She made one sound, almost approval, almost grief.

Sofia said, “Crew, command copies pilot recommendation. Asterion entering passive hold. Systems, prep burst.”

Amara: “Already screaming at it.”

“Biology, secure trays.”

Leila: “Tray four is no longer stable.”

“Secure what remains.”

“I am.”

“Medical, crew status.”

Elias began with bodies. “Jun responsive, jaw tension, hand report intact. Sofia voice controlled. Mateo tachy by sound, muted and probably furious. Leila says hands intact. Amara angry, which is baseline. I am—”

The channel clipped.

Not dropout. Clip. His sentence cut in the middle of the self.

“Medical,” Sofia said.

Static answered.

“Elias,” Jun said.

Static folded into a breath that might have been anyone’s.

Then Elias came back, overloud. “—fine. I’m fine. We dropped half a second.”

“We dropped eight,” Amara said.

“No,” Elias said.

“Yes,” Jun said. His timer had jumped. “Eight point two.”

“I was speaking.”

“You were gone.”

“I was speaking,” Elias repeated, less to them than to the part of the room where time had taken offense.

The cockpit glass showed the old star field again. Not outside now. On the left inner pane, reflected where no display should be bright enough to write. Jun raised one hand and the stars crossed his knuckles.

He should have been able to make a joke. Something about cheap projection work. Something about Mars needing better stagehands. His mouth opened and found nothing shaped for people.

Sofia’s command voice had no extra air left in it. “Burst status.”

Amara: “Archive burst armed. Command records, cockpit audio, structural, tray four, nav state. I have checksum warnings before send.”

“Send anyway.”

“Already did. Again. It says first send completed in three minutes.”

“That is future tense,” Sofia said.

“It thinks it isn’t.”

The ship gave a long, low metallic complaint.

Jun felt it through the brace first. Not sound, not data. A load path moving through a vessel built by people who had believed every stress could be modeled if the budget was large enough and the equations honest enough. The complaint passed under his foot and up his leg. The hull did not shake like a craft in turbulence. It tightened, as if a giant hand had found a seam.

“Structural load forward,” he said.

Amara: “I have bay two, bay one, spine—no. No. That is not load. That is not—”

Sofia: “Okonkwo.”

“I have compression across discontinuous members.”

“Meaning.”

“It is pressing parts that do not touch.”

Sofia’s face in reflection shifted. Her jaw worked once. “All crew brace.”

Jun locked both feet. The straps across his thighs bit down. The cockpit glass held Mars in a red circle and the flat black around it. The repeated stars occupied the wrong distance. The service corridor on radar showed a handrail patch he had touched that morning on his way to coffee.

He pictured the handrail, polished by six people. Elias with knees tucked, pushing off too hard. Leila avoiding Mateo’s drifting basalt pouch. Sofia aligning a loose strap before public feed because loose objects made history untidy. Amara swearing at a maintenance notch. Mateo rehearsing a lesson to children who would never forgive them for being symbols.

His own hand hovered above a dead manual pad.

Skill remained in the hand. That was the insult. Nothing had taken it. He could have docked blind, killed spin, found a landing out of dust by the chatter in a frame. He could still read drift in his bones. The body had brought its whole education to a locked door.

“All crew braced,” Elias said, though he could not know that.

Sofia: “Asterion command record, Arendt. We have entered a Boundary anomaly or unknown physical—”

Amara cut across her. “Commander, forward hull temperature split.”

“Magnitude.”

“Impossible.”

“Number.”

“Forty-one point eight at one fastener. Adjacent cold.”

Jun looked at the fastener line above the glass. One screw head, ordinary and tiny, had caught a bead of light. For half a second he hated it personally.

The radar corridor filled the auxiliary panel. It now showed a second geometry behind the first. Cockpit. His cockpit. A nested wire trace of the place he occupied, returned from ahead of them as if space had swallowed the plan and offered it back wet.

“Radar returns cockpit,” he said.

Sofia: “Live?”

“It’s us.”

“Delay?”

“I don’t know.”

On the wire trace, a small shape marked the pilot couch. It had a heat signature. It had his heat signature. The figure’s right hand rested on the manual pad.

Jun’s actual right hand was hooked under the restraint.

He laughed then, or did not. The record would argue.

In his body, no laugh came. His throat locked around a dry click. His tongue pressed to the roof of his mouth. He watched the returned figure keep its hand on the pad and felt the old superstition rise so sharply it had a taste: if he could make his hand match the return, if he could close the disagreement with touch, the ship might become flyable again.

“Jun,” Elias said from far away.

He did not move his hand.

The wire trace flickered. The returned figure’s mouth opened in a little brightness the radar had no right to know.

Asterion rolled again.

Mars slid out from behind the center strut and then, without moving, appeared still centered on optical A. The window and the panel disagreed on where a planet was. His inner ear chose the window. His training chose neither.

Sofia said, “Park, report.”

“Window Mars differs from optical Mars,” he said. “Star field repeat on A. C flat black with Mars. Radar cockpit return includes pilot state inconsistent with present.”

“Can you maintain passive?”

“No.”

The word came clean. No drama. No heroics.

Sofia’s next breath hit the mic. “Can you recover any control?”

Jun looked at his hands.

They were steady. That offended him more than shaking would have. Two hands, still trained, still warm, resting in a craft that no longer honored the pact between input and result.

“No,” he said.
