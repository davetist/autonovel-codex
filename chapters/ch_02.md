## Chapter 2: Quito Selection Tape

The first frame gave Mara the underside of Mars.

 Polymer over alloy ribs, nine meters across, suspended from three black cables above the Quito Interplanetary Assembly Hall. Its southern ice cap had been painted with more mercy than data allowed. Under it, six chairs waited on polished basalt.

Mara’s archival note, 2039, Selection Record QIAH-AST-CREW/PSY-7:

Stage geometry exerts useful pressure. Candidates enter through rear aisle under public-facing Mars model. Audience sightline places model above candidate shoulders during interview. Recommend retaining for announcement sequence. Strong collective effect without overt nationalist load.

Later, in the review copy, someone from Public Integration had highlighted useful pressure and written, in green, Excellent.

Mara had answered in the margin with a smaller hand:

Pressure is not neutral.

The tape stuttered at the thirty-one-second mark. A checksum patch appeared in the lower right. The Assembly Hall lights corrected themselves from gold to white.  Outside the glass, Quito dropped away in pale morning layers. Inside, every surface had been cleaned until it could carry a ceremony.

The candidates entered without music.

 She wore no public smile until the cameras found her. Jun Park followed, loose in the shoulders, one foot bracing out of habit on a floor that offered him gravity. Amara Okonkwo came in with a pencil behind her ear, which Protocol later requested removed for the live announcement and which she later returned to the same place. Leila Haddad adjusted the cuff at her wrist before she sat. Mateo Ríos looked up at the model of Mars and laughed once, too softly for the hall microphones but not for the selection tape.

Elias Venn came last.

Mara, watching from the psychology table in the second tier, had made a tick beside his entrance time.  She could see the left hand first, long fingers, the crooked one from the basketball hoop behind their mother’s church. He had broken it at eleven and lied for six hours because Mara was supposed to be watching him while their mother slept through the afternoon with curtains closed.

On tape, Elias found the chair with his nameplate and leaned toward Mateo.

ELIAS VENN: Did they tell you the planet would be overhead?

MATEO RÍOS: I assumed symbolism was included in the travel package.

ELIAS: Good. I hate hidden fees.

A few people in the audience laughed. The sound was small, grateful, too quick. The candidates had been in closed assessment for three weeks, and public observers had begun to treat any unscripted warmth as proof that the mission could survive its own cost.

Mara’s note:

E. Venn uses humor to reduce status heat without undermining procedure. Ríos responds well. Arendt observes, does not correct. Okonkwo looks at overhead suspension, not speaker. Haddad scans audience before smiling. Park watches cameras.

The suspended Mars turned by one degree every four minutes. 

She had won that argument and taken pride in it.

The stress interview began with Sofia. Then Jun. Then Amara, Leila, Mateo. Elias was scheduled fifth, but the panel changed order at 10:12 local after a sponsor delegate requested more “medical reassurance” before lunch. Mara objected through the table channel. The objection logged and failed.

CHAIR ORTEGA: Dr. Venn, you have reviewed the confinement incident packet from Tycho Recovery?

ELIAS: Yes.

CHAIR ORTEGA: You understand why the panel raises it.

ELIAS: I understand why the panel keeps raising it.

A breath moved through the room. Sofia’s head turned a few degrees.

CHAIR ORTEGA: You find the question repetitive?

ELIAS: I find it consistent. Different diagnosis.

PANELIST HUANG: Then answer it again. If Commander Arendt becomes incapacitated during transfer burn and Pilot Park is occupied with manual control, who directs crew medical triage?

ELIAS: I do.

HUANG: If Specialist Ríos is conscious and Engineer Okonkwo is not?

ELIAS: I direct triage. Mateo assists if trained, if oriented, and if his hands are steady. If his hands aren’t steady, he tells me. If he lies, I take the task back.

MATEO: I’m right here.

ELIAS: That was the public education portion.

This time the laughter came from the candidates first. Even Sofia’s mouth shifted. Amara took the pencil from behind her ear and held it between two fingers, as if keeping herself from throwing it at him.

Mara wrote:

Good recovery after hostile framing. Converts hierarchy question into bodily criteria. Watch: excessive ease under pressure may conceal self-erasure.

She had paused before writing the last word. The screen on the psychology table reflected her own face over the transcript line. Thirty-six years old, hair pinned low, jacket too warm for Quito’s glass heat, no jewelry except the old seminary ring she no longer wore on the correct finger. She was already known for the conversion drills. Not famous. Useful. A person called in when astronauts, flood coordinators, reactor teams, or lunar miners needed fear turned into steps.

She had written those four channels on a whiteboard in Houston two years earlier. Elias had visited the training room, taken one of the markers, and added:

Fear into snacks.

Then:

Fear into sibling complaint.

The panel tape showed him now under the model of Mars, making the room breathe.

CHAIR ORTEGA: Dr. Venn, describe the purpose of a conversion drill in your own terms.

Elias looked toward the second tier.

Not long. Half a second. On the recording, it could pass for scanning the panel.

ELIAS: You’re asking the wrong Venn.

ORTEGA: We are asking the candidate.

ELIAS: Then the candidate says a conversion drill gives fear somewhere to go besides the nearest throat.

PANELIST KOVÁCS: That is polished.

ELIAS: I have family in the field.

KOVÁCS: Give us unpolished.

Elias rubbed the crooked finger with his thumb. The gesture was tiny. Mara marked it.

ELIAS: Panic is work without a task. If you give it a task, sometimes it stops flooding the room.

KOVÁCS: Sometimes.

ELIAS: Yes.

KOVÁCS: And when it does not?

ELIAS: Then you stop calling it panic and ask what it knows.

Mara had missed the answer in the room because Ortega’s assistant dropped a tablet at the same instant and the front-row microphones took the sound like a cracked plate. On tape, cleaned and amplified, Elias’s voice came through too level.

Then you stop calling it panic and ask what it knows.

Mara added no note in 2039. In the later review copy, after Asterion, after Orpheus, after the paused Asterion wreck frame recovered by Orpheus had entered children’s bedrooms and court filings, she typed one line beneath the blank:

This should have troubled me more.

The tape advanced.

  He carried an Atacama stone in a sample pouch, not for luck, he said, but because every geologist was allowed one private absurdity.

The panel brought up the education payload in the ninth minute of his interview.

PANELIST SINGH: You understand that the educational stream attached to your role will reach an estimated one-point-eight billion minors over mission duration.

MATEO: That is too many minors.

SINGH: Excuse me?

MATEO: For one person. For one stream. For one face.

CHAIR ORTEGA: The question concerns readiness.

MATEO: I am ready to teach. I am less ready to be laminated.

Jun made a sound off microphone. Amara looked down at her shoes.

SINGH: The Accord believes public education is central to mission legitimacy.

MATEO: I know.

SINGH: Do you object to the obligation?

Mateo looked up at Mars again. The model’s shadow crossed his cheek and moved on.

MATEO: No. I object to pretending it is smaller than it is.

Mara’s note:

Ríos identifies symbolic load accurately. No evidence of avoidance. Anger present, contained. His discomfort is protective. The role risks mascot compression by media apparatus. With preparation, he may resist flattening better than candidates who enjoy visibility.

Below that, in the summary field, she wrote the phrase that would follow him into three classified memos and one family lawsuit:

Symbolically mission-critical.

 A geologist who could make Mars a place rather than a flag could keep public attention from curdling into spectator appetite. Mateo’s presence helped the mission hold. It also placed a classroom under his skin.

PUBLIC INTEGRATION COMMENT, appended 2039:

Dr. Venn’s phrasing confirms Ríos should anchor youth continuity programming.

MARA VENN PRIVATE REPLY, unsent:

That is not what the phrase means.

The unsent reply remained in the archive because the selection software auto-saved drafts every ninety seconds. Mara had forgotten this until the review bundle reopened it twelve years later on a shuttle with bad coffee and Pacific weather warnings stacked at the edge of the screen.

On tape, Mateo leaned forward.

MATEO: If I tell a child Mars is waiting, do you want that as geology or as promise?

SINGH: Both, if possible.

MATEO: Then no.

The room missed its cue. A sponsor aide turned toward the public camera bank. Chair Ortega’s pen stopped.

MATEO: Geology can wait. Promise is something adults should issue with a warranty.

The panel took a recess after that. Officially, water break. Actually, containment.

Mara walked down from the second tier with her tablet hugged against her ribs. The air inside the hall tasted of glass chill and old stone warmed by lights. A catering assistant had put bowls of oranges near the candidate corridor because someone in Public had decided citrus read international and clean. The fruit smelled too sweet in the thin air.

Elias stood beside the water station, unscrewing a bottle. He had already loosened his collar. Mateo was three paces away, being handled gently by two media psychologists who kept using his first name.

“You’re making friends,” Mara said.

Elias turned. “I was promised no sibling commentary until post-selection.”

“You waived that when you looked at me during a panel question.”

“I looked at the general area of moral contamination.”

“Answer the question.”

“I did.”

“Answer me.”

His smile stayed, but it lost the public angle. He capped the bottle without drinking.

“He’s right,” Elias said.

“Mateo?”

“If we ask him to make Mars bearable for children, we should admit that’s what we’re doing.”

“We do admit it.”

“In rooms like this?”

Mara glanced toward the basalt wall where the mission flags hung. The Pan-African Launch Compact flag had been placed half a meter from the European Space Directorate banner because a draft arrangement had put extraction oversight behind life support and caused a thirty-six-hour diplomatic fight. The public would see harmony. The rigging showed through from the side.

“We admit it where it can change procedure,” she said.

Elias laughed once. Not cruelly. Tired.

“That is a very Mara sentence.”

“Is it wrong?”

“No. That’s the problem.”

A staffer approached, saw their faces, and veered toward the oranges.

Mara lowered her voice. “You are too smooth in there.”

“I’m sweating through a shirt that costs more than my first car.”

“Your answers.”

“They asked me to perform calm.”

“And you performed it.”

“I am applying for a job where panic is discouraged.”

“You said fear should be asked what it knows.”

“It sounded good.”

“It sounded rehearsed.”

“Mara.”

She looked at his hand. He had rubbed the crooked finger red.

“Do you know when you are frightened?” she asked.

He opened the bottle again, took two swallows, and wiped his mouth with the back of his hand.

“Usually after.”

“Elias.”

“What do you want me to say? I can list symptoms. Tight throat, tingling hands, less patience for donors. Mild urge to fake appendicitis and leave by service lift.”

“Use that sentence in the room.”

“I’m not giving Ortega appendicitis.”

“Use the fear.”

He leaned closer, dropping into the older rhythm, kitchen rhythm, hospital hallway rhythm, the voice he used when their mother asked whether he had called the insurance office and he had already called twice.

“You taught me that.”

The corridor noise moved around them. Mateo said, too loudly, “I know what youth continuity means,” and one of the media psychologists answered in the soft tone used on people near cameras.

Mara held the tablet harder.

“I taught you to convert it,” she said. “Not erase it.”

Elias looked at the suspended Mars through the open hall doors. From this angle, the model’s support cables were visible. So was a maintenance worker in a black harness, waiting above the lighting rail with a cloth in his hand to remove fingerprints from a planet no one had touched.

“Maybe I’m converting,” Elias said.

The recess ended.

ARCHIVE INSERT: Panel Resumption. Audio stable. Visual angle QIAH-CAM-4. Candidate E. Venn recalled for supplemental stress scenario at request of Medical Board.

CHAIR ORTEGA: Dr. Venn, final scenario. You are seventy-six days into transit. Sleep debt across crew. Communication delay significant. Commander Arendt orders conservation measures that reduce private-recording time. Specialist Haddad objects on religious grounds. Engineer Okonkwo objects on morale grounds. Pilot Park makes light of the disagreement and worsens it. What do you do?

ELIAS: I check whether anyone has eaten.

ORTEGA: Assume nutrition is adequate.

ELIAS: I don’t assume that in a conflict.

HUANG: Nutrition is adequate.

ELIAS: I ask Haddad whether the objection concerns prayer itself or privacy around prayer. I ask Okonkwo whether the morale concern is crew function or dislike of command tone. I tell Park to stop helping.

JUN PARK: Fair.

ELIAS: I ask Commander Arendt whether the conservation measure is reversible and what failure it prevents. Then I make everyone drink something with salt in it.

KOVÁCS: You reduce a values conflict to hydration?

ELIAS: No. I remove preventable stupidity from the values conflict.

 It would be used in medical school recruitment after the launch. After the Break, a private company selling anti-collapse supplements stole the phrase for an advertisement until Ruth Venn joined a class-action complaint and made them take it down.

KOVÁCS: And if the conflict remains?

ELIAS: Then it gets named properly.

KOVÁCS: Which is?

Elias breathed in. The microphone caught it.

ELIAS: Whether the mission owns all private acts because the public paid for the ship.

The hall quieted.

KOVÁCS: Does it?

ELIAS: No.

CHAIR ORTEGA: That answer may concern Public Integration.

ELIAS: It should concern them less than the alternative.

ORTEGA: Which is?

ELIAS: A crew that hides its real life from review because review keeps taking pieces for broadcast.

Mara’s note:

High trust value for crew. Medium risk for public-framing friction. Strong boundary recognition between private act and mission asset. This will matter during transit. Recommend clear private-log protections before launch.

That recommendation went to Legal. Legal returned language that preserved private recording in principle and gave Mission Integration broad exceptions for historical, educational, psychological, and safety review. 

The tape moved into candidate-comparison review.

The room changed after the public panel. Cameras powered down. The suspended Mars kept rotating because no one had sent the command to stop it. Without audience light, the model looked cheaper. Brush marks showed near Olympus Mons. One cable hummed faintly against an air vent.

Mara sat with Dr. Yuna El-Amin from Medical, Commander Arendt’s naval assessor, two IIA observers, and Pavel Ilyin, who was not yet Director in title but already had the stillness of someone seated near doors because people would eventually need to pass him. He wore a dark suit too heavy for Quito and took notes on paper while everyone else used tablets.

The final medical slot came down to two candidates.

Dr. Elias Venn: flight surgeon, aerospace medicine, crisis fieldwork, strong confinement tolerance, high crew-regulation value, sibling relation to mission psychologist disclosed and firewalled.

Dr. Ansel Vo: flight surgeon, aerospace medicine, Antarctic overwinter command medical lead, equivalent technical score, lower public warmth, superior sleep discipline, no family conflict.

Yuna set both profiles side by side.

YUNA EL-AMIN: From medical metrics alone, Vo is cleaner.

PAVEL ILYIN: Cleaner in what sense?

YUNA: Fewer attached narratives.

Pavel looked at Mara. “Dr. Venn?”

The old tape showed Mara younger, straighter in the chair, professional pride still intact enough to be mistaken for neutrality.

MARA VENN: Attached narratives can be risk or function.

YUNA: They can also become failure points.

MARA: Yes.

PAVEL: Speak plainly.

Mara touched the stylus to the table once. The contact made a small click. In the recovered audio, the click sounded louder than her voice.

MARA: Vo is stable. Venn stabilizes others.

The naval assessor shifted.

ASSESSOR BRIGGS: You are related to him.

MARA: Yes.

BRIGGS: That should narrow your language.

MARA: It has.

PAVEL: How?

MARA: I am excluding affection as evidence.

No one answered. The suspended Mars turned above them, red and obedient, built to rotate on command.

YUNA: You cannot exclude it cleanly.

MARA: No.

Pavel’s pen moved. He wrote one line, stopped, underlined something.

PAVEL: Then tell us what remains.

Mara’s 2039 note, attached to closed deliberation:

E. Venn’s calm is not absence of fear. It is trained relational management reinforced by family role and medical practice. Risk: delayed self-report, overidentification with crew stability, use of humor to defer own distress. Benefit: immediate reduction of group arousal, high likelihood of preserving function during ambiguous threat, strong capacity to translate fear into task without contempt for fear.

A. Vo: excellent candidate. Lower symbolic load. Strong self-care profile. Less spontaneous regulation of others. Better individual resilience; lower crew-field effect.

Recommendation: Venn, with mandated private supervision and explicit monitoring for calm-mask behavior.

On the tape, Briggs read the last phrase aloud.

BRIGGS: Calm-mask behavior.

YUNA: That concerns me.

MARA: It concerns me too.

BRIGGS: Yet you recommend him.

MARA: Because the risk can be monitored.

YUNA: Can it?

Mara watched herself answer.

MARA: Better than a crew panic field in transit.

It was a good answer. It was also a door.

Pavel leaned back.

PAVEL: You are making him structural.

MARA: I am naming an existing load.

PAVEL: There is a difference?

MARA: In engineering, yes.

Pavel gave the faintest smile. “This is not engineering.”

Amara Okonkwo was not in the room to object. If she had been, she might have said that all loads became engineering once a structure depended on them.

Yuna opened the private psych packet. “There is another issue. His family role. Lowering room temperature, as the note says.”

Mara kept her hands still.

YUNA: He may experience visible fear as failure of care.

MARA: That is why I wrote mandated supervision.

PAVEL: By whom?

MARA: Not me.

YUNA: Good.

Mara looked toward the closed hall doors. The candidates waited somewhere behind them with water bottles, media handlers, and bowls of bright fruit. Elias would be asking if anyone had eaten. Mateo would be angry and trying to make anger sound like principle. Sofia would be aligning her folder edges. Jun would be bored enough to become charming. Amara would have found a vent panel to distrust. Leila would be quiet until someone made her prayer public property.

Pavel tapped the paper.

PAVEL: If we accept this recommendation, and if the relation becomes public objection?

MARA: We disclose that I recused from final medical clearance.

YUNA: You have not recused.

MARA: I will.

BRIGGS: After giving the ranking that sets the medical clearance frame.

Mara looked at him then.

MARA: Yes.

Briggs sat back. Yuna rubbed the bridge of her nose. Pavel continued to watch Mara with a patience that would later become one of his weapons.

PAVEL: Dr. Venn, is your brother the best candidate for Asterion?

The answer she gave in the room came quickly.

MARA: For this crew, yes.

The archive preserved the next two seconds with unusual clarity. Air system, chair creak, someone’s sleeve against paper. Above them, the model Mars clicked once in its suspension track.

PAVEL: Not best alone.

MARA: There is no alone on Asterion.

He wrote that down too.

Selection Addendum, private psychological ranking:

1. Arendt: command authority. Monitor sealed-compartment reflex under moral ambiguity.
2. Park: high manual confidence. Risk when threat resists skill translation.
3. Okonkwo: material truth anchor. Risk contempt for symbolic needs.
4. Haddad: privacy integrity, disciplined spiritual practice. Protect from public instrumentalization.
5. Venn: crew regulator. Calm stabilizes others. Monitor mask.
6. Ríos: symbolically mission-critical. Preparation must include refusal scripts for youth programming.

Below the list, Mara added the conversion drill schedule:

Week 1: fear naming under task load.
Week 2: humor and deflection, allowed uses and limits.
Week 3: private act versus public mission.
Week 4: care without rescue fantasy.
Week 5: silence tolerance.
Week 6: failure of horizon language.

The last item had been provisional. She had written it after Galveston, after a woman on an evacuation bus asked a priest whether God could see through floodwater and the priest answered by handing her a bottle of water and lifting her child onto his lap. Failure of horizon language meant the moment when the story that brought a person to the task stopped carrying them. In training, Mara treated that moment as expected. The candidate learned to stop reaching for the broken phrase and pick up the next action.

She had not yet asked what happened when the broken phrase was Mars.

The selection vote passed Elias by one abstention. Vo was named alternate medical. The official record framed the decision as compatibility, clinical breadth, public communication skill. Mara’s private packet carried the sharper sentence.

Calm stabilizes others.

The candidates returned for the announcement rehearsal in late afternoon. The hall had changed again. Public lights restored. Mars polished. Citrus removed after Leila asked why the table fruit was being staged beneath a planet with no biosphere. The answer from Public Integration was not recorded.

Each candidate stepped onto the basalt mark as their name was called for rehearsal. No applause, only cue tones.

SOFIA ARENDT.

She moved as if the floor were a deck at depth.

JUN PARK.

He saluted the wrong camera and got told not to do that.

AMARA OKONKWO.

She had put the pencil back behind her ear.

LEILA HADDAD.

She looked at the suspended Mars, then at the crew, as if checking which object the room intended to worship.

ELIAS VENN.

He stepped forward. His smile came half a second late.

MARA VENN ARCHIVAL NOTE:

Delay before public expression. Fatigue or reluctance. Recheck after final notification.

MATEO RÍOS.

Mateo stepped onto the mark and, instead of facing the camera, turned toward the empty rows where schoolchildren would sit for the announcement the next day. The rehearsal coordinator called his name again.

MATEO: I heard.

COORDINATOR: Camera three, please.

MATEO faced camera three. “Sorry,” he said, though his tone offered none.

The tape ended there in the standard selection archive.

The reopened bundle included one more file.

QIAH SERVICE CORRIDOR AUDIO, source: hall maintenance recorder. Chain incomplete. Content deemed non-decisional, retained due to candidate privacy review. Transcript confidence 0.81.

The image was black. Only audio. Water through pipes. Two people walking. A door seal releasing.

ELIAS: You got it.

MATEO: So did you.

ELIAS: I had nepotism.

MATEO: That is not funny.

ELIAS: No. It is a little funny.

MATEO: Is she why?

A pause. Fabric against wall.

ELIAS: Mara? No.

MATEO: That was fast.

ELIAS: She would cut me if I failed.

MATEO: I mean is she why you are like that.

Another pause. Longer.

ELIAS: Like what?

MATEO: Calm enough to make everyone else look loud.

The recorder caught Elias laughing under his breath. No public brightness in it.

ELIAS: That sounds expensive. I should invoice.

MATEO: Doctor.

ELIAS: I don’t know.

MATEO: That is the first answer today I believe.

The door seal engaged. The file clipped.

Mara had never seen that service-corridor transcript in 2039. Candidate privacy review had locked it outside her packet because the relation made her access improper. She saw it first during the post-loss audit, when improper had become the architecture of her life.

On the shuttle to Pacific relay, years later, the archive asked whether she wanted to append an interpretive note for Release Environment Review.

The cursor waited under Elias’s last line.

I don’t know.

Mara placed two fingers against the inside of her wrist, then removed them before the habit completed itself. The shuttle cabin smelled of bad coffee and warm insulation. Rain struck the small oval window in hard diagonal lines. Beyond the cloud, beyond the ocean and the gantry, Witness was approaching the place where all her clean recommendations had gone to lose their names.

She typed:

Quito record establishes that crew selection carried explicit symbolic burden before any Boundary evidence existed. My recommendation of E. Venn was professionally grounded and personally contaminated. Both facts are load-bearing.
