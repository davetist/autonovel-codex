Nadine closed the Asterion viewer with two fingers.

The last frame went black in the crew bay. For one second Witness showed Mara only the reflection of four faces in the dead glass, stretched by the curve of the display. Sera’s sleeves were pulled over her knuckles. Malik had one hand on the narrowband console, thumb resting beside the release queue as if pressure there could stop Earth from asking. Tomas Vale looked at the blank screen and began counting under his breath.

“Unscheduled viewing ends now,” Nadine said.

No one moved.

The bay kept its own small weather: air through vents, velcro lifting from a cuff, somebody’s swallow too close to a mic. The ship smelled of sterilized fabric and skin. A strip of handrail beside the viewer had been rubbed bright where crew reached for it during public check-ins. Mara had noticed the polish on day two and logged it under ordinary confinement stress. On day four she had added a private tag: repetitive grip behavior, broadcast-associated.

Now Sera’s hand was on that same patch.

Nadine keyed the lockout. “Archive access to Asterion public feed, wreck frame, and crew-private derivatives moves to scheduled review only. Two-person authorization. Medical or mission necessity.”

“Define necessity,” Mara said.

Nadine turned her head. “That was an order, not a vocabulary exercise.”

Mara took the sentence without answering. The bay display held the restriction notice in soft gray: ASTERION MATERIAL: DELAYED ACCESS. CREW SAFETY PROTOCOL 7. Malik looked away from it first.

“We have review windows already,” Sera said. “Data hygiene requires recurrence checks.”

“Data hygiene gets you scheduled recurrence checks.”

“The compulsion profile worsens if we pretend the material is contraband,” Mara said.

Nadine’s mouth held still. “The compulsion profile worsens if I let my crew spend off-watch time watching six dead people become instrument noise.”

“They were crew before they were material.”

“Then honor them by sleeping.”

Malik gave a small, ugly laugh. It sounded mechanical and embarrassed him at once. He wiped his palm down his thigh and leaned into the comms board. “Earth packet arrived during playback.”

“What class?” Nadine asked.

“Continuity digest. Public pressure index, ward signal, market advisory. Mumbai pre-open movement flagged.”

“Route to my station and Doctor Venn’s.”

“Already done.”

Mara looked at him.

Malik’s fingers paused above the keys. “You asked yesterday.”

“For public-fragment clips.”

“This packet includes release-risk language. Your profile gets it.”

Her profile. The ship had learned to place her grief into menus. Mara felt the small hard pulse in her wrist before she touched it. She kept her hands on the restraint strap instead.

Nadine saw anyway.

“Doctor Venn,” she said, lower. “Your monitoring plan goes live after sleep cycle.”

“It is live now.”

“Then update it. Asterion viewing is restricted.”

“Crew compliance will degrade if they think you are hiding evidence from them.”

Nadine unhooked her hand from the rail. She was taller than everyone in the bay, narrow in the shoulders, built less like command posters than a door kept shut during weather. “Crew compliance will degrade if they think the commander cannot close a screen.”

Sera’s gaze stayed on the blank viewer. “I need the final public seconds for language correction.”

Mara glanced at her. “For what language?”

“The narrowband summary draft from Houston. It says, ‘renewed analysis raises public concern that Mars may be false.’”

Tomas stopped counting.

Nadine said, “Who cleared that wording?”

“No one with a telescope,” Sera said. “Or a conscience.”

Malik sent the draft to the wall. The notice vanished. In its place came a compressed Earth-side advisory with tracked edits bleeding red along the margins. The file had passed through Houston, Quito, two Continuity Office reviewers, and someone in Pavel’s staff who favored calm verbs over honest ones.

Mara read the offending line twice.

Public concern persists that new Asterion and Witness material may imply Mars is fake or inaccessible in any meaningful sense.

Sera said, “Delete fake.”

Mara heard the sharpness in it. “The public uses the word.”

“The public also uses exit. We do not inherit every dangerous noun because it trends.”

“It trends because people are trying to name a wound.”

“No. It trends because bad language saves them from harder uncertainty.” Sera pulled one sleeve back and tapped the phrase with the stylus. “Mars is visible. Measurable. Spectroscopy is still boringly loyal. Dust, ice, methane fluctuations. The rover debris signatures are present within margins. We know our reach is failing. We do not know Mars is fake.”

“Reach is a comfort word,” Mara said.

“It is an instrument word.”

“It sounds like failure of effort.”

“It is failure of access.”

Mara looked at the draft again. The sentence lay there with all its small poisons. Fake gave denial blocs something to mock and Exit feeds something to sell. Inaccessible in any meaningful sense gave the Finite Earth channels a sermon by morning. Pavel’s staff would choose neither and publish “under review,” which was a different injury.

Nadine stepped behind Sera, reading over her shoulder. “Replace with Sera’s language.”

Sera corrected the line herself.

Current evidence supports continued visibility and measurement of Mars while reachability remains unresolved under the failing Boundary trajectory model.

“Too long,” Malik said.

“Good,” Sera said. “It will resist slogans.”

Mara caught the phrase failing Boundary trajectory model. Sera had not put that in public drafts before. She kept one hand cupped around the stylus after she wrote it, thumb rubbing the pale band where a ring had once sat.

“You changed model language,” Mara said.

“I clarified it.”

“From coordinate drift to trajectory failure.”

Sera’s face shifted a fraction. Not guilt. Calculation. “The old phrasing invited a spherical-container reading. We have never had a sphere. We have bad approach families and worse corrections.”

Nadine’s eyes went to her. “Is this from your private model?”

“Partly.”

“Partly is not a release category.”

“It is not a release. It is a correction to prevent false closure.”

Mara said, “You are telling them the failure may attach to attempts to go, not distance.”

“I am telling them nothing that precise.”

“You just did, if anyone reads like you.”

“No one reads like me,” Sera said. Then, too fast, “That was imprecise.”

Malik’s board chimed. A narrowband packet crawled into the queue, delayed by relay rotation. Earth arrived aboard Witness as fragments and risk scores, voices stripped down until grief fit through compression. Mara opened the Continuity digest on her slate.

HOUSTON CONTINUITY ANNEX  
NARROWBAND RELEASE ENVIRONMENT SUMMARY  
Window: WITNESS outbound transit, cycle 12  
Status: public rumor load rising around restricted Asterion access.  
Market note: Boundary-linked derivatives moved pre-advisory in Mumbai. Leak hypothesis active.  
Clinical note: St. Odran’s reports two adolescent relapses after circulation of edited Elias laugh clip. Clip source: last clean public feed, not duplicated cockpit file. Correction requested.  
Family note: Asterion Families’ Circle renews demand for direct notification before any Venn-specific material review.

Mara’s thumb stopped at Venn-specific.

Nadine read from her own slate. “Malik.”

“Flagging the Mumbai line.”

“Do more than flag.”

“I can route a leakage query to Earth, but delay is delay. By the time they answer, the Pit will have eaten it and called it weather.”

“Do it.”

He entered the command. His jaw was set. His mother had gone flat after a raw dump from a contractor archive, though no official record used that phrasing. The record said Boundary-media exposure proximal to intake refusal. Mara had read it because crew histories were clearance material. Malik knew she had read it. Every private wound aboard Witness had become someone else’s operational context.

Sera said, “The clinical note separates Elias’s laugh.”

Mara kept her eyes on the slate. “It should.”

“It did not last week.”

“I know.”

“The Exit channels are cutting the off-screen laugh into Jun’s duplicate cockpit laugh again. They want continuity where the evidence has rupture.”

Mara closed the digest. “They want permission.”

Sera looked at her then. “So do you.”

The bay went still in the wrong way, not from shock but because each person knew the sentence had been waiting somewhere in the air.

Nadine said, “Sera.”

“No,” Mara said. “Let her finish.”

Sera’s mouth tightened. “That was badly timed.”

“Timing is not the problem.”

“It is part of the problem,” Nadine said.

Mara faced Sera. The ship’s light flattened the fine lines around Sera’s eyes. She looked cold even here, under recycled warmth, a woman who carried her own winter through ducts and sleeping bags. “Say it cleanly.”

Sera took one breath. “Your grief is in the mission risk model. Mine is too. Nadine’s is. Tomas’s is. Malik’s is. But yours has a release pathway attached to it.”

Mara’s fingers curled against the strap.

“I am not saying you are unfit,” Sera added.

“You are saying fit is conditional.”

“It is for all of us.”

Nadine’s hand returned to the rail, hook-fingered, steady. “Enough for crew bay.”

Mara heard the command and the mercy inside it. She hated both.

“Doctor Venn,” Nadine said, “begin interviews after sleep. Scheduled. No Asterion playback unless I authorize it.”

“Crew monitoring without the object of compulsion is theater.”

“Then monitor the people. They are the mission.”

Sera turned back to the advisory draft. Malik lowered the bay audio, though no one had asked him. Tomas resumed his count under his breath.

“Eight M-four captive,” he whispered. “Twelve M-three captive. Fourteen shear pins, primary bracket. Fourteen shear pins, secondary. Sixteen washers, labeled, if the manifest is not lying.”

“Tomas,” Nadine said.

He startled. “Yes.”

“You are off Candle inventory until 1800.”

“I am only checking the fastener delta.”

“The fastener delta was checked this morning.”

“It was checked by inventory. I am checking by memory.”

“That is not a maintenance category.”

“It should be.”

Sera rubbed the old ring mark harder. “If Candle fasteners have changed, we have larger problems than viewing protocol.”

“They haven’t changed.”

“Then why are you counting them?”

Tomas looked at the blank viewer. Then at the floor. He had ink on the side of his index finger, blue-black under the nail from the hand labels he still wrote even though every part carried a scan code. “Because I know where they are.”

No one answered. The sentence landed too plainly to touch.

Nadine released the bay. Sera stayed to finish the correction. Malik stayed because packets never stopped arriving. Tomas drifted half a meter toward the corridor, caught the handrail, and gripped the polished patch without seeming to know it.

Mara followed him.

Witness corridors were narrower than Asterion’s had been in the training mockups. More shielding, fewer windows, less ceremony. The handrails had bright places at every junction. Polished fear. Polished discipline. She had written once that repeated touch could be healthy in constrained environments, a way for the body to mark continuity. After Asterion, the same language looked like a cover letter for panic.

Tomas moved toward the Candle access lock.

“You heard Nadine,” Mara said.

“I heard her.”

“Candle inventory at 1800.”

“This is adjacent inventory.”

“That is not a category either.”

He stopped at the lock window. Candle sat beyond two sealed partitions, docked belly-in, white hull dull under service light. It was too small for the word craft and too human-rated for the word probe. Its ugly nose bristled with capped sensor ports. A warning tag hung from the external umbilical, curling at the edge.

Through the maintenance window Mara could see the couch.

It faced nothing. No hero shot, no forward glass, no theatrical alignment with Mars. Just a padded human shape bracketed into a machine that everyone insisted should remain empty. Restraint arms folded back. Head cradle sealed in plastic. The couch had the shameful neatness of funeral chairs stacked before a wake.

Tomas looked past Mara, past the lock status, somewhere into a bill of materials only he could bear.

“Four couch rail bolts,” he said. “Two load path brackets. Pyro isolation at the base. Manual oxygen line capped.” His lips moved again without sound.

“You count when someone mentions it.”

“I count when people stop mentioning it.”

Mara leaned one shoulder against the wall, keeping her distance from the lock. “What did you expect people to say?”

“That it’s obscene.”

“They do.”

“No. They say it is politically complex. They say human-rated asset. They say legal ambiguity. They say recovery posture.” His voice stayed soft, almost mild. “Obscene would save time.”

“You designed around it.”

“I designed despite it.”

“Those are different moral claims.”

He gave a short breath that might have been a laugh if there had been more air in it. “You sound like the review board.”

“The review board let the couch stay.”

“The review board let everything stay. Sponsors, family injunctions, insurance clauses. If I had removed the couch, Candle would have become a lawsuit with thrusters.”

“And counting fasteners helps?”

“It keeps the couch from becoming a person.”

Mara looked again. Empty, it still changed the corridor. Crew slowed when they passed the lock. Malik took the longer route to comms after night cycle. Sera opened raw datasets in the adjacent alcove and forgot to blink. Nadine never looked through the window unless procedure required it. The couch had its own gravity, though Witness pretended otherwise.

Tomas touched the lock frame, then withdrew his hand. “You want to ask about the linkage.”

“I want to ask why you keep inventorying hardware already sealed.”

“That is not what you want to ask.”

Mara’s wrist pulsed. “Did you disable more than one manual life-support path?”

His head turned.

“I read the redacted design dispute,” she said. “The redaction was lazy.”

“That file is restricted.”

“So is half this ship.”

“Doctor Venn.”

“If someone tries to occupy Candle, will the couch support them?”

Tomas’s mouth opened. Closed. His eyes went to the camera over the lock, then to the corridor mic. Witness monitored them both. Nadine would have the transcript. Yuna on Earth might have the stress markers later. Malik could route the raw channel if ordered. There was no private place aboard, only places where people agreed to look away.

He said, “Candle is unmanned.”

“That is doctrine. I asked hardware.”

“It is not doctrine. It is design intent.”

“Tomas.”

A flush climbed above his collar. “One manual linkage is disabled. The automated isolation remains. Emergency bottled oxygen remains. The couch could keep a person alive during docked ground handling, if ground handling happened here, which it doesn’t. It cannot support an occupied Boundary approach without invasive rework.”

“You made that choice.”

“I refused another one.”

The corridor vent clicked. A stale sweetness came through, canned mandarin from someone’s opened ration pouch in the crew galley. Mara tasted Asterion’s last public frame at the back of her mouth: orange peel turning under cabin light, Elias laughing outside the lens, Mars small and red over Jun’s shoulder.

“You understand why that would be called sabotage,” she said.

“Yes.”

“Do you understand why it might be called care?”

He looked at her then, and for the first time since launch his plainness gave way to anger. It came early, before he had built a safe structure for it. “Care would have been removing the couch. Care would have been telling the families we cannot rescue bodies from a place that breaks bolts before it admits force. Care would have been telling sponsors their insured symbol is a coffin-shaped lie. I am doing warranty fraud against a sacrament.”

Mara had no answer ready. The wrongness of that almost steadied her.

The lock camera light shifted from amber to green. Someone had opened an observation channel from command.

Nadine’s voice came over the corridor speaker. “Vale. Away from Candle access.”

Tomas shut his eyes for half a second. “Acknowledged.”

“Doctor Venn. My station.”

Mara stayed until Tomas pushed off from the lock and took the corridor toward engineering. He counted again, barely audible.

“Four rail bolts. Two brackets.”

Then only his hand on each polished rail, bright place to bright place.

Nadine’s station was not a bridge in the old public sense. Witness had no room built to flatter command. The operations deck was a flattened oval of workstations, restraint loops, audio baffles, and one forward display reserved for navigation. Mars was available there by instrument feed, not window. A red disk in a data frame. Measured, delayed, unreachable.

Nadine had Sera’s corrected language on one side of her board and Mara’s crew monitoring template on the other.

“You are adjusting my protocol,” Mara said.

“I am reading it.”

“You moved Elias material into my risk column.”

“It was already there.”

“You formalized it.”

“I signed it.”

Mara clipped into the restraint opposite her. “On whose recommendation?”

“Mine.”

No delay. No committee buffer. Mara looked at the commander’s hands. Long fingers, one tucked through a rail loop. Under stress Nadine spoke more softly, and now her voice had gone almost private.

“You are monitoring my crew,” Nadine said. “I am monitoring the monitor.”

“That undermines the therapeutic frame.”

“This is not therapy.”

“It uses therapeutic instruments.”

“It uses air recyclers too. Still a ship.”

Mara held her slate too tightly. The edges pressed into her palm. “If Earth sees that my grief is an active mission variable, Pavel can use it to remove me from interpretive review.”

“Earth already sees it. Pavel helped write it. I changed the weight.”

“To what?”

Nadine tapped the board. Mara’s profile expanded.

MARA VENN  
Roles: Mission Psychologist; Authorized Interpretive Officer  
Known compromise vectors: sibling relationship to Elias Venn; prior Asterion selection authority; public trust volatility; suspected exposure to unauthorized family-specific transcript material.  
Protective factors: subject-matter fluency; collapse communication expertise; crew trust conditional; high tolerance for contradictory evidence.  
Operational note: Need for Elias-specific answer may bias classification of contaminated records toward narrative coherence.

Mara read the last line until the words lost shape.

“Suspected exposure,” she said.

Nadine’s face changed by less than a blink.

Mara felt the ship around them: pressure hull, checklists, sealed channels, every system built from the premise that leaks kill and seals fail. “Who told you?”

“No one needed to tell me.”

“That is not an answer.”

“It is the answer you get.”

Mara’s throat tightened around something useless. She removed adjectives. “Have you heard it?”

“No.”

“Have you seen the transcript?”

“No.”

“Then your note is speculation.”

“It is risk language.”

“It is accusation language.”

“It is survival language.” Nadine leaned closer, voice still low. “You touch your wrist whenever Elias enters audio. You ask for timestamps before content. You corrected Malik yesterday when he said Venn-specific and the packet did not include a name. You know there is something coming before the archive admits it.”

Mara set the slate down. If she held it another second she would throw it, and the absurdity of throwing approved polymer in microgravity during a risk conversation almost made her laugh.

“What do you want?” she asked.

“No private playback.”

“That is already protocol.”

“No private playback,” Nadine repeated. “No family-specific material accessed without Sera or Malik present. No interpretive notes sealed under personal privilege. If you see Elias first, you call another human into the room.”

Mara looked at the forward display. Mars burned inside its measurement frame. Not a promise. Not a fraud. A red fact behind a failing approach.

“You asked for me aboard.”

“I asked for a person who could read damage without worshiping it.”

“And now?”

“Now I have that person, plus a sister.”

The sentence did what comfort usually failed to do. It made Mara colder.

Nadine unclipped the monitoring template and sent it back to Mara’s slate. “I am not removing you. I am making it harder for the mission to lie through you.”

Before Mara could answer, Malik called from comms. “Command, Houston narrowband holding a private advisory from Pavel’s office. Header says immediate psychological routing.”

Nadine’s eyes stayed on Mara. “To whom?”

“Doctor Venn. Copy command. Copy medical ground. It includes a family access tag.”

Mara’s fingers moved to her wrist before she stopped them. Nadine saw. Of course she saw.

“Queue it,” Nadine said.

Malik hesitated over the open channel. “There’s also a public-stream rumor attached. Mumbai desks are pricing restricted log access in the next window.”

Sera’s voice came from the science station, sharp through the deck audio. “That is impossible. No schedule went public.”

Malik said, “Markets do not require truth. Only appetite.”

Nadine muted the channel and looked at Mara. “We handle Pavel first.”

“No,” Mara said. “We handle the rumor first. If Mumbai is moving on log access, someone thinks Asterion material is about to open.”

“Is it?”

The question hung.

Mara’s slate chimed. Pavel’s advisory arrived as a sealed gray pane. The access tag glowed beside it: VENN, E. RESTRICTED CREW-PRIVATE LOG. FIRST REVIEW NOTICE.

Her mouth dried.

Nadine read the header from her own station. “This is scheduled?”

“No.” Mara heard her own voice from a distance and pulled it back into her body. “My first Elias restricted window was not until after the cycle thirteen psych baselines.”

“Malik,” Nadine said over deck audio. “Confirm custody chain.”

“Working.”

Sera appeared at the edge of command, tablet tucked against her chest. “If Pavel moved access early while markets are already pricing it, that is not coincidence.”

“Do not build sequence yet,” Nadine said.

“I am building suspicion.”

“That is allowed.”

Mara opened the custody metadata without opening the file. Elias’s name sat in the archive designation with all its institutional clothing.

A-VENN-E / PRIVATE MEDICAL-PSYCH LOG / MD117  
Integrity: partial stable  
Audio: present  
Video: no  
Transcript: machine provisional with analyst corrections  
Release status: restricted family-scientific review  
Continuity risk: pending

MD117. Not the final day. Not the porch-light rumor, if the rumor belonged where Mara feared it belonged. This log sat earlier, before the public feed cut, before orange peel and loose strap and Elias laughing because Amara made history obscene before history could do it first.

A first restricted log. Safe, by comparison. That was the trap in the word first.

Nadine said, “We can hold it.”

Mara shook her head. “If Pavel sent it early, holding creates another record.”

“Opening creates one too.”

Sera stepped closer. “I will sit in.”

“No,” Mara said too quickly.

There. The room heard it.

Sera’s expression closed. Nadine’s did not.

Mara corrected herself. “Not you. If the log contains medical crew material, I need Malik for chain and command for authorization. Sera should not be first exposure unless scientific content requires it.”

Sera gave a thin nod. “That is a defensible reason.”

It was also true. Mara held to that.

Nadine said, “My cabin. Malik routes read-only. No export. No replay without mutual consent.”

“Pavel copied medical ground,” Mara said.

“Ground is delayed. We are here.”

The commander’s cabin had a fold-down desk, a sleep sleeve strapped flat against the wall, and a rail polished brighter than the others near the hatch. Nadine left the hatch open. That was not trust. It was procedure shaped to look like it.

Malik routed the file from comms. His voice came through dry and close. “Read-only session. Local cache disabled. Screen capture disabled. Audio watermark active. I am logging all access events, including pauses.”

“Understood,” Nadine said.

Mara sat at the desk restraint. Nadine remained behind her left shoulder, close enough to see the screen, far enough to avoid becoming comfort.

The archive pane opened.

Asterion Private Crew Log  
Mission Day 117 / timestamp confidence 0.82  
Recorder: Elias Venn, flight surgeon  
Channel: private psych-medical  
Addressee: Dr. Mara Venn, mission psychology archive  
Analyst caution: file recovered from damaged memory block; two checksum repairs; six seconds duplicated at 00:03:11 with non-identical waveform. No Boundary-proximity classification assigned at current review.

Mara’s skin tightened under her collar. Audio present.

Nadine said, “You can still stop.”

Mara pressed play.

Static first. Not theatrical. Ordinary static, dry as dust in a connector. Then a soft knock, two fingers against plastic. Med-kit latch. Elias had done it when thinking since childhood, after he broke his finger and learned to test pain with rhythm.

His voice came in too close.

“Private log. Elias Venn. Day one-one-seven, unless the mission clock is practicing theology, in which case I withdraw the number.”

Mara put her hands flat against her thighs.

A rustle. A swallow.

“Crew medical summary is already in the formal channel. This is for Mara’s little confessional machine. Hi, Mara.”

Nadine shifted, barely.

Elias breathed out through his nose. “No, I did not eat enough today. Yes, I am aware that weakens my authority. Leila caught me with the electrolyte pouch and gave me the look people give bad lab technique. Amara says if I prescribe one more hydration check while looking like dried fruit, she’ll report me to myself.”

The joke struck wrong, too alive for the room. Mara saw his long hands around a pouch, one crooked finger braced against the seal. No image came with the file. The mind supplied what the archive withheld.

“Crew stress within expected range,” Elias continued. “Sofia is sleeping too tight again. Jun is narrating drift that isn’t there. Mateo rehearsed tomorrow’s basalt segment in the hygiene bay and then pretended he was singing. Leila washed past necessity after tray review and stopped when I looked at her. Amara rebuilt the same valve model twice. I’m listing tells because you asked for tells.”

A click. He moved the recorder.

“I’m also listing them because yours work. That is the annoying part. Fear naming, humor limits, care tasks, silence tolerance. We all sound like your notebooks when the lights go strange.”

Mara’s pulse moved under both wrists now.

“Useful,” Elias said. “Dangerous. I know, that is not a diagnosis. I can hear you objecting from however many million kilometers away. Fine. Provisional observation: conversion drills convert. They don’t absolve. A person can turn fear into procedure and still be afraid. A person can make the joke and still want his sister to say the mission is worth the size of the dark.”

The audio crackled. Malik’s watermark clicked once in the background, a soft proof that Witness was hearing this in the present tense.

Elias said, quieter, “That was unfair. Delete that in your head. The archive won’t.”

Mara’s mouth had gone numb.

Nadine said nothing.

The file skipped. Six seconds duplicated, the same breath returning with a different edge.

“Jun says Mars looks closer when he’s tired. It doesn’t. Before you ask, I checked the optics. I checked him too. Pupils fine, hands steady. He says a destination with manners is worse than a hostile one.”

A pause.

“I laughed. That probably matters to no one unless we all die and everyone starts mining tone. So, future ghouls, if you are listening: I laughed because Jun was being Jun and because I was tired. Please hydrate and stop using dead people as furniture.”

Mara made a sound. Small, cut off. Nadine’s hand moved once toward her shoulder, then returned to the rail.

Elias shifted again. Cloth against mic. Asterion hummed behind him, alive in its systems, not yet image.

“Real note,” he said. “If this goes to review, watch Mateo. He is carrying children as payload and nobody wrote mass for that. Watch Leila because people will try to make her prayer public property. Watch Amara because if matter lies to her, she may take it personally. Watch Sofia because command makes a poor blanket. Watch Jun because hands are not sacraments, even when they save you.”

He stopped long enough that Mara looked at the counter. Still running.

“And watch me,” Elias said.

The room lost its air by half.

“I’m calm in the way a fever is warm. Functional. Misleading if you like the patient.” Another tap against the med-kit latch. “You once told us panic is floodwater. Get it into channels, make it turn a pump. Good drill. Truly. But sometimes floodwater tells the truth about the wall.”

Mara shut her eyes. Nadine let her. The log continued in the dark.

“If you hear this, don’t make my fear behave better than it did. That’s all. End log before I become profound and ruin both our reputations.”

The file clicked out.

No one spoke. From the open hatch came the operations deck: Malik requesting custody confirmation, Sera correcting a data label, the ship moving its cooled air through narrow places. Earth waited in packets. Mumbai priced rumor. Somewhere behind them, in a sealed bay, Candle kept its empty couch facing nothing.

Mara opened her eyes.

The archive pane offered replay.

Nadine reached past her and closed the session before either of them could touch it again.
