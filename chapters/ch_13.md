## Chapter 13: Near Things First

The pump coughed brown water over Ivo Tan’s boots and spat a strip of blue plastic into his hand.

“Cut power,” he said.

The apprentice above him slapped the breaker with the heel of her palm. The motor died badly, one last metal complaint rolling through the repair pit under the floodwall. Rainwater kept ticking through the concrete seam. Behind Ivo, on the wall of the old nursery yard, Mars had been painted over in civic gray. The red disk still showed through where salt had lifted the new paint. A child had scraped one small crater back into sight with a coin.

Ivo held up the plastic strip. “Wrapper. From the intake.”

“It came through the screen?” the interviewer asked.

He looked toward her camera, then past it to the leak crossing the floor. The media crew had been told to keep behind the yellow rope. They kept drifting forward when the light changed.

“Everything comes through when nobody cleans the screen,” Ivo said. “That is the lesson. Use that.”

The interviewer smiled as if he had given her the sentence she needed. She was young, hair tied back in a cloth printed with loop-sky galaxies. The pattern irritated him. The same three galaxies repeated down the blue fabric, neat as factory damage.

“Can we start again with your name and prior work?” she asked.

“No.”

The apprentice laughed once.

Ivo pointed the plastic strip at her. “You too. Do not laugh near the bearing.”

She stopped. Her name was Lani, nineteen, recruited from a hospital maintenance program after three senior pump technicians returned to orbital contracts. She had hands that learned fast and a habit of saying sorry to machines when she dropped screws. She carried a glucose tablet in her shirt pocket for her younger brother, who sometimes went flat after school assemblies.

The interviewer checked her wrist screen. “Mr. Tan, the Continuity Office package needs basic identification.”

“Then take it from the permit.”

“It helps people hear you.”

“It helps people decide whether they can dismiss me.”

A man from the city communications desk cleared his throat. He wore dry shoes. Ivo had already decided not to like him.

The pit smelled of river water and overheated insulation. Above them, traffic moved along the repaired flood road in short bursts, buses groaning through standing water. On the nursery wall, the gray-painted Mars watched through its salt wounds. Someone had written NEAR THINGS FIRST across the lower part of the mural in black letters tall enough to be seen from the road. Under the slogan, smaller and newer, another hand had added: WHAT IF I STILL WANT FAR THINGS?

The camera operator noticed it. She shifted left.

“Do not frame the child’s writing like a confession,” Ivo said.

“It isn’t a child’s writing.”

“It is.”

“How do you know?”

He bent back into the pump housing. “Adults use straighter guilt.”

The interviewer let the line sit. He heard the camera servo adjust.

Lani passed him the seal pick. “Power is tagged.”

“Tag number?”

“Blue seven, red three.”

“Say full.”

“Breaker B-seven, lockout red three, signed Tan and Rivera, time 14:06.”

“Good.”

He opened the housing and found the gasket swollen at the lip. Cheap polymer, wrong grade. Procurement had bought emergency stock after the last typhoon and stored it in a hot container near the docks. The material had softened in its own wrapper before it ever saw water. Above, someone had painted over Mars with the same mind: cover the wound, declare the wall repaired, send a photograph.

“This gasket is trash,” he said.

The interviewer stepped closer. “Is that because of the funding diversion?”

Ivo laughed, and the sound came out too hard. Lani looked at him.

“Funding diversion,” he repeated. “That is a clean phrase.”

“Witness appropriations were partially rebalanced after the Finite Earth amendments. Your project received—”

“Three pumps that do not match our pipe standard, twelve boxes of gaskets from a warehouse that should have been condemned, and seventy-four volunteers who arrived wearing Mars-red mourning bands and no gloves.” He pulled the gasket free. It tore in his hand, slick and gray. “We also got two good civil engineers who quit deep-space design after the Break. We got nurses who know how to make people drink water before speeches. We got enough concrete to close Bay Four before the king tide. Do you want yes or no?”

“I want what is true.”

“Then stop asking for one sentence.”

The city man shifted again. Dry shoes squeaked on the ramp.

From above, a child’s voice said, “Uncle Ivo?”

Ivo closed his eyes for half a second, then opened them because closed eyes in a pit were bad practice.

“Mika, rope,” he said.

“I am behind it.”

“You are touching it.”

The rope moved. A girl of perhaps ten stood in the old nursery yard with one hand caught in the sagging yellow line. Her school shirt was wet at the collar. A red plastic planet hung from her backpack by a cracked chain. She had been at the site all week, waiting for her mother’s shift to end at the sandbag station, drawing little boxes around the drains in chalk and asking the names of tools.

Ivo pointed at the painted line on the ground. “Behind the blue.”

“There is water in the classroom again.”

“That is why we are repairing the pump.”

“It smells like eggs.”

“That is not eggs.”

Mika looked at the camera and pulled her backpack strap across the red planet until it vanished under her arm.

The interviewer softened her voice. “Do you attend school here?”

Mika stared at her.

Ivo stood, knees clicking. “She is not part of your package.”

“My mother said I can talk if I want.”

“Do you want?”

Mika shrugged. The red planet slipped back into view.

Rain tapped the corrugated cover overhead. The pump pit collected sounds and made every small thing public: drips, breath, the camera’s focus click, Lani peeling tape for the replacement gasket. Ivo wiped his hands on a rag already too wet to help.

The old nursery had been built during the Mars curriculum years, when every district wanted dunes on the walls and greenhouses painted beside alphabet charts. After the Break, the school board voted to paint over the red hills. The first coat failed inside a month. The second coat peeled during flood season. Now gray paint hung in scales above the pump intake, and under it children in bubble helmets emerged in pieces: one white glove, half a boot, a smiling mouth without a face.

Mika touched the little planet on her strap. “They said no Mars projects this term.”

“Who said?”

“Ms. Garcia. The district. I don’t know. We have pipe maps now.”

“Pipe maps are useful.”

“I know.”

Lani slid the replacement gasket from its packet. “Pipe maps are better than the old habitat math. My little brother had to calculate oxygen for imaginary potatoes for three years. He still can’t read a water meter.”

Mika gave her a look. “Potatoes are food.”

“Water is also food,” Lani said.

The interviewer lifted one hand to pause them. “This is good. Could we get that again in frame? The curriculum change is part of the civic shift.”

Ivo turned on her. “These are not actors.”

The woman flushed. “I did not mean—”

“You meant exactly that. You saw a child under painted Mars and a leaking pipe, and your hands went for framing. It is what the whole planet does now. Door around Mars. Candle in glass. Child with wrench. Everybody needs an altar.”

The city man stepped down into the pit far enough to be seen. “Ivo.”

“No. You wanted me angry. Here it is.”

The camera stayed on. Good. Let it.

He held the torn gasket up between two fingers. “We were told there was always elsewhere. Bad coast? Move inland. Bad inland? Build higher. Bad Earth? Go red. Every budget meeting for twenty years had Mars sitting in the corner like a rich uncle who might pay for the funeral. We let sea walls rot because the species had a launch window. We let drainage maps age because some minister could stand under a suspended planet and say outward. Now the pipe is leaking here.”

He slapped the pipe with his palm. It answered with a dull ring.

“Show me the universe where that is not enough work.”

The camera operator swallowed. The interviewer’s eyes flicked to the city man, then back to Ivo.

Mika asked, “Is wanting Mars selfish now?”

The pit changed.

Not quiet. The water still struck the bucket. The road still carried buses above them. Lani’s tape hung half-pulled from the roll, adhesive trembling in the air from her stopped hand.

Ivo knew the cruel answer first. It arrived ready-made, wearing his own voice. Yes, if wanting Mars lets your street flood. Yes, if your dream eats the pump crew. Yes, if far things make you despise the hand already reaching for yours.

Mika’s fingers tightened around the red planet until the cracked chain pressed white into her skin.

The interviewer looked away. That was worse than watching. The city man examined his dry shoes. Lani’s face had gone hard, not at Mika, but at him, because she knew him well enough to hear the answer forming before he spoke.

Ivo crouched, bringing himself level with the blue line without crossing it. His knees protested. Mud had dried in patches up his shins, then been wetted again until it looked like old paint.

“What do you want about Mars?” he asked.

Mika frowned. “What?”

“Do you want to go there tomorrow instead of fixing the classroom?”

“No.”

“Do you want the pump money sent to a rocket?”

“No.”

“Do you want Ms. Garcia to put the red hill back?”

Mika’s mouth moved, then shut.

There. The valve under the valve.

“I liked the hill,” she said. “It was behind the reading table. When we had lockdown drills, I sat under it. It was quiet there.”

Ivo lowered the torn gasket. His hand had become stupid around it.

The interviewer whispered, “Keep rolling,” and for once he had no anger to spend on her.

Mika kicked the blue line with the toe of one shoe. “My mother says Mars made people forget the water. My uncle says the glass means God closed the wrong door. Ms. Garcia says we do not know what the Boundary means. Lani’s brother says if you draw a door around Mars you get transferred to clinic.”

“Your brother says too much,” Lani said, but softly.

“He says because it happened to Jayden.”

“That was different.”

“How?”

Lani looked to Ivo. He had put himself at the front of this work, had stood under slogans, had shouted in committee rooms that the city should repair what could still be touched. He had said no elsewhere until the phrase became a tool. A good tool. Good tools still cut fingers when passed around carelessly.

Mika waited.

Ivo said, “Wanting Mars is not selfish.”

The words scraped. Too little. Too late. Also true.

The city man exhaled as if a pipe had released pressure.

Ivo kept his eyes on Mika. “Using Mars to abandon people is selfish. Selling Mars to children so adults can protect contracts is selfish. Calling every far dream a sin is also selfish.”

Lani tore the tape with her teeth. “Don’t let Ms. Garcia hear you.”

“Ms. Garcia can come repair the intake.”

Mika’s face did not clear. Children after the Break had learned not to reward adults too fast.

“Can I draw it small?” she asked.

“What?”

“The hill. Not the whole sky. Just the hill.”

Ivo looked up at the mural. The gray paint was failing everywhere now. Red dust color around the scrape marks. White greenhouse ribs emerging under the slogan. A bubble helmet without its child. The old Mars had been ugly, cheerful, sponsored by an orbital cement company whose logo still ghosted the corner. He had hated it before the Break for selling escape to children who lived below sea level. He hated it after for making them ashamed of missing what had been taken.

“Draw it where water will not reach,” he said.

Mika considered this as a site instruction. “On paper?”

“Paper gets wet.”

“On the back of the pipe map?”

“Ask Lani for scrap laminate.”

Lani snorted. “So now my inventory is art supply.”

“Log it as morale sealant.”

“That is fraud.”

“That is education.”

The child’s fingers loosened. The red planet swung once against her backpack.

The interviewer lowered her microphone. “Mr. Tan, can I ask about the slogan?”

“No.”

“You painted it?”

“Yes.”

“Why those words?”

He stood too fast and felt a pulse in his bad ankle. “Because near things were dying while far things got poems.”

“Do you still mean it?”

Ivo handed the ruined gasket to Lani and took the replacement. “Yes.”

Mika’s shoulders moved.

He saw it. So did the camera. Damn the camera.

“I mean it as order of repair,” he said. “Not order of worth.”

The interviewer’s expression changed. The sentence had landed in her as usable. He wanted to take it back before it could become a banner.

“Do not print that alone,” he said.

“We don’t print. It’s a live-delay civic feed.”

“Then do not cut it alone.”

The city man said, “All feeds are subject to Continuity review.”

Ivo turned. “That does not comfort me.”

“It is meant to prevent harm.”

“It also makes clean knives.”

The city man’s jaw worked. “This project exists because the amendments passed. You know what the opposition calls us? Mud cultists. Drain priests. Anti-human. They say we are shrinking the species to fit a flooded street.”

“Maybe stop helping them.”

“Ivo.”

“No. You put children under painted-over planets and tell them repair is dignity, but you let donors clap when a child gives up the sky. I have sat in those rooms. They love a converted dream. It makes a tidy receipt.”

Lani fixed the new gasket into place with both thumbs. “Seal seated.”

Ivo checked it. Good enough to test. “Torque.”

She gave him the wrench.

The interviewer’s microphone had drifted close again. “Converted dream,” she said. “Can you explain that?”

He almost laughed. Explain. There were rooms where explaining became another pump nobody maintained. He fitted the wrench and tightened the housing bolt until the mark aligned.

“When I left orbital materials,” he said, “three committees called me brave. Nobody calls you brave when you stay because your auntie needs dialysis or because the pump roster lost two people. Brave is for a camera angle. Work is for Tuesday.”

The bolt mark held.

“I had a Mars transfer slot once,” he said.

He had not planned to say it. Lani looked up. Mika leaned forward against the rope again, then remembered and stepped back.

The interviewer’s voice went careful. “A crewed program?”

“Materials habitat shell. Surface systems. Pre-Asterion derivative work.”

“You never mention that in your public biography.”

“It embarrasses everyone. The space people think I betrayed the future. The Finite people think I had to be purified.”

“Did you want to go?”

The question came too bare. He could have refused it. He could have pointed to the permit, to the leak, to the pump. Instead he saw the old render in his apartment, hidden behind a stack of flood maps after the Break: a work light on red dust, his own gloved hand closing a pressure seam, the silly private oath he had made at twenty-six to touch another world and then come home with dirt under the nails of history.

“Yes,” he said.

Mika listened as if adults confessing broken things was more dangerous than any feed.

“I wanted it badly,” he said. “I still do, sometimes. Then a tide came through Tondo and the evacuation pumps failed. My cousin carried his mother through water with fuel in it. I was in a clean-room review arguing about abrasion tolerance for regolith nobody could reach. So I came back.”

“Do you regret it?”

The pump pit waited.

Ivo set the wrench down. “Regret is too clean.”

Lani whispered, “Breaker test?”

He nodded.

She climbed the pit ladder and crossed to the panel. The camera followed her until Ivo raised one hand.

“Stay on the pump,” he said.

The camera stayed.

“Power in three,” Lani called. “Two. One.”

The motor caught with a rough shudder, then smoothed. Water moved through the intake, first brown and foamed, then less brown. The leak at the pipe seam thinned to a silver thread.

“Pressure?” Ivo called.

“Forty-two and climbing,” Lani said.

“Forty-eight max on that line.”

“I know.”

“Say it.”

“Forty-eight max.”

The interviewer watched the water drain from the classroom threshold above them. Mika watched too, but her eyes kept returning to the half-buried Mars.

At forty-six, Lani throttled the flow. The thread leak remained.

“There,” she said. “Still leaking.”

“I see.”

“Clamp patch?”

“Temporary. We replace the section after the rain band.”

The city man checked his phone. “Can we call this functional for the briefing?”

Ivo gave him a look.

“Partially functional,” the man corrected.

“Temporarily stabilized,” Lani said.

Ivo pointed at her. “That.”

The interviewer smiled. “You train them hard.”

“They will be left with the work.”

“That sounds bleak.”

“That sounds scheduled.”

Mika crouched and picked up a flake of gray paint that had fallen from the mural. Underneath it, red showed on the wall like old brick.

“Don’t eat that,” Lani said.

Mika made a disgusted face. “I’m ten.”

“Ten-year-olds eat paint if the day is long enough.”

“My mother says you all say bad things about Mars.”

Ivo wiped his hands again. “Your mother has heard me on bad days.”

“Did Mars do anything wrong?”

The city man muttered, “God.”

Ivo stared at him until he shut his mouth.

“No,” Ivo said to Mika. “Mars did not do anything wrong.”

“It’s just there?”

“As far as we know.”

“Can it see us?”

“No.”

The answer came fast, maybe too fast. Mika accepted it anyway. Children preferred a firm no to a decorated maybe when the question was about being watched.

“Then why paint over it?”

Ivo heard Lani breathe in. The interviewer did not move. Above the floodwall, a bus hissed to a stop, and people’s voices rose in the wet street.

“Because adults get angry at pictures when they cannot punish what hurt them,” he said.

Mika turned the paint chip over in her fingers. Gray on one side, red on the other. “That’s stupid.”

“Yes.”

“Did you paint it?”

“The slogan, yes. The gray, no.”

“Good.”

“Good?”

“The gray is ugly.”

Lani laughed again, quieter. Even the city man’s mouth twitched.

Ivo looked at the mural. The words NEAR THINGS FIRST had begun to streak where rain found cheap paint. FIRST bent at the S. It looked less like an order now and more like something asking to be repaired.

The interviewer said, “May I ask you one last question?”

“You will anyway.”

“Witness is approaching the Boundary. There are people who say Earth repair is only a way to look away from that. What do you say to them?”

He hated the form of the question. He hated how ready he was for it. For months he had answered versions of it in halls that smelled of floor wax and wet umbrellas. He had said that no ship would bail out a neighborhood. He had said Candle would not patch a seawall. He had said every peso sent upward should be weighed against every child with mold in her lungs. Each sentence had been true enough to damage someone.

He looked at Mika, who had moved under the painted-over planet. Not for meaning. For shelter from the rain coming sideways through the yard gate.

“I say watch,” he said. “Study. Do not lie about what is up there.”

The interviewer waited.

“And then fix the pipe.”

“That’s your answer?”

“That is two answers. People keep trying to make them fight.”

Mika lifted the paint chip toward the mural, matching red to red.

Ivo said, “Near things first.”

The interviewer’s face sharpened at the slogan.

“First,” he said again, before she could keep only the clean part. “Not only.”

The repair feed arrived aboard Witness nineteen minutes late and already argued over.

Mara watched it in the archive bay with the audio capped low enough that Sera’s breathing and the ship’s ventilation still held the room. The public packet carried Continuity labels: civic repair, low collapse risk, Finite Earth language monitored, minors present, no raw Boundary footage. Someone had flagged Ivo Tan’s interview for interpretive relevance to release framing. Someone else had added a caution mark beside the phrase converted dream.

Sera had gone to command two minutes before, leaving the console locked under Mara’s credentials and the second-reviewer light still green. Mara should have closed the feed. Her work queue held Asterion fragments with worse teeth.

On screen, Ivo crouched in muddy water and asked a child what she wanted about Mars.

Mara stopped the playback.

The child stood beneath the painted-over planet, backpack strap cutting across a red plastic charm. The wall behind her carried gray paint, red bleed, black slogan. A leaking pipe crossed the lower frame with a bright, persistent thread of water.

Mara rolled back seven seconds and watched Ivo fail to answer quickly.

That failure had weight. It held more care than his slogan, more danger too. She had spent years fearing Exit logic: far things made lethal, death dressed as a door, the dead used as proof that local pain could be outflanked. Ivo showed the opposite wound. Nearness could become doctrine as easily as escape. Bread could be turned into a weapon. Repair could ask a child to apologize for wanting a red hill above the reading table.

She opened a release-language note and typed:

Do not frame continued desire for Mars, spaceflight, or outward study as pathology or moral failure. Distinguish abandonment from longing. Distinguish repair priority from worth hierarchy.

The cursor blinked after hierarchy. The word looked too smooth. She deleted it.

Distinguish repair order from worth.

The ship clicked around her. Somewhere below, Candle waited in the hangar with its ugly white shell, its archive of lullabies and court transcripts, its empty human-rated couch left bolted in place by lawsuits and hunger.

On the frozen feed, the child held up a chip of paint: gray on one side, red on the other. Ivo looked at it as if it were a failed gasket he could not throw away.

Mara saved the note under public-release constraints.

The next item in her queue opened without sound: CANDLE STATIC TEST / PAYLOAD AND INTERIOR REVIEW / TOMAS VALE PRESENCE REQUIRED.

She left Ivo’s final sentence visible in the small preview pane while the Candle checklist loaded over it. First, not only.
