## Chapter 13: Near Things First

The pump coughed brown water over Ivo Tan’s boots and spat a strip of blue plastic into his hand.

“Cut power.”

Lani slapped the breaker with the heel of her palm. The motor died under the floodwall with one last metal complaint. Rainwater kept ticking through the concrete seam. Behind Ivo, on the old nursery wall, Mars had been painted over in civic gray. Salt had lifted the new coat in flakes. Under one lifted place, a child had scraped a red crater back into sight with a coin.

Ivo held up the plastic strip. “Wrapper. Intake.”

“It came through the screen?” the interviewer asked.

He looked past her camera to the water crossing the floor. The media crew had been told to stay behind the yellow rope. The rope had become a suggestion.

“Everything comes through when nobody cleans the screen,” he said.

The interviewer checked her wrist screen. “Can we start again with your name and prior work?”

“No.”

Lani laughed once.

Ivo pointed the plastic at her. “You too. Do not laugh near the bearing.”

She stopped.

Traffic moved above them along the repaired flood road. On the nursery wall, black letters ran across the lower mural: NEAR THINGS FIRST. Below it, someone had written: WHAT IF I STILL WANT FAR THINGS?

The camera operator shifted left.

“Do not frame that like a confession,” Ivo said.

The interviewer followed his look. “The writing?”

“It is a child’s.”

“How do you know?”

“Adults use straighter guilt.”

The camera servo adjusted. Ivo heard the line being saved.

Lani passed him the seal pick. “Power is tagged.”

“Tag number.”

“Breaker B-seven, lockout red three, signed Tan and Rivera, time 14:06.”

“Good.”

He opened the housing and found the gasket swollen at the lip. Wrong grade. Cheap polymer. Emergency stock stored hot near the docks until it softened inside its own wrapper. Someone had bought bad gaskets and gray paint with the same idea of repair.

“This gasket is trash.”

The interviewer stepped closer. “Is that because of the funding diversion?”

Ivo pulled the gasket free. It tore slick and gray between his fingers. “Clean phrase.”

“Witness appropriations were partly rebalanced after the Finite Earth amendments. Your project received—”

“Three pumps that do not match our pipe standard. Twelve boxes of bad gaskets. Volunteers with mourning bands and no gloves.” He held up the torn seal. “Enough concrete to close Bay Four if the pipe arrives before tide. Do you want yes or no?”

“I want what is true.”

“Then stop making the gasket wait for it.”

A city communications man cleared his throat from the ramp. His shoes were dry.

Ivo looked at him. “You need this feed to clear review.”

The man checked his slate. “Package ID 13-FE-schoolyard. Civic Repair Instructional. Minor present if she stays. Boundary-adjacent language flagged. Deadline seventeen hundred.”

“That was not an answer.”

“If Continuity holds the package, Procurement holds the second levy tranche. If Procurement holds, the replacement pipe does not release today.” He swallowed. “I am here because the form asks for visual proof of low-risk programming.”

Lani crouched beside the housing. “Seal groove is chewed.”

“How bad?”

“Bad enough to make the next gasket hate us.”

“Deburr. Light.”

From above, a child’s voice said, “Uncle Ivo?”

Ivo closed his eyes for half a second, then opened them. Closed eyes in a pump pit were poor practice.

“Mika. Rope.”

“I am behind it.”

“You are touching it.”

The rope sagged around Mika’s hand. Her school shirt was wet at the collar. A red plastic planet hung from her backpack by a cracked chain.

“Behind the blue line,” Ivo said.

“There is water in the classroom again.”

“That is why we are repairing the pump.”

“It smells like eggs.”

“That is not eggs.”

Mika saw the camera and pulled her strap over the red planet until it disappeared under her arm.

The interviewer softened her voice. “Do you attend school here?”

Mika stared at her.

Ivo stood. His knees clicked. “She is not part of your package.”

“My mother said I can talk if I want,” Mika said.

“Do you want?”

Mika shrugged. The planet slipped back into view.

Rain tapped the corrugated cover. The pit made every small sound public: drips, breath, focus clicks, Lani peeling tape for the replacement gasket.

Mika touched the planet on her strap. “They said no Mars projects this term.”

“Who said?”

“Ms. Garcia. The district. We have pipe maps now.”

Lani slid the new gasket from its packet. “Pipe maps beat habitat math. My brother calculated oxygen for imaginary potatoes for three years and still can’t read a water meter.”

“Water is also food,” Ivo said.

The interviewer lifted one hand. “Could we get that again in frame?”

Ivo turned on her. “These are not actors.”

“I didn’t mean—”

“You saw a child under painted Mars and a leaking pipe, and your hands went for framing.”

The city man stepped down. “Ivo.”

“No. You wanted the site. This is the site.”

The camera stayed on. Its tally light burned red under the rain shield.

Ivo held the torn gasket between two fingers and slapped the pipe. It answered dull.

“Now the pipe is leaking here. That is enough work.”

Mika asked, “Is wanting Mars selfish now?”

The pit stopped.

Lani’s tape hung from the roll. The interviewer looked away. The city man stared at his dry shoes.

Ivo knew the cruel answer first. His jaw set around it. Mika’s fingers tightened around the red planet until the cracked chain pressed white into her skin.

He crouched level with the blue line. “What do you want about Mars?”

“What?”

“Do you want pump money sent to a rocket?”

“No.”

“Do you want the classroom flooded while adults make launch posters?”

“No.”

“Do you want Ms. Garcia to put the red hill back?”

Mika’s mouth moved, then shut.

“I liked the hill,” she said. “It was behind the reading table. During lockdown drills, I sat under it. It was quiet there.”

Ivo lowered the torn gasket.

Mika kicked the blue line with one shoe. “My mother says Mars made people forget water. My uncle says the glass means God closed the wrong door. Ms. Garcia says we do not know what the Boundary means.”

Lani looked to Ivo.

He had said no elsewhere until the phrase became a tool. Good tools still cut fingers.

Mika waited.

“Wanting Mars is not selfish,” he said.

Too little. True.

The city man exhaled as if pressure had released behind a valve.

Ivo kept his eyes on Mika. “Using Mars to abandon people is selfish. Selling Mars to children so adults can protect contracts is selfish.” His mouth kept moving, and he caught it late. “If I tell you every far dream is dirty because I am angry, that is me putting the wrench on the wrong bolt.”

Lani tore the tape with her teeth. “Don’t let Ms. Garcia hear you.”

“Ms. Garcia can come repair the intake.”

Mika’s face did not clear. Children after the Break knew better than to pay adults too fast.

“Can I draw it small?” she asked. “Not the whole sky. Just the hill.”

Ivo looked at the mural. Red dust showed around scrape marks. White greenhouse ribs ghosted under the gray. He had hated the old Mars for selling escape to children below sea level. He hated the gray for making them ashamed of missing it.

“Draw it where water will not reach,” he said.

“Back of the pipe map?”

“Ask Lani for scrap laminate.”

Lani snorted. “My inventory is art supply now.”

The interviewer lowered her microphone, then lifted it again. “Mr. Tan, the slogan. You painted it?”

“Yes.”

“Do you still mean it?”

He stood too fast and felt his ankle pulse. “Yes.”

Mika’s shoulders moved.

He saw it. So did the camera.

“I mean fix the thing flooding first,” he said. “I do not mean cut the sky out of a child’s drawing.”

On the interviewer’s wrist screen, the live transcript boxed the sentence before he finished breathing. A yellow marker appeared: EXCERPT CANDIDATE.

“Unmark that,” Ivo said.

“It is just a marker.”

“It is a knife.”

The city man leaned over his slate. “Clip marker helps routing.”

“The pipe waits on your box?”

“On whether this reads as repair education or political recruitment.”

Mika looked between them. “I’m not recruitment.”

“No,” Ivo said. “Start the clip at her question.”

The operator moved the marker back until Lani’s gasket tape and the open pipe filled the thumbnail.

“There,” Ivo said.

The city man’s slate chimed. “Context length exceeds advisory. Manual review if we submit before sixteen hundred.”

“What time?”

“Fifteen forty-one.”

“Then stop standing there.”

Lani seated the new gasket with both thumbs. “Seal in.”

Ivo checked it. “Torque.”

She handed him the wrench.

The microphone drifted close again. “You worked in orbital materials. Did you want to go?”

Ivo tightened one housing bolt until the mark aligned. “Badly. Then a tide came through Tondo and the evacuation pumps failed. I came back.”

Lani gave him the escape. “Breaker test?”

He nodded. She climbed to the panel. The camera followed her until Ivo raised one hand.

“Stay on the pump.”

“Power in three,” Lani called. “Two. One.”

The motor caught with a rough shudder. Brown water jumped in the intake, foamed, then began to pull. The leak at the seam thinned to a silver thread.

“Pressure?” Ivo called.

“Forty-two and climbing.”

“Forty-eight max on that line.”

“I know.”

“Say it.”

“Forty-eight max.”

At forty-six, Lani throttled the flow. The silver thread held.

Then the bypass valve knocked once.

Ivo looked up.

It knocked again, harder, and the pressure needle snapped to fifty-one.

“Cut it,” he shouted.

Lani hit the breaker. The motor died, but the seam blew before the line settled. Brown water burst sideways across the pit and struck the camera square in the lens. The tally light blurred red through mud.

Mika screamed once. Ivo was already at the rope.

“Behind the blue. Now.”

“I am!”

“Back farther.”

Lani slid down the ladder and landed hard. “Bypass jam.”

“No. Screen plug shifted.” He grabbed the intake hook. “Light.”

The camera operator wiped his lens with a sleeve.

“Do not touch your panel,” Ivo snapped. “Wet hands.”

“My rig is sealed.”

“Your fingers are not.”

The city man had both arms around his slate under his jacket. “Can we still call it functional?”

Ivo stared at him.

“Temporarily stabilized?” the man tried.

“Call it what it is. Failed test. Partial flow. Replacement section required before night tide.”

“That classification delays—”

“Then attach the water.”

The interviewer looked at the mud running down her camera housing. For once she did not ask for a cleaner sentence.

Lani hooked the intake screen and dragged it into view. A wad of gray paint flakes clung to the mesh, mixed with leaves and plastic. Red showed on the underside of one flake.

Mika’s face changed.

Ivo saw it. “Did you scrape the wall today?”

Mika looked at the ground.

“With what?”

“My lunch coin.”

“Mika.”

“It was already coming off.”

“The flakes went into the drain.”

“I wanted the red back.”

The city man made a small sound, almost pity, almost paperwork.

Ivo took the paint wad from the screen. Gray on top, red beneath. It sagged in his glove like wet skin.

Mika said, “Did I break the pump?”

“No,” Ivo said too fast.

Lani looked at him.

He corrected. “You helped clog the intake. The bad gasket broke the seal. The wrong pipe made the pressure ugly. The rain did the rest.”

“That is a lot.”

“Yes.”

“Am I in trouble?”

Ivo looked at the camera, then at the city man’s slate, then back to her. “You are going to help clean the screen from behind the blue line.”

Her chin lifted. “I can do that.”

“No hands in water. Hook only. Lani watches.”

Lani passed a smaller hook across the rope. “Congratulations. Lowest-rank intake assistant.”

Mika took it as if accepting office.

The city man’s slate chimed again. “Sixteen oh three.”

Ivo stripped off one glove and held out his hand. “Give me the pipe-order form.”

The man handed it over. Checkboxes waited: minor present, Boundary-adjacent language, finite Earth slogan, procurement hold.

“If we mark high?” Ivo asked.

“Tomorrow at best.”

“Low?”

“Auto clearance if the transcript does not flag.”

“And if the feed gets used as recruitment?”

The man’s jaw worked. “It will. By someone.”

“Medium,” Ivo said.

“Medium triggers manual.”

“Same-day?”

“If the reviewer accepts repair-primary.”

“Then write that.”

The man typed with wet thumbs. “Medium. Repair instruction primary. Slogan secondary. Child question included. Failed test included. Replacement section urgent.”

The interviewer said, “Failed test makes the site look worse.”

“The site is worse,” Lani said.

Mika worked the hook through the screen with fierce care. A gray paint flake came loose, red underside flashing before it dropped into the bucket.

“Uncle Ivo,” she said. “Mars didn’t do anything wrong.”

“No.”

“Then why paint over it?”

“Because adults get angry at pictures when they cannot punish what hurt them.”

“That’s stupid.”

“Yes.”

“Did you paint the gray?”

“No.”

“Good.”

The words NEAR THINGS FIRST had begun to streak where rain found cheap paint. FIRST bent at the S. It looked less like an order than work left unfinished.

The interviewer wiped water from her microphone. “Witness is approaching the Boundary. Some people say Earth repair is a way to look away from that. What do you say?”

Ivo held up the clogged screen. “Watch. Study. Do not lie about what is up there.”

He dropped the screen into the rinse bucket. Brown water climbed his wrist.

“And fix the intake before the rain comes back.”

“That’s your answer?”

“That is the answer I have today.”

The city man looked at his slate. “Submitted.”

“Approved?”

“Queued.”

“Queued does not hold a floodwall.”

“No.”

The pump knocked once more, softly, from trapped pressure in the line.

Lani crouched at the seam. “Clamp patch?”

Ivo looked at the sky beyond the pit, rain coming harder now. “Clamp patch. Then we clear the screen again. Then we wait for the order like civilized idiots.”

Mika lifted the hook. A red paint chip clung to the tip.

“Pocket or bucket?” she asked.

Ivo looked at it. “Pocket, if it’s dry enough.”

“It isn’t.”

“Then lay it where water will not reach.”

She set the chip on the blue line pail, red side up.

* * *

At 16:52 Manila time, the repair feed cleared medium review and released the pipe order.

The notice reached Witness under three labels: civic repair, school guidance, public-language constraint. Mara opened it in the archive bay. She should have closed the feed. Her queue held Asterion fragments with worse teeth.

On screen, Ivo Tan stood shin-deep in pump water while a city communications worker read from a slate as if each checkbox had a fuse attached: minor present, slogan secondary, failed test included, procurement hold released.

Mara stopped playback on Mika beside the painted-over Mars: gray paint, red bleed, black letters, leaking pipe.

She rolled back nine seconds. The clip marker first made Ivo’s line a slogan, then widened to catch Mika’s question, the torn gasket, Lani’s wet hands, the failed pressure test.

Mara opened a release-language note.

Public release must not classify continued desire for Mars, spaceflight, or outward study as collapse symptom, civic betrayal, or moral failure. Preserve surrounding exchange where possible. Do not isolate slogans from corrections, especially when minors are present.

A ground annotation arrived beneath the feed. Same-day replacement pipe authorized. Pipe-map curriculum could include student-selected Mars material when framed as history, grief, or scientific uncertainty. No door imagery. No exit language.

Mara saved the note.

The ship clicked around her.

A new item opened in her queue without sound.

CANDLE STATIC TEST / PAYLOAD AND INTERIOR REVIEW / TOMAS VALE PRESENCE REQUIRED.

Mara closed Ivo’s feed before the transcript could offer her another clean sentence, and loaded the checklist.
